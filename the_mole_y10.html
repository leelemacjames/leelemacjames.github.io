The Mole

The biggest counting number you'll ever need — and why it exists
Year 10 Physics & Chemistry — Particles, Counting & Probability

The problem

Atoms are extremely small. A single water molecule has a mass of about \(0.000\,000\,000\,000\,000\,000\,000\,03\) grams. Writing numbers like that is exhausting and useless — you can't weigh a single molecule on any balance that exists.

So instead of counting atoms one by one, chemists and physicists use a batch size — a standard "packet" of particles that's big enough to weigh on a normal scale. That batch is called a mole.

This page explains what a mole is, how big it is, why it has the value it does, and why you'll never find all the air on one side of the room even if you wait forever.

§1 Counting in Batches
Why do we count things in groups instead of one by one?▾

You already do this. You don't buy "336 sheets of paper" — you buy a ream (500 sheets). You don't ask for "24 flowers" — you ask for two dozen. You don't count out 1,000 nails — you buy them by the kilogram.

Each of these is a batch size — a convenient number that saves you counting individual items:

Batch nameHow manyUsed for
A pair2Shoes, gloves
A dozen12Eggs, donuts
A gross144 (= 12 × 12)Pencils, buttons
A ream500Paper
A mole602,200,000,000,000,000,000,000Atoms, molecules
A dozen eggs is a convenient batch because you can hold it. A mole of atoms is a convenient batch because you can weigh it — it comes out to a few grams, which fits on a lab balance.
What exactly is a mole?▾

A mole is a specific number of things — just like a dozen is 12. The number is called Avogadro's constant:

\(N_A = 6.022 \times 10^{23}\)

Written out in full, that's:

602,200,000,000,000,000,000,000

That's 602 sextillion. Six hundred million million million. It is, to put it mildly, a lot.

One mole of anything is \(6.022 \times 10^{23}\) of that thing. A mole of carbon atoms. A mole of water molecules. A mole of tennis balls. A mole of moles (the animal). The number is always the same — only the object changes.

The mole is the chemist's dozen. A dozen eggs is 12 eggs. A mole of carbon atoms is \(6.022 \times 10^{23}\) carbon atoms. The only difference is the number is much, much bigger — because atoms are much, much smaller than eggs.
§2 How Big Is \(6 \times 10^{23}\)?
I can't picture \(6 \times 10^{23}\). Can you give me comparisons that actually help?▾

Here are some ways to get a feel for how absurdly large this number is:

Counting: If you counted one atom per second, 24 hours a day, it would take you \(6 \times 10^{23}\) seconds = 19 million billion years. The universe is only 14 billion years old. You'd need about 1.4 million universe-lifetimes to finish counting one mole.
Grains of sand: The total number of grains of sand on all the beaches on Earth is estimated at roughly \(10^{19}\) — that's about 10,000 times less than a mole. A mole of sand grains would cover the Earth's entire surface hundreds of metres deep.
Stacking pennies: A stack of \(6 \times 10^{23}\) 1p coins would be about \(10^{18}\) km tall. The distance to the nearest star is \(4 \times 10^{13}\) km. Your penny stack would reach the nearest star — and then keep going for another 25,000 times that distance.
A glass of water: A 250 mL glass of water contains about \(8.4 \times 10^{24}\) molecules — roughly 14 moles. That many molecules, in your hand, right now. Every sip is a few moles disappearing down your throat.
The point is not to memorise these comparisons — it's to feel, in your gut, that \(10^{23}\) is not just "a big number." It is so far beyond human experience that our normal intuitions about counting, stacking, and distributing simply don't apply.
§3 Why \(6.022 \times 10^{23}\) — and Not Some Other Number?
What's special about Avogadro's number? Why not use \(10^{20}\) or \(10^{30}\)?▾

The number was chosen so that one mole of an element has a mass in grams equal to its atomic mass number.

Carbon has an atomic mass of 12.0 u (atomic mass units). So one mole of carbon atoms weighs exactly 12.0 grams.

Oxygen has an atomic mass of 16.0 u. One mole of oxygen atoms weighs 16.0 grams.

Iron has an atomic mass of 55.8 u. One mole of iron atoms weighs 55.8 grams.

ElementAtomic mass (u)Mass of 1 mole (g)
Hydrogen (H)1.01.0
Carbon (C)12.012.0
Oxygen (O)16.016.0
Iron (Fe)55.855.8
Gold (Au)197.0197.0

This is not a coincidence — it's the definition. The number \(N_A\) was chosen to make this conversion clean. It's the bridge between the atomic scale (mass units) and the lab scale (grams).

A mole of hydrogen (1 g) and a mole of gold (197 g) have exactly the same number of atoms — \(6.022 \times 10^{23}\). The gold atoms are just 197 times heavier than the hydrogen atoms. Same count, different weight.
Water is H₂O — two hydrogen atoms and one oxygen. What is the mass of one mole of water?▾

Add up the atomic masses:

\(M_{\text{water}} = 2 \times 1.0 + 1 \times 16.0 = 18.0\text{ g per mole}\)

So one mole of water molecules (\(6.022 \times 10^{23}\) of them) weighs 18.0 grams — about a tablespoon and a half.

Try it: Carbon dioxide is CO₂. What is the mass of one mole?
Answer: \(12.0 + 2 \times 16.0 = 44.0\) g per mole.
Try it: You have 36 g of water. How many moles is that? How many molecules?
Answer: \(36 / 18 = 2.0\) moles = \(2.0 \times 6.022 \times 10^{23} = 1.2 \times 10^{24}\) molecules.
§4 Doing the Maths
How do you convert between grams, moles, and number of particles?▾

There are just two conversion steps:

\(\text{mass (g)} \xrightarrow{\div \text{ molar mass}} \text{moles} \xrightarrow{\times N_A} \text{number of particles}\)

And in reverse:

\(\text{number of particles} \xrightarrow{\div N_A} \text{moles} \xrightarrow{\times \text{ molar mass}} \text{mass (g)}\)

Key formulas:

\(n = \dfrac{\text{mass}}{M} \qquad \qquad N = n \times N_A\)

where \(n\) = number of moles, \(M\) = molar mass (g/mol), \(N\) = number of particles.

How many atoms are in a 1p coin? (A 1p coin is made of copper-plated steel and weighs about 3.6 g. Assume it's pure iron, Fe, molar mass 55.8 g/mol.)▾

Step 1 — Mass to moles:

\(n = \dfrac{3.6}{55.8} = 0.0645\text{ mol}\)

Step 2 — Moles to atoms:

\(N = 0.0645 \times 6.022 \times 10^{23} = 3.9 \times 10^{22}\text{ atoms}\)

That's 39 thousand million million million atoms in a single penny. Every one of them is vibrating right now.

A chemistry student has \(1.5 \times 10^{24}\) atoms of aluminium (Al, molar mass 27.0 g/mol). What mass is this?▾

Step 1 — Atoms to moles:

\(n = \dfrac{1.5 \times 10^{24}}{6.022 \times 10^{23}} = 2.49\text{ mol}\)

Step 2 — Moles to grams:

\(\text{mass} = n \times M = 2.49 \times 27.0 = 67.2\text{ g}\)

That's about the mass of a tennis ball — made entirely of aluminium atoms.

§5 Why Doesn't All the Air Rush to One Side of the Room?
Each air molecule moves randomly. Could they all, by chance, end up on the left side of the room?▾

In principle — yes. There's no law of physics that forbids it. But let's work out the probability.

Start simple. Imagine just 2 particles in a box. Each particle is equally likely to be on the left half or the right half. The probability that both are on the left:

\(p = \dfrac{1}{2} \times \dfrac{1}{2} = \dfrac{1}{4} = 25\%\)

Not bad. Now try 10 particles:

\(p = \left(\dfrac{1}{2}\right)^{10} = \dfrac{1}{1024} \approx 0.1\%\)

Unlikely, but you might see it if you waited a while. Now try 100 particles:

\(p = \left(\dfrac{1}{2}\right)^{100} = \dfrac{1}{2^{100}} \approx \dfrac{1}{10^{30}}\)

That's a 1 in a million million million million million chance. And a room full of air has not 100 particles but about \(10^{26}\).

OK so what's the actual probability for a real room?▾

A typical room contains roughly \(N \approx 10^{26}\) air molecules. The probability that all of them happen to be on the left half at the same instant:

\(p = \left(\dfrac{1}{2}\right)^{10^{26}}\)

How small is this? Taking the logarithm:

\(\log_{10}(p) = 10^{26} \times \log_{10}(1/2) \approx -3 \times 10^{25}\)

So the probability is \(10^{-3 \times 10^{25}}\). That's a decimal point followed by 30 million billion billion zeros and then a 1.

Let's put that in perspective:

The age of the universe is about \(10^{17}\) seconds. Even if the molecules rearranged themselves a trillion times per second, and you waited a trillion universe-lifetimes, you would not come remotely close to seeing this happen. The number of "tries" you'd get (\(\sim 10^{41}\)) is laughably small compared to the number you'd need (\(10^{3 \times 10^{25}}\)).
This is why the second law of thermodynamics works. It's not that disorder is compulsory — it's that order is so spectacularly improbable that it never happens by chance. The air could all go to one side. It just won't — not in any conceivable timeframe, not even close.

The probability doesn't decrease gradually — it collapses. By the time you reach 100 particles, the event is already effectively impossible. By the time you reach a mole (\(10^{23}\)), the word "impossible" doesn't do it justice. This exponential collapse is the mathematical engine behind everything from thermodynamics to the arrow of time.
§6 A Mole of Moles 🐾
What if "mole" means the animal? How much would a mole of moles weigh?▾

A mole (the animal, Talpa europaea) weighs about 100 g = 0.1 kg.

A mole (the number) of moles (the animal) is:

\(6.022 \times 10^{23} \text{ moles (animals)} \times 0.1\text{ kg each}\)
\(= 6.0 \times 10^{22}\text{ kg}\)

Now — how heavy is the Moon?

\(M_{\text{Moon}} = 7.35 \times 10^{22}\text{ kg}\)

Compare:

ObjectMass (kg)
A mole of moles 🐾\(6.0 \times 10^{22}\)
The Moon 🌕\(7.35 \times 10^{22}\)
\(\dfrac{\text{Mole of moles}}{\text{Moon}} = \dfrac{6.0 \times 10^{22}}{7.35 \times 10^{22}} \approx 0.82\)

A mole of moles weighs about 82% of the Moon.

The fact that Avogadro's number of 100-gram animals is comparable to the mass of the Moon tells you something profound about the scale of \(N_A\). It bridges the gap between the everyday world (a small furry animal you can hold) and the astronomical (a celestial body visible in the night sky). That's exactly its job — to bridge the gap between atoms and grams.
What about other absurd things — a mole of footballs? A mole of grains of rice?▾

A mole of footballs:

A football has a diameter of about 22 cm (volume ≈ 5.6 litres = \(5.6 \times 10^{-3}\) m³).

\(\text{Total volume} = 6 \times 10^{23} \times 5.6 \times 10^{-3} \approx 3.4 \times 10^{21}\text{ m}^3\)

The volume of the Earth is \(1.08 \times 10^{21}\) m³. So a mole of footballs would fill about 3 Earths.

A mole of grains of rice:

A grain of rice weighs about 0.03 g.

\(\text{Total mass} = 6 \times 10^{23} \times 0.03\text{ g} = 1.8 \times 10^{22}\text{ g} = 1.8 \times 10^{19}\text{ kg}\)

The mass of the Earth is \(6 \times 10^{24}\) kg. A mole of rice grains weighs about \(1/300{,}000\) of the Earth — still an absurd amount of rice, but the Earth is heavier because each grain of rice is much lighter than each atom's molar-mass equivalent in grams.

Your turn: Estimate the mass of "a mole of students." Assume an average student mass of 55 kg. How does it compare to the mass of Jupiter (\(1.9 \times 10^{27}\) kg)?

Answer: \(6 \times 10^{23} \times 55 = 3.3 \times 10^{25}\) kg. Jupiter is \(1.9 \times 10^{27}\) kg, so a mole of students is about \(1.7\%\) of Jupiter — roughly 60 times lighter.
§7 Why Should I Care?
What's the point of all this for physics?▾

Three reasons the mole matters beyond chemistry class:

1. It connects the invisible to the measurable. You can't weigh one atom, but you can weigh a mole of atoms — and that gives you all the information you need. The gas law \(pV = nRT\) uses \(n\) (moles) to connect pressure, volume, and temperature to the actual number of molecules.

2. It explains why thermodynamics works. The probability argument in §5 shows that with \(10^{23}\) particles, "unlikely" becomes "impossible." This is why heat flows from hot to cold, why gases expand to fill their container, and why you can't unscramble an egg. The mole-scale number of particles makes statistics into certainty.

3. It sets the scale of everyday matter. A mole of water is a tablespoon. A mole of iron is a small nail. A mole of air fills about 24 litres at room temperature (a bit more than a basketball). These are human-sized quantities, which is the whole point — the mole was invented to make atoms accessible.

The mole is not a difficult concept. It's just a number — a very large number, chosen so that atoms become weighable. Everything else (molar mass, Avogadro's constant, gas laws) flows from that one practical choice.
