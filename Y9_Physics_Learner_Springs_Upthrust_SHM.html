Section 1

Hooke's Law

The extension of a spring is directly proportional to the applied force — as long as the elastic limit is not exceeded.

F = k x F = force (N) · k = spring constant (N m⁻¹) · x = extension (m) E = ½ k x² elastic potential energy (J)

Spring Constant k

The gradient of an F–x graph. Steeper = stiffer spring = larger k. Units: N m⁻¹.

Elastic Limit

Beyond this point the spring is permanently deformed. Hooke's law no longer holds; the F–x graph curves away from the origin.

Work done = area

The area under an F–x graph equals the elastic PE stored — a triangle = ½ × base × height = ½Fx = ½kx².

Springs in Series & Parallel

Combining springs changes the effective spring constant of the system — often in a counterintuitive direction.

Springs in Series (end-to-end)

k_eff = ?
1/k_eff = 1/k₁ + 1/k₂ for two springs in series

Why weaker?

Each spring stretches independently. The same force F causes extension x₁ in spring 1 and x₂ in spring 2. Total extension = x₁ + x₂ — so you get more extension for the same force. More extension per newton = smaller k.

Worked example
  1. k₁ = 100 N m⁻¹, k₂ = 100 N m⁻¹ in series
  2. 1/k_eff = 1/100 + 1/100 = 2/100
  3. k_eff = 100/2 = 50 N m⁻¹
  4. Two identical springs in series → half the spring constant

Springs in Parallel (side-by-side)

k_eff = ?
k_eff = k₁ + k₂ for two springs in parallel

Why stiffer?

Both springs share the load. Each only carries F/2. But both experience the same extension x. The combined force for extension x is k₁x + k₂x = (k₁+k₂)x — so the system behaves as a single spring with k_eff = k₁ + k₂.

Upthrust & Archimedes' Principle

Any object submerged in a fluid experiences a net upward force — because pressure increases with depth, so the upward force on the bottom face always exceeds the downward force on the top.

F_up = V · ρ_fluid · g V = submerged volume (m³) · ρ = fluid density (kg m⁻³) P = hρg pressure at depth h

Archimedes' Principle

The upthrust equals the weight of fluid displaced. So F_up = V_submerged × ρ_fluid × g.

Key insight

Upthrust depends only on the volume of fluid displaced — not on the object's density, material, or weight.

Depth doesn't matter

Once fully submerged, pushing the object deeper does not increase the upthrust — the pressure difference ΔP = ρgΔh stays the same regardless of depth.

Upthrust does not depend on how deep the object is (once fully submerged). It depends only on the volume of fluid displaced and the density of that fluid.

Worked example — steel sphere in water
  1. Volume of sphere = 50 cm³ = 50 × 10⁻⁶ m³
  2. Upthrust = V × ρ_water × g = 50×10⁻⁶ × 1000 × 10
  3. Upthrust = 0.50 N
  4. Weight of steel sphere ≈ 3.9 N → net downward → sphere sinks

Floating & Sinking

An object floats when upthrust exactly equals its weight. The fraction submerged equals the ratio of densities.

submerged fraction = ρ_object / ρ_fluid floats when ρ_object < ρ_fluid

Wood in water (ρ = 700)

Fraction submerged = 700/1000 = 70%. The wood displaces 70% of its volume of water, generating upthrust = weight.

Ice in water (ρ = 917)

Fraction submerged = 917/1000 = 91.7%. About 8% of an iceberg is visible — matching the classic "9/10 below surface" rule.

Wood in meths (ρ = 800)

Fraction = 700/800 = 87.5%. The same block sinks deeper in a less dense fluid — more volume needed to displace enough weight.

Iron in water (ρ = 7800)

Fraction = 7800/1000 = 7.8 — greater than 1. Can't float. Even fully submerged the upthrust is less than the weight.

When ice melts in water, the water level does not rise. Floating ice displaces exactly its own mass of water. When it melts it becomes that same mass of water — precisely filling the volume it displaced.

Why does a steel ship float?

The ship's average density (steel hull + enclosed air) is less than water. The large air volume means the ship displaces a huge volume of water before fully submerging — enough to equal its weight.

Ballast tanks

A submarine pumps water in (heavier → sinks) or out (lighter → rises). At hover, weight = upthrust exactly.

Ship calculation
  1. Ship mass M = 8 × 10⁶ kg, seawater ρ = 1025 kg m⁻³
  2. For floating: Upthrust = Weight → V_disp × 1025 × 10 = M × 10
  3. V_disp = M / ρ_sea = 8×10⁶ / 1025 ≈ 7 805 m³
  4. Remove 2×10⁶ kg of cargo → new V_disp = 6×10⁶/1025 ≈ 5 854 m³ → ship rises

Energy in SHM

Kinetic and elastic potential energy exchange continuously. Their sum — total mechanical energy — remains constant in ideal (undamped) SHM.

E_total = ½kA² PE = ½kx² KE = ½k(A²−x²) KE = PE when x = A/√2 ≈ 0.707A

At equilibrium (x = 0)

PE = 0. All energy is kinetic. Speed is at its maximum = ωA.

At amplitude (x = ±A)

KE = 0. All energy is elastic PE = ½kA². Velocity = 0 momentarily.

KE = PE crossover

Occurs at x = A/√2 ≈ 0.707A. Half the total energy is kinetic, half is potential.
