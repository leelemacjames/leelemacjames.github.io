Linearisation

Making curved relationships confess their secrets
IB Physics SL/HL — Measurement, Data Processing & Experimental Technique
§1 Why Linearise?
Why not just draw the curve and be done with it? ▾

Because a straight line is the only graph you can fully characterise by eye. You can:

• Judge whether data fits it (deviations from a straight line are obvious; deviations from a curve are ambiguous).

• Read off two numbers — gradient and intercept — that encode the physics.

• Estimate uncertainties by drawing max/min lines of best fit.

A curve tells you "something is happening". A straight line tells you exactly what.

Linearisation is not a mathematical trick — it is an experimental strategy. You reshape the data so that the physics falls out of the gradient and intercept of \(y = mx + c\).
What is the general strategy for linearising a relationship? ▾

Start with the theoretical equation. Rearrange it into the form:

\(\underbrace{(\text{plot on } y\text{-axis})}_{y} = \underbrace{(\text{physics constant})}_{m} \times \underbrace{(\text{plot on } x\text{-axis})}_{x} + \underbrace{(\text{maybe zero})}_{c}\)

The variables you measure appear as \(y\) and \(x\). The constants you want to find appear as \(m\) (gradient) or \(c\) (intercept).

The key decision is what to plot on each axis. You might need to square a variable, take its reciprocal, or take its logarithm — whatever turns the equation into \(y = mx + c\).

§2 Power Laws: \(y = kx^n\)
How do you linearise a power law like \(T = k\ell^{1/2}\) (pendulum period)? ▾

The pendulum period is \(T = 2\pi\sqrt{\ell/g}\), which has the form \(T \propto \ell^{1/2}\). There are two approaches:

Approach 1 — Square it:

\(T^2 = \frac{4\pi^2}{g}\,\ell\)

Plot \(T^2\) vs \(\ell\). Gradient \(= 4\pi^2/g\). Intercept \(= 0\).

Approach 2 — Log both sides:

\(\ln T = \tfrac{1}{2}\ln\ell + \ln\!\left(\frac{2\pi}{\sqrt{g}}\right)\)

Plot \(\ln T\) vs \(\ln\ell\). Gradient \(= \tfrac{1}{2}\) (confirms the power law). Intercept gives \(g\).

Approach 1 is cleaner when you already know the power. Approach 2 is better when you're testing what the power is — the gradient tells you the exponent directly.
Intensity follows an inverse-square law: \(I = \frac{P}{4\pi d^2}\). How would you verify this experimentally? ▾

Rearrange:

\(I = \frac{P}{4\pi}\cdot\frac{1}{d^2}\)

Plot \(I\) vs \(1/d^2\). If the law holds, you get a straight line through the origin with gradient \(P/(4\pi)\).

Alternatively, take logs:

\(\ln I = -2\ln d + \ln\!\left(\frac{P}{4\pi}\right)\)

Plot \(\ln I\) vs \(\ln d\). Gradient should be \(-2\). If you get \(-1.87\) or \(-2.15\), you've found evidence that the law isn't perfectly inverse-square — perhaps absorption or reflection is at play.

The log-log approach is more powerful here: it tests the exponent rather than assuming it. IB examiners love asking "what would you plot to verify the relationship?" — the answer is nearly always a log-log graph when the power is under investigation.
Boyle's Law says \(pV = \text{const}\). What are the linearisation options? ▾

\(pV = k\) is a power law with exponent \(-1\): \(p = k \cdot V^{-1}\).

Option 1: Plot \(p\) vs \(1/V\). Gradient \(= k = nRT\). Intercept \(= 0\).

Option 2: Plot \(pV\) vs \(V\) (or vs \(p\)). If Boyle's law holds, the graph is a horizontal line at height \(k\). Deviations from horizontal reveal non-ideal behaviour.

Option 3: \(\ln p = -\ln V + \ln k\). Plot \(\ln p\) vs \(\ln V\). Gradient \(= -1\).

Option 2 is under-used but elegant — it amplifies deviations. A curve on a \(p\) vs \(1/V\) graph might look straight; the same data on a \(pV\) vs \(V\) graph will show the wobble clearly.
§3 Exponential Relationships: \(y = Ae^{kx}\)
How do you linearise an exponential decay like \(N = N_0 e^{-\lambda t}\)? ▾

Take the natural log of both sides:

\(\ln N = \ln N_0 - \lambda t\)

This is \(y = c + mx\) with:

RoleExpressionWhat it tells you
\(y\)\(\ln N\)Plot this on the vertical axis
\(x\)\(t\)Plot this on the horizontal axis
\(m\)\(-\lambda\)Gradient gives the decay constant
\(c\)\(\ln N_0\)Intercept gives the initial count (after taking \(e^c\))

From \(\lambda\), the half-life follows: \(t_{1/2} = \ln 2 / \lambda\).

This is the single most common linearisation in IB Physics. It appears in radioactive decay, capacitor discharge, Beer-Lambert attenuation, and cooling curves. Learn this one cold.
A capacitor discharges through a resistor: \(V = V_0 e^{-t/RC}\). How do you extract \(RC\) from experimental data? ▾

Take logs:

\(\ln V = \ln V_0 - \frac{1}{RC}\,t\)

Plot \(\ln V\) vs \(t\). The gradient is \(-1/RC\), so:

\(RC = -\frac{1}{\text{gradient}}\)

Worked example: Your ln-V vs t graph gives a gradient of \(-0.25\) s⁻¹. Then \(RC = 1/0.25 = 4.0\) s. If \(R = 10\) kΩ, then \(C = 4.0/10{,}000 = 400\) μF.

Common mistake: plotting \(\log_{10} V\) instead of \(\ln V\). The gradient becomes \(-1/(RC \cdot \ln 10)\) — an unnecessary factor of 2.303. Stick to natural logs for exponential relationships.
What about relationships like \(y = A(1 - e^{-t/\tau})\) — exponential approach to a limit? ▾

This appears in capacitor charging and terminal velocity. You can't take the log directly because of the \((1 - \cdots)\) structure. Rearrange first:

\(A - y = Ae^{-t/\tau} \implies \ln(A - y) = \ln A - \frac{t}{\tau}\)

Plot \(\ln(A - y)\) vs \(t\). Gradient \(= -1/\tau\).

For terminal velocity: \(v = v_{\text{term}}(1 - e^{-t/\tau})\), so plot \(\ln(v_{\text{term}} - v)\) vs \(t\). You need to know (or estimate) \(v_{\text{term}}\) first — read it off as the plateau value from the raw \(v\) vs \(t\) graph.

The trick is always: isolate the exponential, then take the log. Don't try to take the log of a sum.
§4 The Two Master Plots
When do I use log-log, and when do I use ln-linear? ▾

This is the single most important decision in linearisation:

RelationshipPlot typeWhat gradient gives
\(y = kx^n\) (power law)\(\ln y\) vs \(\ln x\)The exponent \(n\)
\(y = Ae^{kx}\) (exponential)\(\ln y\) vs \(x\)The rate constant \(k\)

How to tell them apart from raw data:

• If the curve passes through the origin and bends — try a power law (log-log).

• If the curve has a non-zero intercept and bends — try an exponential (ln-linear).

• If in doubt, try both. Only one will give a straight line.

Log-log says "the variables are related by a power". Ln-linear says "one variable is in the exponent". These are fundamentally different physics — log-log is geometry (\(A \propto r^2\)), ln-linear is process (\(N \propto e^{-\lambda t}\)).
§5 Extracting Physics from the Graph
How do you correctly read a gradient and intercept — with units? ▾

The gradient has units of \([\text{y-axis}] / [\text{x-axis}]\). The intercept has units of \([\text{y-axis}]\).

Example: You plot \(T^2\) (s²) vs \(\ell\) (m) for a pendulum. Gradient has units s²/m. You know gradient \(= 4\pi^2/g\), so:

\(g = \frac{4\pi^2}{\text{gradient}} \quad [\text{m s}^{-2}] \quad \checkmark\)

On a log graph: both axes are dimensionless (you're plotting \(\ln(\text{number})\)), so the gradient is a pure number. But it represents a physical quantity — the exponent, the decay constant, etc. Be explicit about what it means.

IB examiners penalise gradients calculated from data points rather than from the line of best fit. Use two points on the line, as far apart as possible. Never use your original data points unless they happen to lie exactly on the line.
How do you handle uncertainties on a linearised graph? ▾

The IB method:

1. Plot the data with error bars. (For \(\ln y\), the error bar is \(\Delta(\ln y) \approx \Delta y / y\) — from the fractional uncertainty.)

2. Draw the best-fit line through the error bars.

3. Draw the steepest and shallowest lines that still pass through most error bars.

4. The uncertainty in the gradient is:

\(\Delta m = \frac{m_{\max} - m_{\min}}{2}\)

5. Propagate this into your final answer. If gradient \(= -\lambda\), then \(\Delta\lambda = |\Delta m|\), and:

\(\Delta t_{1/2} = t_{1/2} \times \frac{\Delta\lambda}{\lambda}\)
Error bars on a \(\ln y\) axis are asymmetric in raw units but symmetric in log units. This is a feature, not a bug — it correctly represents the fact that multiplicative uncertainties become additive after taking logs.

§7 Common Pitfalls
What are the mistakes that lose marks? ▾

1. Forgetting to transform the units. If you plot \(T^2\), the y-axis unit is s², not s. If you plot \(\ln V\), the axis is dimensionless — label it "\(\ln(V/\text{V})\)" or just "\(\ln V\)" with a note.

2. Using data points instead of the line. The gradient comes from the best-fit line, not from \(\Delta y / \Delta x\) between two data points.

3. Confusing \(\ln\) and \(\log_{10}\). For exponentials, use \(\ln\) (base \(e\)). If you use \(\log_{10}\), your gradient is off by a factor of \(\ln 10 \approx 2.303\). The IB expects \(\ln\).

4. Not checking the intercept. If your theory predicts a zero intercept (e.g. \(T^2\) vs \(\ell\)), a non-zero intercept reveals systematic error — perhaps a timing offset or a non-zero effective length at the pivot.

5. Plotting \(\ln y\) vs \(\ln x\) when you should plot \(\ln y\) vs \(x\). The first tests a power law; the second tests an exponential. They are different hypotheses about the physics.

The most devastating mistake is plotting the wrong variables. Before touching a ruler, write down the theoretical equation, identify the form (\(y = mx + c\)), and label your axes. Then plot.
Σ The Method

Step 1. Write down the theoretical equation.

Step 2. Identify the form: power law (\(y = kx^n\)) or exponential (\(y = Ae^{kx}\)).

Step 3. Transform: square, reciprocal, or \(\ln\) — whatever produces \(y = mx + c\).

Step 4. Calculate your transformed data in a table.

Step 5. Plot with error bars. Draw best-fit, max, and min lines.

Step 6. Read gradient and intercept from the line (not from data points).

Step 7. Extract the physics. State the result with uncertainty and units.

Linearisation is the physicist's confession: we only trust straight lines. Everything else — exponentials, power laws, oscillations — must be rewritten until a straight line appears. The line is the interface between theory and measurement.
