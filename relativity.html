01 · What survives a change of frameFrom Galileo to the Interval

Relativity, in both its versions, is an argument about which quantities every observer agrees on. Galileo's answer and Einstein's answer differ in exactly one place, and that single difference reorganises the whole of mechanics.

Galileo's bargain

Picture two observers, one standing on a platform and one gliding past at constant velocity $v$. Galileo asks that they share a single universal clock and a single notion of length, so that to convert one observer's coordinates into the other's you simply slide the spatial origin along while time runs identically for both:

$$x' = x - vt,\qquad t' = t.$$

Under this transformation, two things are held sacred. Time intervals are absolute, since $\Delta t' = \Delta t$ for everyone, and the spatial distance between events measured at the same time is the same in every frame. Velocities then simply add, $u' = u - v$, which is the ordinary common sense of throwing a ball forward on a moving train.

The crack in the bargain

Maxwell's equations deliver a definite speed for light, $c = 1/\sqrt{\mu_0\varepsilon_0}$, with no reference to any source or observer. Galilean velocity addition insists that speed should change from frame to frame, so either electromagnetism secretly singles out one special frame, the luminiferous aether, or the rule $t'=t$ is wrong. The experiments in tab 03 force the second option.

Einstein's replacement

Einstein keeps the principle of relativity, that the laws of physics read the same in every inertial frame, and adds a second postulate that the speed of light in vacuum is the same $c$ for every inertial observer regardless of the motion of the source. Holding $c$ fixed forces time and space to mix, and the transformation that does the mixing is the Lorentz transformation, written here with $\beta = v/c$ and the Lorentz factor $\gamma = 1/\sqrt{1-\beta^2}$:

$$x' = \gamma\,(x - vt),\qquad ct' = \gamma\,(ct - \beta x).$$

Notice that $t'$ now depends on $x$; the moving observer's clock reading is contaminated by position, which is the seed of every effect that follows. Galileo is not discarded, since setting $\beta \ll 1$ returns $\gamma \to 1$ and the old equations reappear, so the familiar world is simply the low-speed corner of a larger structure.

The quantity everyone agrees on

If neither $\Delta t$ nor $\Delta x$ is absolute any longer, the natural question is whether anything is. There is, and it is the combination that the Lorentz transformation leaves untouched, the spacetime interval between two events:

$$\Delta s^{2} \;=\; (c\,\Delta t)^{2} - (\Delta x)^{2} \;=\; (c\,\Delta t')^{2} - (\Delta x')^{2}.$$

The sign of $\Delta s^2$ sorts every pair of events into one of three families, and this classification is itself frame-independent, which is why it can carry the entire causal structure of the universe. 02 · Geometry of eventsWhen the Axes Learn to Lean

A spacetime diagram plots an event by where and when it happened, with time running up the page and space across it. The whole conceptual jump from Galileo to Einstein can be read off how a second observer's axes are drawn onto the first observer's chart.

By convention we put $ct$ on the vertical axis so that both axes carry the same units and the path of a light ray sits at a tidy $45^\circ$. A stationary object traces a vertical worldline; a moving object leans over, and because nothing outruns light, every physical worldline stays steeper than the light line.

Galileo — a shear

The moving observer's time axis tilts, because their origin drifts through space, but their lines of simultaneity stay flat. Everyone shares the same horizontal "now," so the diagram is merely sheared and time remains absolute.

Einstein — a scissor

Holding $c$ fixed forces both axes to rotate toward the $45^\circ$ light line by the same angle, $\tan\theta = \beta$. The space axis lifts as the time axis leans, so "now" tilts and simultaneity becomes a matter of who is asking.

Switch the model on the diagram below to feel the difference directly. In Galilean mode the $x'$ axis stays horizontal; in Einstein mode it scissors up to meet the leaning $ct'$ axis, and the dashed line of simultaneity through your event tilts with it. The two hyperbolae are calibration curves, the loci $\,(ct)^2 - x^2 = \pm 1$, which mark off a unit tick along the skewed axes and explain why the leaning rulers and clocks appear stretched.

Reading the dashed line

The dashed line through your event collects all the events the moving observer calls simultaneous with it. In Galilean mode it is horizontal and matches S exactly; in Einstein mode it tilts, so two observers passing through the same event no longer agree on what is happening "right now" elsewhere. That single tilt is the subject of tab 04.

03 · Why we believe itThe Evidence on the Bench and in the Sky

A theory this strange earns its place only by surviving measurement, and special relativity arrived already cornered by three independent lines of evidence, one optical, one astronomical, and one woven into electromagnetism itself.

Michelson and Morley — the aether refuses to appear

If light travelled through a stationary aether, then the Earth ploughing through it at orbital speed should produce an "aether wind," and a beam sent along the wind would take a measurably different time from a beam sent across it. Michelson and Morley split a single beam down two perpendicular arms, recombined them, and looked for the interference fringes to shift as the apparatus was slowly rotated.

Muons — the clock in the sky runs slow

Cosmic rays striking the upper atmosphere create muons roughly $15\ \mathrm{km}$ up, moving at close to $c$, with a mean lifetime at rest of only $\tau_0 = 2.2\ \mu\mathrm{s}$. In a world without time dilation a muon would travel only a few hundred metres before decaying, so almost none should reach sea level, yet detectors at the surface count them in abundance.

Relativity resolves the count in two mutually consistent ways. From the ground the muon's internal clock is dilated to $\gamma\tau_0$, so it survives long enough to cross the atmosphere; from the muon's own frame the atmosphere is length-contracted to a thin slab of thickness $h/\gamma$, which it crosses in its ordinary short lifetime. Both descriptions predict the same survival fraction, $N/N_0 = e^{-h/(\gamma\, v\, \tau_0)}$, and the calculator lets you watch the classical and relativistic predictions diverge.

Electromagnetism — relativity was hiding in Maxwell all along

The deepest evidence is theoretical. Maxwell's $c$ is fixed by constants of the vacuum and knows nothing of who is watching, which is precisely the second postulate stated in the language of fields. More striking still, magnetism turns out to be electricity seen from a moving frame: a current-carrying wire is electrically neutral to an observer at rest, yet to a charge drifting alongside it the length contraction of the moving lattice unbalances the charge densities, producing a net electric force that the stationary observer had been calling "magnetic." The two fields are one object viewed from two frames, which is why they transform into each other and why relativity is not an addition to electromagnetism so much as its hidden grammar.

04 · The relativity of nowSimultaneity Comes Apart

Of all the consequences, the relativity of simultaneity is the one that most offends intuition and the one from which length contraction and the paradoxes both descend, since it says that the very notion of "two things happening at the same time" depends on who is moving.

Einstein's train

A train moves along an embankment, and lightning strikes both ends of the train at once according to an observer standing at the midpoint of the embankment, who sees the two flashes arrive together. An observer seated at the middle of the train is rushing toward the front flash and away from the rear one, so the light from the front reaches them first; for the train observer the front strike happened before the rear strike. Neither observer is mistaken, because each is correctly reporting the order of events in their own frame, and there is no privileged frame to overrule them.

The rule of leading clocks

Quantitatively, if a row of clocks is synchronised in its own rest frame and then observed from a frame moving along the row, the clocks toward the front read behind those at the rear by an amount $\Delta t = vL/c^2$ per length $L$. This "leading clocks lag" rule is just the position-dependent term $-\beta x$ sitting inside the Lorentz transformation for $t'$, and once you accept it the famous paradoxes stop being paradoxical, because each apparent contradiction is two observers disagreeing about which events were simultaneous.

Where contraction comes from

To measure a moving rod's length you mark both ends at the same instant, but "the same instant" is exactly the thing two frames disagree about. Length contraction is therefore not a force squeezing the rod; it is the geometric shadow of measuring its two ends along a tilted line of simultaneity.

05 · Apparent contradictionsTwo Paradoxes, Both Resolved

A relativistic paradox is a story engineered to make two frames seem to contradict each other; the resolution is always that the frames disagree about simultaneity, never about any event that actually occurs, so the physics stays single-valued.

The pole and the barn

A runner carries a pole of rest length $5\ \mathrm{m}$ toward a barn of rest length $4\ \mathrm{m}$ with both doors open, running fast enough that $\gamma = 1.25$. In the barn's frame the pole is contracted to exactly $4\ \mathrm{m}$ and momentarily fits, so a farmer could slam both doors shut at the same instant with the pole entirely inside. In the runner's frame the barn is the thing contracted, down to $3.2\ \mathrm{m}$, and the $5\ \mathrm{m}$ pole can never be enclosed, which appears to flatly contradict the farmer.

Both are right, because the two door-slams are simultaneous only in the barn's frame. Scrub the animation and watch the door events: in the runner's frame the far door opens again before the near door closes, so there is never a moment when both doors are shut and the pole is trapped, and the contradiction dissolves into a disagreement about timing.

The travelling twin

One twin flies to a distant star at high speed and returns, while the other stays on Earth, and they reunite to find the traveller younger. The puzzle is the apparent symmetry, since each twin sees the other recede and might claim the other's clock ran slow, yet only one ends up younger and the situation cannot be symmetric after all.

The asymmetry is real and lives in the turnaround. The stay-at-home twin occupies a single inertial frame throughout, drawing one straight worldline, whereas the traveller must accelerate to come back and so switches frames, drawing a bent worldline. In spacetime geometry the straight path between two events carries the greatest proper time, so the bent path simply logs fewer ticks; at the turn the traveller's line of simultaneity swings across a large slab of the Earth twin's history, which is the geometric bookkeeping for the years the traveller "skips."

The common thread

Both stories are defused by the same move. Identify the events that one frame calls simultaneous, notice that the other frame disagrees, and the contradiction evaporates. Simultaneity is the hinge on which all of special relativity turns.
