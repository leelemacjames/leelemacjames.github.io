Pressure & Gases

Why things push, squeeze, pop, and float
Year 10 Physics — Forces, Particles & Matter

What this is about

You already know that gases fill their container, that balloons pop if you squeeze them too hard, and that a bike pump gets hot when you use it. This page explains why all of that happens, using one idea: gas is made of tiny particles that move fast and bounce off things.

We'll start with something you can feel — weight on a surface — and build up to the gas laws that explain everything from weather to engines.

§1 What Is Pressure?
What does "pressure" actually mean?▾

Pressure is how spread out a force is. It's the force acting on each square metre of a surface:

\(\text{pressure} = \dfrac{\text{force}}{\text{area}} \qquad p = \dfrac{F}{A}\)

Units: pascals (Pa). 1 Pa = 1 N/m².

The same force creates more pressure on a small area than on a large one. That's why a drawing pin hurts your thumb — the same push is concentrated onto a tiny point.

Same person, same weight — but the stiletto heel exerts 200× more pressure than the flat shoe. Pressure is about concentration, not strength.
A 60 kg student stands on both feet. Each foot has an area of 150 cm². What pressure do they exert on the floor?▾

Step 1 — Find the weight (force):

\(F = mg = 60 \times 10 = 600\text{ N}\)

Step 2 — Find the total area (both feet):

\(A = 2 \times 150 = 300\text{ cm}^2 = 300 \div 10\,000 = 0.030\text{ m}^2\)

Step 3 — Calculate pressure:

\(p = \dfrac{F}{A} = \dfrac{600}{0.030} = 20\,000\text{ Pa} = 20\text{ kPa}\)
Try it yourself: What happens to the pressure if they stand on one foot instead? (Answer: the area halves, so the pressure doubles to 40 kPa.)
A brick weighs 30 N. It can be placed on three different faces: 20 cm × 10 cm, 20 cm × 6 cm, or 10 cm × 6 cm. Which face gives the highest pressure?▾
FaceAreaArea (m²)Pressure (Pa)
20 × 10 cm200 cm²0.02030 / 0.020 = 1 500
20 × 6 cm120 cm²0.01230 / 0.012 = 2 500
10 × 6 cm60 cm²0.006030 / 0.0060 = 5 000

The smallest face gives the highest pressure — the same weight is concentrated onto less area.

Common mistake: Forgetting to convert cm² to m². Remember: 1 m² = 10 000 cm². Divide your cm² value by 10 000 to get m².
What is atmospheric pressure, and how big is it?▾

The atmosphere is a thick blanket of air above us. Its weight pushes down on every surface. The pressure at sea level is about:

\(p_{\text{atm}} \approx 100\,000\text{ Pa} = 100\text{ kPa}\)

That's 100 000 N pressing on every square metre — equivalent to the weight of a small car sitting on your desk.

Why doesn't it crush you? Because the pressure pushes equally from all sides (including from inside your body), so the forces cancel.

When you use a suction cup, you're not "sucking" it on — you're pushing the air out from behind it, so the atmospheric pressure on the front has nothing to balance it. Atmospheric pressure does the holding.
§2 Why Do Gases Have Pressure?
What are gas particles doing, and how does that create pressure?▾

Gas is made of incredibly tiny particles (atoms or molecules) that are:

• Moving very fast — hundreds of metres per second at room temperature.

• Moving in random directions — no pattern, just chaos.

• Bouncing off the walls of their container and off each other.

Each time a particle bounces off a wall, it pushes the wall a tiny bit. Billions of particles bouncing billions of times per second add up to a smooth, steady force — and force ÷ area = pressure.

You can't feel individual molecular hits (they're too small and too fast), but you can feel the combined effect — that's what you're sensing when you feel air pressure or when a balloon pushes back against your squeeze.
What happens to the pressure if you heat the gas (but keep the container the same size)?▾

Heating a gas makes the particles move faster. Faster particles:

• Hit the walls harder (more force per collision).

• Hit the walls more often (they cross the container faster).

Both effects increase the pressure.

\(\text{Higher temperature} \rightarrow \text{faster particles} \rightarrow \text{more pressure}\)
Real-world example: A sealed aerosol can says "Do not expose to temperatures above 50°C." Why? Because heating increases the gas pressure inside. If the pressure gets too high, the can explodes.
What happens to the pressure if you squash the gas into a smaller space (but keep the temperature the same)?▾

Making the container smaller means the particles have less distance to travel between walls. They hit each wall more often — so the pressure goes up.

\(\text{Smaller volume} \rightarrow \text{more frequent collisions} \rightarrow \text{more pressure}\)

This is why it gets harder and harder to push a bike pump as you compress the air — you're squeezing the same gas into a smaller space, so it pushes back harder.

Notice: the particles don't speed up (temperature is unchanged). They just hit the walls more often because they're closer together. That alone is enough to increase the pressure.
§3 What Temperature Really Means
If temperature is about particle speed, what is absolute zero?▾

Temperature measures how fast the particles are moving on average. Hotter = faster. Colder = slower.

If you cool a gas down enough, the particles slow down. Eventually, at −273°C, they would stop completely. This is absolute zero — the lowest possible temperature.

Scientists use the kelvin scale which starts at absolute zero:

\(T(\text{K}) = T(°\text{C}) + 273\)
CelsiusKelvinWhat's happening
−273°C0 KAbsolute zero — particles stop
0°C273 KWater freezes
20°C293 KRoom temperature
100°C373 KWater boils
Quick practice: Convert 37°C (body temperature) to kelvin. Answer: 37 + 273 = 310 K.
Why does a bike pump get hot when you use it?▾

When you push the piston down, you're doing work on the gas — you're pushing against the gas particles. The piston hits the particles as it moves in, giving them extra kinetic energy.

Faster particles = higher temperature. So the gas heats up — and the pump gets hot.

If you let the gas expand back, the reverse happens: the gas pushes the piston out, loses energy, and cools down.

This is the same principle a diesel engine uses. The fuel-air mixture is compressed so much that it heats up enough to ignite — no spark plug needed.
§4 Boyle's Law — Pressure and Volume
If you keep the temperature constant and change the volume, what happens to the pressure?▾

Boyle's Law: At constant temperature, pressure and volume are inversely proportional. If you halve the volume, the pressure doubles. If you triple the volume, the pressure drops to a third.

\(p_1 \times V_1 = p_2 \times V_2 \qquad \text{(at constant temperature)}\)
Think of it this way: the same number of particles hitting a smaller wall, more often. The product \(p \times V\) stays constant — if one goes up, the other must come down.
A syringe contains 80 cm³ of air at 100 kPa. You compress it to 20 cm³. What is the new pressure?▾
\(p_1 V_1 = p_2 V_2\)
\(100 \times 80 = p_2 \times 20\)
\(p_2 = \dfrac{8000}{20} = 400\text{ kPa}\)

The volume went down by a factor of 4, so the pressure went up by a factor of 4.

Try it: A balloon has a volume of 2.0 litres at sea level (100 kPa). It rises to a height where the pressure is 50 kPa. What is the new volume?
Answer: \(V_2 = (100 \times 2.0) / 50 = 4.0\) litres. The balloon doubles in size!
A diver's lungs hold 6.0 litres of air at the surface (100 kPa). She dives to a depth where the pressure is 300 kPa. What volume will the air in her lungs occupy?▾
\(V_2 = \dfrac{p_1 V_1}{p_2} = \dfrac{100 \times 6.0}{300} = 2.0\text{ litres}\)

The pressure tripled, so the volume shrank to one-third.

This is why divers must never hold their breath while ascending. As they rise, the pressure drops and the air in their lungs expands — if the lungs are sealed (breath held), they can rupture. Always breathe out while rising.
§5 Charles's Law — Volume and Temperature
If you keep the pressure constant and heat a gas, what happens to its volume?▾

Charles's Law: At constant pressure, volume is proportional to temperature (in kelvin). If you double the kelvin temperature, you double the volume.

\(\dfrac{V_1}{T_1} = \dfrac{V_2}{T_2} \qquad \text{(at constant pressure)}\)
You must use kelvin. If you use Celsius, the formula gives wrong answers. Always convert: \(T(\text{K}) = T(°\text{C}) + 273\).
A balloon has a volume of 500 cm³ at 20°C. It is heated to 80°C. What is its new volume? (Assume constant pressure.)▾

Step 1 — Convert to kelvin:

\(T_1 = 20 + 273 = 293\text{ K}\)
\(T_2 = 80 + 273 = 353\text{ K}\)

Step 2 — Apply Charles's Law:

\(V_2 = V_1 \times \dfrac{T_2}{T_1} = 500 \times \dfrac{353}{293} = 500 \times 1.205 = 602\text{ cm}^3\)

The gas expanded by about 20% — even though in Celsius the temperature "only" went from 20 to 80.

Try it: A gas has a volume of 3.0 litres at 300 K. The temperature drops to 150 K at constant pressure. What is the new volume?
Answer: \(V_2 = 3.0 \times (150/300) = 1.5\) litres. Half the temperature → half the volume.
§6 Pressure and Temperature (Constant Volume)
If a sealed container of gas is heated, what happens to the pressure?▾

At constant volume, the pressure is proportional to the kelvin temperature:

\(\dfrac{p_1}{T_1} = \dfrac{p_2}{T_2} \qquad \text{(at constant volume)}\)

Double the kelvin temperature → double the pressure. This is Gay-Lussac's law.

A car tyre has a pressure of 200 kPa at 15°C in the morning. After driving, the temperature rises to 45°C. What is the new pressure?▾

Convert to kelvin:

\(T_1 = 15 + 273 = 288\text{ K}\)
\(T_2 = 45 + 273 = 318\text{ K}\)

Apply the law:

\(p_2 = p_1 \times \dfrac{T_2}{T_1} = 200 \times \dfrac{318}{288} = 200 \times 1.104 = 221\text{ kPa}\)

A 10% increase in pressure — this is why tyre pressure gauges tell you to check when the tyres are cold. After driving, they read higher.

The volume of a tyre changes very little (it's rigid), so this is approximately a constant-volume process. The pressure increase is entirely due to the faster-moving air molecules inside.
