A Smallest Ruler building a black hole's entropy out of units alone

Hawking and Bekenstein found that a black hole carries the entropy \( S = \dfrac{\pi A k_{B} c^{3}}{2hG} \). Almost all of that formula can be recovered without any deep physics, using only the demand that both sides of an equation carry the same units. This page lets you assemble the pieces by hand: read rates off a lattice, drive a dimension toward a target, and count the arrangements that entropy is really measuring.

01 / rates without calculus

Reading a rate off a lattice

Calculus measures how a quantity changes at a single instant, and it leans on ideas of continuity and limits. We can step around all of that by recording a quantity only at equally spaced moments, like photographs on a steady beat, so that it becomes a list of numbers. The change across one step is the first difference \( \Delta s_n = s_{n+1}-s_n \), and the change in that change is the second difference. A first difference divided by the tick is a discrete speed; a second difference is the discrete acceleration. Change the rule below and watch the cascade and the chords respond.

Why are the first differences of n² the odd numbers?

Because \( (n+1)^2 - n^2 = 2n+1 \), which runs through \(1,3,5,7,\dots\) as \(n\) increases. Their own differences are all \(2\), the constant second difference, and a constant second difference is exactly the fingerprint of uniform acceleration, the discrete form of Galileo's odd-number rule for a body released from rest.

What happened to continuity and the tangent line?

The dashed curve is the smooth path the calculus would draw, and its tangent at a point is what you would reach by letting the step shrink toward zero. We never take that limit here; every rate we need is read as a chord between two real samples, which carries the same dimensions a tangent would, so nothing is lost for the purpose of finding units.

02 / the homogeneity engine

Driving a dimension to its target

Every constant arrives through a defining equation, and from that equation its units follow. Tracking only the base dimensions, mass \(\mathsf{M}\), length \(\mathsf{L}\), time \(\mathsf{T}\) and temperature \(\Uptheta\), each quantity becomes a short list of powers.

\( [c] = \mathsf{L}\,\mathsf{T}^{-1} \)  from \(c=d/t\)  ·  \( [h] = \mathsf{M}\,\mathsf{L}^{2}\,\mathsf{T}^{-1} \)  from \(E=hf\)  ·  \( [G] = \mathsf{M}^{-1}\mathsf{L}^{3}\mathsf{T}^{-2} \)  from \(F=Gm_1m_2/r^2\)
\( [k_{B}] = \mathsf{M}\,\mathsf{L}^{2}\,\mathsf{T}^{-2}\,\Uptheta^{-1} \)  from \(\bar E=\tfrac32 k_B T\)  ·  \( [A] = \mathsf{L}^{2} \)  an area

Now build a product of powers and try to drive its total dimension onto the target. Step each exponent up or down and watch the four dimension chips; when every chip turns green you have a dimensionally valid formula. The two targets behave very differently, which is the whole point.

Three constants, three base dimensions in play, so the equations are just enough to fix a single answer with nothing left over:

\[ \ell_{P} = \kappa\, c^{-3/2} h^{1/2} G^{1/2} = \kappa\sqrt{\dfrac{hG}{c^{3}}}\approx 1.6\times10^{-35}\ \text{m}. \]

The pure number \(\kappa\) stays invisible to units; the accepted value uses \(\hbar=h/2\pi\).

Five exponents against four equations leaves one free choice. Temperature is carried only by \(k_B\), so its power is forced to one; the leftover freedom is removed only by Bekenstein's physical claim that the entropy is proportional to the area, \(b=1\):

\[ S = \kappa\, k_{B}\,A\,c^{3} h^{-1} G^{-1} = \kappa\,\dfrac{k_{B} A c^{3}}{hG},\qquad \kappa=\tfrac{\pi}{2}. \]
Why can't this method ever find the π/2?

A pure number has dimension \(1\): it changes no unit. Matching dimensions therefore cannot see it, whether it is \(\pi/2\), \(4\) or \(\sqrt{2\pi}\). Pinning the leading number down needs the full physics of fields near the horizon, which is the part this shortcut deliberately skips.

Why is the length unique but the entropy is not?

It is a counting match. The Planck length has three unknown powers and three base dimensions, so the equations are exactly enough and the answer is forced. The entropy has five unknown powers against the same four base dimensions, one short, so a whole family of formulas is dimensionally legal and a single physical fact, \(S\propto A\), is needed to choose among them.

03 / what entropy counts

Counting the arrangements

Entropy counts how many internal arrangements a system could be in while looking the same from outside, \( S = k_B \ln\Omega \). A shuffled pack of \(52\) cards has \(\Omega = 52!\) orderings, a number far too large to write out, yet the entropy needs only its logarithm. Stirling's approximation, \( \ln n! \approx n\ln n - n + \tfrac12\ln(2\pi n) \), reaches that logarithm without ever forming the factorial. Why use ln Ω rather than Ω itself?

The count \(\Omega\) for a deck sits near \(10^{68}\), and for a gas or a black hole it is unthinkably larger; the logarithm tames it into a number you can hold in your hand, around \(156\) for the deck. The logarithm also turns the multiplying together of independent systems into plain addition, so their entropies simply add, which is exactly the behaviour a measure of disorder should have.
