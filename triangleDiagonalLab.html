Bidiagonal Lab

Ln as the operator  ·  Pascal · Eulerian · Delannoy

Treat each row of the triangle as a vector. The row-to-row map is a lower bidiagonal matrix. Change the matrix, watch the triangle.

The framing

Let vn = (T(n,0), T(n,1), T(n,2), ...)T be row n as a column vector. The recurrence T(n,k) = α(n,k)·T(n−1,k) + β(n,k)·T(n−1,k−1) says vn = Ln vn−1, where Ln is a lower bidiagonal matrix with Ln[k,k] = α(n,k) on the main diagonal and Ln[k,k−1] = β(n,k) on the subdiagonal.

That is the entire operator. The triangle is its orbit on a seed vector v0.

Some triangles need more. Delannoy's recurrence references the same row it is filling (the T(n, k−1) term), which means the row-to-row map post-composes the bidiagonal with a cumulative-sum operator U. Pascal then row-accumulation gives Delannoy. The toggle below switches that on.

Reading the presets

Pascal: α=1, β=1, no accumulator. Seed (1, 0, 0, ...).

Eulerian / Worpitzky: α = k+1, β = n−k, no accumulator. Seed (1, 0, 0, ...). Bidiagonal but the entries depend on (n, k).

Delannoy: α=1, β=1, accumulator ON. Seed (1, 1, 1, ...). Pascal composed with cumulative sum, on a different seed. Riordan factorisation made visible.

Stirling 2nd kind: α = k, β = 1, no accumulator. Seed (1, 0, 0, ...). Counts set partitions; row sums are Bell numbers.

What the spines see

A spine is a sequence read along a line through the array. Each spine has its own recurrence, derived from Ln by projection. Pascal row sums are 2n. Eulerian row sums are n!. Delannoy main diagonal is 1, 3, 13, 63, 321, 1683, ... Stirling row sums are Bell numbers 1, 1, 2, 5, 15, 52, 203, ...

What changes between the three classical cases

Pascal is the base case: bidiagonal, constant entries, single seed. Eulerian varies one thing: the entries of Ln become functions of (n, k) rather than constants. The bidiagonal structure survives. Delannoy varies a different thing: it post-composes Ln with a row-accumulator U and lifts the seed to all ones. The entries of Ln stay constant. Pascal and Delannoy share Ln; Pascal and Eulerian share both the seed and the absence of U; Delannoy and Eulerian share nothing structurally except the bidiagonal stencil itself.

That is the actual statement of how these three relate. Not three pretty arrays with similar-looking recurrences. Three points in a small structured space: (entries of Ln: constant or affine in (n,k)) × (post-compose with U: yes or no) × (seed: e0 or 1).

Stirling sits in the same space at (α=k, β=1, no U, seed e0). It belongs.
