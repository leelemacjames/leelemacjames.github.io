One Matrix. Three Readings.

How the multiplication map of a quadratic ring encodes its norm, its geometry, and its complex structure, all in a 2×2 matrix.

§1. Building the Multiplication Matrix

Start with something familiar: i² = −1. Now generalise it: θ² = pθ + q. Every quadratic ring comes from this one rule. Adjoin an element θ to the ordinary integers ℤ, declare what θ² equals in terms of θ and a constant, and everything follows.

The elements of the ring are expressions a + bθ with a, b ordinary integers. Two parameters, p and q, are the entire DNA.

The Gaussian case in detail

Take θ = i with the rule i² = −1, so p = 0, q = −1. Elements look like a + bi. Multiplication by a + bi is a linear transformation on the plane. To find its matrix, track where the two basis elements go:

Multiply by 1

(a + bi) × 1 = a + bi

In coordinates: (1, 0) goes to (a, b). This is the first column of our matrix.

Multiply by θ = i

(a + bi) × i = ai + bi² = ai + b(−1) = −b + ai

In coordinates: (0, 1) goes to (−b, a). This is the second column of our matrix.

M(a, b) =
a−b ba
det = a² + b²

This is exactly the rotation-scaling matrix you already know. It rotates the plane by arg(a + bi) and scales distances by |a + bi| = √(a² + b²). Its determinant, the area scaling factor, is a² + b², the Gaussian norm.

The general recipe

The same procedure works for any θ² = pθ + q:

Multiply by θ

(a + bθ) × θ = aθ + bθ² = aθ + b(pθ + q) = bq + (a + bp)θ

Coordinates: (0, 1) goes to (bq, a + bp). Second column.

M(a, b) =
abq ba + bp
det = a² + abp − b²q

That determinant, a² + abp − b²q, is the norm form of the ring. It captures the area distortion of the multiplication map, the geometry of the lattice, and the arithmetic of the ring. Two parameters determine everything.

The discriminant of the equation θ² = pθ + q is Δ = p² + 4q. This same Δ is the discriminant of the norm form as a binary quadratic, and it governs the entire character of the ring.

§3. The Determinant is the Norm

The determinant of the multiplication matrix M(a, b) is the signed area distortion when you multiply the entire lattice by the element a + bθ. This is precisely the norm N(a + bθ). The two notions are the same object:

N(z) = det(Mz). The norm of a ring element is the determinant of its multiplication map. Multiplicativity of the norm (N(z₁z₂) = N(z₁)N(z₂)) is just the fact that det(AB) = det(A)det(B).

The character of the determinant depends on whether the off-diagonal entries create rotation (elliptic), shear (parabolic), or boost (hyperbolic):

ℤ[j]p=0, q=1+4a² − b²hyperbolic

The elliptic norms are positive definite: always positive for nonzero elements. The multiplication map always preserves orientation and never degenerates. The Gaussian a² + b² is the sum of two squares; the Eisenstein a² − ab + b² is its hexagonal cousin. These are the "circular" forms.

The hyperbolic norm a² − b² is the classical difference of two squares. It takes both positive and negative values, orientation can flip, and the norm-zero locus is a pair of real lines (the "light cone"). The golden-ratio norm a² + ab − b² is its tilted cousin. These are the "hyperbolic" forms, and the Fibonacci connection lives here.

The parabolic norm a² is degenerate, blind to the b direction entirely. The multiplication map is a shear: it moves things along one direction without stretching or rotating. This is the transitional case between elliptic and hyperbolic.

§4. The Lattice and the Unit Curve

The unit equation N(z) = 1 defines a curve in the (a, b) plane. For elliptic rings it's an ellipse (compact, finitely many integer points). For hyperbolic rings it's a hyperbola (non-compact, infinitely many). For the parabolic ring it degenerates to two vertical lines.

§5. J² and the Three Structures

We now have a family of matrices M(a, b) parametrised by the ring element z = a + bθ. One member of this family deserves special attention: the case a = 0, b = 1. This is J = M(0, 1), the multiplication-by-θ map. It is the structure map of the ring: it tells you what θ does geometrically, stripped of any contribution from the identity.

Two matrices, two roles

M(a, b) is the general multiplication map. It depends on the element you multiply by. J is the special case: multiplication by θ alone. Between them they capture the full picture:

MatrixWhat it isWhat it tells you
M(a, b)Multiplication by a + bθdet = norm, area distortion, orientation
J = M(0, 1)Multiplication by θJ² classifies the ring's geometry
I = M(1, 0)Multiplication by 1The identity (trivially)

In fact, every M(a, b) decomposes as M(a, b) = aI + bJ. The entire family of multiplication matrices is generated by just I and J. The ring's geometry is the geometry of this matrix pencil.

What happens when you apply J twice?

Since θ satisfies θ² = pθ + q, the matrix J satisfies the same relation:

J² = pJ + qI

For the Gaussian case, p = 0 and q = −1, so J² = −I. This is an almost-complex structure: a real-linear map that squares to minus the identity. Multiplying by i twice gives multiplication by −1. Geometrically, J rotates by 90°; apply it twice and you've rotated 180°, which is −I.

But only the Gaussian ring has J² = −I on the nose. For the others, J² has extra terms. We can always extract a normalised structure J̃ = (2J − pI)/√|Δ|, and the character of J̃² depends on the sign of Δ. This is the key question: what happens when you apply J twice?

J acting on the unit circle

J² acting on the unit circle

The trichotomy. Δ < 0 gives J̃² = −I (almost-complex, rotation): the eigenvalues of J̃ are ±i, splitting ℂ into holomorphic and anti-holomorphic halves. Δ > 0 gives J̃² = +I (para-complex, boost): the eigenvalues are ±1, splitting ℝ² into two real eigenlines, the null directions. Δ = 0 gives J̃² = 0 (nilpotent, shear): the single degenerate direction where the two null lines have collapsed together.

§6. The Full Picture

A quadratic ring ℤ[θ] is completely determined by two integers p and q (from θ² = pθ + q). From these, everything follows:

Every M(a, b) decomposes as aI + bJ. So I and J generate the entire family. The general multiplication matrix is a point in the matrix pencil spanned by the identity and the structure map.

The determinant of M(a, b) plays three roles at once: the algebraic norm N(a + bθ), the signed area distortion of the lattice under multiplication, and the orientation indicator (+/−/0).

J² tells you the geometry. It equals pJ + qI, and after normalisation, J̃² is −I (rotation), 0 (shear), or +I (boost). One sign. Three worlds.

The sign of q in the matrix, whether the top-right entry bq pulls against b (q < 0, elliptic) or with it (q > 0, hyperbolic), is the single structural choice that determines everything. It is the difference between rotation and boost, between finite and infinite unit groups, between J̃² = −I and J̃² = +I. Between circles and hyperbolas. Between Pythagoras and Fibonacci.
