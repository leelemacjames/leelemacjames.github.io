Multiplication, Norm & Structure

How the multiplication map of a quadratic ring encodes its norm, its geometry, and its complex structure — all in a 2×2 matrix.

§1 — Building the Multiplication Matrix

Every quadratic ring is built from the ordinary integers by adjoining a single new element θ — a "square root" of something. The integers of the ring are expressions a + bθ, with a and b ordinary integers. Addition works component-wise. Multiplication almost does, except that whenever θ² appears, we need a rule to replace it.

That replacement rule — the equation θ² = pθ + q for some fixed integers p and q — is the entire DNA of the ring. Everything that follows comes from it.

The Gaussian case in detail

Start with the most familiar example. Adjoin θ = i to ℤ, with the rule i² = −1. So p = 0 and q = −1. Elements look like a + bi.

To multiply every element of the ring by a fixed element a + bi, we need to know where the two basis elements 1 and i are sent:

Multiply by 1

(a + bi) × 1 = a + bi

In coordinates: (1, 0) goes to (a, b). This is the first column of our matrix.

Multiply by θ = i

(a + bi) × i = ai + bi² = ai + b(−1) = −b + ai

In coordinates: (0, 1) goes to (−b, a). This is the second column of our matrix.

M(a, b) =
a−b ba
det = a² + b²

This is a rotation-dilation matrix. It rotates the plane by arg(a + bi) and scales distances by |a + bi| = √(a² + b²). Its determinant — the area scaling factor — is a² + b², the Gaussian norm.

The general recipe

The same procedure works for any quadratic ring. Given θ² = pθ + q:

Multiply by θ

(a + bθ) × θ = aθ + bθ² = aθ + b(pθ + q) = bq + (a + bp)θ

Coordinates: (0, 1) goes to (bq, a + bp). Second column.

M(a, b) =
abq ba + bp
det = a² + abp − b²q

That determinant — a² + abp − b²q — is the norm form of the ring. It captures everything: the area distortion of the multiplication map, the geometry of the lattice, and the arithmetic of the ring. Two parameters, p and q, determine everything.

The discriminant of the quadratic equation t² − pt − q = 0 is Δ = p² + 4q. This same number is the discriminant of the norm form as a binary quadratic. It governs the entire character of the ring.

§3 — The Determinant is the Norm

The determinant of the multiplication matrix M(a, b) is the signed area distortion when you multiply the entire lattice by the element a + bθ. This is precisely the norm N(a + bθ). The two notions are the same object:

N(z) = det(Mz) — the norm of a ring element is the determinant of its multiplication map. Multiplicativity of the norm (N(z₁z₂) = N(z₁)N(z₂)) is just the fact that det(AB) = det(A)det(B).

The character of the determinant depends on whether the off-diagonal entries create rotation (elliptic), shear (parabolic), or boost (hyperbolic):

The elliptic norms are positive definite — they're always positive (for nonzero elements), which means the multiplication map always preserves orientation and never degenerates. The Gaussian a² + b² is the sum of two squares; the Eisenstein a² − ab + b² is its hexagonal cousin. These are the "circular" forms.

The hyperbolic norm a² − b² is the classical difference of two squares. It takes both positive and negative values, orientation can flip, and the norm-zero locus is a pair of real lines (the "light cone"). The golden-ratio norm a² + ab − b² is its tilted cousin. These are the "hyperbolic" forms — and the Fibonacci connection lives here.

The parabolic norm a² is degenerate — it's blind to the b direction entirely. The multiplication map is a shear: it moves things along one direction without stretching or rotating. This is the transitional case between elliptic and hyperbolic.

§5 — J² and the Three Structures

The multiplication-by-θ map, with a = 0 and b = 1, gives us the matrix J = M(0, 1). This is the "structure map" of the ring — it tells you what θ does geometrically. Since θ satisfies θ² = pθ + q, the matrix J satisfies:

J² = pJ + qI

For the Gaussian case, p = 0 and q = −1, so J² = −I. This is an almost-complex structure: a real-linear map that squares to minus the identity. It's the algebraic expression of the fact that multiplying by i twice gives you multiplication by −1. Geometrically, J rotates by 90° — apply it twice and you've rotated 180°, which is −I.

But only the Gaussian ring has J² = −I on the nose. For the others, J² has extra terms. We can always extract a normalised structure J̃ = (2J − pI)/√|Δ|, and the character of J̃² depends on the sign of Δ:

J acting on the unit circle

J² acting on the unit circle

The trichotomy: Δ < 0 gives J̃² = −I (almost-complex, rotation) — the eigenvalues of J̃ are ±i, splitting ℂ into holomorphic and anti-holomorphic halves. Δ > 0 gives J̃² = +I (para-complex, boost) — the eigenvalues are ±1, splitting ℝ² into two real eigenlines (the null directions). Δ = 0 gives J̃² = 0 (nilpotent, shear) — the single degenerate direction where the two null lines have collapsed together.

§6 — Pulling it Together

A quadratic ring ℤ[θ] is completely determined by two integers p and q (from θ² = pθ + q). From these:

The multiplication matrix M(a, b) = [[a, bq], [b, a + bp]] encodes ring multiplication as a linear map on ℝ².

Its determinant a² + abp − b²q is the norm form — simultaneously the area distortion of the map, the arithmetic norm of the element a + bθ, and a binary quadratic form of discriminant Δ = p² + 4q.

The structure map J = M(0, 1) satisfies J² = pJ + qI, and its normalised version J̃ = (2J − pI)/√|Δ| squares to −I (elliptic), 0 (parabolic), or +I (hyperbolic), classifying the ring's geometry.

The unit curve N(z) = 1 is an ellipse, a degenerate pair of lines, or a hyperbola — compact with finitely many lattice points, or non-compact with infinitely many.

The sign of q in the matrix — whether the top-right entry bq pulls against b (when q < 0, elliptic) or with it (when q > 0, hyperbolic) — is the single structural choice that determines everything. It's the difference between rotation and boost, between finite and infinite unit groups, between J² = −I and J² = +I. Between circles and hyperbolas. Between Pythagoras and Fibonacci.
