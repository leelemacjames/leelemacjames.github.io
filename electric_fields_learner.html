Electric Fields, Coulomb's Law & Parallel Plates

Forces between charges, fields as intermediaries, and the deep structural parallel between gravity and electrostatics.

What this section is about

Electrostatics is the physics of charges at rest (or moving slowly enough that magnetic effects are negligible). At its heart is Coulomb's law — the electric analogue of Newton's law of gravitation — and the concept of the electric field as the thing that mediates the force.

This section covers slides D13–D22: Coulomb's law, permittivity, electric field strength, the gravitational–electric analogy, uniform fields between parallel plates, energy and speed calculations, charge density, and Millikan's experiment. The Q&A targets the conceptual errors and calculation mistakes the IB tests hardest.

A recurring theme: the structural identity between gravitational and electric fields. Every formula in one has a partner in the other. If you learn one, you get the other free — but the sign differences and the physical interpretation matter enormously.

The Three Core Equations

Almost everything in this section derives from three relations:

F = kq₁q₂ / r²   (Coulomb's law — force between charges)

E = F/q   (field = force per unit positive test charge)

W = qV   (work = charge × potential difference)

From these three you can derive everything: field strength from a point charge, potential from a point charge, uniform field between plates, kinetic energy gained by an accelerated charge, and Millikan's balancing condition. Master the three, and the ten slides fall into place.

SL D13 — Electric Fields & Coulomb's Law

An electric field is the force experienced by a positive test charge placed at that point. Field lines go from positive to negative. The field is a vector — it has magnitude and direction.

Electric field strength E is force per unit positive charge:

E = F / q    [N/C]

Coulomb's law gives the force between two point charges:

F = k q₁q₂ / r²

Where k = 8.99 × 10⁹ N m²/C² is the Coulomb constant and r is the separation. Like charges repel; unlike charges attract. The force is along the line joining the charges.

The test charge must be "small". E = F/q uses an idealised positive test charge that's small enough not to disturb the field it's measuring. If you dump a large charge into the field, it rearranges the source charges and you measure the wrong thing. This is a theoretical idealisation — but it's why the definition says "per unit charge", not "for a specific charge".

Q1Two charges of +3 μC and −5 μC are 0.2 m apart. What is the force between them, and is it attractive or repulsive?▶
Answer

F = kq₁q₂/r² = 8.99 × 10⁹ × 3 × 10⁻⁶ × 5 × 10⁻⁶ / (0.2)² = 8.99 × 10⁹ × 15 × 10⁻¹² / 0.04

F = 3.37 N. The charges are opposite signs, so the force is attractive.

Q2If you double the separation between two charges, what happens to the force?▶
Answer

F ∝ 1/r². Doubling r gives F/(2²) = F/4. The force drops to one-quarter. This inverse-square dependence is identical to gravity.

SL D14 — Permittivity of Materials

Permittivity (ε) measures how well a material responds to electric fields — how easily it stores electrical energy. The Coulomb constant k is related to the permittivity of free space by:

k = 1 / (4πε₀)    where ε₀ ≈ 8.854 × 10⁻¹² C² N⁻¹ m⁻²

In a medium with permittivity ε, the force between charges is reduced: replace ε₀ with ε in the denominator. Higher permittivity → weaker electrostatic force. Water (ε ≈ 779 × 10⁻¹²) is ~88 times more permittive than vacuum — which is why ionic compounds dissolve so readily in water.

Q1Two charges are 10 cm apart in vacuum with force 0.5 N. If they're placed in water, what's the new force?▶
Answer

Force in a medium is reduced by the relative permittivity εr = ε/ε₀ ≈ 88. New force = 0.5/88 ≈ 5.7 × 10⁻³ N. The electrostatic force is dramatically weakened by the polar water molecules screening the charges.

SL D15 — Fields, Forces & Deriving Speed from Potential

Three equations and the chain of reasoning that connects them:

E = F/q  →  F = qE    (force on charge in field)
W = qV    (work done = charge × pd)
½mv² = qV    (KE gained = work done by field)

The last line is the energy method for finding speed: a charge q accelerated through potential difference V gains kinetic energy qV. Rearrange for speed:

v = √(2qV / m)

This works for any charge (electrons, protons, ions, oil drops) and avoids needing to know the field geometry. It's pure energy conservation.

Q1A proton (m = 1.67 × 10⁻²⁷ kg, q = 1.60 × 10⁻¹⁹ C) is accelerated through 500 V. What is its final speed?▶
Answer

KE = qV = 1.60 × 10⁻¹⁹ × 500 = 8.0 × 10⁻¹⁷ J

v = √(2 × 8.0 × 10⁻¹⁷ / 1.67 × 10⁻²⁷) = √(9.58 × 10¹⁰) = 3.10 × 10⁵ m/s

Q2Why doesn't the distance between the plates matter for calculating final speed?▶
Answer

Because we're using energy conservation, not forces. The work done W = qV depends only on the potential difference, not on the path or distance. Whether the plates are 1 mm or 1 m apart, the charge gains the same KE if V is the same. (The field strength E = V/d would differ, and the acceleration would differ, but the final speed is the same.)

D16 — Gravitational vs Electric: The Structural Parallel

The deepest insight in this section: gravity and electrostatics share the same mathematical structure. Every gravitational quantity has an electric counterpart, obtained by replacing mass with charge and G with k.

Key differences: (1) Gravity is always attractive (hence the − sign). Electric force can attract or repel. (2) Gravitational potential is always negative. Electric potential can be positive or negative. (3) There is no "gravitational shielding" — you can't screen gravity. Electric fields can be screened by conductors (Faraday cage).

Is the analogy between gravity and electrostatics a coincidence or a clue? At the level of classical physics, the parallel is formal — both are inverse-square central forces. But they arise from completely different mechanisms (spacetime curvature vs photon exchange). The shared mathematical structure may reflect a deeper unity, or it may be an artefact of the 1/r² geometry of 3D space. When two different physical phenomena share the same equations, what does that tell us about the nature of physical law?

Q1Why is gravitational potential always negative, while electric potential can be positive?▶
Answer

Gravitational potential Vg = −Gm/r is defined with V = 0 at infinity. Since G and m are always positive, Vg is always negative. You'd need to add energy to escape from a mass. Electric potential VE = kq/r can be positive (near positive charge) or negative (near negative charge), because q can be either sign. Near a proton, V > 0; near an electron, V < 0.

Q2At what point between a +4 μC and +1 μC charge (30 cm apart) is the net electric field zero?▶
Answer

The zero-field point lies between the charges, closer to the smaller one. Let x = distance from the +4 μC charge. At the zero point, kq₁/x² = kq₂/(0.3 − x)².

4/x² = 1/(0.3 − x)² → 4(0.3 − x)² = x² → 2(0.3 − x) = x → 0.6 = 3x → x = 0.20 m (20 cm from the larger charge, 10 cm from the smaller).

SL D17–18 — Uniform Field Between Parallel Plates

Between two parallel plates with potential difference V and separation d, the field is uniform (constant magnitude and direction):

E = V/d    [V/m or N/C]

This is the simplest field geometry: straight, parallel, equally-spaced field lines running from + plate to − plate. The force on a charge q is F = qE = qV/d, constant everywhere between the plates.

Worked Example — Charged Water Droplet

Given: Plate separation d = 80 mm = 0.08 m. Droplet charge q = 11.2 × 10⁻¹⁹ C. Electric force on droplet F = 3.6 × 10⁻¹⁴ N.

Find the potential difference:

E = F/q = 3.6 × 10⁻¹⁴ / 11.2 × 10⁻¹⁹ = 32 140 N/C V = Ed = 32 140 × 0.08 = 2571 V...

Wait — the handout gives V = 2.9 V. Let me recheck: if q = 11.2 × 10⁻¹⁹ C = 7e (since e = 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹), then:

V = Fd/q = (3.6 × 10⁻¹⁴ × 0.08) / (11.2 × 10⁻¹⁹) = 2.88 × 10⁻¹⁵ / 1.12 × 10⁻¹⁸ = 2571 V

The handout's answer of 2.9 V appears to use different input values. The method is correct: V = Fd/q. Always check your units.

Find the mass (droplet stationary → electric force = weight):

F = mg → m = F/g = 3.6 × 10⁻¹⁴ / 9.81 = 3.67 × 10⁻¹⁵ kg

E = V/d only applies between parallel plates (uniform field). For a point charge, E = kq/r² and the field is NOT uniform. Students sometimes use V/d for point charges — this gives nonsense. Check the geometry before choosing your equation.

Q1Plates are 5.0 cm apart with pd = 200 V. What force acts on an electron between them?▶
Answer

E = V/d = 200/0.05 = 4000 V/m.

F = qE = 1.60 × 10⁻¹⁹ × 4000 = 6.4 × 10⁻¹⁶ N toward the positive plate.

Q2If you halve the plate separation but keep the same pd, what happens to E and F?▶
Answer

E = V/d. Halving d doubles E (and therefore doubles F on any charge between the plates). Same voltage across a narrower gap means a stronger field. This is why sparks jump more easily across small gaps.

SL D19 — Electron Accelerated Through a Potential Difference
Worked Example — Electron through 180 V

Given: V = 180 V. Electron: e = 1.602 × 10⁻¹⁹ C, m = 9.109 × 10⁻³¹ kg.

ΔKE = eV = 1.602 × 10⁻¹⁹ × 180 = 2.88 × 10⁻¹⁷ J ½mv² = ΔKE → v = √(2 × 2.88 × 10⁻¹⁷ / 9.109 × 10⁻³¹) v = √(6.33 × 10¹³) = 7.95 × 10⁶ m/s

(The handout gives v ≈ 4.63 × 10⁶ — likely a rounding or transcription issue. The method is identical.)

This speed (8 × 10⁶ m/s) is about 2.7% of c. At what point do you need relativistic corrections? The IB doesn't expect relativity in electrostatics problems, but be aware: above ~10% of c, the classical formula ½mv² underestimates the required energy. For electrons accelerated through > ~25 kV, relativistic effects become measurable.

Q1An alpha particle (q = 2e, m = 6.64 × 10⁻²⁷ kg) is accelerated through 1000 V. What speed does it reach?▶
Answer

KE = qV = 2 × 1.602 × 10⁻¹⁹ × 1000 = 3.204 × 10⁻¹⁶ J

v = √(2 × 3.204 × 10⁻¹⁶ / 6.64 × 10⁻²⁷) = √(9.65 × 10¹⁰) = 3.11 × 10⁵ m/s

Much slower than the electron through 180 V, despite much higher pd — because the alpha is ~7300× heavier.

AHL D20 — Charge Density & Field from Parallel Plates

Surface charge density σ is the charge per unit area on a plate:

σ = Q/A    [C/m²]

The electric field due to a single infinite plate is E = σ/(2ε₀). Between two parallel plates (charges +σ and −σ), the fields add in the gap and cancel outside:

Etotal = σ/ε₀    (between the plates)

This derivation explains why E = V/d: combining σ/ε₀ with V = Ed gives σ = ε₀V/d, and Q = σA = ε₀AV/d — which is the capacitance formula C = ε₀A/d in disguise.

Q1Why is the field zero outside the parallel plates (in the ideal case)?▶
Answer

Each plate produces a uniform field σ/(2ε₀) pointing away from the positive plate (or toward the negative plate). Between the plates, these fields point in the same direction → they add: E = σ/ε₀. Outside the plates, they point in opposite directions → they cancel: E = 0. This is superposition in action.

SL D21 — Millikan's Oil Drop Experiment

Millikan's experiment determined the charge of the electron by balancing gravity and electric force on tiny charged oil droplets.

Method: Oil droplets fall between parallel plates. With the field off, they fall at terminal velocity under gravity vs air drag. With the field on, the electric force can hold a droplet stationary (or move it up/down). By measuring the balancing voltage, you find the charge on each droplet.

Stokes' Law (drag on a sphere): Fdrag = 6πηrv, where η is air viscosity and r is the droplet radius.

At terminal velocity (field off): weight − buoyancy = drag:

⁴⁄₃ πr³(ρoil − ρair)g = 6πηrv

Stationary in field: qE = mg → q = mg/(V/d)

Millikan found that every measured charge was an integer multiple of e = 1.602 × 10⁻¹⁹ C — charge is quantised.

Millikan's data selection. Historical evidence suggests Millikan selectively discarded data points that didn't fit his expected result, labelling them as arising from experimental errors. He still got the right answer. Does the end justify the means in experimental science? When is discarding outliers legitimate, and when is it fraud? This is one of the most famous cases in the ethics of scientific practice.

Q1Why did Millikan use oil rather than water droplets?▶
Answer

Oil evaporates much more slowly than water. A water droplet would shrink during the experiment, changing its mass and radius — making the calculation impossible. Oil droplets remain nearly constant in size for the duration of the measurement.

Q2A droplet of mass 4.9 × 10⁻¹⁵ kg is stationary between plates separated by 12 mm with pd = 360 V. What is the charge on the droplet?▶
Answer

Stationary → qE = mg. E = V/d = 360/0.012 = 30 000 V/m.

q = mg/E = (4.9 × 10⁻¹⁵ × 9.81)/30 000 = 4.81 × 10⁻¹⁴ / 30 000 = 1.60 × 10⁻¹⁸ C = 10e.

Exactly 10 electron charges — confirming quantisation.

Q3Why does the experiment show charge is quantised, rather than just very small?▶
Answer

Because every measured charge is an integer multiple of the same smallest value (e = 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ C). You never find 1.5e or 2.3e. If charge were continuous but small, you'd expect a continuous distribution of values. The clustering at exact multiples is what proves quantisation. The greatest common divisor of all measured charges gives e.

SL D22 — Proton in a Uniform Electric Field
Worked Example — Proton moved 15 cm, losing 350 eV of PE

Given: Distance r = 0.15 m. ΔVE (decrease in PE) = 350 eV.

Finding the potential difference:

ΔV = −ΔVE/q = −350 eV / e = −350 V

(The proton moves from higher potential to lower potential, losing PE and gaining KE — consistent with moving along the field direction.)

Finding the electric field component along the motion:

E = −ΔV/r = −(−350)/0.15 = 2.33 kV/m

Sign conventions matter here. A positive charge moves from high V to low V (it "falls" through the potential). Its PE decreases (ΔVE < 0) and its KE increases. The minus signs in E = −ΔV/Δr and VE = −W encode this direction. If you drop the signs and just use magnitudes, track directions with words ("from + to −") and check your answer makes physical sense.

Q1An electron moves 5 cm against the electric field direction. If E = 1000 V/m, what happens to its KE?▶
Answer

The electron has negative charge, so the electric force on it is opposite to E. Moving against E means moving in the same direction as the electric force on the electron → it gains KE.

ΔV = E × d = 1000 × 0.05 = 50 V. The electron moves from lower to higher potential (for a negative charge, this means gaining KE).

ΔKE = eV = 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ × 50 = 8.0 × 10⁻¹⁸ J = 50 eV gained.

Q2A positive charge is released from rest in a uniform field. Describe its subsequent motion.▶
Answer

The charge accelerates uniformly in the direction of the field (from + plate toward − plate). The motion is like free fall under gravity — constant acceleration, increasing speed, straight-line trajectory. If released with a velocity component perpendicular to E, it follows a parabolic path — exactly like projectile motion, with E playing the role of g.
