§1 — What is Simple Harmonic Motion?

Pull a mass on a spring and release it. It doesn't just fall — it bounces, repeatedly, in a pattern so regular you could set your watch by it. This is simple harmonic motion (SHM): oscillation where the restoring force is always proportional to displacement, always pointing back toward equilibrium.

Restoring force — Hooke's Law
F = −kx

The minus sign is the physics. When you stretch the spring (x > 0), the force pulls back (F < 0). Compress it, the force pushes forward. The spring is always trying to return the mass to x = 0.

Why "simple"?
SHM is "simple" because the restoring force is linear in displacement. Real springs obey this well for small displacements. Large stretches introduce nonlinearities — the maths gets harder, the motion less regular. Year 10 lives in the linear world.

The equation of motion

Newton's second law gives F = ma, so combining with Hooke's Law:

Equation of motion — SHM
mẍ + kx = 0

ẍ + ω²x = 0     where ω² = k/m

The solution to this differential equation is a sinusoid:

General solution
x(t) = A cos(ωt + φ)

v(t) = −Aω sin(ωt + φ)
a(t) = −Aω² cos(ωt + φ)

Three quantities define the oscillation completely: amplitude A (maximum displacement), angular frequency ω (how fast), and phase φ (where we start in the cycle). The period T = 2π/ω = 2π√(m/k) is strikingly independent of amplitude — swing harder, but not faster.

Key quantities at a glance
Angular frequency: ω = √(k/m) rad/s
Period: T = 2π/ω seconds
Frequency: f = 1/T Hz
Maximum speed: v_max = Aω (at x = 0)
Maximum acceleration: a_max = Aω² (at x = ±A)

Energy in SHM

The total mechanical energy is constant and shuttles between kinetic and potential:

Energy conservation in SHM
E_total = ½kA²
KE = ½mv² = ½k(A² − x²)
PE = ½kx²

At the endpoints (x = ±A) all energy is stored in the spring. At x = 0 all energy is kinetic — the mass moves fastest here. This energy exchange is one of the most beautiful balancing acts in physics.

§2 — Introducing Damping

Real oscillators don't run forever. Energy leaks out through friction, air resistance, or — in our case — fluid drag. This is damping, and it changes everything about the long-term behaviour while leaving the short-term physics recognisable.

The simplest model adds a drag force proportional to velocity:

Damping force — viscous drag
F_drag = −bẋ

mẍ + bẋ + kx = 0

The parameter b is the damping coefficient, in units of kg/s. The equation now has three terms: inertia (mẍ), damping (bẋ), and restoring force (kx). These three quantities are always in balance.

The three regimes

Define the damping ratio ζ = b / (2√(mk)). This single number determines which of three qualitatively different behaviours you get:

Underdamped solution

When ζ < 1 the system still oscillates but at a slightly reduced frequency, and the amplitude decays exponentially:

Underdamped solution
x(t) = Ae^(−γt) cos(ω_d t + φ)

γ = b/(2m)     (decay rate)
ω_d = √(ω₀² − γ²)     (damped angular frequency)

The factor Ae^(−γt) is the decaying envelope — an exponential shroud around the oscillation. The damped frequency ω_d is always less than the natural frequency ω₀; damping slows the oscillation slightly.

Observation task
Use Simulator 2. Start with b low — you'll see oscillation with a decaying envelope. Gradually increase b. Watch carefully for the moment the oscillation disappears. That transition is critical damping (ζ = 1). Keep increasing and notice how the return to zero actually slows down — the overdamped case.

§3 — Spring-Mass in a Fluid

Now make the damping physical. Submerge the spring-mass system in a fluid. The drag force on an object moving through a viscous fluid at low speeds is given by Stokes' Law:

Stokes drag — sphere of radius r
F_drag = 6πηrv = bv

b = 6πηr

Here η is the dynamic viscosity of the fluid (in Pa·s). Water has η ≈ 0.001 Pa·s. This tells us b is determined by the fluid's resistance to flow, and by the size of the object moving through it.

Newtonian fluids

Water is a Newtonian fluid: its viscosity is constant, independent of how fast you stir it. The drag is simply proportional to velocity. Submerge a spring-mass system in water and you get textbook underdamped motion for a light enough bob, gradually losing amplitude as energy dissipates into warming the water.

Real viscosities
Water at 20°C: η ≈ 0.001 Pa·s
Olive oil: η ≈ 0.1 Pa·s
Honey: η ≈ 10 Pa·s
Oobleck (at rest): η ≈ 100–10,000 Pa·s (highly variable!)

Non-Newtonian fluids — oobleck

Mix cornstarch and water in roughly a 2:1 ratio by mass (cornstarch to water) and you get oobleck — named after Dr. Seuss. It is profoundly strange. Poke it hard and it solidifies; let it flow gently and it behaves like a liquid. This is called shear-thickening behaviour: viscosity increases with shear rate.

Oobleck is a dilatant fluid. Its cornstarch particles pack together under rapid compression, creating momentary interlocking structures — essentially jamming. The faster you move through it, the harder it resists. This makes the damping force deeply nonlinear:

Generalised power-law drag (Ostwald–de Waele model)
F_drag = −K |ẋ|^(n−1) ẋ

n < 1 → shear-thinning (ketchup, blood)
n = 1 → Newtonian (water)
n > 1 → shear-thickening (oobleck, wet sand)

For oobleck, n ≈ 1.5–3 depending on concentration. This means the drag ramps up super-linearly with velocity — slow oscillations are barely damped, fast ones are crushed.

What to notice with oobleck
Switch between Water and Oobleck 2:1. At large amplitudes (fast motion) the oobleck damps much more aggressively than the linear model predicts. But watch what happens as the oscillations decay and become slow — the oobleck behaves more like water. This velocity-dependent viscosity is the signature of shear thickening.

§4 — Phase Portraits

A position-time graph tells one story. A phase portrait — displacement x on the horizontal axis, velocity v on the vertical — tells another. In SHM the trajectory is a perfect ellipse. Damping spirals the trajectory inward toward the origin.

Amplitude A 0.25
Reading the phase portrait
Each point on the spiral encodes the complete state of the oscillator at one moment — both where the mass is and how fast it's moving. The inward spiral visualises energy loss. A perfect ellipse (b = 0) means no damping — the system revisits every state forever.

Damping is not merely a correction to perfect SHM — it is the physics of the real world intruding. Every real oscillator is dissipative. Engineers tune damping deliberately: car suspensions aim for near-critical damping; earthquake-resistant buildings use fluid dampers; the human ear exploits fluid damping in the cochlea to distinguish frequencies.

Oobleck sits at an unusual corner of this landscape: a fluid that becomes temporarily solid under stress. It's not just a curiosity — body armour research, protective packaging, and even road surfaces have explored oobleck-like materials. The physics you've encountered here — nonlinear drag, velocity-dependent viscosity, power-law models — is active research territory.
