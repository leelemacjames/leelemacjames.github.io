Sums in pairs

Mystic Martha tells you the sums but never the numbers. Five levels, one rising idea: when the trick stops working, build the method that survives.

Pre · learning

What needs to be in the room before you start

This panel is for the teacher running the session. Most of these prerequisites are conceptual scaffolding rather than content. A pupil can do every Level 1–5 calculation without ever hearing the words "ordinality" or "constraint", but the session lands deeper if the teacher has those words ready to deploy at the right moment.

Concept
Usually there?
What is at stake without it
Ordinality vs cardinality
Almost never named
The inequality A < B < C < D is purely ordinal. The pair-sums are cardinal. Half the task — pinning which sum is which in Levels 4 and 5 — is an ordinal argument that produces cardinal equations. Without the distinction, pupils treat A < B < C as decoration. With it, they see ordinality as a tool that does mathematical work before any arithmetic begins.
Parity (odd + odd, etc.)
Listed in the deck
Needed for Level 3. The three rules — odd+odd=even, even+even=even, odd+even=odd — must be reflex by the time pupils meet the parity filter.
Two-digit addition fluency
Yes at Y7/8
Level 2 fails on shaky arithmetic. The whole pivot from trial to method is only persuasive if the arithmetic itself isn't the obstacle.
Letters as unknowns
Variable
Bar models cushion this brilliantly — a pupil who can't yet handle A, B, C symbolically can handle three bars labelled with names. But the teacher should be ready to walk pupils between the two registers.
The idea of a constraint
Almost never named
A < B is a constraint. A + B = 8 is a constraint. The pupil's job is to find the values that satisfy all the constraints at once. Naming this is the entry point to thinking like a problem-solver rather than an equation-cruncher. It also rehabilitates inequality as a first-class mathematical object, not a B-list relation that lives in the shadow of equality.
Triangular numbers
Sometimes seen
The handshake bridge is much cleaner if pupils already know 1, 3, 6, 10, 15 as a named sequence. Without it, the count of pair-sums (3, 6, 10) feels coincidental rather than structural.
Pascal's triangle
Sometimes glimpsed
The deep home of n(n−1)/2 is the third diagonal of Pascal: n choose 2. A pupil who has seen Pascal can be told that the handshake count, the count of pair-sums, the number of edges of Kn, the triangular numbers, and the third Pascal diagonal are the same object viewed from five angles. That is a major representation-switching experience in its own right.
Weighted graph (vertices & edges, edge labels)
Almost never at Y7
This is the cleanest framing of the whole task: given a weighted complete graph where each edge weight is the sum of the two vertex weights, recover the vertex weights. The teacher doesn't need to use those words with pupils, but holding them in mind transforms what the teacher sees in the classroom.
Necessary vs sufficient
Almost never at Y7
Parity is necessary but not sufficient. Pupils very rarely meet this distinction before A-level proof work, but it lives natively here. If you have a rapid-grasper class, this is a place to plant the vocabulary in the teacher's voice even if not in the pupil's.

The bigger philosophical seed

School mathematics trains pupils to chase equalities. "Solve for x" produces a single value, which feels like victory. Most of higher mathematics, by contrast, runs on inequalities: bounding, sandwiching, estimating, "this is at most that". Mystic Martha's puzzle, especially in its four-friend two-solution form, is where pupils can feel for the first time that an answer is a region carved by constraints, not a point produced by an equation. That experience, at thirteen, is worth more than any technique the session teaches.

If a pupil leaves this session thinking that inequalities do real mathematical work — that A < B < C < D is half of the answer — the session has succeeded irrespective of which Mystic Martha problems they solved on the day.

Level 01

Three friends, small numbers

Ali, Biko and Casie each pick a positive whole number. Biko's is bigger than Ali's. Casie's is bigger than Biko's. They keep their numbers hidden, but Mystic Martha tells you the three pair-sums.

Mystic Martha says
Ali and Biko's numbers sum to 8.
Ali and Casie's numbers sum to 13.
Biko and Casie's numbers sum to 15.

Before any method, before any algebra: have a go. Try numbers. See what fits.

Two numbers that add to 8, with the smaller being Ali's: that's 1+7, 2+6, 3+5. For each, check whether Casie can be a whole number bigger than Biko that works with the other two sums.

Try Ali = 3, Biko = 5. Then Ali + Casie = 13 gives Casie = 10. Check the last one: Biko + Casie = 5 + 10 = 15. ✓

Ali = 3  ·  Biko = 5  ·  Casie = 10

What this level is doing

Trial and error is the correct first move here. The numbers are small enough that pupils can land the answer through systematic listing. This is the moment to praise adventurousness — trying something without knowing it will work — and systematic listing of the pair (1,7), (2,6), (3,5).

Do not teach a method yet. Pupils need to feel the success of trial and error first, so that when it breaks in Level 2, the need for a method is genuine and not imposed.

If a rapid grasper writes A + B = 8, A + C = 13, B + C = 15 and solves algebraically: praise it, then ask them to also justify by trial. Algebra is a method that arrives; here we are building the soil it grows in.

Level 02

The same problem, with big numbers

Ali, Biko and Casie pick new numbers. The constraints are identical. Only the sums have changed.

Mystic Martha says
Ali and Biko's numbers sum to 156.
Ali and Casie's numbers sum to 193.
Biko and Casie's numbers sum to 215.

Have a go with trial and error. Notice how it feels.

For Ali + Biko = 156, the possible pairs run from (1, 155) all the way down. Trial and error is still possible, but the search has exploded. The numbers themselves haven't changed the problem; the size of the search space has.

This is the pedagogical pivot. The method must now do what trial cannot.

Xia's idea — bar models

Xia draws each sum as two bars side by side. Each person becomes a bar of unknown length, repeated wherever they appear.

Three pair-sums as bars · using the small-number version to see the idea

Stack (A + B) on top of (A + C). That gives one A, another A, one B, and one C. The total is 8 + 13 = 21, which equals A + A + B + C, which is 2A + (B + C).

Subtract the third bar, B + C = 15: 21 − 15 = 6 = 2A. Halve: A = 3.

Then everything falls out: B = 8 − 3 = 5 and C = 13 − 3 = 10.

Yana's idea — add all three

Add all three pair-sums: 8 + 13 + 15 = 36. Every person appears in exactly two of the pair-sums, so the total is 2(A + B + C). Halve: A + B + C = 18.

To find A, subtract the sum that doesn't contain A — that is B + C = 15. So A = 18 − 15 = 3. Same answer. Different route.

Now apply either method to the big problem. Both methods are arithmetic of bars, not algebra. Try Xia's method with the 156 / 193 / 215 sums.

Solver — Three Friends

Enter the three pair-sums. The solver applies Xia's method and shows the working.

What this level is doing

The pivot from trial to method is the entire raison d'être of the session. Pupils must feel that trial has failed before a method feels welcome. If you teach the method first, you have removed the reason for it to exist.

The two methods (Xia, Yana) embody two different aesthetics: local elimination (subtract to isolate one variable) versus global accounting (sum the whole system, then peel off). Both reappear constantly in higher mathematics — Gaussian elimination is Xia's; trace identities are Yana's.

Ask: which method do you prefer, and why? The mathematical content is the comparison, not the answer.

Level 03

From bars to networks — and a parity surprise

Now redraw the problem. Three circles for A, B, C. Three lines joining them. Label each line with the sum of the two people it connects.

Same problem, new picture · sums live on edges, people live on vertices

The bars haven't disappeared. They have been compressed into a network. The same calculation — 10 + 15 − 19 = 6 = 2A — now reads off the diagram directly: add the two edges meeting at A, subtract the edge opposite A.

Mystic Martha gives you these sums: 10, 13, 20. Apply the method. Twice A is 10 + 13 − 20 = 3. So A = 1.5.

Not a whole number. The problem has no integer solution. The numbers Mystic Martha gave you could not have come from three positive integers.

The parity rule

Add all three pair-sums. By Yana's argument, the total is 2(A + B + C). That is always even. So:

If the three pair-sums don't add to an even number, there is no integer solution. This is a necessary condition — a test the data must pass before any method can succeed.

Why does parity matter so much in mathematics? Because it's free information. You don't need to solve anything to apply it. It tells you whether to bother.

Parity Checker

Try your own three pair-sums. Does the data even have a chance of being valid?

What this level is doing

Three things happen here, and they are deeply connected.

First, a change of representation: bar models → networks. The mathematics is identical; the picture is new. This is the single most powerful move in problem-solving: representations don't change what is true, they change what is visible.

Second, the introduction of a necessary condition (parity). Necessary conditions are the working mathematician's compass. They tell you whether you are even in the right country before you start looking for the city.

Third, the dignity of no solution. Year 7 pupils often believe every problem has an answer because every exercise they've met has had one. The mathematical reality is that most data are inconsistent, and recognising inconsistency is a skill, not a failure.

Level 04

Four friends, six sums — and an ambiguity

Now Ali, Biko, Casie and Dev. Same rules: each successive person has a larger number. Mystic Martha gives you all the pair-sums. How many will there be?

The pairs are AB, AC, AD, BC, BD, CD. That's six sums. Already this is the handshake problem in disguise: six handshakes among four people. Hold that thought; we return to it.

Mystic Martha says (in increasing order)
20,   32,   35,   38,   41,   53

Some sums you can identify immediately. Some you cannot.

The smallest sum must be A + B (the two smallest numbers). The next smallest must be A + C. The largest must be C + D. The second largest must be B + D.

So: A+B = 20, A+C = 32, B+D = 41, C+D = 53.

Two sums remain: 35 and 38. They must be B + C and A + D, but we cannot say which is which from the ordering alone.

Use the parity rule from Level 3 on the inner triangle A, B, C. The three sums A+B, A+C, B+C must add to an even total.

20 + 32 + B+C = even. So B+C must be even. Between 35 and 38, only 38 is even. So B+C = 38, A+D = 35.

Then 2A = 20 + 32 − 38 = 14, so A = 7. B = 13, C = 25, D = 28.

Two solutions, same data

Now try this set: 11, 13, 16, 18, 21, 23.

The pinned sums: A+B = 11, A+C = 13, B+D = 21, C+D = 23. The middle two — 16 and 18 — are B+C and A+D, but parity does not disqualify either.

If B+C = 16: A = 4, B = 7, C = 9, D = 14. ✓
If B+C = 18: A = 3, B = 8, C = 10, D = 13. ✓

Both solutions are genuinely valid. The same six sums correspond to two different sets of four integers. Mystic Martha's data is under-determined.

This is profound. Information is not the same as solution. You can have all the pair-sums and still not know the numbers. The mathematics has handed you a fork, and the fork is honest.

Solver — Four Friends

Enter six pair-sums in increasing order. The solver pins what it can and reports any ambiguity or contradiction.

What this level is doing

The combinatorial structure changes. With three people, the network was a triangle — one sum per pair, three sums total, no ordering ambiguity once you know which person is which. With four, the network is the complete graph K₄: six edges, and the middle two pair-sums are not pinned by order.

This level transitions pupils from "is there a solution?" (Level 3) to "how many solutions?" Both questions are mathematical, and the second is often harder. Pupils meet, perhaps for the first time, the idea that a problem can have multiple correct answers without anyone being wrong.

This is also where parity stops being a yes/no test and becomes a tie-breaker between candidate configurations. The technique deepens because the problem demands more of it.

Level 05

Five friends, ten sums — and a global trick

Ali, Biko, Casie, Dev, Eve. Increasing order of numbers. How many pair-sums?

AB, AC, AD, AE, BC, BD, BE, CD, CE, DE. That's ten sums. With n friends, the number of pair-sums is always n(n−1)/2.

That formula will mean something specific in a moment.

Mystic Martha says
8,   10,   13,   14,   16,   19,   20,   21,   22,   25

With ten sums, even working out which is which is hard. Pinning by order only gets you four: A+B, A+C, C+E, D+E. The middle is a maze. So instead of starting locally, start globally.

Add all ten sums. Each of the five people appears in exactly four pair-sums (each person pairs with each of the other four). So:

total of all pair-sums = 4 · (A + B + C + D + E)

8 + 10 + 13 + 14 + 16 + 19 + 20 + 21 + 22 + 25 = 168. Therefore A + B + C + D + E = 42.

Now subtract the smallest pair-sum (which is A + B) and the largest (which is D + E): 42 − 8 − 25 = 9 = C.

From there: A = 1, B = 7, D = 12, E = 13. Check by recomputing all ten sums.

Notice what just happened. The method we used here — total of all sums equals (n − 1) times the grand total — is Yana's idea generalised. When trial and error fails, when local elimination is too tangled, the global identity rescues you. Mathematics rewards stepping back.

Solver — Five Friends (Global Method)

Enter ten pair-sums in any order. The solver applies the global identity to extract the middle person C, then peels the rest.

What this level is doing

Two ideas land here. First, the global identity: each person appears in exactly (n−1) of the pair-sums, so summing all pair-sums gives (n−1) times the grand total. This is the first sighting of a combinatorial double-counting argument, which is one of the most pervasive techniques in all of discrete mathematics.

Second, the formula n(n−1)/2 for the number of pair-sums. Pupils have seen 3 sums (n=3), 6 sums (n=4), 10 sums (n=5). Ask them: do you notice anything? These are the triangular numbers. They will appear again as handshakes.

Bridge

Why this is the handshake problem

If n people walk into a room and everyone shakes hands with everyone else exactly once, how many handshakes happen?

n = 2
1 handshake · 1 pair-sum
n = 3
3 handshakes · 3 pair-sums
n = 4
6 handshakes · 6 pair-sums
n = 5
10 handshakes · 10 pair-sums

The same picture. Every time we counted pair-sums, we were counting handshakes. Every triangle, every K₄, every K₅ is a network in which the people are vertices and the pair-sums are edges.

The handshake formula is exactly n(n − 1)/2. Two people, one handshake. Three, three. Four, six. Five, ten. These are the triangular numbers: 1, 3, 6, 10, 15, 21, ... and they arise here for the same reason they always arise — every pair is counted once.

Why does this matter?

Once you see that Mystic Martha's problem is a problem on a complete graph Kn, you have unlocked a vast territory. The complete graph is the basic object of Ramsey theory, the part of mathematics that asks:

Imagine colouring every edge of Kn either red or blue. How big must n be before you are guaranteed to find three people who all shook hands with the same colour?

The answer is n = 6. This is Ramsey's theorem, and it is one of the most beautiful results in twentieth-century mathematics. It says: complete disorder is impossible. In any sufficiently large structure, some kind of order must emerge.

The session you have just worked through is not "about" Ramsey theory. But it lives in the same building. The instinct to draw the network, count the edges, and ask what must be true — those are the same instincts a research mathematician uses when they meet K17 and ask whether five mutual handshakes of the same colour are unavoidable. (They are. We're not sure about six.)

What to say to a Year 7 class

You don't need to mention Ramsey by name unless they're hungry. But the deep idea — that the network is the problem, and the network has structural rules that survive every change of question — is the single most transferable lesson of the session.

Suggested closing: "Today you have not just solved puzzles. You have practised the move that all mathematicians make: redraw the question until the question redraws itself."

Bridges

What you have just been doing was actually…

This panel is the teacher's panel, addressed to whoever is teaching the teacher. It names the higher-mathematics homes of the techniques the pupils have just practised, so that the session is held inside its full context. None of this needs to reach the pupils unless they ask. All of it should reach the teacher.

The weighted graph framing

The cleanest sentence anyone has ever written about Mystic Martha's puzzle is this: given a weighted complete graph Kn where each edge weight is the sum of the two vertex weights, recover the vertex weights.

Vertices are people. Edges are pair-sums. The complete graph Kn is the object that contains every possible pair, and so it is the natural home of every pair-sum problem. Once a pupil has been shown that the triangle in Level 3 is K3, the K4 diagram is no longer a new picture, it is the same picture with one more vertex. The handshake formula n(n−1)/2 tells you how many edges that picture has.

Stare at the first row. That row is Xia's method. "Take one of the first sum, one of the second, minus one of the third, then halve." The inverse matrix has remembered the algorithm. The other two rows are Xia's method applied to recover B and C respectively. The whole session's local-elimination technique is captured in a single 3×3 matrix.

Yana's method also lives in the matrix, differently. Sum the rows of M: you get (2, 2, 2). So (1,1,1)·s = 2(A+B+C), which is exactly Yana's global identity. Same matrix, two readings — and the two readings are the two methods the pupils discovered.

Four friends: the matrix gets taller

For K4, M is 6×4 — six edges, four vertices. The system is over-determined: more equations than unknowns. Generic data of six numbers has no solution as four-vertex weights. Mystic Martha's data has a solution only because she generated it consistently. The session's parity rule is the simplest integrality test on whether the data lies in the column space of M.

For K5, M is 10×5. Still over-determined. The Level 5 global identity — sum all rows of M to get (4, 4, 4, 4, 4), hence summing all pair-sums gives 4(A+B+C+D+E) — is the statement that the all-ones vector lies in the row space of M with a known coefficient. In general, for Kn, summing rows gives (n−1, n−1, …, n−1), and that is Yana's identity, scaled to any n.

The Level 4 two-solution case, properly understood

Once you assign each of the six pair-sums to a specific edge of K4, the linear system has a unique solution. The ambiguity in Level 4 is not algebraic — it is combinatorial, and it lives one level above the linear algebra. The data of Mystic Martha consists of an unordered multiset of six numbers, and there is more than one way to assign these numbers to the six edges of K4 in a way consistent with the ordering A < B < C < D.

This is a beautifully clean example for a teacher: a problem that has both an algebraic layer (uniquely solvable once the assignment is fixed) and a combinatorial layer (the assignment is not always unique). Inverse problems in physics and engineering routinely have this exact two-stage structure.

The list of higher-mathematics homes

Beyond linear algebra, what else has the pupil quietly practised?

Double counting

Yana's argument — each person appears in exactly (n−1) pair-sums, so summing all pair-sums gives (n−1) times the total — is a double-counting argument. You count the same total in two different ways and equate the results. This is one of the foundational moves of combinatorics, and pupils have just done one in Year 7.

Invariants

"What does not change?" The sum of all pair-sums is an invariant of the labelling: rename the people however you like, the total stays the same. Searching for invariants is one of the great recurring instincts in mathematics, from school problems to Noether's theorem.

Identifiability and inverse problems

When does the data uniquely determine the answer? The Level 4 two-solution case is a clean and honest example of non-identifiability: data that allows more than one explanation. This is the central question of statistics, signal processing, and inverse problems across the sciences.

Binomial coefficients and Pascal

The number of pair-sums is C(n, 2) = n(n−1)/2. These are the entries of the third diagonal of Pascal's triangle and they are also the triangular numbers. The five views — pair-sums, handshakes, edges of Kn, triangular numbers, the third Pascal diagonal — are the same object. A pupil who feels that equivalence has had an experience that compresses several years of separate-looking topics into a single shape.

Necessary and sufficient conditions

Parity is necessary but not sufficient. Pupils almost never meet this distinction explicitly until A-level. Here it lives natively: the parity test can rule a problem out, but passing the test does not rule it in. The asymmetry is the lesson.

Inequalities as substrate

The constraints A < B < C < D do half the work of the session and they are inequalities. The two-solution case in Level 4 is a region carved by constraints, not a point produced by an equation. Pupils have just lived inside the way higher mathematics actually behaves.

The single sentence to give the teacher

Mystic Martha's puzzle is an inverse problem on the weighted complete graph Kn, solvable by an incidence-matrix linear system whose row sums encode the global trick and whose individual rows encode the local trick. The pupil-facing language for any of that is the bar model and the network diagram. The teacher who can hold both languages at once teaches the session twice over, once for the room and once for the future.

Notes

For the teaching of teachers

This learner is built to be usable in two registers at once: a Year 7 / Year 8 student walks through Levels 1 to 5 and meets a complete arc of investigation. A teacher who has run this with their own class then revisits the same pages with the "For the Teacher" panels open and sees the architecture beneath.

The five skills this session builds

1. Adventurous trial

The willingness to try numbers when you don't yet know they'll work. Level 1 rewards this. Level 2 deliberately punishes it. Pupils need both experiences.

2. Representation switching

Bars → networks. Same maths, different picture. Pupils who can move between representations solve problems pupils who can't, can't.

3. Necessary conditions

Parity arrives as a free filter. It cannot prove a solution exists; it can prove one cannot. Pupils need to feel the asymmetry. (Necessary, not sufficient is a vocabulary worth introducing here in the teacher's voice even if not in the pupil's.)

4. Multiplicity

Four friends, the same six sums, two valid answers. Mathematical problems can have many solutions; mathematical problems can have none. The notion that every problem has exactly one answer is a school artefact, not a mathematical truth.

5. Global thinking

When the local view drowns in possibilities, sum everything. Yana's instinct, scaled. This is the same instinct that gives rise to integrals, traces, partition functions, and most of what physicists do.

Where to dwell, where to move on

The slowest part of the session should be the transition from Level 1 to Level 2. The fastest part can be Level 4's solver — pupils who have grasped the parity argument will sprint through it. Level 5 is where the strongest pupils should be encouraged to make their own problems and challenge each other.

Vocabulary worth seeding

Pair-sum, network, edge, vertex, parity, necessary, sufficient, under-determined, global identity, complete graph. None need to be tested. All should be heard.

The deeper rationale

Sums in Pairs is not really about pair-sums. It is about the way mathematicians take a piece of data and ask three questions in sequence: can I solve it locally? if not, can I redraw it? if I redraw it, what survives? Those three questions are the engine of mathematics, and a pupil who learns to ask them at thirteen is unlikely to stop asking them at thirty.

Mystic Martha will never tell you the numbers. She tells you what is preserved under summation. The pupil's job is to recover what was hidden. The teacher's job is to make sure they remember the recovery, not just the result.
