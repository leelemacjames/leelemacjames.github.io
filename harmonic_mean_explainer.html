When averages
lie about rates

You already know the arithmetic mean. But for springs in parallel, resistors in parallel, and any quantity that adds as reciprocals, a different average — the harmonic mean — is the one that actually describes the physics.

Section 1

The arithmetic mean you already know

The arithmetic mean of a set of numbers is what you normally call "the average": add them up, divide by how many there are.

Arithmetic mean of n numbers

A = (x₁ + x₂ + … + xₙ) / n

Example: mean of 60 and 40 → A = (60 + 40) / 2 = 50

This works perfectly when you are averaging additive quantities — heights, temperatures, masses. But it fails when you are averaging rates or quantities whose reciprocals add together.

The classic trap. A car drives 60 km at 60 km/h, then the same 60 km at 40 km/h. The arithmetic mean speed is 50 km/h — but the actual average speed for the whole journey is 48 km/h. At 50 km/h the journey would take 2.4 h; it actually takes 2.5 h. The arithmetic mean overcounts the faster leg.

The true average speed is the harmonic mean of 60 and 40. The same idea appears everywhere rates or conductances add.

Harmonic mean — general definition

H = n / (1/x₁ + 1/x₂ + … + 1/xₙ)
step 1 Take the reciprocal of every value:   x → 1/x
step 2 Find their arithmetic mean (add, divide by n)
step 3 Take the reciprocal of that result to flip back
For two values:   H = 2ab / (a + b)    (simplification of the formula above)

Section 3

A worked example: H of 60 and 40

1
Take reciprocals
1/60 ≈ 0.01667     1/40 = 0.025
2
Add the reciprocals
1/60 + 1/40 = 2/120 + 3/120 = 5/120 = 1/24
3
Find the arithmetic mean of the reciprocals (÷ 2 because n = 2)
(1/24) / 2 = 1/48
4
Flip back — take the reciprocal
H = 1 / (1/48) = 48

The arithmetic mean is 50. The harmonic mean is 48. The harmonic mean is always less than or equal to the arithmetic mean (equal only when all values are identical).

Quick check for the car problem. At 60 km/h for 60 km: time = 1 h. At 40 km/h for 60 km: time = 1.5 h. Total: 120 km in 2.5 h → average speed = 120/2.5 = 48 km/h. ✓ The harmonic mean is correct; the arithmetic mean (50) is not.

Section 4

Why does the harmonic mean appear in physics?

Whenever a physical law says that reciprocals add, the combined quantity is a harmonic mean (scaled by n). This happens in two situations you have studied:

Situation Law (what adds) Combined formula Relation to H
Resistors in parallel Conductances add: G = 1/R 1/R = 1/R₁ + 1/R₂ R = H/2   (for two equal R)
Springs in parallel Stiffnesses add: k_total = k₁ + k₂ k = k₁ + k₂ Arithmetic sum — see note below
Springs in series Compliances add: 1/k 1/k = 1/k₁ + 1/k₂ k = H/2   (for two equal k)
Key pattern. When the reciprocal of the quantity adds — stiffness adds for parallel springs, conductance (= 1/R) adds for parallel resistors, compliance (= 1/k) adds for series springs — the combined value is always a harmonic-mean type expression. The harmonic mean is what you get when you put two identical such elements together.

Series spring system

Same force through each spring; extensions add. Compliances (1/k) add, so k_eff is a harmonic-mean expression.

1/k_eff = 1/k₁ + 1/k₂

Resistors in parallel

Parallel resistor circuit

Same voltage across each; currents add. Conductances (1/R) add, so R_eff is a harmonic-mean expression.

1/R_eff = 1/R₁ + 1/R₂
R_eff = (R₁ × R₂)/(R₁ + R₂) = H(R₁,R₂)/2
Effective resistance R_eff
6.67 Ω

Bonus: springs in parallel (arithmetic sum)

Springs in parallel share the same extension; forces add. So stiffnesses simply add — this is the arithmetic case, not harmonic.

k_parallel = k₁ + k₂    (arithmetic — not harmonic)
This is why a parallel system is stiffer than either spring alone, while a series system is softer.

Section 6 — Going further

Four kinds of average — and how they rank

Mathematicians recognise four standard means of two positive numbers a and b. They always obey the same inequality:

NameFormula (for a, b)Physics use
Quadratic (RMS) Q = √((a²+b²)/2) AC voltage/current; rms speed of gas molecules
Arithmetic A = (a+b)/2 Average of additive quantities (mass, temperature)
Geometric G = √(ab) Compound growth; impedance of LC circuits
Harmonic H = 2ab/(a+b) Parallel resistors; series springs; average speed
The means inequality — always true for a ≠ b
Q (quadratic)   ≥   A (arithmetic)   ≥   G (geometric)   ≥   H (harmonic)
Equality holds everywhere only when a = b

You can see this with a = 60, b = 40:

Q = √((3600+1600)/2) = √2600 ≈ 50.99
A = (60+40)/2 = 50.00
G = √(60×40) = √2400 ≈ 48.99
H = 2×60×40/(60+40) = 4800/100 = 48.00
The big picture. The harmonic mean is the smallest of the four means (for unequal values). It is always pulled towards the smaller value — which is physically correct for parallel circuits, where the smallest resistance dominates the combined resistance.

Section 7 — Practice

Three worked calculations to try yourself

P1
Resistors R₁ = 10 Ω, R₂ = 20 Ω, R₃ = 30 Ω in parallel
1/R = 1/10 + 1/20 + 1/30 = 6/60 + 3/60 + 2/60 = 11/60
R_eff = 60/11 ≈ 5.45 Ω
Note: the harmonic mean of 10, 20, 30 would be 3/(11/60) = 180/11 ≈ 16.4 Ω, which is 3 × R_eff. So R_eff = H/3 for three resistors.
P2
Springs k₁ = 600 N/m and k₂ = 200 N/m in series
1/k_eff = 1/600 + 1/200 = 1/600 + 3/600 = 4/600
k_eff = 600/4 = 150 N/m
The softer spring (200 N/m) dominates — the series system is always softer than the weakest spring.
P3
Average speed over equal distances: 60 km/h then 40 km/h
H = 2×60×40 / (60+40) = 4800/100 = 48 km/h
Arithmetic mean (50 km/h) gives the wrong answer. The harmonic mean (48 km/h) gives the right total time.
