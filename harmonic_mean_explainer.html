A puzzle about
average speed

You know how to find an average. But some averages are wrong — not because of arithmetic mistakes, but because the type of average is the wrong one for the job. This page starts with a puzzle and ends with a pattern that appears in springs, circuits, and mathematics itself.

Section 1

The car problem — before you read any further, have a guess

A car drives from London to Brighton — exactly 60 km — at a speed of 60 km/h. It then drives the same 60 km back at 40 km/h.

What is the car's average speed for the whole journey?

Your instinct: most people say 50 km/h — the arithmetic mean of 60 and 40. Write down your guess before clicking below.
  • 1
    Time for the outward leg (60 km at 60 km/h):
    time = distance / speed = 60 / 60 = 1.0 hour
  • 2
    Time for the return leg (60 km at 40 km/h):
    time = 60 / 40 = 1.5 hours
  • 3
    Total distance and total time:
    total distance = 120 km    total time = 2.5 hours
  • 4
    True average speed:
    average speed = 120 / 2.5 = 48 km/h
The arithmetic mean gives 50 km/h, but the correct answer is 48 km/h. If you drove the whole 120 km at 50 km/h it would take 2.4 hours — but the real journey takes 2.5 hours. The arithmetic mean is wrong here, not because of a calculation error, but because speed is a rate: time spent at each speed is not equal.

The car spends more time at the slower speed (1.5 h vs 1.0 h), so the slow leg has more influence on the average. The arithmetic mean treats both legs equally — which is only fair when equal time is spent at each speed, not equal distance.

So: what kind of average gives 48? Let's investigate.

Arithmetic mean A = (a + b) / 2 = 50. This is the instinctive answer — and we showed in Section 1 that it's wrong for this problem. Let's look for something else.

The geometric mean of two numbers is G = √(a × b). It is used for quantities that multiply rather than add — compound growth, for instance. Let's compute it and see where it sits.

Geometric mean
G = √(60 × 40) = √2400 ≈ 48.99

Interesting — G ≈ 48.99, which is close to 48 but not exact. The geometric mean undershoots the arithmetic mean. But it is still slightly above 48.

Notice the direction: A (50) > G (≈ 48.99) > 48. The true answer seems to sit just below G.

The geometric mean squared is simply G² = a × b. For 60 and 40 that is 2400. Now — what if we divide G² by A?

The ratio G² / A
G² / A = 2400 / 50 = 48 ✓ Written out: (a × b) / ((a+b)/2) = 2ab / (a+b)
G² / A = 48 exactly. This matches the true average speed from Section 1. We have found a formula that works.

Let's write it cleanly. Using A = (a+b)/2 for the arithmetic mean and G² = ab for the geometric mean squared:

G² / A = ab / ((a+b)/2) = 2ab / (a+b)

This quantity has a name. Mathematicians call it the harmonic mean.

Definition — Harmonic Mean
H = 2ab / (a + b)
equivalently:
H = G² / A     ← the relationship we just discovered
H = n / (1/x₁ + 1/x₂ + … + 1/xₙ)     (general form for n values)

The harmonic mean is always less than or equal to the arithmetic mean, and less than or equal to the geometric mean. Equality holds only when a = b. This gives the famous chain:

Means inequality — always true when a ≠ b
Q (quadratic/rms) ≥ A (arithmetic) ≥ G (geometric) ≥ H (harmonic)
For 60 and 40:   Q ≈ 50.99  ≥  A = 50  ≥  G ≈ 48.99  ≥  H = 48

Section 3

Why does G² / A give the right average speed?

The formula 2ab/(a+b) comes directly from the distance-over-time calculation. Here is the algebra:

Derivation — average speed over equal distances
Let each leg have distance d, leg 1 at speed a, leg 2 at speed b. average speed = total distance / total time = 2d / (d/a + d/b) = 2d / [d(1/a + 1/b)] = 2 / (1/a + 1/b) = 2ab / (a + b) = H ✓

The d cancels — the result is independent of the actual distance, which is why the formula works for any equal-distance two-leg journey. The key move is dividing by time: time is distance divided by speed, which introduces a reciprocal (1/speed). That reciprocal structure is what makes the harmonic mean the right tool.

The general principle. Whenever a physical law involves quantities whose reciprocals add — resistances in parallel, spring compliances in series, rates over equal intervals — the combined result is a harmonic-mean type expression. The arithmetic mean overcounts the larger values because it does not account for the unequal time (or displacement) spent at each value.

Section 6 — Conductance & Conservation of Flow

Why parallel resistors add their conductances — not their resistances

The reason the harmonic mean appears in parallel circuits is not arbitrary — it follows directly from a conservation law and a natural change of variable. The same logic reappears in water pipes, and in a slightly different form in spring systems.

The conservation law at a junction. At any junction in a network, whatever is flowing must be conserved: charge cannot pile up at a node in a circuit; water cannot pile up at a pipe junction. This is known as Kirchhoff's Current Law (circuits) and the Continuity Equation (fluids). It forces the individual branch flows to add up to the total flow.

Step 1 — Define conductance as the natural variable

Resistance R measures how hard it is for current to flow. Its reciprocal, conductance G = 1/R (unit: siemens, S), measures how easy it is. Ohm's law rewritten in terms of conductance becomes:

Ohm's law — two equivalent forms
V = IR    ⟺    I = GV    where G = 1/R Current = conductance × voltage. The higher the conductance, the more current for the same voltage.

Step 2 — Apply conservation at the junction (parallel circuit)

In a parallel circuit, the same voltage V appears across every branch. Conservation of charge (KCL) says the total current equals the sum of the branch currents:

KCL — current conservation at the junction
I_total = I₁ + I₂ = G₁V + G₂V = (G₁ + G₂)V ∴ G_eff = G₁ + G₂    (conductances ADD in parallel) Converting back: R_eff = 1/G_eff = 1/(1/R₁ + 1/R₂) = R₁R₂/(R₁+R₂) = H/2
The harmonic mean is inevitable here. Once you accept conservation of charge (currents must add) and Ohm's law (current = conductance × voltage), the formula for parallel resistance follows without any choice. Adding conductances, then inverting, is exactly the three-step harmonic-mean recipe.

Section 7 — The Water Pipe Analogy

Conservation of mass in pipe networks — the same mathematics

Water flowing through a pipe network obeys exactly the same conservation principle as charge in a circuit: mass (and volume, for an incompressible fluid) is conserved at every junction. This is the Continuity Equation — what flows in must flow out.

The driving difference and flow resistance

In a circuit, current is driven by a voltage difference. In a pipe, volumetric flow rate Q (m³/s) is driven by a pressure difference ΔP. For laminar (smooth) flow in a cylindrical pipe, the Hagen-Poiseuille law plays the role of Ohm's law:

Hagen-Poiseuille law — the fluid Ohm's law
Q = ΔP / R_f    where    R_f = 8ηL / (πr⁴) η = fluid viscosity, L = pipe length, r = pipe radius. Notice the r⁴ dependence — doubling the radius increases flow 16-fold.

Hydraulic conductance (also called hydraulic transmissivity or flow coefficient) is then K = 1/R_f = πr⁴/(8ηL), exactly parallelling electrical conductance G = 1/R. Higher K means more flow for the same pressure difference.

Pipes in parallel — conservation forces conductances to add

When two pipes share the same inlet and outlet pressure ΔP, the total flow rate is the sum of the individual flows — by conservation of mass:

Continuity equation at a junction
Q_total = Q₁ + Q₂ = C_f1·ΔP + C_f2·ΔP = (C_f1 + C_f2)·ΔP ∴ C_f,eff = C_f1 + C_f2    (fluid conductances ADD in parallel) Equivalent fluid resistance: R_f,eff = 1/(C_f1 + C_f2) = R_f1·R_f2/(R_f1+R_f2) = H/2
Pipes in parallel — flows add (Q₁ + Q₂)
K_eff = K₁ + K₂  →  R_f,eff = H(R_f1, R_f2)/2
Pipes in series — same flow Q throughout
R_f,eff = R_f1 + R_f2  (arithmetic — not harmonic)

Pipes in series — like resistors in series — share the same flow rate Q. The pressure drops add: ΔP_total = ΔP₁ + ΔP₂ = Q·R_f1 + Q·R_f2 = Q(R_f1 + R_f2), so the effective fluid resistance is the simple arithmetic sum of the individual resistances — no harmonic mean involved.

The term used for hydraulic conductance — the pipe-flow analogue of electrical conductance — is sometimes called hydraulic transmissivity or simply the flow coefficient. The symbol K (or K_h) is standard: K = 1/R_f = πr⁴/(8ηL). Just as electrical conductance G = 1/R measures ease of current flow per volt, hydraulic conductance K measures ease of fluid flow per pascal of pressure difference.

The deep unity. In every case, the harmonic-mean formula appears because (i) a conservation law forces the flows to add, and (ii) each flow equals conductance × driving difference. Adding conductances, then inverting back to resistance, is the harmonic-mean recipe. The mathematics does not care whether the flow is charge, water, or mechanical extension — the structure is identical.

Why series gives the arithmetic mean and parallel gives the harmonic mean

Series — shared flow
Same current/flow/force
Driving differences add
ΔV = I·R₁ + I·R₂ = I(R₁+R₂)
R_eff = R₁ + R₂ (arithmetic)
Parallel — shared driving difference
Same voltage/pressure/force
Flows (currents) add
I = G₁V + G₂V = (G₁+G₂)V
G_eff = G₁+G₂ (conductances, arithmetic!)
R_eff = 1/G_eff (harmonic of R values)

Notice the satisfying symmetry: resistances add arithmetically in series; conductances add arithmetically in parallel. In both cases something adds arithmetically — the choice of whether to work with resistance or conductance determines which configuration looks "simple".
