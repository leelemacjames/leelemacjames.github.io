Rectangle Numbers, Oblongs, Squares and Odd Numbers

A visual learner for seeing composites as arrays, comparing perimeter and area, and discovering why consecutive square numbers differ by odd numbers.

Area = number of unit squares Perimeter = distance around the outside Composite = can be made as a non-trivial rectangle Odd numbers = square growth layer

1. Preamble: area and perimeter

A rectangle with width w and height h contains w × h unit squares.

The perimeter counts the outside boundary: top + bottom + left + right.

Area = w × h
Perimeter = 2w + 2h = 2(w + h)

The important comparison is not just area or perimeter alone, but their ratio:

Perimeter : Area = P / A = 2(w + h) / wh

Long thin rectangles have a lot of outside for the amount of inside. Squarer rectangles pack area more efficiently.

4×6. The closer a rectangle is to square, the less boundary it needs for the same area.

2. Composite numbers as rectangle numbers

A number is composite if it can be arranged as a rectangle in more than one row and more than one column.

n is composite if n = a × b with a > 1 and b > 1.

A prime number cannot make a proper rectangle. It only makes a line: 1 × p.

So factorisation is geometry: finding the side lengths of possible rectangles.

3. Oblong numbers: almost squares

An oblong number is made by multiplying two consecutive whole numbers.

Oblong number = n(n + 1)

Examples:

1×2 = 2, 2×3 = 6, 3×4 = 12, 4×5 = 20, 5×6 = 30

Oblongs are special because they sit exactly between square shapes:

n² < n(n + 1) < (n + 1)²

They are rectangles, but only just not squares.

Area 30
Perimeter 22
P/A 0.733

4. Squares: the most compact rectangles

A square number forms an n by n square.

Square number = n²

For a square:

Area = n²
Perimeter = 4n
P/A = 4n / n² = 4/n

As the square gets larger, P/A falls. Bigger squares have less edge per unit of area.

Area 36
Perimeter 24
P/A 0.667

P/A decreases. A squarer shape encloses more area with less boundary. This is why squares have a special compactness among rectangles.

6. Difference between two squares

Now compare two square numbers. The difference between them is the area left after removing the smaller square from the larger square.

a² − b² = (a − b)(a + b)

This is not just algebra. It is a cut-and-rearrange rectangle result.

The gap between two squares factorises into the difference of the side lengths and the sum of the side lengths.

8² − 5² = 64 − 25 = 39 = (8 − 5)(8 + 5)

7. Consecutive squares produce odd numbers

The most important special case is when the two squares are consecutive:

(n + 1)² − n²

Visually, to grow an n by n square into an (n + 1) by (n + 1) square, we add:

n cells along the right + n cells along the bottom + 1 corner cell

So the added layer has:

n + n + 1 = 2n + 1

That is always odd. Consecutive square numbers differ by odd numbers.

6² − 5² = 36 − 25 = 11 = 2×5 + 1
Key claim: Why are odd numbers the natural growth layers of squares?
Because each new square layer adds two equal arms and one corner: n + n + 1 = 2n + 1.

9. Final connection

Composite numbers are numbers with rectangular structure. Oblong numbers are the rectangles nearest to squares. Square numbers are the most symmetric rectangle numbers. Their growth is controlled by odd numbers.

1 + 3 + 5 + ... + (2n − 1) = n²

A square is built by stacking odd layers. Odd numbers are not merely numbers that fail to divide by 2. They are the visible increments of square growth.
