Estimation & Significant Figures

Knowing how much you know — and how much you don't
IB Physics SL/HL — Measurement & Uncertainties (Topic 1)

Why precision matters

Every measurement is an approximation. When you write \(m = 1.00 \times 10^2\) g, the three digits are a statement: "I am confident in this value to about 1 part in 100." The number of significant figures you report is a compact encoding of the precision of your measurement — or, in a calculation, of the precision your inputs permit.

Reporting too many figures is dishonest — it claims a precision you don't have. Reporting too few throws away information you've earned. The IB is strict about this: an otherwise correct answer with the wrong number of significant figures will lose marks.

This page covers the rules for counting significant figures, how to propagate them through calculations, how to make order-of-magnitude estimates, and the classification of physical quantities into scalars and vectors — the first step in knowing what kind of mathematical object you're working with.

§1 Counting Significant Figures
What are significant figures, and how do you count them? ▾

Significant figures (s.f.) are the digits in a number that carry meaningful information about its precision. The rules:

1. Non-zero digits are always significant.
789 → 3 s.f.   4.56 → 3 s.f.

2. Zeros between non-zero digits are significant.
709 → 3 s.f.   1.003 → 4 s.f.

3. Leading zeros are never significant. They are placeholders only.
0.023 → 2 s.f.   0.00508 → 3 s.f.

4. Trailing zeros after a decimal point are significant. They show deliberate precision.
1200.0 → 5 s.f.   3.50 → 3 s.f.

5. Trailing zeros without a decimal point are ambiguous. Convention treats them as not significant.
1200 → 2 s.f.   100 → 1 s.f.

6. Exact numbers have infinite significant figures. Counting numbers and defined constants are exact.
"12 eggs" → exact.   \(\pi\) → exact.   "2" in \(E_k = \tfrac{1}{2}mv^2\) → exact.

Scientific notation removes all ambiguity. \(1.200 \times 10^3\) is unambiguously 4 s.f. \(1.2 \times 10^3\) is 2 s.f. When in doubt, use scientific notation.
Quick test: how many significant figures in each of these?   0.0040  |  30200  |  6.022 × 10²³  |  10.0 ▾

\(0.0040\)2Leading zeros don't count. The trailing zero after the 4 does count (it's after a decimal point).

§2 Significant Figures in Calculations
What is the rule for significant figures in multiplication and division? ▾

The result has the same number of significant figures as the least precise input.

Example — Volume of ice:

Given: \(m = 1.00 \times 10^2\) g (3 s.f.) and \(\rho = 0.917\) g cm⁻³ (3 s.f.).

\(V = \frac{m}{\rho} = \frac{1.00 \times 10^2}{0.917} = 109.05... \;\text{cm}^3\)

Both inputs have 3 s.f., so the answer is reported to 3 s.f.:

\(V \approx 109 \;\text{cm}^3\)
Notice: \(m\) has 3 s.f. despite having 4 digits (the "1.00" part). The leading "1" counts, both zeros after the decimal count, and the \(\times 10^2\) is just a scale factor. Always count s.f. from the coefficient in scientific notation.
Example — finding current from power and resistance. Given \(P = 1.35\) W (3 s.f.) and \(R = 1.00 \times 10^2\) Ω (3 s.f.), find the current. ▾

Using \(P = I^2 R\), rearrange to \(I = \sqrt{P/R}\):

\(I = \sqrt{\frac{1.35}{1.00 \times 10^2}} = \sqrt{0.0135} = 0.11619...\;\text{A}\)

\(P\) has 3 s.f. and \(R\) has 3 s.f., so the result is reported to 3 s.f.:

\(I \approx 0.116 \;\text{A}\)
IB trap: the square root does not change the sig fig rule. You might think "taking a root should halve the precision" — it doesn't. The rule is about the inputs, not the operation. \(\sqrt{P/R}\) still has 3 s.f. if both inputs have 3 s.f.
What about addition and subtraction — is the rule different? ▾

Yes. For addition and subtraction, you match the number of decimal places, not the number of significant figures.

Example: \(12.52 + 1.7 = 14.22 \to 14.2\)

The least precise input (\(1.7\)) has 1 decimal place, so the result is rounded to 1 decimal place.

Why the rules differ:

Multiplication/division are about relative precision (percentage uncertainty). Addition/subtraction are about absolute precision (the smallest increment you're sure about). Relative precision is encoded in sig figs; absolute precision is encoded in decimal places.

In IB Physics, most calculations involve multiplication and division (formulae like \(F = ma\), \(E = mc^2\), \(V = IR\)). The "match the fewest s.f." rule covers perhaps 90% of what you'll encounter. The addition rule matters most when combining uncertainties or subtracting close values.
Should I round at every step, or only at the end? ▾

Only at the end. Keep extra digits (at least 2 more than needed) throughout intermediate steps and round only the final answer. Premature rounding introduces cumulative errors.

Example: A student calculates \(I = 0.116\) A (correctly to 3 s.f.), then uses it to find power dissipated \(W = IV\Delta t\). If they rounded \(I\) to 0.12 A first, the error in \(W\) could be 3–4% — enough to lose marks.

The IB calls this the "guard digit" principle. Work with at least one extra significant figure during intermediate steps. Round to the appropriate number of s.f. only in the final reported answer.
§3 Orders of Magnitude & Estimation
What is an order-of-magnitude estimate, and when does the IB expect one? ▾

An order-of-magnitude estimate gives the answer as a power of 10. It says "about \(10^n\)" without worrying about the precise coefficient. The goal is to get within a factor of ~3 of the real answer.

Example: How many heartbeats in a human lifetime?

\(\sim 70 \text{ beats/min} \times 60 \text{ min/hr} \times 24 \text{ hr/day} \times 365 \text{ days/yr} \times 75 \text{ yr}\)
\(\approx 70 \times 5 \times 10^5 \times 75 \approx 70 \times 75 \times 5 \times 10^5 \approx 2.6 \times 10^9\)

Order of magnitude: \(\sim 10^9\) heartbeats. (The precise answer is about \(2.8 \times 10^9\) — we're well within a factor of 3.)

The IB expects you to be comfortable estimating physical quantities to the nearest power of 10. Common ones to know:

The point of estimation is not to get the right number — it's to get the right scale. If your calculation gives a nuclear radius of \(10^{-10}\) m, you've confused atomic with nuclear, and the order-of-magnitude check catches it instantly.
How do you estimate the number of molecules in a glass of water? ▾

This is a classic IB estimation. Work with round numbers:

A glass holds about 250 mL = 250 g of water (since \(\rho_{\text{water}} \approx 1\) g/mL).

Molar mass of water: \(M \approx 18\) g/mol.

\(n = \frac{250}{18} \approx 14 \;\text{mol}\)

Number of molecules:

\(N = 14 \times 6 \times 10^{23} \approx 8 \times 10^{24} \sim 10^{25}\)

Order of magnitude: \(\sim 10^{25}\) molecules.

Notice the estimation technique: \(250/18\) is "a bit more than 10" — call it 14. Multiply by \(6 \times 10^{23}\) — that's "a bit less than \(10^{25}\)". You don't need a calculator; you need a sense of scale.
§4 Scalars & Vectors
What is the difference between a scalar and a vector? ▾

A scalar is described completely by its magnitude (a number and a unit). A vector requires both magnitude and direction.

The physical test: if the quantity changes when you rotate your coordinate system, it's a vector. If it doesn't, it's a scalar.

Notice the pairs: distance (scalar) vs displacement (vector); speed (scalar) vs velocity (vector). Distance tells you how far you travelled. Displacement tells you where you ended up relative to where you started. You can walk 5 km (distance) and have zero displacement if you return to the start.
Is electric current a scalar or a vector? It has a "direction" (conventional current flows from + to −), so why isn't it a vector? ▾

Current is a scalar. The "direction" of current is a convention about sign (positive = towards lower potential), not a geometric direction in space. The test for a vector is whether it obeys the parallelogram law of addition.

Consider a junction where two wires meet a third. The currents obey \(I_1 + I_2 = I_3\) (Kirchhoff's junction rule), which is scalar addition. They do not combine like arrows tip-to-tail. If the two input wires meet at 90°, the output current is \(I_1 + I_2\), not \(\sqrt{I_1^2 + I_2^2}\).

This is a favourite IB exam trap. Current has a sign and flows through a circuit, but it is not a vector. The underlying vector quantity is current density \(\vec{J}\) (current per unit area), which does point in a spatial direction.
Why is energy a scalar even though it can be "directed" (like kinetic energy of a moving object)? ▾

Kinetic energy is \(\tfrac{1}{2}mv^2\). The velocity is a vector, but \(v^2 = \vec{v} \cdot \vec{v}\) is the dot product of the velocity with itself — which always produces a scalar. Squaring "kills" the direction.

This is why a car moving north at 30 m/s has exactly the same kinetic energy as a car moving east at 30 m/s. The energy doesn't care about direction — only about magnitude of speed.

The same logic applies to potential energy (\(mgh\) depends only on height, not horizontal direction), to thermal energy (temperature is a scalar), and to work (\(W = \vec{F} \cdot \vec{s}\) is a dot product — scalar output).

The dot product is nature's way of extracting scalar information from vectors. Work = force · displacement. Power = force · velocity. KE = ½m(velocity · velocity). Every time energy appears, a dot product has already projected away the directional information.
§5 Common Pitfalls
A student uses a value \(I = 0.368\) A (from a previous calculation) and \(\Delta t = 1.00\) s to find energy \(W = IV\Delta t\), but \(V\) is not given. What went wrong? ▾

The formula \(W = IV\Delta t\) requires three inputs: current, voltage, and time. With only two, the calculation is incomplete. This sounds obvious, but it appears in multi-step problems where students carry a result forward and forget they still need another quantity.

The sig fig lesson here is subtler: never conflate "precision" with "completeness". Having \(I\) to 3 s.f. and \(\Delta t\) to 3 s.f. is useless if \(V\) is missing. The precision of the inputs only matters when you have all the inputs.

In IB exams, if a quantity is not given and cannot be derived from other information, state this explicitly. Writing "insufficient data" is a valid and expected answer. Don't invent a value.
When should I give 2 s.f., 3 s.f., or 4 s.f. in an IB answer? ▾

The IB rule of thumb:

Match the precision of the data given in the question. If the inputs are given to 3 s.f., report your answer to 3 s.f. If inputs have mixed precision (e.g. one value to 2 s.f., another to 4 s.f.), report to the least precise — typically 2 or 3 s.f.

In practice for IB Physics:

• Most data in IB questions is given to 3 s.f. → report to 3 s.f.

• If the question says "estimate" or "order of magnitude" → 1 or 2 s.f. is fine.

• If you're asked to "show that" a value is approximately some target → show at least one more s.f. than the target to demonstrate it rounds correctly.

• In the IA (Internal Assessment) → match the precision of your instruments. If your ruler reads to ±0.5 mm, don't report a length to 6 decimal places.

The safe default in IB: 3 significant figures. This matches most question data, looks professional, and almost never loses marks. When in doubt, give 3.
