Topology

Euler's formula
V − E + F = 2

For any convex polyhedron, count its vertices V, edges E, and faces F. The alternating sum is always the same number:

V − E + F = 2

This is not arithmetic luck — it is a topological invariant. The number 2 = χ is the Euler characteristic of a sphere. Any convex polyhedron can be continuously deformed onto a sphere without tearing, so it inherits χ = 2.

Three quick checks

The topological key: Euler proved this by flattening any polyhedron onto a plane — projecting it as a planar graph. Every planar graph satisfies V − E + F = 2. This triangulation argument will reappear to prove Pick's theorem.

Proof

Pick's theorem
proved by Euler

For a polygon with vertices on the integer square lattice:

A = I + B/2 − 1

where I = interior lattice points, B = boundary lattice points. The proof is a clean application of Euler's formula on a triangulation.

Before the proof — why a primitive triangle has area ½

You already know that a triangle has area = ½ × base × height. On the integer lattice we can always place one vertex of the triangle at the origin and read off the other two as vectors v₁ and v₂. The base is |v₁| and the height is |v₂|sin θ, where θ is the angle between them:

Area = ½ × |v₁| × |v₂| × sin θ = ½ |det[v₁, v₂]|

The second equality is a standard fact: the determinant of the 2×2 matrix whose columns are v₁ and v₂ equals |v₁||v₂|sin θ — the signed area of the parallelogram they span, so half that is the triangle area.

A triangle is primitive precisely when the two edge vectors v₁, v₂ from any vertex form a basis for ℤ² — meaning every integer lattice point can be written as an integer combination of them. This is exactly the condition |det[v₁,v₂]| = 1. No lattice points can sneak inside the triangle or onto its edges, because any such point would be a non-trivial fractional combination — contradicting the basis condition.

This is the one fact we need. Everything else in the proof is combinatorics.

Five steps

1
Triangulate. Subdivide the polygon into primitive triangles. As shown above, each has area exactly ½. Let T be the number of such triangles.
2
Apply Euler. The triangulation is a planar graph: V = I + B, F = T + 1 (triangles plus the outer face). Euler gives V − E + F = 2, so T = E − V + 1.
3
Count edges two ways. Each interior edge borders 2 triangles; each of the B boundary edges borders 1. So 3T = 2E − B, giving E = (3T + B) / 2.
4
Substitute. T = E − V + 1 expands to T = (3T+B)/2 − (I+B) + 1. Rearranging: T = 2I + B − 2.
5
Conclude. Each primitive triangle has area ½, so A = T/2 = (2I + B − 2)/2 = I + B/2 − 1. ∎
The pivotal fact — to be developed shortly: the area ½ per primitive triangle came entirely from |det| = 1 for the square lattice basis vectors. The determinant of the basis matrix is the fundamental area unit. On a different lattice with a different basis, the determinant changes — and Pick's formula inherits that factor directly.

Linear Algebra

Basis vectors
& the determinant

A lattice is all integer combinations of two basis vectors. The fundamental parallelogram spanned by those vectors is the unit tile. Its area equals the absolute value of the determinant of the basis matrix.

Eigenvectors — the natural axes of a matrix

A matrix M acts on vectors by stretching and rotating. A vector v is an eigenvector if M only scales it:

M·v = λ·v

The scale factor λ is the eigenvalue. Eigenvectors point along the transformation's natural axes. The product of all eigenvalues equals the determinant — which is why det measures how much the transformation scales area.

New lattice

The Eisenstein lattice
ℤ[ω]

The Gaussian integers ℤ[i] give the square lattice — basis vectors at 90°. Now we introduce a second lattice built on cube roots of unity. Let ω = e2πi/3, a primitive cube root of unity satisfying ω³ = 1 and ω² + ω + 1 = 0.

ω = −½ + (√3/2)i = e2πi/3

The Eisenstein integers ℤ[ω] = {a + bω : a,b ∈ ℤ} form a triangular lattice — the densest packing of equal circles in the plane. The two basis vectors are 1 (along the real axis) and ω (at 60° to it).

The single line that changes is: primitive triangle area = ½|det| = ½ · √3/2 = √3/4. So the generalised Pick formula for any lattice with fundamental domain area Δ is: A = Δ · (I + B/2 − 1).
