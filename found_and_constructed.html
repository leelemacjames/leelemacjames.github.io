The Found and the Constructed

On searching the parameter space of generalised recurrences, finding that almost nothing is named, and what string theorists already know about this kind of disappointment.

Architect Carbon · Lee McCulloch-James

The Stirling triangle is the binomial triangle with one weight upgraded from a constant to a position-dependent function. Pascal's recurrence has both coefficients equal to one. Stirling's recurrence has the column index $k$ in place of the constant one, on the first term:

S(n,k) = k · S(n−1,k) + S(n−1,k−1)

The upgrade has consequences. The new array counts set partitions, the row sums are the Bell numbers, the Bell numbers are the moments of a Poisson distribution. A single coefficient promoted from constant to function lands us, by a route that is structural and not at all metaphorical, in three independently meaningful pieces of mathematics. Set partitions are studied because partitions are studied. Bell numbers are studied because the row sums of a famous array are studied. The Poisson connection is studied because Poisson distributions arise everywhere counting processes occur.

That triple coincidence is the kind of thing that suggests a method. If promoting a constant to a function inside Pascal's recurrence gave us set partitions, surely promoting a constant inside Delannoy's recurrence will give us something similarly meaningful. Delannoy has three terms instead of two and three constants to promote, so the method seems to offer not one but three candidate generalisations, three fresh combinatorial families waiting to be discovered. The conjecture writes itself.

I want to record what happens when one actually carries out the experiment, because the result is not the obvious one and the disappointment teaches something worth knowing.

The three candidates

Delannoy's recurrence is

D(m,n) = D(m−1,n) + D(m,n−1) + D(m−1,n−1)

with three terms, three constants all equal to one. Promote the column index $n$ into each in turn and one obtains three sister recurrences:

V1:   n · D(m−1,n) + D(m,n−1) + D(m−1,n−1)
V2:   D(m−1,n) + n · D(m,n−1) + D(m−1,n−1)
V3:   D(m−1,n) + D(m,n−1) + n · D(m−1,n−1)

Each is a well-defined recurrence. Each generates a clean integer array. Each has a coherent combinatorial reading: V2, for instance, counts lattice paths from the origin to $(m,n)$ using three step types, where each right-step entering column $j$ carries a label drawn from $\{1, 2, \ldots, j\}$. The verification of this combinatorial interpretation against the recurrence is exact for every entry I checked.

V2 also produces structural surprises. Its boundary row is the factorials: $V_2(0, n) = n!$. The first interior row admits a closed form involving the harmonic numbers, $V_2(1, n) = n!\,(1 + n + H_n)$. The whole array satisfies a recursive integration formula in which each row is built from the previous one by an Abel-style summation with $n!$ in the denominator. These are the kinds of structural features that, in the Stirling case, would have been good news.

None of it matters. The central spine of V2 produces the sequence

1, 3, 22, 255, 4006, 79240, 1888076, 52603299, 1677297910, ...

and the sequence is, as far as I can tell, in no standard reference for any reason. Its values are clean, its recurrence is short, its closed form is partially expressible in classical functions. It counts something — by construction, the labelled lattice paths described above — but the thing it counts is not a thing anyone was previously asking about. The labels on the right-steps are not properties the paths intrinsically possess. They were imposed to make the recurrence work.

V1 and V3 are similar. Each is consistent. Each has a forced combinatorial reading. None of the three counts an object that mathematics had previously thought worth studying.

What Stirling actually had

To see why the experiment failed, return to the Stirling case and look closely at what made it work. The recurrence $S(n,k) = k \cdot S(n-1,k) + S(n-1,k-1)$ is not just formally consistent. It matches, term by term, a question a combinatorialist was already asking: how to build a partition of an $n$-element set into $k$ blocks. Element $n$ joins one of the $k$ existing blocks of a partition of $\{1, \ldots, n-1\}$ into $k$ blocks, contributing $k \cdot S(n-1,k)$, or it opens a new block by joining a partition into $k-1$ blocks, contributing $S(n-1,k-1)$. The factor $k$ is the number of choices the new element actually faces, not a number chosen to balance the equation. The recurrence and the combinatorial question are the same statement.

In V2 there is no such match. The factor $n$ on the right-step is not the number of choices a path actually faces; the path, as a sequence of $U$, $R$, $D$ moves, faces no choices that scale with the column it has reached. We inserted the factor and then constructed an interpretation that justifies it. The resulting object is a coherent combinatorial entity but a manufactured one, made-to-order for the recurrence rather than discovered as an answer to a counting problem.

Stirling's meaning was found, not constructed. V2's would have to be constructed.

This is the distinction worth stating clearly. There is found meaning and there is constructed meaning, and they are not the same thing. Found meaning is what happens when a formal structure aligns with an independent question that was already being asked. Constructed meaning is what happens when one writes down a formal structure and then engineers a question to which it is the answer. Both are real in the sense that both produce coherent mathematical objects with their own internal logic. But found meaning carries weight that constructed meaning does not, because the alignment with an independent question is what gives the object purchase outside the formalism that produced it.

The moduli space

Physicists working on string theory have been living with a version of this problem for forty years. The theory comes equipped with a parameter space, sometimes called the moduli space or the landscape, of formally consistent vacua. Compactify the extra dimensions one way and one gets one vacuum, compactify them another way and one gets another, choose the flux configurations differently and the count multiplies. The estimates of how many points there are in this space range from very large to formally infinite, and the controversial part is not the size but the selection: how does nature pick the point we observe? The Standard Model, with its three families of fermions and its specific gauge group and its small positive cosmological constant, corresponds to a point in this landscape, but the formalism by itself does not say why this point. Almost every other point in the landscape would describe a universe with different physics, mostly a universe in which we are not here to ask the question.

The parallel with the combinatorial situation is not perfect, but it is structural enough to be useful. In both cases there is a parameter space of formally consistent constructions, generically much larger than the subset of constructions corresponding to anything independently meaningful. In both cases the formalism does not, by itself, select the meaningful subset. The selection is made externally: in physics, by the observational fact that we exist in a universe with these particular features; in mathematics, by the historical fact that some combinatorial questions were being asked before anyone wrote down the recurrence that answered them. Set partitions had a literature before Stirling numbers had a name. The labelled lattice paths that V2 counts have a literature only because I just wrote one paragraph about them.

The analogy slips at one important point and I want to be honest about it. In string theory there is at least a candidate selection mechanism — anthropic reasoning, controversial but argued in detail — that explains why the meaningful point in the landscape is the one we find ourselves at. In combinatorics there is no equivalent. Stirling is not the chosen point because we exist in a universe where set partitions matter; Stirling is the chosen point because set partitions, for purely mathematical reasons, were already being studied and Stirling's recurrence happens to match them. The analogy gives us the structure of the parameter space and the rarity of meaningful points, but it does not give us a mechanism. Pure mathematics has no anthropic principle.

What the analogy does give us, and what makes it more than ornament, is a vocabulary for what we are doing when we generalise. Generalising a famous recurrence is exploring the local neighbourhood of a meaningful point in a parameter space. The neighbourhood is mostly empty of meaning. The nearby points are formally consistent but combinatorially anonymous. The meaningful point at which we started is meaningful because of its connections beyond the formalism, and those connections do not propagate smoothly to its neighbours. Stirling is one point in the parameter space of recurrences and set partitions are one body of independently studied combinatorial knowledge, and the line that connects them does not have other lines parallel to it leading from nearby points to nearby bodies of knowledge.

What this is worth knowing

The pedagogical temptation when one teaches Pascal's triangle, Stirling numbers, and Bell numbers in sequence is to suggest a method: see how this small change to the recurrence produces this rich theory, and imagine what other small changes might produce other rich theories. The temptation is wrong in a way that is worth being explicit about. The richness of Stirling's theory is not an inevitable consequence of having modified the Pascal recurrence in a structurally clean way. It is a consequence of the modification having landed on an object that combinatorialists had independent reasons to care about. Other clean modifications, even ones that look just as natural and produce just as coherent integer arrays, do not land on such objects, and there is no formal way to tell in advance which ones will.

This has a humbling implication for what we are doing when we write down generalisations. We are exploring a landscape, in the same loose sense the string theorists use the word. Most of the landscape is anonymous. The named points are named because of connections that the formalism did not create and cannot generate, and the connections cannot be conjured by formal moves alone. The work of finding a meaningful generalisation is not the work of writing down a clean recurrence; it is the work of finding the recurrence that matches a question that was already being asked.

I will continue to explore the local neighbourhood of Delannoy, because the exploration is interesting in itself and because the local features — V2's harmonic-number closed form for its first row, its factorial boundary, its Abel-style integration formula — are worth understanding even if no famous object lies at the end of the path. But I will not pretend that the exploration is on the way to a discovery, because the discovery requires an external connection that I cannot produce by formal manipulation, and the absence of an external connection for V2 is not a failure of my search but a feature of the landscape we are searching.

The named members of a family are named because of their connections beyond the formalism. Formal consistency is cheap. External meaning is rare.

There is, finally, a small consolation that the comparison with the string landscape offers. The physicists have not given up. They have learnt to live with a parameter space they cannot fully enumerate and a selection problem they cannot fully solve, and they have developed habits of thought that take the landscape seriously without pretending the difficulty is not real. The mathematical version of the problem is far smaller and far more tractable, and the habits of thought transfer easily. One can write down generalisations of famous recurrences, observe their structural features, work out their closed forms, and refuse to be disappointed when none of them turns out to count something with a name. The landscape contains very few named points, and the work of mathematics is partly the work of accepting how few there are.
