Spectral Conditions for Two-Colour Replacement Urns

From the general balanced replacement matrix down to Pólya, Friedman, and Ehrenfest as worked specialisations.

The general object

An urn holds balls of two colours. Each step draws a ball with probability equal to that colour's current proportion, then returns balls according to a fixed rule. The entire asymptotic theory is read from one matrix.

Let \(Z_n = (Z_n^{(1)}, Z_n^{(2)})\) be the colour counts after \(n\) draws and \(X_n = Z_n / \|Z_n\|_1\) the proportion vector. The dynamics are fixed by a replacement matrix

\[ A = \begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{pmatrix} \]

where row \(i\) records how many balls of each colour are added when colour \(i\) is drawn: drawing colour 1 adds \(a\) of colour 1 and \(b\) of colour 2, and so on.

Balance condition The classical theory assumes each row sums to the same constant, \(a+b = c+d = S\). Then the total ball count grows deterministically as \(n S\) and the proportion is the right object to track. Without balance the urn needs the heavier non-balanced stochastic-approximation machinery; everything below assumes balance unless stated.

Under balance, \(S\) is automatically the dominant (Perron) eigenvalue of \(A\), with the left eigenvector giving the limiting proportion direction. The work is all in the second eigenvalue.

The spectral classification

Order the eigenvalues \(\lambda_1 \ge \operatorname{Re}\lambda_2\). Under balance \(\lambda_1 = S\). The single decisive quantity is the ratio

\[ \rho = \frac{\operatorname{Re}\lambda_2}{\lambda_1}. \]
Eigenvalue-ratio theorem (two colours)

The proportion \(X_n\) converges almost surely to the deterministic left-eigenvector direction of \(\lambda_1\). The fluctuations about that limit obey a trichotomy:

If \(\rho < \tfrac12\): fluctuations are Gaussian of order \(n^{-1/2}\). Standard central limit behaviour; the past is forgotten.

If \(\rho = \tfrac12\): still Gaussian, but with a \(\sqrt{\log n}\) inflation of the variance scale. The critical case.

If \(\rho > \tfrac12\): fluctuations are of order \(n^{\rho-1}\), which does not vanish on the CLT scale. The limit law is non-Gaussian and history-dependent. This is the lock-in phase.

The threshold \(\rho = \tfrac12\) is the whole conceptual payload. It is the line separating processes that forget their history from processes whose terminal state is decided by early, randomly amplified fluctuations. Everything in the specialisations below is just locating each named urn relative to this line.

Mechanically: writing the proportion recursion as a stochastic approximation \(X_{n+1} = X_n + \gamma_n\big(h(X_n) + \text{noise}\big)\) with step \(\gamma_n \sim 1/n\), the linearised drift near the fixed point has Jacobian eigenvalue \(\rho\). The competition between the \(n^{-1/2}\) noise injection and the \(n^{\rho-1}\) memory of past noise crosses over exactly at \(\rho = \tfrac12\). That is where the threshold comes from rather than being imposed.

Specialisation I — Classic Pólya

Draw a colour, return it, add one more of the same colour. Pure positive reinforcement.

\[ A = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}, \qquad S = 1. \]

Eigenvalues \(\lambda_1 = \lambda_2 = 1\), so \(\rho = 1 > \tfrac12\). This sits at the extreme of the lock-in phase. The degeneracy is not incidental: it pushes the model past mere non-Gaussian fluctuation into a random limit. The proportion converges almost surely, but to a \(\mathrm{Beta}(\alpha,\beta)\) random variable set by the initial composition, not to a fixed number.

Reading The terminal state is decided by the early draws and then frozen. Different runs of the same urn converge to different limits. This is the canonical positive-feedback attractor: the past gets a permanent vote.

Specialisation II — Friedman (anti-momentum)

Draw a colour, return it, add one of the opposite colour. Negative reinforcement, the mean-reverting cousin.

\[ A = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}, \qquad S = 1. \]

Eigenvalues are \(\lambda_1 = +1\) and \(\lambda_2 = -1\), so \(\rho = -1 < \tfrac12\). Firmly in the CLT phase. The proportion converges to the deterministic value \(\tfrac12\) with Gaussian fluctuations of order \(n^{-1/2}\). No randomness in the limit, no lock-in.

Reading The negative second eigenvalue is the algebraic signature of restoring oscillation: a transient excess of one colour actively biases the next addition against it. Same balance constant \(S=1\) as classic Pólya, opposite asymptotic universe, and the only thing that changed was moving the \(1\)s off the diagonal.

Specialisation III — Ehrenfest (removal-based reversion)

Here the pedagogy must be honest: Ehrenfest does not fit the additive balanced template, and seeing why is the lesson.

The Ehrenfest scheme has \(N\) balls split between two urns; each step picks a ball uniformly and moves it to the other urn. In replacement language this is a removal rule with a state-dependent transition probability, not a constant additive matrix. It therefore falls outside the affine-replacement family entirely.

The consequence is a different asymptotic object. The additive urns above have proportions that converge (to a point, or to a random variable). The Ehrenfest urn instead has a genuine stationary distribution that it relaxes toward and then maintains: \(\mathrm{Binomial}(N, \tfrac12)\). Nothing converges to a single value; a distribution is held in place.

The right linear-algebraic handle is no longer the replacement matrix but the Markov transition operator \(P\) on occupation states. Its eigenvalues are

\[ \mu_k = 1 - \frac{2k}{N}, \qquad k = 0, 1, \dots, N. \]

The spectral gap \(1 - \mu_1 = 2/N\) sets the relaxation rate toward stationarity. This parallels the \(\rho\) story — a subdominant eigenvalue controlling how fast memory of the initial condition decays — but on a different matrix doing analogous work.

The deliberate distinction Additive balanced urns: subdominant eigenvalue of the replacement matrix \(A\) governs whether proportions lock in. Ehrenfest: subdominant eigenvalue of the transition operator \(P\) governs relaxation toward a maintained distribution. Same idea (a second eigenvalue runs the asymptotics), two structurally different operators. Conflating them is the classic error; separating them cleanly is the point of including Ehrenfest at all.

The general parameterised matrix

Collapse the three instances back into one tunable object. Take the balanced two-colour replacement matrix with row sum \(S=1\):

\[ A(p) = \begin{pmatrix} p & 1-p \\ 1-p & p \end{pmatrix}, \qquad p \in [0,1]. \]

This is symmetric and doubly balanced, so its eigenvectors are fixed regardless of \(p\): the Perron vector \((1,1)/2\) and the contrast vector \((1,-1)\). The eigenvalues are

\[ \lambda_1 = 1, \qquad \lambda_2 = 2p - 1, \qquad \Rightarrow \qquad \rho(p) = 2p - 1. \]

The single dial \(p\) now sweeps the entire trichotomy, and the named urns are just landmarks on it:

\(p\)\(\rho = 2p-1\)RegimeNamed instance
0 −1 CLT, deterministic ½ Friedman (anti-momentum)
¼ −½ CLT —
¾ ½ Critical, \(\sqrt{\log n}\) phase boundary
⅞ ¾ Lock-in, \(n^{\rho-1}\) (elephant-walk analogue)
1 1 Random Beta limit Classic Pólya

The pedagogically clean statement: p < ¾ forgets its history, p = ¾ is the knife-edge, p > ¾ remembers it, and p = 1 is the degenerate endpoint where memory becomes total and the limit itself goes random. Ehrenfest sits deliberately off this line — not a value of \(p\), but a different operator class — which is exactly why it earns a separate treatment rather than a row in this table.

What the later simulation must confirm, not discover

Fix \(p\) and estimate the fluctuation exponent of \(X_n\) about its limit by regressing \(\log \mathrm{Var}(X_n)\) on \(\log n\). The predicted slope is \(-1\) for \(p < \tfrac34\) and \(2\rho - 2 = 4p - 4\) for \(p > \tfrac34\), with a \(\log n\) inflation exactly at \(p = \tfrac34\). The simulation's only job is to land on these analytically fixed lines.
