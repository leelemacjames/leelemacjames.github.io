Spectral Conditions for Two-Colour Replacement Urns

Two concrete urns first. The contrast between them forces the general object. The classification is then the answer to a question you already hold.

Start concrete — Classic Pólya

An urn with one red and one blue ball. Draw one, return it, add a second ball of the colour you drew. Repeat. Pure positive reinforcement: whatever leads, leads harder.

Run it and watch the red proportion. It wanders, then settles. Run it again from the same start and it settles somewhere else. The surprising fact, the one to sit with before any theory: the proportion converges almost surely, but the value it converges to is itself random. For the symmetric start it is uniform on \([0,1]\); in general

\[ X_n \longrightarrow \mathrm{Beta}(\alpha_0, \beta_0), \]

with parameters set by the initial composition. The limit is not a number the urn is seeking. It is a number the early draws happen to decide, after which the dynamics freeze.

Hold onto this Different runs of the identical urn reach different terminal proportions. The early, randomly amplified fluctuations get a permanent vote. Whatever invariant of this process explains "the past never washes out" is the thing we are eventually going to name.

Now its mirror — Friedman

Same urn, same draw, one change: add a ball of the opposite colour to the one you drew. Negative reinforcement. Whatever leads gets pushed back.

Run it and watch again. This time every run does the same thing. The red proportion converges — to \(\tfrac12\), every time, with small Gaussian wobble of size \(n^{-1/2}\) around it. No randomness in the destination, no dependence on how the early draws happened to fall.

Hold onto this too Friedman differs from Pólya by a single structural choice: the added ball goes to the other colour instead of the same one. The rule changed by almost nothing. The asymptotic behaviour changed completely — from a random frozen limit to a deterministic forgotten-history one.

The bridge

The question both urns have now forced

Encode each urn as the matrix that says, for each drawn colour, how many of each colour get added. Pólya is the identity \(\big(\begin{smallmatrix}1&0\\0&1\end{smallmatrix}\big)\). Friedman is the swap \(\big(\begin{smallmatrix}0&1\\1&0\end{smallmatrix}\big)\). Both have rows summing to 1. They differ only in whether the entries sit on or off the diagonal.

One freezes into a random limit, the other reverts to a fixed one. What single invariant of a \(2\times 2\) matrix flips between these two outcomes?

That is the entire motivation for going general. We are not introducing a replacement matrix because the theory wants one; we are introducing it because we have two concrete urns whose only difference is the placement of two 1s, and we need to know which property of the matrix is doing the work. The answer turns out to be the second eigenvalue, and once that is the suspect, the right move is to write down every matrix of this shape at once and read the property off directly.

The general object

Let \(Z_n\) be the colour counts after \(n\) draws and \(X_n = Z_n/\|Z_n\|_1\) the proportion vector. Draw colour \(i\) with probability equal to its current proportion, then add \(A_{ij}\) balls of colour \(j\), where

\[ A = \begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{pmatrix} \]

is the replacement matrix: row \(i\) is what gets added when colour \(i\) is drawn. Pólya and Friedman are now just two points in this family.

Balance condition The clean theory needs each row to sum to the same constant, \(a+b=c+d=S\). Then the total ball count grows deterministically as \(nS\) and the proportion is the meaningful object. Both motivating urns satisfy this with \(S=1\). Without balance the analysis needs heavier non-balanced stochastic-approximation tools; everything below assumes balance.

Under balance, \(S\) is automatically the dominant (Perron) eigenvalue \(\lambda_1\), and its left eigenvector gives the deterministic limiting direction of \(X_n\). All the interesting behaviour — the very thing separating our two urns — lives in the second eigenvalue \(\lambda_2\).

The classification — answering the bridge

Order the eigenvalues \(\lambda_1 \ge \operatorname{Re}\lambda_2\). The single decisive quantity is the ratio

\[ \rho = \frac{\operatorname{Re}\lambda_2}{\lambda_1}. \]
Eigenvalue-ratio theorem (two colours)

\(X_n\) converges almost surely to the deterministic \(\lambda_1\)-direction. Its fluctuations about that limit obey a trichotomy:

\(\rho < \tfrac12\): Gaussian fluctuations of order \(n^{-1/2}\). History is forgotten.

\(\rho = \tfrac12\): still Gaussian, with a \(\sqrt{\log n}\) inflation. The knife-edge.

\(\rho > \tfrac12\): fluctuations of order \(n^{\rho-1}\), not vanishing on the CLT scale; non-Gaussian, history-dependent limit. Lock-in.

Now resolve the bridge with the two urns in hand:

Pólya. \(A=\big(\begin{smallmatrix}1&0\\0&1\end{smallmatrix}\big)\), eigenvalues \(1,1\), so \(\rho=1\). Past the threshold, and at the degenerate extreme where \(\lambda_1=\lambda_2\): this is exactly why the limit is not merely non-Gaussian but a random Beta variable. The frozen-random behaviour you watched is the \(\rho=1\) endpoint.

Friedman. \(A=\big(\begin{smallmatrix}0&1\\1&0\end{smallmatrix}\big)\), eigenvalues \(+1,-1\), so \(\rho=-1\). Far below the threshold. Deterministic limit \(\tfrac12\), Gaussian \(n^{-1/2}\) wobble. The negative second eigenvalue is the algebraic fingerprint of the restoring oscillation you observed.

So the invariant the bridge asked for is \(\rho\), and the line that flips the behaviour is \(\rho=\tfrac12\). The two urns were never qualitatively different processes with different rules; they were the same family evaluated on opposite sides of one eigenvalue threshold.

Where the threshold comes from, briefly: write the proportion recursion as a stochastic approximation \(X_{n+1}=X_n+\gamma_n(h(X_n)+\text{noise})\) with \(\gamma_n\sim 1/n\). The linearised drift near the fixed point has Jacobian eigenvalue \(\rho\). Fresh noise enters at scale \(n^{-1/2}\); remembered past noise decays at scale \(n^{\rho-1}\). They cross exactly at \(\rho=\tfrac12\). The threshold is derived, not imposed.

The deliberate exception — Ehrenfest

It is tempting to throw every "balls and urns" model into the family above. Ehrenfest is the instructive case where you must not, and seeing why sharpens the whole framework.

The Ehrenfest scheme: \(N\) balls split between two urns; each step moves a uniformly chosen ball to the other side. In replacement language this is a removal rule with a state-dependent transition probability — not a constant additive matrix. It falls outside the affine balanced family entirely.

The consequence is a different kind of limit. The additive urns have a proportion that converges (to a point, or to a random variable). Ehrenfest has no converging proportion at all; instead it relaxes toward and then maintains a stationary distribution, \(\mathrm{Binomial}(N,\tfrac12)\).

The right linear-algebraic handle is therefore not the replacement matrix but the Markov transition operator \(P\), with eigenvalues

\[ \mu_k = 1 - \frac{2k}{N}, \qquad k=0,1,\dots,N, \]

and spectral gap \(1-\mu_1 = 2/N\) setting the relaxation rate. This parallels the \(\rho\) story — a subdominant eigenvalue running the asymptotics — but on a different operator doing analogous work.

The distinction worth teaching Additive balanced urns: second eigenvalue of the replacement matrix \(A\) decides whether proportions lock in. Ehrenfest: second eigenvalue of the transition operator \(P\) decides how fast a maintained distribution is reached. Same idea, structurally different operators. Forcing Ehrenfest into the \(A\)-family is the standard error; keeping it adjacent-but-separate is the point of including it.

One dial through the whole family

Collapse the additive case into a single tunable matrix. Balanced, symmetric, row sum \(1\):

\[ A(p) = \begin{pmatrix} p & 1-p \\ 1-p & p \end{pmatrix}, \qquad p \in [0,1]. \]

Symmetric and doubly balanced, so the eigenvectors are fixed for all \(p\): Perron \((1,1)/2\) and contrast \((1,-1)\). The eigenvalues are

\[ \lambda_1 = 1, \qquad \lambda_2 = 2p-1, \qquad \Rightarrow \qquad \rho(p) = 2p-1. \]

The two motivating urns are now just the endpoints of one slider:

Ehrenfest is deliberately not a value of \(p\) — different operator class — which is exactly why it earns separate treatment rather than a row here.

What a later simulation must confirm, not discover

Fix \(p\), estimate the fluctuation exponent by regressing \(\log\mathrm{Var}(X_n)\) on \(\log n\). Predicted slope: \(-1\) for \(p<\tfrac34\), and \(4p-4\) for \(p>\tfrac34\), with a \(\log n\) inflation exactly at \(p=\tfrac34\). The numerics' only job is to land on these analytically fixed lines.
