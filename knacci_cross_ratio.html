The k-nacci Cross-Ratio

The cross-ratio of a linear recurrence strips away the dominant growth and exposes the conjugate roots — the algebraic structure that doesn't dominate but does determine the projective invariant.

I

The Cross-Ratio

The cross-ratio is the sole numerical invariant of projective geometry. Given four collinear points with coordinates a, b, c, d:

ξ(A,B,C,D) = (a − b)(c − d)(b − c)(d − a)
The only quantity preserved by every projective transformation

Distances change under projection. Ratios of distances change. But this combination does not. Drag the observer to verify.

II

The Fibonacci Cross-Ratio

The cross-ratio of four consecutive Fibonacci numbers converges as n → ∞. Asymptotically Fn ≈ Cφn. Substituting, C cancels, and the remaining powers of φ simplify:

Derivation

Set a = φn−3, b = φn−2, c = φn−1, d = φn:

(1−φ)(φ²−φ³) / [(φ−φ²)(φ³−1)] = φ²(1−φ)² / [φ(1−φ)(φ³−1)]

One (1−φ) and one φ cancel, leaving φ(1−φ)/(φ³−1). The characteristic equation φ² = φ + 1 gives φ³ = 2φ + 1, so φ³ − 1 = 2φ:

ξ = φ(1−φ)/(2φ) = (1−φ)/2

Vieta gives φ + φ* = 1, so 1 − φ = φ*:

ξFib = φ*2 = −12φ ≈ −0.30902
Half the conjugate golden ratio
III

The General Formula

The derivation depends on φ only through Tn ≈ Cλn. For any dominant root λ:

The cancellation

C cancels. After extracting powers of λ:

(1 − λ)(λ² − λ³) / [(λ − λ²)(λ³ − 1)] = λ²(1−λ)² / [λ(1−λ)(λ³−1)]

One (1−λ) and one λ cancel, giving λ(1−λ)/(λ³−1). Factor λ³−1 = (λ−1)(λ²+λ+1) and cancel (λ−1):

ξ∞ = −λλ² + λ + 1
Cross-ratio limit for any recurrence with dominant root λ
IV

Tribonacci: −1/τ²

The Tribonacci characteristic equation τ³ = τ² + τ + 1 gives τ² + τ + 1 = τ³ directly:

ξTrib = −ττ³ = −1τ² ≈ −0.29560
A pure inverse-square expression in the Tribonacci constant

VI

The Companion Matrix

Every k-nacci recurrence is generated by a k × k companion matrix M: first row all 1s, subdiagonal all 1s, everything else zero. The eigenvalues of M are exactly the roots of the characteristic polynomial — and this is where φ* enters the picture concretely.

Fibonacci: M is 2 × 2

M = ⎡1  1
1  0⎤

This matrix has two eigenvalues: φ ≈ 1.618 and φ* ≈ −0.618. They are both eigenvalues of the same matrix — not of different matrices or submatrices. Their product is the determinant: φ · φ* = det(M) = −1. Their sum is the trace: φ + φ* = tr(M) = 1.

The corresponding eigenvectors are [φ, 1]T and [φ*, 1]T. This is straightforward to verify:

M · [φ, 1]T = [φ+1, φ]T = [φ², φ]T = φ · [φ, 1]T

using φ² = φ + 1; and identically for φ* since (φ*)² = φ* + 1 (same characteristic equation).

The state vector [Fn, Fn−1]T decomposes in this eigenbasis as:

c₁ · φⁿ · [φ, 1]T  +  c₂ · (φ*)n · [φ*, 1]T

The first term dominates; the second decays as |φ*/φ|n = (1/φ²)n ≈ (0.382)n. This ratio |φ*/φ| — the spectral ratio — determines how fast the subdominant contribution vanishes. It is the single number that controls how quickly the cross-ratio converges to its limit.

Tribonacci: M is 3 × 3

M = ⎡1  1  1
1  0  0
0  1  0⎤

This matrix has three eigenvalues: the real dominant root τ ≈ 1.839, and a complex conjugate pair α ≈ −0.420 + 0.606i and ᾱ ≈ −0.420 − 0.606i, with |α| = |ᾱ| ≈ 0.737.

Again, all three are eigenvalues of the same matrix. Vieta's formulas read off the matrix invariants: τ + α + ᾱ = tr(M) = 1, and τ · α · ᾱ = det(M) = 1. The last relation gives |α|² = 1/τ — the bridge between the dominant root and the subordinate modulus.

The spectral ratio is |α/τ| ≈ 0.401, larger than Fibonacci's 0.382. The subdominant contribution decays more slowly: convergence to the cross-ratio limit is slower for Tribonacci than for Fibonacci.

VII

The Span Matrix M³

The cross-ratio uses four consecutive terms Fn−3, Fn−2, Fn−1, Fn — a window of three transitions. The matrix connecting the state at step n−3 to the state at step n is M³.

Fibonacci M³

M³ = ⎡3  2
2  1⎤

The eigenvalues of M³ are the cubes of the eigenvalues of M: φ³ ≈ 4.236 and (φ*)³ ≈ −0.236. The determinant det(M³) = (det M)³ = (−1)³ = −1. The trace tr(M³) = φ³ + (φ*)³ = 4.

The ratio of subdominant to dominant eigenvalue is |(φ*)³/φ³| = |φ*/φ|³ ≈ 0.056. After three applications of M, the subdominant eigenvector's contribution has been suppressed by a factor of ~18 relative to the dominant. This is the mixing rate over the cross-ratio window: by the time four consecutive terms are used, the state is already 94% aligned with the dominant eigenvector. The cross-ratio, as a projective quantity, sees only the algebraic relationship between the eigenvalues — it is a projective invariant of the transition structure that M³ represents.

Tribonacci M³

The eigenvalues of M³ are τ³ ≈ 6.222, α³ ≈ 0.389 + 0.097i, ᾱ³ ≈ 0.389 − 0.097i. The spectral ratio |α³/τ³| = |α/τ|³ ≈ 0.064. Mixing is slightly slower than Fibonacci — the complex subdominant roots decay less aggressively over three steps. The trace tr(M³) = τ³ + α³ + ᾱ³ = 7; the determinant det(M³) = 1.

Dividing the master formula through by λ² yields a form that makes the approach to −2/7 transparent:

ξ∞ = −1λ + 1 + 1/λ
When λ → 2, the denominator → 2 + 1 + ½ = 7/2, giving ξ → −2/7

XI

−2/7 and the Feigenbaum Allusion

The differences δk = ξk − ξk+1 shrink geometrically, with their ratio converging to 2.

The companion-matrix formulation makes the parallel with Feigenbaum's period-doubling picture more vivid. As k increases, the matrix M grows by one dimension, the memory depth of the Markov chain extends by one step, and the spectral gap narrows (|α/λ| moves closer to 1). Each increment is a structural change in the transition matrix — a kind of bifurcation — that shifts the projective invariant toward its accumulation point −2/7. The cross-ratio functions as a bifurcation measure: a single number assigned to each member of the family, converging geometrically to a universal limit with a characteristic scaling constant.

Where the analogy breaks

The structural parallel is genuine, but the universality classes are different. Feigenbaum's δF ≈ 4.669 arises from a fixed point of the renormalisation operator on function space — a nonlinear mechanism that is the same for any smooth unimodal map. The scaling ratio 2 follows from elementary analysis: λk − 2 ~ 1/2k (a consequence of the geometric series in the characteristic equation), and ξ is smooth in λ, so δk inherits the rate.

Attempts to enrich the scaling by normalising with the subdominant eigenvalue — dividing δk by some power of |αk| — do not produce a new constant. The subdominant modulus 1 − |αk| decays at a fundamentally different rate from ξk + 2/7, and no fixed power of |α| can match the exponential convergence rate of the dominant root. The cross-ratio lives in a linear world; Feigenbaum's constant lives in a nonlinear one.

The allusion is instructive precisely because it clarifies the boundary. The k-nacci cross-ratio is a projective invariant of a linear transition structure with variable memory depth, converging to a universal limit through algebraic exhaustion. Feigenbaum's constant is a projective-like invariant of a nonlinear dynamical system, converging through renormalisation. Same shape of convergence; different engine driving it.
