The k-nacci Cross-Ratio

The cross-ratio of four consecutive terms of a linear recurrence converges to a limit determined entirely by the dominant root. The recurrence controls the algebra; the cross-ratio extracts it.

I

Recap: The Fibonacci Limit

In the projective geometry learner, we saw that the cross-ratio of four consecutive Fibonacci numbers Fn−3, Fn−2, Fn−1, Fn converges as n → ∞ to a value involving the conjugate golden ratio:

ξFib = 1 − φ 2  =  φ* 2  ≈ −0.30902
where φ = (1+√5)/2 and φ* = (1−√5)/2 is the conjugate golden ratio

But why this value? The answer lies in a general formula that applies to any linear recurrence — and seeing how it specialises to Fibonacci, Tribonacci, Tetranacci, and beyond reveals the algebraic structure at work.

II

The General Formula

Consider any linear recurrence whose dominant characteristic root is λ. Asymptotically, the n-th term behaves as Tn ≈ Cλn for some constant C. The cross-ratio of four consecutive terms Tn−3, Tn−2, Tn−1, Tn then simplifies by substituting a = Cλn−3, b = Cλn−2, c = Cλn−1, d = Cλn:

The cancellation

The constant C cancels immediately (appearing equally in every factor). The powers of λ factor as:

(1 − λ)(λ² − λ³) / [(λ − λ²)(λ³ − 1)] = λ²(1−λ)² / [λ(1−λ)(λ³−1)]

One factor of (1−λ) cancels, one power of λ cancels, leaving:

ξ∞ = λ(1 − λ) λ³ − 1
General cross-ratio limit for any recurrence with dominant root λ

This is the master formula. Everything else follows by substituting the characteristic equation of the specific recurrence, which lets us simplify the denominator λ³ − 1 in terms of lower powers of λ.

III

Specialisation by Recurrence

Each k-nacci recurrence — Tn = Tn−1 + Tn−2 + ⋯ + Tn−k — has a characteristic polynomial whose largest real root λk determines the asymptotic growth. Plugging each λk into the master formula and using the characteristic equation to simplify:

λ² = λ + 1 → φ³ = 2φ + 1

From φ² = φ + 1, multiply by φ: φ³ = φ² + φ = (φ+1) + φ = 2φ + 1.

So λ³ − 1 = 2φ + 1 − 1 = 2φ, and:

ξ = φ(1−φ)/(2φ) = (1−φ)/2 = φ*/2 ✓

The φ in numerator and denominator cancels — leaving a rational function of φ alone.

λ³ = λ² + λ + 1 → λ³ − 1 = λ² + λ = λ(λ+1)

The characteristic equation directly gives λ³ − 1 = λ² + λ = λ(λ+1), so:

ξ = λ(1−λ)/[λ(λ+1)] = (1−τ)/(τ+1)

Again the λ cancels. The limit depends only on the Tribonacci constant τ — the unique real root of x³ − x² − x − 1 = 0 — and the formula is algebraically clean.

The k-nacci dominant root approaches 2

As k → ∞, the characteristic polynomial xk = xk−1 + xk−2 + ⋯ + 1 has a dominant root that approaches 2 (the sum of a geometric series 1 + 1/2 + 1/4 + ⋯ = 2 acts as the "attractor"). Substituting λ = 2:

ξ = 2(1−2)/(8−1) = −2/7

The cross-ratio limits converge monotonically from φ*/2 ≈ −0.309 toward −2/7 ≈ −0.286 as the recurrence depth increases. The cross-ratio "feels" how many terms participate in the recurrence.

V

The Limit Landscape

The master formula ξ = λ(1−λ)/(λ³−1) defines a curve in the (λ, ξ) plane. Each k-nacci recurrence picks out a specific point on this curve — determined by its dominant root λk. The curve is a hyperbola with a vertical asymptote at λ = 1 (where the denominator vanishes) and the horizontal asymptote ξ → 0 as λ → ∞.

The physically relevant portion is the interval λ ∈ (1, 2], where all k-nacci dominant roots live. Drag the λ indicator to see how the cross-ratio limit varies continuously with the dominant root.

VI

What This Tells Us

The cross-ratio is a projective invariant — it sees through all the stretching and compression that projection introduces. Applied to recurrence sequences, it strips away the exponential growth (which is an artefact of scale) and the multiplicative constant (which depends on initial conditions), leaving only the algebraic content of the characteristic equation.

The formula ξ = λ(1−λ)/(λ³−1) says: the cross-ratio limit depends on exactly one number — the dominant root — and nothing else. Not the initial conditions. Not the normalisation. Not which four consecutive terms you pick (provided n is large enough). Just the root of the characteristic polynomial.

The cross-ratio of a linear recurrence extracts its algebraic soul: the dominant root of the characteristic polynomial, disguised as a projective invariant.

Connection to Digital Fibonacci

In the Zeckendorf–Ulam explorer, the mortality parameter g modifies the Fibonacci recurrence: finite g truncates the lineage, effectively changing the characteristic equation. The dominant root shifts, and with it the cross-ratio limit. The visual texture of the Ulam spiral changes because the underlying algebraic structure — encoded in λ — changes. The cross-ratio is the invariant that quantifies this shift.
