The k-nacci Cross-Ratio

The cross-ratio of a linear recurrence strips away the dominant growth and exposes the conjugate roots — the algebraic structure that doesn't dominate but does determine the projective invariant.

I

The Cross-Ratio

The cross-ratio is the sole numerical invariant of projective geometry. Given four collinear points A, B, C, D with coordinates a, b, c, d:

ξ(A, B, C, D) = (a − b)(c − d) (b − c)(d − a)
The only quantity preserved by every projective transformation

Distances change under projection. Ratios of distances change. But this particular combination of ratios does not. Drag the observer point below to see how projection distorts the spacing of four points while preserving their cross-ratio exactly.

In Klein's Erlangen programme, a geometry is defined by its group of allowed transformations and the invariants those transformations preserve. Projective geometry — the geometry of PGL(n+1) — sits at the top of the hierarchy: maximum symmetry, minimum structure. The cross-ratio is its only invariant, the last quantity standing before measurement begins.

II

The Fibonacci Cross-Ratio

The cross-ratio of four consecutive Fibonacci numbers Fn−3, Fn−2, Fn−1, Fn converges as n → ∞. Asymptotically Fn ≈ Cφn, where φ = (1+√5)/2 is the golden ratio. Substituting into the cross-ratio formula, the constant C cancels (it appears equally in every factor), and the remaining powers of φ simplify:

Derivation

Set a = φn−3, b = φn−2, c = φn−1, d = φn. Then:

(a−b)(c−d) / [(b−c)(d−a)] = (1−φ)(φ²−φ³) / [(φ−φ²)(φ³−1)]

Factoring: the numerator gives φ²(1−φ)², the denominator gives φ(1−φ)(φ³−1). One factor of (1−φ) and one power of φ cancel, leaving φ(1−φ)/(φ³−1).

Now the characteristic equation φ² = φ + 1 implies φ³ = 2φ + 1, so φ³ − 1 = 2φ. The formula collapses:

ξ = φ(1−φ)/(2φ) = (1−φ)/2

Since Vieta's formula for x² − x − 1 = 0 gives φ + φ* = 1, so 1 − φ = φ* = (1−√5)/2, the conjugate golden ratio. The Fibonacci cross-ratio limit is:

ξFib = φ*2  =  −12φ  ≈ −0.30902
Half the conjugate golden ratio — the projective invariant of the Fibonacci recurrence

The second form, −1/(2φ), uses φ · φ* = −1 (the product-of-roots relation). Both expressions make the same point: the cross-ratio is built from the subordinate root — the one that doesn't dominate the growth.

III

The General Formula

The Fibonacci derivation depends on φ only through the asymptotic form Tn ≈ Cλn. Any linear recurrence whose dominant characteristic root is λ produces the same structure. Setting a = Cλn−3, b = Cλn−2, c = Cλn−1, d = Cλn:

The cancellation

The constant C cancels immediately — it appears equally in every factor of (a−b), (c−d), (b−c), (d−a). After extracting common powers of λ, the cross-ratio becomes:

(1 − λ)(λ² − λ³) / [(λ − λ²)(λ³ − 1)] = λ²(1−λ)² / [λ(1−λ)(λ³−1)]

One factor of (1−λ) cancels between numerator and denominator. One power of λ cancels. What remains is:

λ(1 − λ) / (λ³ − 1)

The denominator factors as λ³ − 1 = (λ − 1)(λ² + λ + 1). Since 1 − λ = −(λ − 1), the (λ − 1) factors cancel, leaving the master formula:

ξ∞ = −λλ² + λ + 1
Cross-ratio limit for any recurrence with dominant root λ

The denominator λ² + λ + 1 is the cyclotomic-adjacent factor from the difference-of-cubes factorisation. For each specific k-nacci family, the characteristic equation expresses this quantity in terms of powers of λ, producing a simplified closed form. The characteristic equation is the simplification engine.

IV

Tribonacci: −1/τ²

The Tribonacci recurrence Tn = Tn−1 + Tn−2 + Tn−3 has the characteristic equation τ³ = τ² + τ + 1, where τ ≈ 1.83929 is the Tribonacci constant. This hands over the denominator directly:

τ² + τ + 1 = τ³

Substituting into the master formula:

ξTrib = −ττ³  =  −1τ²  ≈ −0.29560
The Tribonacci cross-ratio limit — the negative inverse square of the Tribonacci constant

The parallel with Fibonacci is exact in structure: the characteristic equation simplifies the denominator, and the result is a clean algebraic function of the dominant root. For Fibonacci, ξ = −1/(2φ); for Tribonacci, ξ = −1/τ².

Subordinate roots and what the cross-ratio measures

The Tribonacci polynomial x³ − x² − x − 1 has one real root τ and two complex conjugate roots α, ᾱ. Vieta's formulas give τ · α · ᾱ = 1, so |α|² = 1/τ. It follows that −1/τ² = −|α|⁴: the cross-ratio limit is the negative fourth power of the modulus of the complex conjugate roots.

For Fibonacci, the analogy is cleaner because both roots are real: φ · φ* = −1, so −1/φ = φ*, and ξ = φ*/2.

A second Vieta relation illuminates the un-simplified form (1−τ)/(τ+1). Since τ + α + ᾱ = 1 (sum of roots), the numerator 1 − τ equals α + ᾱ = 2Re(α) — the sum of the complex conjugate roots. The cross-ratio computes a ratio of conjugate-root quantities. It strips away the dominant growth and leaves behind the algebraic structure of the roots that don't dominate.

The oscillation pattern differs by recurrence. Fibonacci alternates cleanly above and below its limit — the subdominant root φ* is real and negative, so the correction term (φ*)n alternates in sign. Tribonacci's approach is more dampened: its subdominant roots are complex conjugates, and the transient oscillation reflects their argument in the complex plane.

VII

The Limit Landscape

The master formula ξ = −λ/(λ²+λ+1) defines a continuous curve in the (λ, ξ) plane. Each k-nacci recurrence picks out a specific point on this curve, determined by its dominant root. The relevant domain is λ ∈ (1, 2], where all k-nacci dominant roots reside.

VIII

−2/7 as an Accumulation Point

The sequence of k-nacci cross-ratio limits ξ2, ξ3, ξ4, … converges to −2/7 as k → ∞. The approach is geometric: each successive difference δk = ξk − ξk+1 shrinks by a factor that converges to 2.

The scaling ratio 2 arises because the dominant root deviation λk − 2 scales as ~1/2k (a consequence of the geometric series underlying the k-nacci characteristic equation). Since ξ is a smooth function of λ, the cross-ratio deviations inherit this rate.

This structure invites comparison with Feigenbaum's universal constant in period-doubling bifurcations.

Period-doubling (Feigenbaum)

A one-parameter family of maps x → fr(x) undergoes bifurcations at parameter values r₁, r₂, r₃, …

The differences shrink geometrically with ratio δF ≈ 4.669, a universal constant arising from a fixed point of the renormalisation operator on function space.

k-nacci cross-ratios

A one-parameter family of recurrences indexed by k has projective invariants ξ2, ξ3, ξ4, …

The differences shrink geometrically with ratio 2, determined by the binary convergence rate of the dominant root sequence λk → 2.

The structural parallel

Both settings exhibit a one-parameter family, an invariant computed at each parameter value, geometric convergence to a universal accumulation point, and a characteristic scaling ratio. The difference is in the origin of the universality: Feigenbaum's constant emerges from renormalisation-group fixed-point theory, while the scaling ratio 2 follows from elementary analysis of λk − 2. The universality is algebraic rather than dynamical.

Nonetheless, −2/7 plays a structural role analogous to the Feigenbaum accumulation point: it is the value beyond which no finite-depth recurrence can reach. The cross-ratio functions as a bifurcation measure in the sense that it assigns a single projective invariant to each member of the family, and these invariants converge geometrically to a universal limit with a characteristic scaling constant.
