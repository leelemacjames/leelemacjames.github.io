Two paths from $\mathbb{C}$

Why the Eisenstein integers lift to the $24$-unit Hurwitz order while the Gaussian integers do not lift to anything analogous: the arithmetic identity $4 \cdot (\tfrac{1}{2})^2 = 1$ that holds only in $4$ dimensions, and what it forces about discrete subrings of quaternion algebras.

L. McCulloch-James · Working Note · v1

§ 1

The question this learner answers

Parts I and II established the Hurwitz order $\mathbb{H}_{\mathrm{H}}$ by adjoining $\rho = \tfrac{1}{2}(1+i+j+k)$ to the Lipschitz order $L$, producing $24$ unit-norm elements out of $L$'s $8$. Part II also flagged that the construction depends on the arithmetic identity $4 \cdot (\tfrac{1}{2})^2 = 1$ that holds exactly in $4$ dimensions. The natural follow-up question: why doesn't a similar half-integer refinement work for the Gaussian integers in $2$ dimensions?

This learner answers the question directly. We start from the two distinguished commutative arithmetic settings — the Gaussian integers $\mathbb{Z}[i]$ and the Eisenstein integers $\mathbb{Z}[\omega]$ — and ask what the natural non-commutative analogue of each looks like. The Eisenstein path leads to $\mathbb{H}_{\mathrm{H}}$ with $24$ units. The Gaussian path runs into an arithmetic obstruction: there is no analogous maximal-order extension because $2 \cdot (\tfrac{1}{2})^2 = \tfrac{1}{2}$, not $1$. The Gaussian arithmetic does not lift.

This is not merely a technical curiosity. It is the reason the Hurwitz unit group has order $24$ and not, say, $16$ or $48$. The arithmetic is forced by the geometry of $\mathbb{R}^4$ — specifically by the fact that the half-integer corner $\tfrac{1}{2}(\pm 1, \pm 1, \pm 1, \pm 1)$ sits exactly on the unit sphere in 4 dimensions and nowhere else.

The question to keep in view: which arithmetic structures of $\mathbb{C}$ have non-commutative analogues, and what determines whether such a lifting is possible? The answer turns out to be a very simple counting fact about half-integers and dimensions.

§ 2

Two natural starting points

The complex numbers $\mathbb{C}$ contain exactly two imaginary quadratic rings of class number one with non-trivial unit groups (Part I §2):

RingGeneratorUnit groupOrder
$\mathbb{Z}[i]$ $i$, with $i^2 = -1$ $\{\pm 1, \pm i\}$ $4$
$\mathbb{Z}[\omega]$ $\omega = e^{2\pi i/3}$, with $\omega^2 + \omega + 1 = 0$ $\{\pm 1, \pm \omega, \pm \omega^2\}$ $6$

Both are maximal orders in their respective number fields $\mathbb{Q}(i)$ and $\mathbb{Q}(\omega)$. The Gaussian path has the square lattice with $4$ units; the Eisenstein path has the triangular lattice with $6$ units. The structural difference: the Eisenstein unit group contains a $\mathbb{Z}_3$ subgroup (the cube roots of unity) beyond the universal $\mathbb{Z}_2$ of charge conjugation; the Gaussian unit group does not.

Now we ask: what is the natural quaternionic analogue of each? Both paths run through the rational quaternion algebra $\mathbb{Q}\otimes\mathbb{H}$, but they reach different destinations.

§ 3

The Gaussian path: Lipschitz

The Gaussian integers $\mathbb{Z}[i]$ have "two perpendicular axes" in $\mathbb{C}$: the real and imaginary directions, generated by $1$ and $i$. The obvious quaternionic version is to add the perpendicular axes $j$ and $k$ as well, producing

$$ L \;=\; \mathbb{Z}\langle 1, i, j, k\rangle \;=\; \{a + bi + cj + dk : a, b, c, d \in \mathbb{Z}\}. $$

This is the Lipschitz order. Its unit group is

$$ L^\times \;=\; \{\pm 1, \pm i, \pm j, \pm k\} \;\cong\; Q_8, \qquad |Q_8| = 8. $$

The Lipschitz order is the natural quaternionic analogue of the Gaussian integers in two precise senses. First, structurally: it adds two more "$\mathbb{Z}[i]$-like" axes to the original Gaussian basis. Second, group-theoretically: $Q_8$ is the smallest non-abelian quaternionic group, and it contains $\mathbb{Z}_4 = \{\pm 1, \pm i\} = U(\mathbb{Z}[i])$ as a subgroup (twice over: also $\{\pm 1, \pm j\}$ and $\{\pm 1, \pm k\}$). So $L$ has three commuting copies of $\mathbb{Z}[i]$ glued together non-commutatively, and the original Gaussian unit group sits as a distinguished maximal cyclic subgroup of $L^\times$.

Now try to enlarge

Part II's construction adjoined a half-integer point $\rho$ to $L$ to get a larger order with $24$ units. Can we do an analogous half-integer adjoining for the pure Gaussian case in $\mathbb{C}$ — adjoining $\sigma = \tfrac{1}{2}(1 + i)$ to $\mathbb{Z}[i]$ — to obtain a richer arithmetic? Let's check.

Attempt · $\mathbb{Z}[i] + \mathbb{Z}\sigma$

Adjoin $\sigma = \tfrac{1}{2}(1+i)$ to $\mathbb{Z}[i]$. Check the norm of $\sigma$:

$N(\sigma) = |\sigma|^2 = \tfrac{1}{4}(1^2 + 1^2) = \tfrac{1}{2}$

Not an integer. So $\sigma$ is not an algebraic integer, and adjoining it produces a ring containing $\tfrac{1}{2}$:

$\sigma^2 = \tfrac{1}{4}(1+i)^2 = \tfrac{1}{4}(2i) = \tfrac{i}{2}$
$\Rightarrow \tfrac{i}{2} \in \mathbb{Z}[i,\sigma]$, hence $\tfrac{1}{2}$ too.
Verdict: fails to be an order.
Comparison · $L + \mathbb{Z}\rho$ in $\mathbb{H}$

Adjoin $\rho = \tfrac{1}{2}(1+i+j+k)$ to $L$. Check the norm:

$N(\rho) = \tfrac{1}{4}(1^2 + 1^2 + 1^2 + 1^2) = 1$

Exactly $1$. So $\rho$ is unit-norm, integral over $\mathbb{Z}$ (minimal polynomial $x^2 - x + 1$), and the resulting ring $\mathbb{H}_{\mathrm{H}} = L + \mathbb{Z}\rho$ is a maximal order with $24$ units.

Verdict: succeeds. The Hurwitz order.

The Gaussian path stops at $\mathbb{Z}[i]$, with its quaternionic extension stopping at $L$. The half-integer trick that worked in $4$ dimensions to produce $\mathbb{H}_{\mathrm{H}}$ does not work in $2$ dimensions: it produces a ring without integrality, which is no longer an order. The Lipschitz order is the end of the Gaussian path, not a stepping stone.

§ 4

The Eisenstein path: Hurwitz

The Eisenstein integers $\mathbb{Z}[\omega]$ have an extra structural feature compared with $\mathbb{Z}[i]$: the half-integer point $\omega = \tfrac{1}{2}(-1 + i\sqrt{3})$. The Eisenstein lattice is triangular, not square, because the basis vectors $1$ and $\omega$ are at $120°$ rather than $90°$. The two extra unit-norm points $\{\pm\omega, \pm\omega^2\}$ that the Eisenstein integers have over the Gaussians come from this half-integer structure.

The Eisenstein arithmetic lifts to the quaternions via the cyclic permutation of $i, j, k$. The element $\rho = \tfrac{1}{2}(1+i+j+k)$ generates a $\mathbb{Z}_6$ subgroup of $(\mathbb{Q}\otimes\mathbb{H})^\times$, with $\rho^2$ generating $\mathbb{Z}_3$, and the Eisenstein subring sits inside $\mathbb{H}_{\mathrm{H}}$ via $\omega \mapsto \rho^2$. So the Eisenstein $\mathbb{Z}_3$ is faithfully preserved in the lift; it is what we earlier called the "internalisation" of the planar $\mathbb{Z}_3$ as the spatial cyclic permutation $i \to j \to k$.

The unit count works as follows. We start from the Lipschitz order's $Q_8$ (8 units) and act on it by the new $\mathbb{Z}_3$ (a 3-cycle), which "triples" the orbit structure:

$$ |\twoT| \;=\; |Q_8 \rtimes \mathbb{Z}_3| \;=\; |Q_8| \cdot |\mathbb{Z}_3| \;=\; 8 \cdot 3 \;=\; 24. $$

This is the semidirect product structure noted in Part II §7. The Hurwitz unit group's order $24$ is the product of $8$ (the size of the Lipschitz core) and $3$ (the size of the Eisenstein cyclic permutation), which is why we sometimes write $24 = 3 \times 8$. The factorisation is not a cyclotomic field extension count; it is a semidirect product of two genuinely different group-theoretic ingredients.

A misleading reading worth avoiding

One might be tempted to read $24 = 3 \times 8$ as a statement about cyclotomic field extensions: $24$ is the order of the unit group of $\mathbb{Z}[e^{2\pi i/24}]$, or $24$ as the dimension of some representation, etc. None of these readings is right. The $24$ is the order of the unit group $\twoT$ of the Hurwitz order, and the factorisation $24 = 8 \times 3$ specifically reflects $\twoT \cong Q_8 \rtimes \mathbb{Z}_3$. The $\mathbb{Z}_3$ comes from $\langle\rho^2\rangle$, the embedded Eisenstein $\mathbb{Z}_3$ acting on the three quaternionic directions.

§ 5

The arithmetic identity that does it all

We can now state the core arithmetic fact that explains why the Eisenstein path produces a $24$-unit Hurwitz order but the Gaussian path does not produce anything comparable.

The dimensional count
For $n$-dimensional vectors with half-integer coordinates $\pm\tfrac{1}{2}$ in each slot, what is the squared norm?
$n$ Algebra $\sum (\tfrac{1}{2})^2$ Half-integer count on unit sphere Refinement possible?
$1$ $\mathbb{R}$ $\tfrac{1}{4}$ $0$ No
$2$ $\mathbb{C}$ $\tfrac{1}{2}$ $0$ No
$3$ $\mathrm{Im}(\mathbb{H})$ $\tfrac{3}{4}$ $0$ No
$4$ $\mathbb{H}$ $1$ $2^4 = 16$ Yes (Hurwitz)
$5$ — $\tfrac{5}{4}$ $0$ No
$6$ — $\tfrac{3}{2}$ $0$ No
$8$ $\mathbb{O}$ $2$ $0$ (no unit; norm $\sqrt{2}$) No (need different vectors)

The table reveals the structural point: half-integer corner points have squared norm exactly $1$ in $4$ dimensions, and in no other dimension. In every other dimension the squared norm is either too small (less than $1$, so the point sits inside the unit sphere and cannot be a unit) or too large (greater than $1$, so the point sits outside the unit sphere). Only at $n = 4$ does the all-corner half-integer sit exactly on the unit sphere.

A side remark on the octonions

The octonions $\mathbb{O}$ have a different but related story: their "Hurwitz integers" (the Cayley integers, or $E_8$ root lattice) are obtained not by half-integer corners (which give squared norm $2$) but by allowing both unit-norm integer points and additional half-integer points with even sign-counts. The result is the $240$-vertex configuration of the $E_8$ root system. So while the Hurwitz construction is special to $4$ dimensions, similar arithmetic-lattice refinements exist for $\mathbb{O}$, just with different specifics. The general phenomenon is "exceptional arithmetic structures in the division algebras"; the quaternion case is the simplest non-trivial one.

§ 6

Why $4$ dimensions is special

The arithmetic identity $4 \cdot (\tfrac{1}{2})^2 = 1$ is a tiny piece of arithmetic, but it has dramatic structural consequences. We pause to record what makes $4$ dimensions special:

Feature of $n = 4$Consequence
$4 \cdot (\tfrac{1}{2})^2 = 1$ Half-integer corners sit on the unit sphere
$24 = 8 + 16$ Total norm-$1$ lattice points (integer + half-integer)
$24$-cell exists in $\mathbb{R}^4$ Self-dual regular polytope unique to 4 dimensions
$F_4$ Lie group has rank $4$ Hurwitz lattice = short roots of $F_4$
$\HH$ is a $4$-D division algebra Quaternionic multiplication exists exactly here
$\mathrm{SO}(4) \cong (\mathrm{SU}(2)\times\mathrm{SU}(2))/\mathbb{Z}_2$ Chirality structure unique to 4 dimensions

None of these is logically independent of the others. They are different faces of the same arithmetic-geometric fact: $\mathbb{R}^4$ is the dimension where the half-integer lattice and the integer lattice coexist coherently on the unit sphere. The structural consequence is the $24$-cell, with vertices that form both a maximal order in the quaternion algebra and a root system of an exceptional Lie group.

§ 7

Hexagon and $24$-cell side by side

The geometric companion to the arithmetic table of §5: the unit-norm points in $\mathbb{Z}[\omega]$ form a regular hexagon in $\mathbb{R}^2$, while the unit-norm points in $\mathbb{H}_{\mathrm{H}}$ form a regular 24-cell in $\mathbb{R}^4$. These are the configurations that the discrete arithmetic produces.

Side by side · hexagon vs 24-cell
Left: the $6$ unit-norm points of $\mathbb{Z}[\omega]$ as vertices of a regular hexagon. Right: the $24$ unit-norm points of $\mathbb{H}_{\mathrm{H}}$, projected onto the $(\mathrm{Re}, \mathrm{Im}_i)$ plane. The $24$-cell appears as interleaved configurations of integer-coordinate units and half-integer-coordinate units.
Both configurations are the vertex sets of regular polytopes — the only regular polytopes whose vertex configuration coincides with the unit group of a maximal arithmetic order. In 2D, the hexagon's six vertices are the six Eisenstein units. In 4D, the $24$-cell's twenty-four vertices are the $24$ Hurwitz units. There is no analogous polytope/unit-group coincidence for the Gaussian integers; the Gaussian "polytope" is just the unit square, which is not a regular polytope in any structural sense beyond two dimensions.

The structural punchline of this comparison is that $\mathbb{Z}[\omega]$ and $\mathbb{H}_{\mathrm{H}}$ are analogous constructions in different dimensions: each is the maximal order in its respective algebra; each has a unit group sitting on a regular polytope; each has a half-integer point as the essential ingredient ($\omega = \tfrac{1}{2}(-1+i\sqrt{3})$ and $\rho = \tfrac{1}{2}(1+i+j+k)$ respectively). The Gaussian integers $\mathbb{Z}[i]$ are not analogous to either; they correspond to the Lipschitz order $L$, which is not maximal.

§ 8

What this three-part learner has done

We started in Part I with the question "what is an order?", and built the Lipschitz order $L$ as the natural-looking quaternionic analogue of $\mathbb{Z}[i]$. We discovered that $L$ is not maximal, with a half-integer point $\rho$ sitting outside it. In Part II we adjoined $\rho$, verified the algebra ($\rho^3 = -1$, the minimal polynomial $x^2 - x + 1 = 0$), described the resulting Hurwitz order $\mathbb{H}_{\mathrm{H}}$ as the union of integer-coordinate and half-integer-coordinate quaternions, and counted its $24 = 8 + 16$ unit-norm elements.

In Part III we placed this construction in the comparison with the Gaussian path. The Lipschitz order $L$ is the natural quaternionic analogue of $\mathbb{Z}[i]$. The Hurwitz order $\mathbb{H}_{\mathrm{H}}$ is the natural quaternionic analogue of $\mathbb{Z}[\omega]$. The two paths from $\mathbb{C}$ reach different non-commutative arithmetics because the arithmetic identity $n \cdot (\tfrac{1}{2})^2 = 1$ holds only at $n = 4$. The Gaussian path is structurally truncated at $L$, because there is no half-integer refinement available — and this is not a defect of the Gaussian path, but the structural reason the Hurwitz construction is uniquely available in $4$ dimensions.

We have, throughout, kept everything discrete and lattice-based: the unit groups are finite, the lattices are finitely generated as $\mathbb{Z}$-modules, the half-integer refinements are explicit point sets in $\mathbb{R}^4$. None of the constructions appeal to continuous group theory, representation theory, or Lie algebra structure (those come later, in the main learner). What we have built is a precise discrete arithmetic substrate, on top of which the larger story can be told.
