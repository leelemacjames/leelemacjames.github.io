Radioactive Decay, Half-Life & Fission

The mathematics of things falling apart — exponential decay, decay constants, and how splitting heavy nuclei powers reactors.

E42 E43 E44 E45a-b E46 E47 E48–49

What this section is about

Radioactive decay is random at the level of individual nuclei but precisely predictable for large samples. You cannot say when a given nucleus will decay — only the probability that it decays per unit time. Despite this fundamental randomness, the macroscopic behaviour is described by an exact exponential law.

This section covers slides E42–E49: the exponential decay law, half-life and decay constant, activity, practical decay problems, the radon isotope data, uranium-235 fission, and a full worked IB exam-style question on energy release from fission products.

AHL students need the formal derivation of the half-life relation and the differential treatment of activity. SL students need the half-life table, the (½)ⁿ rule, and the fission material. The orange dots in the nav mark AHL content.

The Core Idea: Exponential Decay

Radioactive decay obeys a single governing equation:

N(t) = N₀ · e−λt

Where N₀ is the initial number of undecayed nuclei, λ is the decay constant (probability of decay per unit time, in s⁻¹), and t is elapsed time. The half-life connects to λ by:

T½ = ln 2 / λ ≈ 0.693 / λ

Everything else — activity, the (½)ⁿ table, fission energy calculations — is a consequence of these two relations.

AHL E42 — Deriving the Half-Life Relation

Start with the exponential decay law and ask: at what time has half the sample decayed?

N₀/2 = N₀ · e−λT½

Cancel N₀ from both sides, take the natural log:

ln(½) = −λT½
−ln 2 = −λT½
T½ = ln 2 / λ

The decay constant λ is the probability that any one nucleus decays per unit time. Large λ → fast decay → short half-life. Small λ → slow decay → long half-life. The relationship is inversely proportional.

Exponential decay curve showing N vs time, with half-life intervals marked. The curve never reaches zero — it approaches it asymptotically.
Q1 A sample has a decay constant λ = 2.5 × 10⁻³ s⁻¹. What is its half-life? ▶
Answer

T½ = ln 2 / λ = 0.693 / (2.5 × 10⁻³) = 277 s ≈ 4.6 minutes.

Q2 Why is the exponential decay law a consequence of the randomness of individual decays? ▶
Answer

If each nucleus has a fixed probability λ of decaying per unit time, independent of all other nuclei and of how long it has existed, then the rate of decay is proportional to how many undecayed nuclei remain: dN/dt = −λN. This first-order ODE has the unique solution N = N₀e−λt. The exponential form is not assumed — it's a mathematical consequence of constant individual probability.

AHL E43 — Activity & the Decay Constant

Activity (A) is the rate of decay — the number of disintegrations per second:

A = −dN/dt = λN

Units: becquerel (Bq) — 1 Bq = 1 decay per second. Since N decreases exponentially, activity does too:

A(t) = λN₀e−λt = A₀e−λt

The derivation is straightforward: differentiate N = N₀e−λt with respect to t, giving dN/dt = −λN₀e−λt. The negative sign means N is decreasing; activity is defined as the magnitude of this rate.

Key insight: activity is proportional to the number of undecayed nuclei. As the sample decays, activity falls — fewer nuclei remain to contribute decays. A Geiger counter measures activity, not N directly.

Q1 A sample initially has 4.0 × 10¹⁰ atoms with λ = 1.2 × 10⁻⁴ s⁻¹. What is the initial activity? ▶
Answer

A₀ = λN₀ = 1.2 × 10⁻⁴ × 4.0 × 10¹⁰ = 4.8 × 10⁶ Bq = 4.8 MBq.

Q2 After how many half-lives does the activity fall to ~1% of its original value? ▶
Answer

After n half-lives, activity = A₀/2ⁿ. We need 1/2ⁿ ≈ 0.01, so 2ⁿ ≈ 100. Since 2⁶ = 64 and 2⁷ = 128, the answer is approximately 7 half-lives (activity drops to 0.78%). In practice, "10 half-lives" (0.1%) is often used as the threshold for a source being considered negligible.

For SL, the exponential form isn't needed — you can use the (½)ⁿ rule for any integer number of half-lives:

This works for mass, number of atoms, or activity — anything that decays exponentially halves each period.

Determinism and randomness. Each individual decay is genuinely random — quantum mechanics provides no mechanism to predict which nucleus will decay next, or when. Yet the macroscopic behaviour is perfectly deterministic: the exponential law holds with extraordinary precision for large samples. This is one of the deepest features of statistical physics: predictability emerges from unpredictability. Is this an epistemic limitation (we just don't know enough) or an ontological feature of nature (there is nothing more to know)?

Q1 A 200 g sample has a half-life of 3 hours. How much remains after 12 hours? ▶
Answer

n = 12/3 = 4 half-lives. Remaining = 200 × (½)⁴ = 200/16 = 12.5 g.

Q2 Does the half-life change as the sample gets smaller? ▶
Answer

No. Half-life is a property of the isotope, not the sample size. Whether you have 10²⁰ atoms or 10⁴, T½ is the same. What changes is the activity (fewer atoms → fewer decays per second), but the time for half to decay remains constant. This is a direct consequence of each nucleus decaying independently.

SL E45a–b — Worked Problem: Nuclide X → Stable Y

Setup: Nuclide X decays to stable Y. Half-life = 7 days. Initial sample = 240 g of pure X.

Part (a) — Mass of X after 14 days

n = 14/7 = 2 half-lives.

N = (½)² × 240 = ¼ × 240 = 60 g of X remaining
Part (b) — Mass of X after 35 days

n = 35/7 = 5 half-lives.

N = (½)⁵ × 240 = 1/32 × 240 = 7.5 g of X remaining
Part (c) — Time for sample to contain 225 g of Y

If 225 g is now Y, then the mass of X remaining = 240 − 225 = 15 g.

15 = 240 × (½)ⁿ → (½)ⁿ = 15/240 = 1/16 → n = 4 Time = 4 × 7 = 28 days
Q1 What is the mass of X remaining after 10 days? (This is not an integer number of half-lives.) ▶
Answer

n = 10/7 ≈ 1.429 half-lives. Using the exponential form:

N = 240 × (½)10/7 = 240 × 2−1.429 = 240 × 0.3715 = 89.2 g.

Alternatively: λ = ln 2 / 7 = 0.0990 day⁻¹, then N = 240 × e−0.0990 × 10 = 240 × e−0.990 = 240 × 0.372 = 89.2 g. Same answer — the (½)ⁿ form generalises naturally to non-integer n.

Q2 In part (c), why can we assume the mass of Y produced equals the mass of X lost? ▶
Answer

This is an approximation. Strictly, the mass of daughter Y produced is slightly less than the mass of X consumed, because some mass is converted to energy (the decay energy) via E = mc². But the mass defect per decay is tiny compared to the nucleon mass — parts per thousand — so at this level of calculation, mass is effectively conserved. The IB expects you to treat mass as conserved in these problems.

This table is a goldmine. Half-lives span 12 orders of magnitude — from 2.7 × 10⁻⁷ s (²¹⁴Rn, faster than a camera flash) to 3.3 × 10⁵ s (²²²Rn, ~3.8 days). ²⁰⁸Rn is the only stable isotope, with 122 neutrons (a magic number: 126 is nearby, and 82 protons is magic).

²²²Rn is the radon that accumulates in basements — it's a health hazard precisely because its half-life is long enough to build up in enclosed spaces but short enough to deliver a significant radiation dose.

The log₁₀ T½ column is useful for Geiger-Nuttall style analysis: plotting log T½ against decay energy reveals a near-linear relationship for alpha emitters in the same decay chain.

Q1 ²²²Rn has T½ = 3.8 days. What fraction remains after 19 days? ▶
Answer

n = 19/3.8 = 5 half-lives. Fraction remaining = (½)⁵ = 1/32 ≈ 3.1%.

Q2 Why is ²⁰⁸Rn the only stable radon isotope? ▶
Answer

²⁰⁸Rn has Z = 86 and N = 122. Its daughter (²⁰⁴Pb) has Z = 82 and N = 122. The key factor is proximity to the doubly magic ²⁰⁸Pb (Z = 82, N = 126). ²⁰⁸Rn itself sits in a region of the nuclear chart where the balance of strong force and Coulomb repulsion permits stability — though "stable" here may mean an unmeasurably long half-life rather than truly infinite stability.

SL E47 — Uranium-235 Fission

U-235 undergoes neutron-induced fission: it absorbs a slow (thermal) neutron, forming an unstable U-236* compound nucleus, which then splits.

The Fission Process

Step 1: Absorption
²³⁵U + ¹n → ²³⁶U*
Neutron captured; compound nucleus is excited
Step 2: Fission
²³⁶U* → ¹⁴⁴Ba + ⁹⁰Kr + 2n + ΔE
Splits into two fragments + neutrons + energy
Chain reaction
Released neutrons → trigger more fissions
Each fission releases 2–3 neutrons

The fission products are not unique — many splits are possible:

ReactionProductsFree neutrons
Path A¹⁴⁴Ba + ⁹⁰Kr2
Path B¹⁴⁴Ba + ⁸⁹Kr3
Path C¹³²Xe + ⁹²Sr2
Path D¹³²Sn + ¹⁰¹Mo3

In every case: charge (Z), nucleon number (A), and lepton number are all conserved. The products are neutron-rich (they sit above the valley of stability) and undergo further β⁻ decays to reach stable configurations.

Fission was discovered, not invented — but its applications were designed. Hahn and Strassner discovered fission in 1938; Meitner and Frisch provided the theoretical explanation. Within seven years, the same physics had been weaponised. To what extent are scientists responsible for the applications of their discoveries? The IB TOK syllabus specifically flags this as a knowledge question about the relationship between scientific knowledge and ethical responsibility.

Q1 Why must the incoming neutron be "thermal" (slow) for U-235 fission? ▶
Answer

U-235 has a much larger absorption cross-section for slow neutrons (~585 barns) than for fast neutrons (~1–2 barns). A slow neutron spends more time near the nucleus, increasing the probability of capture. This is why nuclear reactors use moderators (water, graphite, heavy water) to slow fission neutrons down to thermal energies (~0.025 eV).

Q2 Why are the fission products "neutron-rich"? ▶
Answer

Heavy nuclei like U-235 have a high neutron-to-proton ratio (N/Z ≈ 1.55) for stability. When they split into medium-mass fragments, those fragments inherit this high N/Z ratio — but medium-mass nuclei are stable with a lower N/Z (≈ 1.2–1.3). So the fission fragments have excess neutrons. They shed them by β⁻ decay (n → p + e⁻ + ν̄), gradually moving toward the valley of stability.

Q3 What determines whether a chain reaction is controlled or uncontrolled? ▶
Answer

The multiplication factor k: the average number of neutrons from one fission that go on to cause another fission. If k = 1 (critical), the chain reaction is self-sustaining at constant power — this is a reactor. If k > 1 (supercritical), the reaction rate grows exponentially — this is a bomb (or a reactor accident). If k < 1 (subcritical), the reaction dies out. Reactors use control rods (boron, cadmium) to absorb neutrons and keep k ≈ 1.

SL E48–49 — Fission Energy Calculation: Full Decay Chain

This is an IB exam-style question that ties together fission, beta decay, and binding energy.

Setup: U-235 undergoes neutron-induced fission producing Xe-140 and Sr-94. These are unstable and beta-decay to the stable end products Ce-140 and Zr-94.

NuclideABE/A (MeV)
U-2352357.591
Ce-1401408.376
Zr-94948.667
Q: Why are the proton numbers of Ce and Zr different from that of U-235?

Answer: The initial fission produces Xe (Z = 54) and Sr (Z = 38), which sum to 92 (= Z for uranium). But Xe-140 and Sr-94 are neutron-rich and undergo multiple β⁻ decays: each β⁻ converts a neutron to a proton, increasing Z. Xe-140 (Z = 54) → Ce-140 (Z = 58): four β⁻ decays. Sr-94 (Z = 38) → Zr-94 (Z = 40): two β⁻ decays. The proton numbers change because the fragments climb toward the valley of stability.

Q: Calculate the total energy released in the complete process

Use total binding energies of the final stable products vs. the initial U-235 (the neutron has zero binding energy):

ΔE = [140 × 8.376 + 94 × 8.667] − [235 × 7.591] ΔE = [1172.64 + 814.70] − [1783.89] ΔE = 1987.34 − 1783.89 = 203.5 MeV

This ~200 MeV is the total energy released across the entire decay chain — fission itself plus all subsequent beta decays. Of this, most (~170 MeV) is kinetic energy of the fission fragments; the rest appears as neutron KE, prompt gammas, beta particles, and neutrinos.

Q1 Why do we use the final stable products (Ce, Zr) rather than the initial fission fragments (Xe, Sr) in the energy calculation? ▶
Answer

Because the question asks for the total energy released across the whole chain, including the subsequent beta decays. If we used Xe-140 and Sr-94, we'd only get the energy from the fission step itself — missing the energy released in all the β⁻ decays that follow. Using the final stable products captures everything in one calculation. This is possible because binding energy is a state function: the total energy released depends only on the initial and final states, not on the path.

Q2 About 10 MeV of the 203.5 MeV is carried away by neutrinos. Why does this matter for reactor design? ▶
Answer

Neutrinos interact almost not at all with matter — they pass straight through the reactor (and the entire Earth). That ~10 MeV per fission is irrecoverably lost: it cannot be converted to heat, electricity, or any useful form. So the usable energy per fission is closer to ~190 MeV. This isn't a design choice — it's a fundamental feature of the weak interaction.

Q3 How many fissions per second are needed for a 1 GW (thermal) reactor? ▶
Answer

1 GW = 10⁹ J/s. Energy per fission ≈ 190 MeV ≈ 190 × 1.6 × 10⁻¹³ = 3.04 × 10⁻¹¹ J.

Fissions/second = 10⁹ / 3.04 × 10⁻¹¹ ≈ 3.3 × 10¹⁹ fissions per second.

That's about 13 grams of U-235 consumed per day — remarkably little fuel for an enormous power output, which is the entire point of nuclear energy.
