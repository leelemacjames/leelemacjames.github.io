Pressure & depth

Why does pressure increase
as you go deeper?

Before the interactive — think about it yourself first.

Imagine you are 10 metres underwater. There is water above you. Why does that water push on you more than the water at the surface pushes on someone swimming there?
A nudge

Think about the column of water sitting above you. At the surface, there's only air above you. At 10 m, there are 10 metres of water above you in addition to the air. Water has mass. Mass means weight. Weight pressing down over an area means pressure.

The question is: whose weight is being supported by the water at your depth? The answer is: everything above you.

The explanation

At any depth, the water must support the weight of everything above it — all the water in the column plus the atmosphere. Deeper means more water above, more weight to support, more pressure per unit area. This is exactly what P = ρgh captures: density (ρ) times gravity (g) times depth (h). The deeper you go, the taller the column, the greater the weight, the higher the pressure.

The equation compresses this into a formula. The physical story is: every layer of fluid carries the weight of everything above it.

Pressure at depth h in a fluid of density ρ P = ρ · g · h

The graph shows a straight line — pressure increasing linearly with depth. What assumption about the fluid does this linearity require?
A nudge

P = ρgh gives a straight line only if ρ stays the same at every depth. What physical property of the fluid would need to hold for that to be true?

The explanation

The linear relationship requires the fluid to be incompressible — density must not change with depth or pressure. Water is nearly incompressible for everyday depths, so the graph is a good model for swimming pools, tanks, and shallow ocean work. Air is compressible: as you go higher in the atmosphere, air becomes less dense, so the pressure-altitude relationship curves rather than remains linear. That's why weather balloons behave differently from submarines.

Upthrust & buoyancy

The upward push —
where does it come from?

The diagram below shows an object submerged in a fluid. Two forces act on it. One points up, one down. Before you look at the labels, try to explain what each is.

upthrust (buoyant force)
weight
The green arrow (upthrust) points upward. But water pushes in all directions. Why does the net result of all that fluid pressure add up to a force that points upward?
A nudge

Think about the pressure on the bottom face of the object versus the top face. Which face is deeper? Deeper means higher pressure. Now: pressure pushes inward on every face. The side forces mostly cancel — left balances right, front balances back. But top versus bottom don't cancel. Why not?

The explanation

The bottom face of the object is deeper than the top face. Deeper means greater fluid pressure. The fluid pushes upward on the bottom face with more force than it pushes downward on the top face. The difference is the net upward force — the upthrust.

This is why upthrust = weight of fluid displaced. The calculation works out exactly because the pressure difference across the object's height equals the weight of the fluid column that occupied that space before the object arrived.

Buoyant force (Archimedes) F_b = ρ_fluid · g · V_displaced

The fluid "knows" nothing about the object — it just presses according to depth. The upthrust is a consequence of that pressure gradient, not a separate mysterious force.

Object floats when...

  • Average density < fluid density
  • Upthrust ≥ weight before fully submerged
  • It reaches a waterline where forces balance
  • Example: ship hull enclosing large air volume

Object sinks when...

  • Average density > fluid density
  • Even fully submerged, upthrust < weight
  • No stable equilibrium at partial submersion
  • Example: solid steel ball
Check your model
A submarine is at 10 m depth in seawater. It descends to 100 m. What happens to the buoyant force acting on it?
Common muddle

"The water lifts the object." This implies water has intent or prefers things to float. The physics is: the pressure gradient produces a net upward force on anything occupying a volume at depth. It's not about what the water "wants." It's about what pressure differences produce when they act on surfaces.

States of matter & phase change

When heat doesn't warm —
where does the energy go?

What you observe

Heating ice: temperature rises, then stays flat at 0°C during melting, then rises again as liquid water. Flat line despite continuous heating.

What's actually happening

During the flat: energy breaks intermolecular bonds (potential energy increases). Kinetic energy stays constant. Temperature measures kinetic energy — so it stays flat.

If temperature measures average kinetic energy of particles, and temperature stays constant during melting — what must be true about the kinetic energy of the molecules during the phase change?
A nudge

Temperature flat → average kinetic energy flat. Energy is still going in from the heat source. Where must it be going, if not into kinetic energy?

The explanation

Kinetic energy is constant during the phase change — by definition, since temperature (which measures it) is constant. The energy from the heat source is going entirely into increasing potential energy: the work done against intermolecular forces as the regular crystal lattice of ice breaks apart into the disordered arrangement of liquid water. Only once all bonds are broken can further heat go back into kinetic energy — and the temperature starts rising again.

The energy required for this is called latent heat — literally "hidden heat," because it doesn't show up on a thermometer.

Evaporation vs boiling — same outcome, different game

Evaporation

  • Surface process only
  • Happens at any temperature
  • High-energy tail of molecules escape
  • Cools what's left behind
  • Suppressed by high humidity

Boiling

  • Bulk process — bubbles throughout
  • Requires vapour pressure = external pressure
  • Temperature-dependent threshold
  • Threshold changes with altitude/pressure
  • Not suppressed by humidity
You leave a glass of water on a windowsill. Over a week, it disappears — without ever boiling. Explain what happened at the particle level, and why this couldn't be boiling.
A nudge

Boiling requires all molecules in the bulk to have enough energy to form vapour — at the point where vapour pressure equals atmospheric pressure. Evaporation doesn't. Think about the spread of molecular energies in the liquid, and which molecules are in the best position to escape.

The explanation

Even at room temperature, water molecules have a distribution of kinetic energies — most near the average, some much higher (the Maxwell-Boltzmann tail). The molecules at the surface with enough kinetic energy to overcome the intermolecular attraction escape into the air. This happens continuously, even far below boiling point. Over a week, enough molecules have escaped that the glass empties.

This couldn't be boiling because boiling requires vapour pressure throughout the bulk liquid to reach atmospheric pressure — a threshold condition only met at 100°C (at sea level). Room temperature water is nowhere near that. The evaporation route doesn't require that threshold — it just requires that some molecules at the surface have enough individual energy, which they always do.

Extension · Reasoning framework

Because · But · So

This is a three-part sentence structure that forces a complete scientific explanation. It's simple but demanding: you need a mechanism (because), a complication or contrast (but), and a consequence (so). AI can generate fluent because-but-so sentences. What it cannot do is ensure yours are physically correct — that's your job.

The structure

[Observation] — state what happens.
because — give the mechanism (the warrant).
but — introduce a constraint, exception, or contrast.
so — state the consequence that follows from all of the above.

Worked examples from the PDF — annotated

The molecules in a solid only vibrate
because
they are fixed in place by strong intermolecular bonds that hold them in a regular lattice.
but
they begin to move more freely when enough energy is supplied to break those bonds — when the solid melts.
so
the solid maintains its shape at room temperature — only vibrational motion, no flow — but loses it when melted.
The temperature increases when we heat an object
because
energy goes into increasing the average kinetic energy of the molecules — and temperature measures exactly that.
but
it stays constant if a change of state is occurring — because the energy is then going into breaking intermolecular bonds, not into kinetic energy.
so
the internal energy can increase even when the temperature doesn't — internal energy includes both kinetic and potential contributions.
Boiling occurs when a liquid transitions into a gas
because
vapour pressure within the bulk liquid reaches external atmospheric pressure, allowing bubbles to form throughout.
but
it is different from evaporation, which is a surface process that can occur at any temperature — no bulk threshold required.
so
the boiling point changes with external pressure (lower at altitude), while evaporation rate depends on surface area, humidity, and temperature — different variables entirely.

Upthrust acts upward on a submerged object
because
but
so
Model answer

because — fluid pressure increases with depth, so the pressure on the lower face of any submerged object is greater than on the upper face. The net vertical pressure force points upward.

but — the side forces (front/back, left/right) cancel because the object is at the same depth across those pairs. Only the top-bottom pressure difference creates a net force.

so — an object floats if this upward pressure-difference force (equal to the weight of fluid displaced) is at least as large as its own weight — i.e. if its average density is less than the fluid's.

Evaporation cools the liquid left behind
because
but
so
Model answer

because — only the highest-energy molecules at the surface have enough kinetic energy to escape the liquid phase. Their removal lowers the average kinetic energy of those remaining.

but — the cooling only works if the molecules actually evaporate. In saturated air (high humidity), the vapour above the surface is already at capacity — evaporation is suppressed, and no cooling occurs even if the surface is wet.

so — evaporative cooling is most effective in hot, dry conditions with air movement — which is why sweating works better on a dry day than a humid one, and why fans help even without lowering air temperature.

Water boils at 120°C in a pressure cooker, not 100°C
because
but
so
Model answer

because — boiling occurs when a liquid's vapour pressure equals external pressure. In a sealed pressure cooker, steam pressure builds above atmospheric, so the water must be heated to a higher temperature before its vapour pressure matches the increased external pressure.

but — the water molecules themselves are chemically unchanged. The boiling point shift is entirely a pressure-temperature equilibrium effect — nothing intrinsic about the water has changed.

so — food cooks faster in a pressure cooker because the water is genuinely hotter (120°C vs 100°C), and the reverse is also true: at high altitude where atmospheric pressure is lower, water boils below 100°C and food takes longer to cook.

Synthesis

The chain that connects everything

Every concept in this learner is a link in the same physical chain. Follow it from matter to floating:

The conceptual chain matter has mass
→ mass creates gravitational weight
→ weight of fluid per unit area creates pressure
→ pressure increases with depth (P = ρgh)
→ pressure gradient across an object creates a net upward force
→ that force equals the weight of fluid displaced (Archimedes)
→ if that force ≥ object's weight → object floats
→ floating condition: average density of object < density of fluid
Write a single because-but-so sentence that captures the entire floating condition — from pressure gradient to whether a steel ship floats.
A nudge

Start from the pressure gradient (because), introduce the constraint about average density vs fluid density (but), and land on the floating/sinking outcome (so). Try to make the "but" carry the key distinction — the geometry of the ship vs the solid nail.

One possible model

A steel ship floats because the pressure gradient in the water creates an upward force equal to the weight of the water displaced by the hull, but the ship's geometry — a steel shell enclosing a large volume of air — gives it an average density far below that of water, so the upthrust balances the ship's weight before the hull is fully submerged, and a stable waterline is reached.

Notice how the "but" does the real work: it's where form (geometry) meets function (density condition for floating). A solid steel nail has no such enclosed air — its average density exceeds water's — so the condition is never met, and it sinks.
