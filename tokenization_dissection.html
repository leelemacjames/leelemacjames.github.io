How tokenisation atomises language differently than linguistics does

A walk through the seam where statistical compression meets human morphology, and a small graph-theoretic vocabulary for naming the kinds of mismatch you will keep encountering.

AudienceHS / Undergrad PrerequisitesSome graph intuition Reading time≈ 14 minutes

§ 01 / THESISTwo atomisers, one signal

Linguistics and tokenisation both chop language into pieces. They do not chop it into the same pieces, and they do not chop it for the same reason.

A linguist asks: what is the smallest unit of meaning? The answer is the morpheme. un means negation; walk means a thing one does on legs; -ing means the doing is happening now. Each morpheme is a stable atom in a chemistry of meaning. Words are molecules built from them.

A tokeniser asks something completely different: what is the smallest unit of recurrence? Given a giant pile of text, which substrings appear often enough that giving them a single integer ID will save bytes? The answer is the BPE token. ▁the appears in millions of places, so it earns a slot. ▁antidisestablishmentarianism earns a slot only if the corpus contained it often. If not, it gets shredded into whatever common chunks survive.

The two atomisers sometimes agree. Far more often they disagree, and the way they disagree is the subject of this dissection.

§ 02 / VOCABULARYThe kinds of mismatch · a small graph theory

Before we look at examples, fix some vocabulary. Picture two graphs sitting on top of each other. The bottom graph is the linguistic graph: nodes are morphemes, edges connect morphemes that compose into a word. The top graph is the token graph: nodes are tokens, edges connect tokens that occur consecutively in some word. The interesting question is the map between them.

Fig. 1 · Bipartite view: gold = tokens, blue = morphemes. Solid gold edges = clean alignment. Dashed orange = forced or broken alignment.

With that picture in mind, here are the four flavours of mismatch you will meet. The algebraic terms are borrowed loosely, for spirit rather than letter:

I. Isomorphism

M ⇌ T   (1-to-1, reversible)

One morpheme, one token, mutual recovery. Rare and beautiful. walk ↔ walk, cat ↔ cat. The two atomisers happen to agree because the morpheme is also a high-frequency string. Lucky alignment, not principle.

II. Homomorphism (many-to-one)

M₁, M₂, M₃ ↦ T

Several morphemes collapse into a single token. redo stays as one piece even though linguistics sees re- + do. The morphological seam is invisible to the model unless something else flags it. Information about the seam is destroyed.

III. Inverse (one-to-many)

M ↦ T₁, T₂, T₃

A single morpheme is shattered across multiple tokens. believe inside unbelievable becomes pieces of unbel and ievable. Linguistics says one atom; the tokeniser says two-and-a-half fragments that do not even align with the atom's edges.

IV. Context-dependent (non-functional)

M ↦ T(context)

The same morpheme maps to different tokens depending on its neighbours. bio survives whole inside biology but splits as bi + o- inside biomechanical. The map is not even a function of the morpheme alone; it depends on what surrounds it. This is the deepest mismatch.

Hold this fourfold scheme. Every example below is one of these four cases.

§ 03 / DISSECTIONSix cases on the operating table

Case 01 · Type I (isomorphism, sometimes)

Morphemes that survive by accident

The suffix -ing is one of the most common substrings in English. Tokenisers learn it as a unit because compression rewards them for it, not because they understand inflection.

walking → walk ing
jumping → jump ing
running → run ning

And immediately the agreement breaks. The doubled n in running distorts the frequency profile, so the tokeniser ends up storing ning as its unit rather than -ing. A linguist sees the same suffix everywhere; the tokeniser sees two different strings.

Case 02 · Type III (one-to-many destruction)

Morphemes obliterated by compression

Linguistic atomisation

un + believe + able

vs
Tokeniser atomisation

unbel + ievable

Linguistics sees three stable atoms with stable meanings. The tokeniser sees two compression-favourable chunks whose boundaries cut through the morpheme believe. The bond is broken in the wrong place. The model still learns to use these words correctly, but it does so without explicit access to the morphological structure.

Case 03 · Type IV (orthography wins over phonology)

Same sound, different tokens

The phoneme sequence /naɪt/ exists in two English spellings. To the ear they are identical. To the tokeniser they are unrelated:

night → night
knight → kn ight

The silent k is invisible to a speaker but is a hard wedge that drives the tokeniser to a different decomposition. Tokenisation is orthographic; human language is phonological first and orthographic second. This is one of the cleanest places where verbal and written language diverge under the model's lens.

Case 04 · Type IV (the bio- problem)

Same morpheme, different boundary

The morpheme bio- is linguistically stable across thousands of compounds. Computationally it is unstable; its tokenisation depends on what comes after it.

biology → biology
biochemistry → bio chemistry
biomechanical → bi omechanical

The same three letters b-i-o sit at the start of all three words. The tokeniser treats them as part of a whole word in the first case, as a clean prefix in the second, and shatters them across an unrelated boundary in the third. The morpheme has no stable computational identity.

Case 05 · Type IV (whitespace and case)

Context-dependent fragmentation

entropy → entropy
Entropy-based → Ent ropy -based
antientropy → anti entropy

Capitalisation makes Entropy a different string from entropy, so it gets a different (rarer) tokenisation. A hyphen breaks the run. A neighbouring prefix can either preserve or destroy the original token. None of these moves correspond to anything a linguist would call structural.

Case 06 · Type II (allomorphy collapsed)

The plural that does not exist

English has at least three plural allomorphs: /s/ as in cats, /z/ as in dogs, /ɪz/ as in buses. A child acquiring English internalises this distinction before age three. A tokeniser internalises nothing of the sort:

cats → cats
dogs → dogs
buses → buses

Each plural is just a string. The phonological rule that selects which allomorph appears is something the model has to re-learn implicitly from co-occurrence statistics, even though linguistics handed it the rule on a plate. The cost of compression is the cost of throwing away the abstraction.

§ 04 / HIERARCHYTwo pictures of one sentence

Take Chomsky's famous test sentence: Colorless green ideas sleep furiously. Linguistics and tokenisation see it through two completely different lenses.

The linguist draws a tree. Words assemble into phrases, phrases into clauses, clauses into a sentence. The structure is hierarchical and constrained: nouns demand determiners, verbs demand subjects, adjectives attach upward to nouns. Each junction obeys grammatical valence rules about what can bond to what.

Fig. 2 · Chomsky parse · hierarchical, constrained
tokeniser ▸ Color less ▁green ▁ideas ▁sleep ▁fur ious ly .

HORIZONTAL · STATISTICAL · POSITIONAL

The two pictures are different geometries of the same sentence. The Chomsky tree is vertical and rule-driven, a structural formula. The token strip is horizontal and frequency-driven, a fragmentation pattern. The word furiously is a single grammatical unit (an adverb) but three tokens (▁fur, ious, ly); Colorless is one adjective but two tokens. The grammar's atoms and the tokeniser's atoms barely speak to each other.

§ 05 / ANALOGYLanguage as molecular chemistry

If syntax is a structural formula, the analogy to chemistry runs deeper than decoration. It is structural in the literal sense. Both fields treat their objects as labelled graphs, and the same primitives appear on both sides:

Atoms, bonds, valence

A molecule is a graph. Atoms are vertices. Bonds are edges. Valence rules are degree constraints: carbon wants four bonds, hydrogen wants one, oxygen wants two. Violate the constraint and you do not have a molecule, you have an error. A grammar works the same way: parts of speech are vertex types, syntactic dependencies are edges, and the rules of agreement and subcategorisation are degree constraints. A transitive verb wants an object; deny it one and the sentence breaks.

A small chemistry detour makes the point concrete. Consider benzene, drawn below. Six carbon atoms sit at the corners of a hexagon. Each carbon also holds one hydrogen (omitted in the standard drawing). The bonds along the hexagon alternate single-double-single-double, and that alternation is what locks the whole molecule into a flat, rigid ring. Move any atom and the whole shape resists.

Benzene, C6H6. Single and double bonds alternating around a six-carbon ring.

Three things to notice from this small picture, each of which carries straight over to language:

Position fixes identity. Move one of the carbons into a different spot in the ring and the molecule is no longer benzene. The atoms are the same; the graph is different; the substance is different. The same logic surfaces in Case 04 above, where bio- as letters takes three different shapes depending on what surrounds it. Same letters, different positions, different tokenisation behaviour. We will return to that example with a graph in a moment.

Bonds come in different strengths. A single bond is a weaker connection than a double bond. In a sentence, the connection between an article and its noun (the cat) is tight in the way a single bond is tight; the connection between a verb and a distant adverbial modifier is looser, more like a long-range interaction. Linguists call this the difference between local agreement and long-distance dependency. Chemists would recognise it on sight.

Some bonds are not really local at all. The double bonds in benzene are drawn as if they sit between specific pairs of atoms, but experimentally the electrons are spread evenly around the entire ring. The picture lies a little. The truth is that those electrons "belong" to the whole molecule rather than to any one bond. This is the property called resonance or delocalisation, and it is the bridge to the next section.

Hold those three observations and the rest of the analogy unfolds naturally:

The crosswalk

Linguistics Chemistry What both share
syntax tree structural formula Hierarchical labelled graph; placement is identity.
tokenisation fragmentation pattern Linear breakdown into recurring substructures, statistically learned.
morpheme functional group Reusable substructure with characteristic behaviour (–OH, un-).
token embedding molecular environment Local chemical / semantic context that modulates the unit's behaviour.
transformer attention long-range interaction · resonance Distributed coupling that reaches well beyond nearest neighbours.
grammatical valence atomic valence Degree constraints: how many edges a vertex can carry.

Three isomers of bio-

Before drawing the picture, a quick morphological aside on the word isomer itself, since it is doing a lot of work in this analogy and the word repays a closer look. Two related Greek-derived terms appear repeatedly across this essay: isomer and isomorphic. They share a prefix and diverge in a revealing way.

Two compound words, one shared root
isomer
iso- + -mer
iso- Greek ísos, "equal, same". -mer Greek méros, "part, share, portion". "Same parts": same atoms, possibly arranged differently.
isomorphic
iso- + -morph- + -ic
iso- "equal, same". -morph- Greek morphḗ, "form, shape". -ic adjectival ending. "Same form": identical structural relations between objects.

The semantic distinction the Greek roots draw is exactly the one we need. Two molecules are isomers when they are made of the same parts (same atoms, same counts) but those parts are arranged differently. Two graphs are isomorphic when their structural relations match perfectly, regardless of what the nodes happen to be made of. Iso-mer says same stuff, possibly different shape; iso-morph says same shape, possibly different stuff.

For our purposes the chemistry term is the closer fit. The three bio- words below contain identical atoms (the letters b, i, o at the start) but the tokeniser arranges them into three different graphs. The structural relations between fragments differ, the bonds land in different places, and the resulting tokenisations behave like three distinct molecules with three distinct properties.

Notice also what happens to the word isomorphic when you tokenise it. A linguist sees a clean three-piece compound: iso-, -morph-, -ic. A typical BPE tokeniser sees something like iso + m + orphic, slicing straight through the morpheme morph. The word that names the concept of structural equivalence is itself a textbook case of structural inequivalence between linguistic and computational atomisation. The lesson keeps repeating at every level you look.

The same letters appear in three different positional contexts and the tokeniser produces three different graphs, exactly as three molecular isomers with the same atoms produce three different molecules with three different boiling points.

Fig. 3 · Three "isomers" of bio-. Same prefix-atoms, three different graphs.

Position determines identity. The lesson from chemistry generalises: in any labelled-graph system, what makes a unit the unit it is depends on its neighbourhood, not on its label alone.

§ 06 / DELOCALISATIONThe deepest analogy: resonance

If the analogy stopped at structural formulas, it would already be useful. But it goes one floor deeper, and the deeper floor is where the modern picture of language meets the modern picture of matter.

In a benzene ring, no individual carbon-carbon bond is single or double. The classical structural formula draws three single and three double bonds alternating around the ring, but reality refuses to commit. The electrons are delocalised, spread across the whole ring, shared rather than placed. Benzene's chemistry, its spectrum, its stability, its smell, all come from this distributed configuration. You cannot point to one atom and say "this carbon is doing X". Each carbon's role is determined by the global pattern.

A token does not possess fixed meaning intrinsically, just as a carbon atom in benzene does not possess a fixed isolated bond identity.

the central claim of this section

The transformer attention mechanism produces something with the same flavour. A token's effective representation in a deep model is not its dictionary entry. It is built up from interactions with every other token in the context window: distant tokens contribute weighted information through attention, and the result is a representation that exists nowhere in the input on its own. The token bank in "river bank" and bank in "bank loan" share the same input embedding and end up at completely different points in representation space, because the surrounding context restructures them. This is delocalisation by another name.

The two-tier picture

So the chemistry analogy stratifies into two levels, and they line up with two eras of linguistics:

Era Chemistry Linguistic counterpart
Classical / structural Lewis structures, fixed bonds, localised valence Traditional generative grammar: discrete categories, hard rules, fixed lexical entries.
Modern / quantum Molecular orbitals, delocalised electron density, resonance Transformer semantics: distributed representations, context-dependent meaning, attention as long-range coupling.

The pattern is general. Once a system becomes large enough that its parts no longer carry stable meaning in isolation, description shifts from "what is this part?" to "what role does this part play within the whole configuration?". Chemistry made that shift in the early twentieth century. Linguistics, arguably, is making it now.

A reader could object here. Chemistry has conservation laws, bond energies, and quantum mechanics underneath it. Tokenisation is an algorithm trained on a text corpus. One appears to belong to physics; the other to data engineering.

The objection weakens on closer inspection because linguistics possesses its own robust statistical regularities, and those regularities are doing real work underneath the tokeniser.

The clearest example is Zipf's law.1 Rank the words of almost any natural-language corpus by frequency. The most common word appears roughly twice as often as the second most common, three times as often as the third, and so on. Frequency scales approximately as 1/n with rank. The same heavy-tailed structure appears in morphemes, character n-grams, substrings, and many other linguistic units. It persists across languages, centuries, and registers. A chemist handed an empirical regularity this stable would call it a law without embarrassment.

Rank-frequency plot, log-log axes, two English corpora (Culpeper's herbal, 17th-century; War of the Worlds, 19th-century). The straight-line decay is the signature of a power law. Plot via Wikimedia Commons (CC-licensed).

BPE tokenisation depends directly on this structure. It is a compression algorithm whose effectiveness presupposes Zipf-like statistics. The merge rule preserves symbol pairs whose co-occurrence is unusually frequent relative to independence. If language followed a uniform distribution rather than a power law, the merges would not concentrate around reusable substrings and the token vocabulary would rapidly degenerate into noise. BPE succeeds because language already contains deep statistical asymmetries.

The situation is not entirely foreign to chemistry. Bond energies are themselves largely empirical objects. Pauling's electronegativity scale emerged from measured regularities long before quantum chemistry supplied cleaner theoretical grounding. Even now, most chemists use tabulated approximations rather than deriving molecular behaviour ab initio. The contrast between "real physical law" and "mere statistics" is therefore less absolute than it first appears.

So the analogy is partly structural and partly substantive. Both chemistry and tokenisation rest on stable empirical regularities and build higher-order behaviour on top of them. Where the analogy genuinely thins is that chemistry possesses a comparatively unified theoretical substrate in quantum mechanics, whereas linguistics still debates why Zipfian structure emerges at all: information-theoretic optimality, cognitive constraints, communicative efficiency, preferential attachment, or the dynamics of language change itself.

A wider pattern: Kleiber's law

Zipf's law sits inside something larger. Power laws of this kind keep appearing in adaptive systems whose elements are functionally interdependent, and the most striking example outside of language comes from biology. In the early 1930s the Swiss-American agricultural physiologist Max Kleiber2 made an unexpected empirical observation about how living things consume energy.

Kleiber's law in one paragraph (for non-biologists)

If you measure the basal metabolic rate B of an animal (the energy per unit time its body uses while at rest) and plot it against the animal's mass M, you do not get a straight line. You get a curve that flattens as animals get larger. Kleiber found that this curve is well described by the relation B ∝ M3/4: metabolic rate scales as mass to the three-quarter power. Concretely: a cat with one hundred times the mass of a mouse uses only about thirty-two times the energy, not one hundred times. Per kilogram, large animals are more metabolically efficient than small ones in a very specific way. The same 3/4 exponent has since been measured across organisms spanning more than twenty orders of magnitude in mass, from bacteria through whales, and even in plants.

Before Kleiber, biologists expected the exponent to be 2/3, on the reasoning that heat dissipates through surface area and surface area scales as volume2/3. The data said 3/4. The deviation is small in number and large in consequence: it tells you that the limiting constraint on metabolism is not heat loss across a surface but something distributed through the entire transport network of the organism.

What makes Kleiber's law interesting in this context is its shape. Like Zipf's law, it is a rank-ordered empirical power law over a heterogeneous population. Like Zipf's law, it has resisted decisive theoretical derivation for nearly a century. The most influential modern attempt (the West-Brown-Enquist or WBE model) derives the 3/4 exponent from the geometry of branching transport networks: vascular systems in animals, xylem networks in plants, under a small set of physical assumptions. WBE is contested in detail, but the broad picture it supplies is suggestive: the law emerges because the units of the system (cells, organs) are not separable from the network that supplies them. Form and function are co-determined.

The same description applies to language. A word's frequency is not a property the word possesses in isolation. It is a property of the word's place in a vast distributed system of communicative acts among speakers, where each utterance is shaped by the expected utterances of others. Words and contexts co-determine each other the way cells and circulatory networks co-determine each other.

The honest claim is therefore more modest than "Kleiber and Zipf are the same kind of law", and more interesting than coincidence. Heterogeneous populations whose elements are functionally interdependent tend to settle into heavy-tailed distributions over their measurable attributes. Kleiber and Zipf are two instances of that broader pattern. The shared mathematical form does not imply a shared generative mechanism. Power laws can arise from quite unrelated processes (preferential attachment, multiplicative noise, criticality, optimisation under constraint), and conflating these is one of the recurring errors in the complex-systems literature.3 What the shared form does suggest is that whenever such a power law appears, we should be looking for the kind of interdependence that makes individual elements undefinable in isolation.

Tokenisation works because language has settled into one of these heavy-tailed regimes. Whether language reached that regime for the same reasons that biological metabolism reached its regime is unsettled. That both reached some such regime is, by now, a finding rather than a hypothesis.

The footing is real. The deeper explanation remains unfinished.

§ 07 / VERIFICATIONPoke at it yourself with Python

Every claim above can be checked in about ten lines of Python. The library tiktoken exposes the exact tokeniser used by GPT-class models; the equivalent for open models is transformers.AutoTokenizer. The two will produce slightly different splits because they were trained on different corpora, and that disagreement is itself part of the lesson.

Setup

# pip install tiktoken
import tiktoken

# cl100k_base is the GPT-4 / GPT-3.5 tokeniser
enc = tiktoken.get_encoding("cl100k_base")

def show(word):
    ids = enc.encode(word)
    pieces = [enc.decode([i]) for i in ids]
    print(f"{word!r:25} -> {pieces}")

Reproducing the cases

# Case 01: the -ing suffix (sometimes preserved, sometimes not)
show("walking")
show("jumping")
show("running")

# Case 02: morphology obliterated
show("unhappy")
show("unbelievable")

# Case 03: silent k
show("night")
show("knight")

# Case 04: the bio- problem
show("biology")
show("biochemistry")
show("biomechanical")

# Case 05: context shifts
show("entropy")
show("Entropy-based")
show(" entropy")   # note the leading space

Representative output (your numbers will match if you run cl100k_base):

>>> 'walking' -> ['walking']
>>> 'jumping' -> ['j', 'umping']
>>> 'running' -> ['running']
>>> 'unhappy' -> ['un', 'happy']
>>> 'unbelievable' -> ['un', 'bel', 'iev', 'able']
>>> 'night' -> ['night']
>>> 'knight' -> ['kn', 'ight']
>>> 'biology' -> ['biology']
>>> 'biochemistry' -> ['bio', 'chemistry']
>>> 'biomechanical' -> ['biom', 'echanical']
>>> 'entropy' -> ['ent', 'ropy']
>>> 'Entropy-based' -> ['Ent', 'ropy', '-based']
>>> ' entropy' -> [' entropy']

A few things worth noticing the first time you run this. 'jumping' is splitting where you would not expect. The leading j is a fragment because jumping at the start of a string (no preceding space) is rarer than jumping mid-sentence. Try show(" jumping") and the split disappears. The space-prefix is a real token-level feature, not a typo.

Likewise ' entropy' with the leading space tokenises as one piece, while 'entropy' at string-start splits. The model's atomisation of the same word is genuinely different depending on whether it sits at the start of a sentence. This is the context-dependence of Type IV in the wild.

A small experiment for students

# Question: how many tokens does the same morpheme generate
# across different host words? Does the answer cluster?

words = ["biology", "biochemistry", "biomechanical",
         "biosphere", "biopsy", "biome",
         "bioluminescence", "biographer"]

for w in words:
    pieces = [enc.decode([i]) for i in enc.encode(w)]
    bio_intact = "bio" in pieces or pieces[0] == "bio"
    print(f"{w:20} {pieces}  bio intact: {bio_intact}")

Run this and ask: under what conditions does bio survive as a token? Is it about the length of the word? The frequency of the suffix? The presence of a clean syllable break? You will not find a tidy linguistic rule. You will find a frequency table.

That last observation is the entire point of the dissection in microcosm. The boundary is wherever compression put it, and you can only know where that is by asking the tokeniser.

§ 08 / SYNTHESISWhat you take away

Linguistics atomises around meaning: morphemes, phonemes, allomorphs, the chemistry of sense.

Tokenisation atomises around frequency: whatever substring earns its place in a fixed-size vocabulary by appearing often enough.

The map between the two is a four-way partition: clean isomorphism (rare), many-to-one collapse, one-to-many fragmentation, and context-dependent shifting (most common, deepest).

Traditional linguistics resembles classical structural chemistry. Transformer semantics resembles delocalised electron density. Both fields, half a century apart, make the same shift: from fixed local identity to distributed configuration.

This is also where the verbal/written split shows up most plainly. Spoken language gives a child phonological abstractions for free: the plural rule, the past-tense rule, the silent letters that are silent. Written language hides phonology behind orthography. Tokenisers see only orthography. So whenever you find a place where a model behaves strangely on homophones, on capitalisation, on hyphenation, on rare proper nouns, you have probably found a place where the orthographic graph and the phonological graph disagree, and the model has only ever seen one of them.
