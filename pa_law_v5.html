Companion to the P/A explorer

Scale invariance
why P/A is the wrong measure

The P/A explorer computed boundary-to-area ratios for polygons on the square and Eisenstein lattices. This companion asks a sharper question: is B/A even a meaningful measure of shape?

Dilation kills B/A

Scale any lattice polygon by n — multiply every vertex coordinate by n. The boundary count scales as nB, the area as n²A (one can verify this from Pick directly). So:

B/A → nB / n²A = (1/n) · B/A → 0 as n → ∞

This holds for every polygon regardless of shape. An n×n square on the square lattice has B = 4n and A = n², giving B/A = 4/n → 0. A thin sliver does the same. B/A carries no shape information — only size.

The scale-invariant quantity

Under dilation by n: B → nB and A → n²A, so B²/A → n²B²/n²A = B²/A exactly. The ratio B²/A is invariant under lattice dilation. It is the natural lattice analogue of the classical isoperimetric ratio P²/A, with B playing the role of perimeter.

B²/A — invariant under lattice dilation — measures shape, not size
A note on what B²/A is not: B is a lattice count, not a Euclidean length. The classical isoperimetric inequality P²/A ≥ 4π holds for the actual Euclidean perimeter P. Replacing P by B gives a related but distinct quantity. The two are proportional for symmetric shapes when all boundary steps have the same Euclidean length, but in general B²/A and P²/A differ. We will return to this when comparing lattices.
Pick's theorem

B²/A via Pick
the determinant as ruler

Pick's theorem is A = Δ(I + B/2 − 1), where Δ = det(M_basis) is the area of the fundamental domain. Substituting into B²/A:

B²/A = B² / [Δ · (I + B/2 − 1)]

The determinant Δ sits in the denominator. For the same combinatorial data (I, B), a larger Δ means more Euclidean area, hence a smaller B²/A. Changing the lattice basis changes Δ and therefore changes the Euclidean area assigned to the same boundary-interior count. This is the central role of the basis determinant in the isoperimetric problem.

The n×n square — scale invariance verified

On the square lattice (Δ=1): an n×n square has I = (n−1)², B = 4n, A = n². Pick confirms: A = (n−1)² + 4n/2 − 1 = n² ✓. Then:

B²/A = 16n² / n² = 16 — exactly constant for all n

Dilating the square by any integer factor leaves B²/A = 16 unchanged. This matches the continuous square: P²/A = (4L)²/L² = 16. The lattice measure agrees with the Euclidean one here because every boundary step has Euclidean length exactly 1.

Where B and P diverge

The agreement above is special. On a diagonal edge, a single boundary step of (1,1) has Euclidean length √2 but counts as 1 in B. So B undercounts P for polygons with diagonal edges. This is why B²/A and P²/A can differ, and why the lattice isoperimetric ratio B²/A is a distinct quantity from the classical P²/A — comparable, but not the same.

The correct claim: B²/A is a scale-invariant lattice isoperimetric ratio, governed by Pick and by Δ. It is analogous to P²/A but measures boundary in lattice units, not Euclidean length. The classical bound P²/A ≥ 4π holds for Euclidean perimeters; it does not bound B²/A directly, because B ≠ P in general.

Square vs Eisenstein

Two lattices
two competing effects

On the square lattice Δ = 1; on the Eisenstein lattice Δ = √3/2. For the same combinatorial polygon (same I and B), Pick gives A_ei = (√3/2)·A_sq — the Eisenstein polygon has less Euclidean area, so B²/A is larger. The denser lattice penalises any fixed combinatorial shape.

But the Eisenstein lattice has 12 shortest visible directions against the square lattice's 8. (Both lattices have infinitely many primitive directions — pairs (a,b) with gcd(a,b)=1 — but the 8 or 12 shortest ones determine what convex polygon shapes are achievable.) More short directions mean a polygon can have more sides and more closely approximate a circle.

The two effects pull in opposite directions: denser lattice inflates B²/A; more directions deflate it. The question is which wins for the optimal polygon on each lattice.

Filled circles = optimal convex polygon for that lattice. Open circles = specific shapes. Dashed line = 4π, the Euclidean reference (not a bound on B²/A).

The Eisenstein 12-gon — a note of caution

The figure shows the Eisenstein 12-gon at B²/A ≈ 12.43, below the 4π reference. This is not a paradox, but it does require care. In a regular 12-gon on the Eisenstein lattice, each boundary step has Euclidean length 1 (the Eisenstein unit). The actual Euclidean perimeter of the polygon is close to B (since steps are roughly equal), and one can verify P²/A ≥ 4π holds for the Euclidean P. The quantity B²/A < 4π simply reflects that B undercounts the effective isoperimetric cost — the lattice count B is a coarser measure than the Euclidean P, and the two are not interchangeable in the classical inequality.

Two competing effects — one winner: the Eisenstein lattice's denser packing (smaller Δ) inflates B²/A for any fixed combinatorial shape. But its 12 shortest visible directions allow a more circle-like optimal polygon. The net result: the optimal Eisenstein polygon (≈12.43) achieves a lower B²/A than the optimal square polygon (≈13.25). In lattice-unit terms, the Eisenstein lattice is more isoperimetrically efficient — but this comparison is between lattice measures, not Euclidean ones.
