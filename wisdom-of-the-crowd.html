Wisdom of the Crowd

Estimating seeds by weighing · a learner
Your First Guess
Before any measurement, make a gut estimate. How many seeds do you think are in the bag? This is your prior — raw intuition before data.
Why guess first? Anchoring affects estimation. By committing a number before seeing others' guesses, you avoid being influenced — keeping each guess statistically independent, which is what makes the crowd powerful.
Collect the Crowd
Ask others to guess. Each person should give a number independently — no conferring. Add their guesses below. You need at least 3 to see the effect.
The wisdom of crowds (Galton, 1907) — when many independent estimates are aggregated, random errors tend to cancel out. The mean of the crowd often beats most individuals, including experts. Variance tells us how spread the crowd is.
Weigh & Count
Weigh the whole bag. Then take small countable samples — weigh each and count the seeds. More samples give a better ratio estimate.
Proportional estimation: if a sample of m grams contains n seeds, then the whole bag of mass M contains roughly N = n × M/m seeds. Each sample gives an independent ratio; averaging them reduces measurement noise.
N ≈ (1/k) × Σᵢ [ nᵢ × M / mᵢ ]

The Final Estimate
All methods compared. The measured estimate is your most reliable figure — but see how it relates to what the crowd guessed.
Verdict —
Why this matters: The crowd mean aggregates independent information. The measurement gives ground truth. In practice — polling, financial forecasting, medical diagnosis — neither pure intuition nor pure measurement stands alone. Each corrects the other.
