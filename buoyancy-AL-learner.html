Before we begin

What AI does — and what you must do

You are working in an era when artificial intelligence can retrieve, restate, and pattern-match scientific facts at speed. This is not the same as scientific understanding. Before we think about buoyancy at all, let us be precise about the cognitive territory.

Recall

Retrieving stored facts. "Archimedes' principle states…" AI does this well. It is necessary but not sufficient.

Analysis

Decomposing a situation into its operative constraints. Asking which version of buoyancy applies here, and why.

Synthesis

Constructing an argument across contexts. Connecting a ship's geometry to a sweat gland's function via the same physics.

The MYP A&L Distinction

The Approaches and Learning criteria are not testing whether you know about buoyancy. They are testing whether you can do things with the concept — pose a precise question, trace how your thinking changed, justify a claim under challenge, engage with a view that differs from yours.

Before you continue: what is the difference between knowing that pressure increases with depth and understanding why?

Knowing is possession of a fact. Understanding is knowing what that fact licenses: what predictions follow from it, what would falsify it, how it changes when you alter the conditions. A student who knows "P = ρgh" but cannot explain why a submarine has a maximum depth, or why a helium balloon rises, does not yet understand pressure — they possess its formula.

Wittgenstein: meaning is what you can do with a term. The question is not "do you know Archimedes' principle?" but "can you use it correctly across different contexts — ship hull, lung inflation, weather balloon?"

The Central Distinction

Form versus Function

The historian of biology and the physicist approach the same phenomenon from opposite directions. This is not a conflict — it is a productive tension that good scientific questions exploit.

The Biologist's Question (Form)

  • What structure achieves this?
  • How is it built? What are its parts?
  • What is its evolutionary or developmental history?
  • The how of mechanism
  • Sweat gland: a coiled tubule, aquaporins, ion channels

The Physicist's Question (Function)

  • What does this achieve thermodynamically?
  • What constraints govern its operation?
  • What would a minimal model preserve?
  • The why of purpose and law
  • Sweat gland: latent heat extraction via phase transition
Why is explaining how a sweat gland makes perspiration harder than explaining why sweating cools you?

The why sits at the level of physical law: water has a high latent heat of vaporisation (~2260 J/g). When it evaporates from skin, it carries that energy away from the body. This is a consequence of the zeroth law (thermal equilibrium) and the thermodynamics of phase transitions — known, precise, universal.

The how requires descending into structural biology: the coiled secretory tubule, the reabsorptive duct, sodium-potassium pumps, aquaporin water channels, the precise ion concentrations that draw fluid from interstitial tissue. This is local, evolved, contingent — much harder to derive from first principles.

A physicist is comfortable with the function before the mechanism. This is not laziness; it is the appropriate level of abstraction for the question being asked.

Pedagogical Implication (Criterion A)

A well-framed scientific question specifies which level it operates at. "Why does sweating cool you?" is a thermodynamic question. "How does the body regulate sweat rate?" is a physiological one. Conflating them produces unanswerable questions — and 1–2 band responses.

The Physics

A ship floats. A nail sinks.
Both are steel.

Pause before reading on. What is the variable that determines whether an object floats — and is it the material it is made of?

The relevant quantity is average density — not the density of the material, but of the object as a whole, including any enclosed air. A ship is a steel shell enclosing a vast volume of air; its average density is far below that of water. A solid steel nail has no such enclosed volume; its average density matches the metal.

This is why the question "which objects float?" cannot be answered by listing materials. It requires knowing the geometry of the object. A steel ball floats if it is hollow enough. This is form determining function — shape is the operative variable, not substance.

Case I — Incompressible Fluid (water)

In an incompressible fluid, density does not change with depth. The buoyant force is simply the weight of fluid displaced, a result independent of depth, pressure, or position within the fluid.

Archimedes' Principle F_b = ρ_fluid · V_displaced · g

The nail sinks because even fully submerged, its volume is too small relative to its mass to displace enough water. The ship's hull displaces an enormous volume of water; even partially submerged, that displacement equals the ship's total weight.

Common Confusion — Examiner Note

"Denser objects always sink." This is false. A ball of modelling clay sinks. The same clay shaped into a bowl floats. Density alone — of the material — is not the criterion. The criterion is whether the object can displace its own weight in fluid before being fully submerged.

Case II — Compressible Fluid (atmosphere / lung)

Air is compressible: its density increases with depth (or pressure). This changes the analysis fundamentally. Buoyancy in a compressible fluid is not simply "weight of displaced gas" — it depends on where in the fluid you are.

A helium weather balloon is released at sea level. As it rises, the atmosphere becomes less dense. What happens to the buoyant force — and will the balloon rise indefinitely?

As the balloon rises, external air pressure drops. The balloon expands (Boyle's law). Simultaneously, the surrounding air becomes less dense — so the buoyant force (weight of displaced air) decreases. But the balloon's average density also decreases as it expands. The equilibrium occurs when the average density of the balloon matches the local atmospheric density.

At that altitude, buoyant force equals weight. The balloon stops rising. Push it higher and it would descend. This is a stable equilibrium — unlike the ship, which has a fixed geometry and floats at a fixed fraction of its hull.

This is qualitatively different physics from the incompressible case — and requires a different question to investigate.

The Wittgenstein Point

"Buoyancy" is not one concept with one set of uses — it names a family of related phenomena, each with different operative constraints. A student who applies Archimedes' principle to a submarine and to a weather balloon without noting the difference in fluid compressibility is making a language-game error: using a term outside the conditions of its correct use.

Form & Function · Applied

The sweat gland as a physicist's object

A sweat gland has a biological form — coiled, embedded in dermis, fed by capillaries, regulated by sympathetic nerves. This is the biologist's territory, and it is genuinely complex. But we can also ask: what thermodynamic function does it perform, and what physical laws govern that function?

The Zeroth Law Connection

The zeroth law establishes what thermal equilibrium means: if A is in equilibrium with B, and B with C, then A and C are in equilibrium. Your body at 37°C is not in thermal equilibrium with a 20°C environment. Heat flows. The sweat mechanism is a thermodynamic engine that accelerates heat transfer by exploiting the phase transition of water.

Heat extracted per gram of sweat evaporated Q = m · L_v ≈ m · 2260 J/g
If the latent heat of vaporisation is ~2260 J/g, and a person sweats at 1 litre per hour during exercise, how much power does evaporative cooling provide — and how does this compare to metabolic heat output during exercise?

1 litre/hour = 1000 g / 3600 s ≈ 0.278 g/s. Power = 0.278 × 2260 ≈ 628 W.

A person exercising vigorously produces roughly 500–1000 W of metabolic heat. So evaporative sweating at 1 litre/hour can in principle remove most of the metabolic heat load — provided the sweat actually evaporates (low humidity, good airflow).

In high humidity, evaporation is suppressed. The sweat is produced but does not evaporate — thermal regulation fails. This is the physical explanation for why high humidity is more dangerous than high temperature alone.

Notice: we derived all of this without knowing anything about the biological structure of the sweat gland. Function before form.

Common Confusion — Examiner Note

"Sweating cools you because water is cold." This is not an explanation — it is a description. The mechanism is the latent heat of vaporisation: energy is absorbed from the skin surface to power the liquid→gas phase transition. The cooling is thermodynamic, not simply thermal contact with cool liquid.

Scientific Thinking

What makes a question scientific?

We have now seen two domains — buoyancy and thermoregulation — in which the same physical concepts apply at different levels of description. Good scientific questions are level-specific, variable-identifying, and falsifiable.

From observation to investigable question

Consider the observation: "A ship floats, but a nail of the same metal sinks."

How would you convert this observation into a 7–8 band criterion A question? What makes the difference between 1–2 and 7–8?

1–2 band: "Why do some things float?" — vague, no variables identified, not testable as posed.

3–4 band: "Does the shape of an object affect whether it floats?" — testable but "shape" is not a measurable variable. No controlling of material, mass, or fluid type.

5–6 band: "How does the ratio of enclosed air volume to total mass affect the floating behaviour of a fixed-mass steel container in fresh water?" — testable, variables identified, controlled.

7–8 band: "To what extent does average object density — varied by changing enclosed air volume in a fixed-mass steel hull — determine the critical submersion depth in an incompressible fluid, and how does this relationship change in a compressible fluid (air)?" — scope is explicit, the underlying concept (average density) is named, the contrast between the two fluid types is part of the question, limitations are anticipated.

The progression is: observation → named variable → operationalised variable → conceptually grounded constraint-aware question.

Wittgenstein's test: does meaning survive perturbation?

Meaning-in-Use

Ludwig Wittgenstein argued that to understand a concept is to be able to use it correctly across contexts. Scientific terms acquire meaning not from definitions but from the inferences they license, the measurements they connect to, the predictions they constrain.

A student correctly states Archimedes' principle for a floating ship. You now ask: "Does the same principle apply to a helium balloon?" Can they maintain the concept across this context shift?

If the student says "yes, straightforwardly," they are applying the formula without interrogating the conditions. Archimedes' principle strictly applies to incompressible fluids. A helium balloon in air involves a compressible fluid — density varies with altitude, the balloon's volume changes, and equilibrium is dynamic, not static.

A student who can say "the principle applies in modified form — we must account for compressibility, and the equilibrium is a floating-at-altitude rather than a fixed waterline" is demonstrating Wittgenstein-level understanding: they can track the concept as the context shifts, noting where it holds and where it requires qualification.

This is precisely what criterion B (Development of Thinking) is measuring: not that you knew more facts, but that you could revise your use of concepts as constraints accumulated.

A student writes: "I initially thought denser objects sink, but then I saw that the ship is denser than water and still floats, so I changed my thinking." Which band — and what is missing for the next band up?

This is a 3–4 band criterion B response. It acknowledges that thinking changed, and identifies the contradicting evidence — these are real cognitive moves. But it stops there.

For 5–6: the student should explain the mechanism of the revision — not just "I saw it floats" but "I realised that the average density of the ship-plus-enclosed-air system is what matters, not the density of the steel hull."

For 7–8: the student should evaluate the prior model — "my initial rule (denser = sinks) works for solid homogeneous objects but fails when geometry introduces enclosed volumes. The more precise model is: an object floats if its average density is less than the fluid's." This is iterative refinement with explicit identification of the prior model's scope and failure mode.

Argumentation Structure

The Toulmin model — warrant is everything

Stephen Toulmin mapped what a defensible scientific claim requires. Students typically produce claims and cite data. What is almost always missing is the warrant — the logical bridge between data and claim — and this is where conceptual understanding lives.

Claim
The ship floats because its average density is less than water.
Data
Steel density ≈ 7800 kg/m³; water ≈ 1000 kg/m³. Ship mass ≈ 50,000 tonnes; displaced water mass when floating ≈ 50,000 tonnes. Enclosed volume of hull is ~50,000 m³.
Warrant
An object floats when it can displace a volume of fluid whose weight equals the object's weight before being fully submerged. The steel hull encloses enough air that the total weight is reached at partial submersion — the ship sits at a stable waterline.
Backing
Newton's third law + pressure equilibrium: at the waterline, the upward pressure force on the hull base equals the downward gravitational force. Archimedes derived this from hydrostatic principles.
Qualifier
Valid for an incompressible fluid; the ship's hull must remain watertight; loading must not cause average density to exceed 1000 kg/m³.
Rebuttal
If the hull is breached, water replaces air, average density increases above 1000 kg/m³, and the ship sinks — Archimedes still applies, but the condition for floating is violated.
What Students Typically Produce

"The ship floats because it is less dense than water. The data shows that ships are made of steel but still float." — Claim present. Data gestured at. Warrant absent. No backing. No qualifier. No rebuttal. This is structurally a 3–4 band criterion C response regardless of how fluently it is written.

A student argues: "Sweating cools you because you lose water, and water is associated with cooling." Identify what is missing in Toulmin terms.

The synthesis — Wittgenstein + Toulmin

Wittgenstein tells you what counts as understanding: correct use of a concept across contexts, surviving perturbation and constraint. Toulmin tells you what counts as justification: a claim supported by data, linked by warrant, grounded in backing, bounded by qualifier, defended against rebuttal. Together, they define scientific inquiry as the disciplined use of concepts under constraint, expressed through structured argument. The MYP criteria operationalise exactly this — with observable behaviours at each band.
