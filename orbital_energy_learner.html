Orbital Energy, Kepler's Laws & Escape Velocity

How gravity, circular motion, and energy conservation combine to explain everything from satellite orbits to why the Moon doesn't fall down.

D5 D6 D7 D8 D9 D10 D11

What this section is about

Orbital mechanics is where Newton's gravitation meets circular motion meets energy conservation. The central move is always the same: set gravitational force equal to centripetal force, then use energy arguments to find speeds, periods, or escape conditions.

This learner covers slides D5–D11: Kepler's third law and its derivation, gravitational potential energy and potential, escape velocity, and the beautiful paradox of atmospheric drag (where a satellite loses energy but speeds up). SL students need Kepler's third law and its applications. AHL students need the potential/energy formalism and escape velocity.

The single most important idea: for a circular orbit, there is a rigid relationship between radius, speed, period, and energy. Change one, and all the others are determined. The orbit has no free parameters once you fix the radius.

The Master Move: Gravity = Centripetal Force

For any object in a circular orbit of radius r around mass M:

GMm/r² = mv²/r = mω²r = m(4π²/T²)r

Cancel m (the orbiting object's mass drops out — orbits don't depend on the satellite's mass). This gives you:

v² = GM/r    T² = (4π²/GM)r³    ω²r³ = GM

Every orbital quantity (v, T, ω, KE, PE, total energy) is determined by r alone (for a given central mass M). This is the physics behind Kepler's third law — and it's why you can weigh the Earth by timing the Moon.

SL D5 — Deriving Kepler's Third Law

Kepler's three laws (empirical, from observation):

  • 1st: Orbits are ellipses with the central body at one focus.
  • 2nd: A line from planet to sun sweeps equal areas in equal times (conservation of angular momentum).
  • 3rd: T² ∝ r³ — the square of the orbital period is proportional to the cube of the orbital radius.

Derivation (assuming circular orbits): set gravitational force = centripetal force:

GMm/r² = mv²/r   →   v² = GM/r

Substitute v = 2πr/T:
(2πr/T)² = GM/r   →   4π²r²/T² = GM/r

T² = (4π²/GM) × r³

The constant of proportionality 4π²/(GM) depends only on the central mass M — not on the orbiting object. Every satellite of Earth obeys the same T²/r³ ratio; every planet around the Sun obeys another.

Kepler found the law empirically (1619); Newton derived it theoretically (1687). Kepler worked from Tycho Brahe's observational data — pattern-matching without a mechanism. Newton showed the pattern follows necessarily from the inverse-square law of gravity. Is a law discovered or derived? Is the empirical pattern the same thing as the theoretical explanation? This is a foundational question in philosophy of science.

Q1Why does the mass of the orbiting object cancel out in the derivation?▶
Answer

Because gravitational force is proportional to m (F = GMm/r²) and centripetal force is also proportional to m (F = mv²/r). The m appears on both sides and cancels. This is why all objects in the same orbit have the same period and speed, regardless of their mass — the ISS and a spanner floating next to it share the same orbit. This equivalence of gravitational and inertial mass is the foundation of general relativity.

Q2Planet A orbits at 2 AU; Planet B at 8 AU. What is the ratio of their orbital periods?▶
Answer

T² ∝ r³. So TB²/TA² = (rB/rA)³ = (8/2)³ = 64.

TB/TA = √64 = 8. Planet B takes 8 times longer to orbit.

SL D6 — Using Kepler's Third Law

The full quantitative form using angular velocity ω = 2π/T:

ω²r³ = GM   →   T² = (4π²/GM)r³

This equation lets you do two things:

  • Find an orbital period given M and r: T = 2π√(r³/GM)
  • Find the central mass given T and r: M = 4π²r³/(GT²)
Worked Example — Mass of the Earth from the Moon's orbit

Given: Moon's orbital period T = 27.3 days = 2.36 × 10⁶ s. Moon's orbital radius r = 3.84 × 10⁸ m.

M = 4π²r³/(GT²) = 4π² × (3.84 × 10⁸)³ / (6.674 × 10⁻¹¹ × (2.36 × 10⁶)²) M = 4π² × 5.664 × 10²⁵ / (6.674 × 10⁻¹¹ × 5.57 × 10¹²) M = 2.236 × 10²⁷ / 3.716 × 10² = 6.02 × 10²⁴ kg

Close to the accepted value of 5.97 × 10²⁴ kg — the small discrepancy comes from rounding.

Q1Jupiter orbits at 7.78 × 10¹¹ m from the Sun (M☉ = 1.99 × 10³⁰ kg). What is its orbital period in years?▶
Answer

T = 2π√(r³/GM) = 2π√((7.78 × 10¹¹)³ / (6.674 × 10⁻¹¹ × 1.99 × 10³⁰))

= 2π√(4.71 × 10³⁵ / 1.328 × 10²⁰) = 2π√(3.546 × 10¹⁵) = 2π × 5.955 × 10⁷

T = 3.74 × 10⁸ s ÷ (3.156 × 10⁷ s/yr) = 11.9 years. Matches Jupiter's known orbital period.

Q2A geostationary satellite has T = 24 hours. What orbital radius does this require?▶
Answer

T = 86 400 s. r = (GMT²/4π²)1/3 = (6.674 × 10⁻¹¹ × 5.97 × 10²⁴ × (86400)² / 4π²)1/3

= (7.54 × 10²² )1/3 = 4.23 × 10⁷ m ≈ 42 300 km from Earth's centre (about 35 900 km above the surface).

AHL D7 — Gravitational Potential Energy

Gravitational potential energy of a mass m at distance r from mass M:

Ep = −GMm/r

The minus sign means the system is bound. At r → ∞, Ep → 0 (the reference point). To move the mass further away (increase r), you must add energy (Ep becomes less negative).

Equipotential surfaces: spherical shells around M where every point has the same Vg. No work is done moving along an equipotential. Field lines are perpendicular to equipotential surfaces.

Worked Example — Satellite Launch Energy

Given: m = 740 kg. RE = 6.37 × 10⁶ m. ME = 5.97 × 10²⁴ kg. Orbit altitude h = 320 km.

rorbit = RE + h = 6.37 × 10⁶ + 3.20 × 10⁵ = 6.69 × 10⁶ m Ep,surface = −GMm/RE = −6.674 × 10⁻¹¹ × 5.97 × 10²⁴ × 740 / 6.37 × 10⁶ = −4.627 × 10¹⁰ J Ep,orbit = −GMm/rorbit = −6.674 × 10⁻¹¹ × 5.97 × 10²⁴ × 740 / 6.69 × 10⁶ = −4.406 × 10¹⁰ J ΔEp = −4.406 × 10¹⁰ − (−4.627 × 10¹⁰) = +2.21 × 10⁹ J ≈ 2.2 GJ

This is just the PE change. The satellite also needs KE to maintain the orbit — the total launch energy is larger.

Q1Why is gravitational PE always negative?▶
Answer

Because the zero of PE is defined at infinite separation (r → ∞). Since gravity is attractive, you'd need to add energy to pull the mass out to infinity. The mass at finite r has less energy than it would have at infinity — hence negative. The more negative the PE, the more tightly bound the system.

AHL D8 — Gravitational Potential & Potential Difference

Gravitational potential Vg is the PE per unit mass:

Vg = −GM/r    [J/kg]

Potential difference between two points: ΔVg = V₂ − V₁. Work done moving mass m: W = mΔVg.

Worked Example — Potential Gain per Metre near Earth's Surface

Calculate Vg at sea level and at sea level + 1 m:

Vsl = −GM/RE = −6.674 × 10⁻¹¹ × 5.972 × 10²⁴ / 6.37 × 10⁶ = −6.253 × 10⁷ J/kg Vsl+1 = −GM/(RE + 1) = −6.674 × 10⁻¹¹ × 5.972 × 10²⁴ / 6.370001 × 10⁶ = −6.25299 × 10⁷ J/kg ΔVg per metre ≈ 9.8 J/kg per metre

This is just g — the gravitational field strength near the surface. The connection: g = −dVg/dr. Field strength is the gradient of potential.

Potential (Vg) is not the same as PE (Ep). Potential is PE per unit mass — a property of the field at a point, independent of what mass you place there. Ep = mVg. Getting these confused is a very common exam error: "the potential energy at height h is −GM/r" (wrong — that's the potential; the PE is −GMm/r).

Q1A 70 kg person climbs 8849 m to the top of Everest. Estimate the work done against gravity using ΔVg.▶
Answer

ΔVg ≈ g × Δh = 9.8 × 8849 = 86 720 J/kg (using the near-surface approximation). W = mΔVg = 70 × 86 720 = 6.07 × 10⁶ J ≈ 6.1 MJ. This equals mgh, as expected — the full −GM/r formula and the simplified mgh agree near the surface.

AHL D9 — Change in Gravitational PE (Satellite Problem)
Worked Example — Satellite changing orbit

Given: m = 850 kg. Vg1 = −6.0 × 10⁷ J/kg. Vg2 = −4.0 × 10⁷ J/kg.

Change in PE:

ΔEp = m(Vg2 − Vg1) = 850 × (−4.0 × 10⁷ − (−6.0 × 10⁷)) = 850 × 2.0 × 10⁷ ΔEp = 1.7 × 10¹⁰ J = 17 GJ (energy added to raise the orbit)

Find the orbital radii:

r = −GM/Vg r₁ = 6.674 × 10⁻¹¹ × 5.972 × 10²⁴ / 6.0 × 10⁷ = 6.64 × 10⁶ m (270 km altitude) r₂ = 6.674 × 10⁻¹¹ × 5.972 × 10²⁴ / 4.0 × 10⁷ = 9.96 × 10⁶ m (3590 km altitude) Δr = 9.96 × 10⁶ − 6.64 × 10⁶ = 3.32 × 10⁶ m ≈ 3320 km increase
Q1The satellite moved to a higher orbit. Did its speed increase or decrease?▶
Answer

Decreased. v² = GM/r, so larger r → smaller v. Higher orbits are slower. This is counterintuitive: you fire rockets to go faster, but you end up in a higher orbit moving slower. The energy went into PE, not KE. The ISS at 400 km orbits at ~7.7 km/s; GPS satellites at 20 200 km orbit at ~3.9 km/s.

Orbital Energy Relations — The Complete Picture

For a circular orbit of radius r around mass M:

KE = GMm/(2r)    (always positive)
PE = −GMm/r    (always negative)
Total E = KE + PE = −GMm/(2r)    (always negative for bound orbit)

Critical relationships: KE = −½ PE and Total E = ½ PE = −KE. The total energy is negative (bound system) and equals minus the kinetic energy. These ratios hold for all circular orbits — they're a consequence of the virial theorem.

As r increases: PE increases (less negative), KE decreases, total E increases (less negative). The satellite is less tightly bound. At r → ∞: E → 0 (unbound, just barely escaping).

Q1A satellite in orbit has KE = 5.0 × 10⁹ J. What are its PE and total energy?▶
Answer

PE = −2 × KE = −1.0 × 10¹⁰ J. Total E = −KE = −5.0 × 10⁹ J. The magnitude of PE is always twice KE, and total energy is always the negative of KE. These ratios are universal for circular orbits under an inverse-square force.

AHL D10 — Escape Velocity

Escape velocity is the minimum speed needed to escape a gravitational field completely (reach r → ∞ with v → 0).

Apply conservation of energy: at the surface, E = ½mv²esc − GMm/r. At infinity, E = 0.

½mv²esc − GMm/r = 0

vesc = √(2GM/r)

Notice: vesc = √2 × vorbital. Escape velocity is exactly √2 times the circular orbital speed at the same radius. To go from orbit to escape, you only need to increase speed by a factor of 1.414.

Worked Examples

Earth: vesc = √(2 × 6.674 × 10⁻¹¹ × 5.972 × 10²⁴ / 6.37 × 10⁶) = 11 200 m/s ≈ 11.2 km/s

Moon: vesc = √(2 × 6.674 × 10⁻¹¹ × 7.35 × 10²² / 1.74 × 10⁶) = 2370 m/s ≈ 2.4 km/s

The Moon's low escape velocity explains why it has no atmosphere — gas molecules at typical thermal speeds exceed 2.4 km/s and escape to space.

Escape velocity does not depend on the escaping object's mass. The m cancels, just as in orbital period. A molecule and a rocket have the same escape velocity. What differs is the energy needed: E = ½mv²esc — a heavier object needs more energy but the same speed.

Escape velocity is not a launch speed requirement. A rocket can escape at any speed — it just needs to keep accelerating. vesc is the speed needed if you're given a single impulse (like firing a cannonball) and then coast with no further thrust.

Q1A planet has twice Earth's mass and twice Earth's radius. What is its escape velocity relative to Earth's?▶
Answer

vesc = √(2GM/r). If M → 2M and r → 2r: vesc' = √(2G(2M)/(2r)) = √(2GM/r) = vesc. Same escape velocity as Earth. The doubling of mass and radius exactly cancel in the √(M/r) dependence.

Q2Why is vesc = √2 × vorbital?▶
Answer

Orbital speed: vorb² = GM/r (from gravity = centripetal). Escape speed: vesc² = 2GM/r (from energy conservation). The ratio is vesc²/vorb² = 2, so vesc/vorb = √2. Physically: a circular orbit has total energy E = −GMm/(2r). To escape (E = 0), you need to add exactly |E| = GMm/(2r) in KE, which means doubling the KE — hence multiplying speed by √2.

AHL D11 — Atmospheric Drag: The Orbital Paradox

This is one of the most counterintuitive results in orbital mechanics, and the IB loves it.

Setup: A satellite in low orbit (380 km, m = 700 kg) experiences atmospheric drag, losing 0.10 W of mechanical energy.

The paradox: Drag removes energy. The satellite spirals to a lower orbit. At lower r, v = √(GM/r) is higher. So the satellite speeds up when it loses energy.

How? The total energy E = −GMm/(2r) becomes more negative (lower orbit), but KE = GMm/(2r) increases. The PE decreases (more negative) by twice the amount that KE increases. Half the lost PE goes to KE; the other half is radiated away as heat from drag.

ΔEtotal = −Ploss × t    (energy removed by drag)
ΔKE = +Ploss × t    (KE increases by the same amount!)
ΔPE = −2Ploss × t    (PE decreases by twice as much)

Why does losing energy make you go faster? In everyday life, drag slows you down. In orbit, drag drops you into a lower, faster orbit. The resolution is that "faster" and "more energy" are not the same thing in a gravitational well. The total energy decreases, but the split between KE and PE changes because PE drops faster than Etotal. Does this violate intuition, or does it reveal that our intuition is calibrated for flat surfaces, not curved spacetime?

Q1The satellite loses 0.10 W for one orbital period (T ≈ 5530 s). How much does its KE change?▶
Answer

Energy lost per orbit = P × T = 0.10 × 5530 = 553 J. Since ΔKE = +Ploss × t:

KE increases by 553 J per orbit. The satellite is 553 J more kinetic-energetic but 553 J less mechanically-energetic overall (PE dropped by 1106 J).

Q2This is how the ISS slowly loses altitude. How is it corrected?▶
Answer

Periodic reboost burns: small rocket firings (or ion thrusters on visiting spacecraft) that increase the ISS's speed and raise its orbit back up. Without reboosts, the ISS would spiral down and re-enter the atmosphere within a few years. The reboost adds energy to the system — the reverse of the drag process.

Q3During re-entry, a spacecraft decelerates violently (drag dominates). Doesn't this contradict "drag makes satellites speed up"?▶
Answer

No — the "speeds up" effect applies to a satellite still in a roughly circular orbit, gently spiralling inward. During re-entry, the drag force is so large that the satellite can no longer maintain a circular orbit. It plunges steeply into thick atmosphere where drag overwhelms gravity's tendency to speed it up. The gentle spiral (orbital mechanics regime) transitions to a ballistic deceleration (atmospheric flight regime). The physics is continuous, but the dominant effect changes.
