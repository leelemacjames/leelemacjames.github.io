Equilibrium does not mean still

// the conceptual foundation

Before a single cone is dropped or a sphere measured, you need this idea locked in.

Common Misconception
A common conflation: equilibrium of forces means the object is stationary. This is wrong. Equilibrium means the net force is zero. Newton's second law is F = ma. Zero net force means zero acceleration, not zero velocity.

An object can move at constant, non-zero velocity perfectly consistent with Newton's laws, provided all forces on it are balanced. This is the first thing terminal velocity teaches.

Newton's Second Law
Fnet = ma
If Fnet = 0 then a = 0, meaning velocity is constant (which includes constant = 0, but also constant = 60 ms-1).

The journey to terminal velocity

Consider an object released from rest in a fluid (air, water, glycerol). Three stages follow:

Stage 1 — Accelerating

At the moment of release, velocity is zero so drag is zero. The only significant force is weight (downward). Net force is non-zero, acceleration is non-zero, so the object speeds up.

Stage 2 — Still accelerating, but less so

As speed increases, drag increases (drag depends on velocity). Weight remains constant. The net downward force decreases. The object still accelerates, but at a diminishing rate.

Stage 3 — Terminal velocity

At some speed, drag exactly equals weight (ignoring buoyancy for now). Net force = 0. Acceleration = 0. Velocity is constant. This constant velocity is the terminal velocity.

Key Statement
Terminal velocity is an equilibrium of forces, producing zero acceleration at non-zero constant velocity. The object is in mechanical equilibrium while moving.

Forces in the fluid

// weight · drag · buoyancy

Ignoring buoyancy (falling through air)

For an object falling through air, the buoyant upthrust is negligible compared to weight. Two forces act:

At terminal velocity (no buoyancy)
Weight = Drag
mg = 6πηrvt

Rearranging: vt = mg / (6πηr)

Since m = ρ · V = (4/3)πr³ρ, substituting gives the compact Stokes result:

Stokes terminal velocity
vt = (2r²ρg) / (9η)

Terminal velocity scales with r² — doubling the radius quadruples the terminal speed.

Including buoyancy (falling through liquid)

When the fluid density is not negligible (a ball bearing through glycerol, a pebble through water), Archimedes' upthrust adds a third upward force.

At terminal velocity (with buoyancy)
Weight = Upthrust + Viscous drag
(4/3)πr³ρobjg = (4/3)πr³ρfluidg + 6πηrvt

vt = 2r²g(ρobj − ρfluid) / (9η)

Only the density difference matters. An object denser than the fluid sinks; less dense, it rises. In both cases, the same force balance applies at the terminal speed.

Viscosity & Stokes' Law

// fluid friction at the microscale

Viscosity η is the measure of a fluid's resistance to relative motion of its layers. When a sphere moves through a fluid, adjacent layers of fluid are dragged relative to each other — this internal friction is the viscous drag force.

Stokes' Law
For a small sphere moving slowly through a viscous fluid (laminar flow):

FD = 6πηrv

where η is dynamic viscosity (Pa·s), r is the sphere radius (m), v is the speed (ms⁻¹).

Conditions for Stokes' Law

Stokes' Law applies only when flow is laminar — smooth, layered flow with no turbulence. The Reynolds number Re = ρvr/η must be much less than 1. At higher speeds, flow becomes turbulent and drag grows as v² rather than v.

Using terminal velocity to find η

The glycerol-and-ball-bearings experiment exploits this beautifully. By measuring terminal velocity v for balls of known radius r:

Rearranged for viscosity
η = 2r²g(ρsteel − ρglycerol) / (9vt)

Plotting vt against r² gives a straight line through the origin with gradient 2g(ρs−ρL)/(9η). The gradient determines η directly.

Why plot v vs r² and not v vs r?

Linearisation. The physics says v ∝ r² (from Stokes + Archimedes). Plotting v against r gives a curve; plotting v against r² gives a straight line whose gradient can be measured precisely. Straight lines are always easier to fit and interpret.

Temperature Warning
Glycerol viscosity is strongly temperature-dependent. A few degrees of change from body heat in the tube can significantly shift results. Always record temperature at the start and end of the experiment.
