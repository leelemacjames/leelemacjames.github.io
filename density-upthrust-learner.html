Physical foundation

What upthrust actually is —
before we measure it

Both experimental methods measure upthrust. Before using either, you need to know where upthrust comes from — not just that it exists.

A cube is submerged in a fluid. Six faces. Fluid pushes on all of them. Why does the net result point upward — and why does it equal the weight of the displaced fluid?
A nudge

Think about the pressure on the bottom face versus the top face. The cube has height Δh. The bottom face is deeper by exactly Δh. Pressure increases with depth at rate ρg. So the pressure difference between bottom and top faces is ρgΔh. That difference acts across the face area A. Force = pressure × area. Now write out what that force is — and notice what ρ × A × Δh equals.

The derivation

The bottom face has pressure P_bottom, the top face P_top. Since pressure = ρgh:

Pressure difference across height Δh P_bottom − P_top = ρ · g · Δh

The net upward force on the cube is this pressure difference times the face area A:

Net upward force F_up = (P_bottom − P_top) · A = ρ · g · Δh · A = ρ · g · V_displaced = weight of fluid that occupied that volume

The side forces (left/right, front/back) cancel exactly because those faces are at the same depth. The top and bottom are not — and the asymmetry of depth creates the asymmetry of force. Upthrust is not a separate phenomenon: it is the pressure gradient, nothing more.

Archimedes' principle — the result F_upthrust = ρ_fluid · g · V_displaced = weight of the fluid that occupied that volume before the object arrived
Method A
Eureka can · weighing the displaced fluid

Catch the overflow.
Weigh it.
That weight is the upthrust.

The Eureka can (or overflow can) has a spout at the water surface. When an object is lowered in, the displaced water spills out of the spout into a collection beaker. You weigh that beaker — before and after — and the difference gives you the weight of displaced water. Which is the upthrust.

Why does the weight of the collected overflow equal the upthrust — exactly? This seems almost too convenient. Convince yourself it must be true.
A nudge

The upthrust on the object equals the weight of fluid displaced (Archimedes). The displaced fluid is exactly the fluid that spilled out — the volume that used to occupy the space the object now occupies. So the weight of the overflow is the weight of displaced fluid. Which is the upthrust. Each step is definitional.

Why it must be true

Archimedes: F_upthrust = weight of displaced fluid. The Eureka can spout sits at the water surface. When the object enters, it pushes exactly V_object worth of water over the spout (assuming fully submerged and can is initially full to the spout). That overflow has mass m_overflow = ρ_fluid × V_object. Its weight is ρ_fluid × g × V_object. Which is exactly F_upthrust. The apparatus is a physical implementation of the proof.

This is what makes the Eureka can elegant: it doesn't require you to measure V_object geometrically. The can does it hydraulically.

The chain of reasoning — Method A

Step 1

Weigh the empty collection beaker: mass m₁. Place it under the spout.

Step 2

Fill the Eureka can to the spout level with the fluid whose density you want to find.

Step 3

Slowly lower the object (fully submerged). Collect all overflow. Weigh beaker + overflow: mass m₂.

Step 4

Mass of displaced fluid: m_disp = m₂ − m₁. Weight of displaced fluid = upthrust: F_b = m_disp · g.

Step 5 — finding fluid density

You need V_object (measure the cuboid's dimensions, or use Method B to get it indirectly). Then:

Fluid density from Eureka can F_b = ρ_fluid · g · V_object ∴ ρ_fluid = F_b / (g · V_object) = m_disp / V_object
What are the practical sources of error in this method — and which direction would each push your calculated density?
A nudge

Think about: what if the object isn't fully submerged? What if some overflow misses the beaker? What if the can wasn't quite full to the spout before you started? What if you measure V_object with a ruler and get it slightly wrong?

Error analysis

Not fully submerged: less overflow than V_object → m_disp is too small → calculated ρ_fluid is too low.

Overflow misses beaker: m_disp is too small → ρ_fluid too low. (Always an underestimate.)

Can not quite full to spout at start: first fraction of submersion doesn't overflow → m_disp too small → ρ_fluid too low.

V_object measurement error: if you overestimate V (e.g. ruler misread), ρ_fluid = m_disp/V → too low. Underestimate V → ρ too high.

Most practical errors in the Eureka method push the density estimate downward — because they result in collecting less overflow than was actually displaced.

Method B
Spring balance + cuboid · feeling the upthrust

Weigh the block in air.
Weigh it in fluid.
The difference is the upthrust.

A spring balance measures the tension in a string. When a block hangs in air, that tension equals the block's weight. When the block is submerged, the fluid provides an additional upward force — the upthrust — so the spring doesn't need to pull as hard. The reduction in reading is the upthrust.

When the block is submerged, three forces act on it: its weight (down), the upthrust (up), and the spring tension (up). Write the equilibrium condition — and rearrange to show that the spring reading equals W − F_b.
A nudge

The block isn't accelerating — it's stationary. So net force = 0. Forces up = forces down. Forces up: tension T + upthrust F_b. Forces down: weight W. Write the balance, then solve for T.

The equilibrium
Static equilibrium of submerged block T + F_b = W ∴ T = W − F_b ∴ F_b = W_air − W_apparent

The spring reads the tension — which is the apparent weight. The upthrust is the difference between true weight (in air) and apparent weight (in fluid). This is measurable directly from two spring balance readings, with no knowledge of the fluid's density required in advance.

Fluid density from spring balance method F_b = ρ_fluid · g · V_cuboid ∴ ρ_fluid = F_b / (g · V_cuboid) = (W_air − W_apparent) / (g · V_cuboid)
The cuboid · measuring what you need

From dimensions
to volume to density —
step by step

Both methods need the volume of the submerged object. A cuboid is the easiest shape: measure three lengths, multiply. But each measurement has uncertainty. Here is how to think carefully about what you're actually doing.

ℓlength (m)
wwidth (m)
hheight (m)
You measure a cuboid with a ruler: ℓ = 0.050 m, w = 0.040 m, h = 0.030 m. Each measurement has ±0.001 m uncertainty. What is the volume — and roughly how uncertain is it?
A nudge

V = ℓ × w × h. For the uncertainty, a useful approximation for multiplied quantities: the fractional uncertainty in V is approximately the sum of the fractional uncertainties in each dimension. Fractional uncertainty = (absolute uncertainty) / (measured value). Try working out each fraction, add them, and multiply by V.

The calculation
Volume and its uncertainty V = 0.050 × 0.040 × 0.030 = 6.0 × 10⁻⁵ m³ δV/V ≈ δℓ/ℓ + δw/w + δh/h = 0.001/0.050 + 0.001/0.040 + 0.001/0.030 = 0.02 + 0.025 + 0.033 ≈ 0.078 δV ≈ 0.078 × 6.0×10⁻⁵ ≈ 4.7×10⁻⁶ m³ V = (6.0 ± 0.5) × 10⁻⁵ m³ — about 8% uncertain

The smallest dimension (h = 0.030 m) contributes the largest fractional uncertainty. To reduce overall uncertainty in V, measure more carefully — or use a larger object where each dimension's relative uncertainty is smaller.

Common mistake

"I multiplied the three sides to get volume in cm³ and then divided by the mass in kg to get density." Units must be consistent throughout: if mass is in kg and V is in cm³ (= 10⁻⁶ m³), the result will be wrong by a factor of 10⁶. Always convert to SI (metres, kilograms) before calculating density in kg/m³.

If Method A gives ρ = 980 kg/m³ and Method B gives ρ = 1020 kg/m³ for the same fluid, the discrepancy is about 4%. Is this a problem — and what would you investigate first?
A nudge

Think about the sources of error in each method — we listed them earlier. Which errors are systematic (always push in one direction) vs. random (vary each trial)? A consistent 4% gap between the two methods suggests a systematic error in one of them, not random variation.

What to investigate

A 4% discrepancy with Method A lower suggests the Eureka method is under-collecting overflow — the most common systematic error. Things to check: was the can genuinely full to the spout before starting? Was all overflow collected (spout too high, beaker misplaced)? Was the object fully submerged?

Method B's error sources are different: spring balance calibration, reading accuracy, whether the object touched the container sides. If B is consistently higher, a miscalibrated spring or parallax error in reading could explain it.

A 4% discrepancy in a school lab is typical — it suggests competent experimental technique, not failure. The question is whether the discrepancy is reproducible (systematic) or varies between trials (random). Repeat and compare.

Worked example

The weight of the overflow from the Eureka can equals the upthrust on the submerged object
because
Archimedes' principle: the upthrust equals the weight of fluid displaced — and the overflow is exactly that displaced fluid, collected in a beaker.
but
this is only exact if the can was initially filled precisely to the spout, the object is fully submerged, and all overflow is collected without spillage — otherwise the collected mass underestimates the displaced volume.
so
measuring the mass of overflow directly gives F_b = m_overflow × g, and combining with V_object gives ρ_fluid = m_overflow / V_object — the fluid's density, found without ever measuring its volume directly.

You complete this one

The spring balance reads less when the block is in fluid than when it hangs in air
because
but
so
Model answer

because — the fluid exerts an upward pressure-gradient force (upthrust) on the submerged block, partially supporting its weight. The spring only needs to provide the remaining tension: T = W − F_b.

but — the spring reads the tension, not the weight. If the block touches the container base or sides, the container also provides a reaction force, reducing the spring reading by more than just the upthrust — making the calculated upthrust too large.

so — the upthrust is F_b = W_air − W_apparent, and since F_b = ρ_fluid × g × V_cuboid, the fluid density is ρ_fluid = (W_air − W_apparent) / (g × V_cuboid) — found from two spring readings and three ruler measurements.

The two methods should give the same fluid density if both are done correctly
because
but
so
Model answer

because — both methods measure the same physical quantity — the upthrust on the same object in the same fluid — via different routes. Archimedes' principle applies equally: F_b = ρ_fluid × g × V. If both measure F_b and V correctly, ρ_fluid must agree.

but — each method has different systematic errors: the Eureka method tends to under-collect overflow (pushing its ρ estimate low); the spring method depends on calibration and on not touching the container walls. The volume measurement (ruler on cuboid) is shared by both and its error propagates into both results in the same direction.

so — a consistent discrepancy between the methods is diagnostic: it points to a systematic error specific to one method. Random scatter around an agreement value suggests measurement precision limits. Comparing both methods on the same fluid is a stronger experimental design than either method alone.

The deeper point

The two methods are not just alternative routes to the same number. They test each other. If they agree, you have corroborating evidence. If they don't, you have information about which method has a systematic flaw. That is what triangulation means in experimental physics — and it is exactly what the because, but, so structure forces you to make explicit: not just the result, but the mechanism, the constraint, and the consequence of each choice.
