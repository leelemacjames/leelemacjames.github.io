Worked example — aluminium cuboid

Ruler + balance → density
1
Measure three sides:
l = 0.060 m w = 0.040 m h = 0.025 m
2
Calculate volume:
V = 0.060 × 0.040 × 0.025 = 6.0 × 10² m³ (= 60 cm³ — roughly the size of a large sugar cube)
3
Weigh on balance:
m = 0.162 kg (= 162 g)
4
Calculate density:
ρ = m / V = 0.162 / (6.0 × 10⁻³)
ρ = 2700 kg/m³ This matches the known density of aluminium — a self-check that the measurements are consistent.
You measure l, w, h each to ±0.001 m. The cuboid is l = 0.060 m, w = 0.040 m, h = 0.025 m. Which dimension contributes the most uncertainty to V?
A nudge

For a product V = l × w × h, fractional uncertainties add: δV/V ≈ δl/l + δw/w + δh/h. Fractional uncertainty = (absolute uncertainty) ÷ (measured value). Calculate each term. The smallest dimension gives the largest fraction.

The calculation

δl/l = 0.001/0.060 = 1.7%  |  δw/w = 0.001/0.040 = 2.5%  |  δh/h = 0.001/0.025 = 4.0%

Total: δV/V ≈ 8.2%. The height h, being the smallest dimension, contributes more than twice the fractional uncertainty of the length. To reduce uncertainty in V most efficiently, measure h more carefully — use a micrometer, or ensure the ruler is perpendicular.

Why this matters for both methods

Both experiments below use V_cuboid to calculate fluid density. Any error in V feeds directly into both results. Getting V right is the first quality-control step in the entire experiment.

Method A · most intuitive route
Eureka can · weighing the displaced fluid

Catch the overflow.
Weigh it.
That weight is the upthrust.

This is the most direct method. You do not need to understand pressure gradients to use it. You need one fact: upthrust = weight of displaced fluid. The Eureka can makes that displaced fluid physical and weighable.

The procedure

Eureka can · finding ρ_fluid
1
Weigh the empty collection beaker: m₁
2
Fill the Eureka can exactly to the spout level with the unknown fluid. Place the beaker under the spout.
3
Slowly lower the cuboid until fully submerged. Collect all overflow. Weigh beaker + overflow: m₂
4
Mass of displaced fluid: m_disp = m₂ − m₁   Upthrust: F_b = m_disp × g
5
Use V_cuboid from your ruler measurement. Find fluid density:
Fluid density — Eureka can
F_b=ρ_fluid · g · V_cuboid
ρ_fluid= F_bg · V_cuboid = m_dispV_cuboid
The second form is elegant: density of unknown fluid = mass of overflow ÷ volume of cuboid. Gravity cancels — no value of g is needed.
The formula ρ_fluid = m_disp / V_cuboid contains no g. Why does gravity cancel — and what does that mean physically?
A nudge

F_b = m_disp × g (weight of overflow). Also F_b = ρ_fluid × g × V_cuboid. Set them equal: m_disp × g = ρ_fluid × g × V_cuboid. Now divide both sides by g.

Why g cancels

Both sides have g as a factor — it divides out. Density is a ratio of mass to volume, not a force. Gravity turns density differences into forces (upthrust), but density itself is a purely mass-volume quantity. The Eureka method works identically on the Moon, at any gravitational field — the ratio m_disp / V_cuboid gives ρ_fluid regardless of g.

What are the main sources of error in the Eureka method, and in which direction does each push the calculated ρ_fluid?
A nudge

Think: what if the can was not quite full to the spout? What if some overflow missed the beaker? Each reduces m_disp. Since ρ_fluid = m_disp / V_cuboid, a smaller m_disp pushes the answer in which direction?

Error directions

Can not full to spout: early submersion causes no overflow → m_disp too small → ρ_fluid underestimated.

Overflow misses beaker: m_disp too small → ρ_fluid underestimated.

Cuboid not fully submerged: V displaced < V_cuboid — both the numerator and the effective denominator are wrong in complex ways.

V_cuboid error: overestimate V → ρ_fluid too low; underestimate V → ρ_fluid too high.

Most practical errors push the Eureka estimate downward. When Method A reads lower than Method B, this is the first suspect.

Deeper understanding · after the intuition

Why does upthrust exist?
Pressure increases linearly with depth.

The Eureka method tells you what upthrust equals. It does not explain why. The answer is in the behaviour of pressure in a fluid. Once you see it, the formula stops being a rule to remember and becomes something you could rederive.

P = ρgh — linear in both density and depth

Consider a vertical column of fluid, density ρ, height h. The weight of that column per unit base area is what creates the pressure. Weight per unit area = mass per unit area × g = (ρ × h) × g:

Pressure at depth h in a fluid of density ρ
P=ρ · g · h
P is proportional to ρ — double the fluid density, double the pressure at every depth.
P is proportional to h — the pressure–depth graph is a straight line through the origin, slope = ρg.
Making the linearity concrete

In fresh water (ρ = 1000 kg/m³): at h = 1 m, P = 9 810 Pa. At h = 2 m, P = 19 620 Pa. Exactly double — linear. In seawater (ρ ≈ 1025 kg/m³), every depth gives 2.5% more pressure. The slope ρg is steeper for denser fluids. Change ρ on the slider below and watch the slope change.

Try it — the pressure–depth graph

The shaded band shows the pressure difference across your cuboid's height — that difference is the upthrust per unit area. Denser fluid → steeper slope → larger pressure difference → larger upthrust.

The graph is a straight line through the origin. What property of the fluid makes this linearity exact — and why does air require a curved graph instead?
A nudge

P = ρgh gives a straight line only if ρ does not change with depth. What physical property keeps density constant regardless of how much pressure the fluid is under?

Incompressibility

Linearity requires the fluid to be incompressible: its density does not change with pressure. Water is nearly incompressible — squeezing it harder does not pack significantly more mass per cubic metre. So ρ stays constant and P = ρgh is a straight line.

Air is compressible. Higher pressure packs molecules closer together — density increases with depth (decreasing altitude). So each additional metre of altitude removes less pressure than the one below. The pressure–altitude relationship becomes exponential, not linear. P = ρgh works for tanks and pools; it fails for the atmosphere across large height changes.

From pressure gradient to upthrust — the derivation

A submerged cuboid has six faces. Side forces cancel (equal depth, equal pressure). The top and bottom do not cancel — the bottom is deeper by exactly the cuboid's height Δh.

Deriving F_b from the pressure gradient
Net upward force = (P_bottom − P_top) × A
P_bottom − P_top = ρ_fluid · g · Δh   (Δh = height of cuboid)
F_b = ρ_fluid · g · Δh · A = ρ_fluid · g · V_cuboid
F_b = ρ_fluid · g · V_cuboid
Archimedes — derived, not memorised. The Eureka can makes it physical; the pressure gradient makes it logical.
Method B
Spring balance + cuboid · feeling the upthrust

Weigh in air. Weigh in fluid.
The difference is the upthrust.

A spring balance measures the tension keeping the block suspended. In air, tension = weight. In fluid, the pressure gradient provides an additional upward force — the upthrust — so the spring needs less tension. The reduction in reading is the upthrust, readable directly.

Write the equilibrium condition for the submerged block and rearrange to show T = W − F_b. Then show how ρ_fluid follows from two spring readings and the volume you measured earlier.
A nudge

Block is stationary → net force = 0. Forces up: T + F_b. Forces down: W. Solve for T. Then substitute F_b = ρ_fluid · g · V and T = W_apparent to isolate ρ_fluid.

The derivation
Equilibrium:   T + F_b = W
Rearranged:   T = W − F_b  →  W_apparent = W_air − F_b
Upthrust:   F_b = W_air − W_apparent
Since F_b = ρ_fluid · g · V_cuboid:
ρ_fluid= W_air − W_apparentg · V_cuboid
Two spring readings + the cuboid volume from your ruler. That is the complete experiment.
Common mistake

Letting the cuboid touch the bottom or sides of the container. Any contact provides an extra reaction force, reducing the spring tension by more than just the upthrust. The calculated upthrust will be too large and ρ_fluid will be overestimated. The cuboid must hang freely, fully submerged, touching nothing.

A nudge

Method A underestimates when overflow is undercollected (pushes result low). Method B overestimates when the block touches the container (pushes result high). Which pattern matches the 4% gap here?

Diagnosing the discrepancy

Method A lower than B: most likely the Eureka can was not filled exactly to the spout, so some displaced volume never overflowed — m_disp is too small → ρ_fluid too low.

Method B higher than A: could indicate the block grazed the container wall, or the spring balance was not zeroed correctly in air.

A 4% spread is typical for school apparatus with careful technique. The true value lies between the two estimates. Repeating reduces random error; fixing the can fill level reduces the systematic bias in Method A.

Because · But · So

Build the complete argument —
write before you reveal

Worked example

The weight of overflow from the Eureka can equals the upthrust on the submerged cuboid
because
Archimedes: upthrust = weight of displaced fluid — and the Eureka can collects exactly that fluid as overflow, so its weight is the upthrust directly.
but
this equality holds only if the can was filled precisely to the spout, the cuboid is fully submerged, and all overflow is caught — any deficit gives a weight less than the true upthrust.
so
dividing overflow mass by cuboid volume gives fluid density directly: ρ_fluid = m_overflow / V_cuboid — with g cancelling out, making the result independent of gravitational field.

Model answer

because — P = ρgh is linear in h because ρ is constant (water is incompressible). Each additional metre of depth adds the same pressure: ρg Pa per metre. The slope of the P–h graph is ρg, constant throughout.

but — air is compressible. At greater depth (lower altitude), higher pressure squeezes air molecules closer, increasing ρ. So each additional metre of descent adds more pressure than the metre above. The relationship becomes exponential, not linear.

so — P = ρgh applies to incompressible fluids (water, most liquids) for tanks, pools, and diver depths. It fails for gases across large height changes; meteorology and aviation require an exponential atmospheric model.

The two methods should give the same fluid density — but in practice they differ
because
but
so
Model answer

because — both measure the same quantity: upthrust on the same cuboid in the same fluid. Archimedes applies equally: F_b = ρ_fluid · g · V. If both measure F_b and V correctly, ρ_fluid must agree.

but — each method has different systematic errors. Method A tends to under-collect overflow (pushes ρ low). Method B depends on calibration and the block hanging freely (contact overestimates upthrust, pushes ρ high). The V_cuboid measurement is shared — its error shifts both results in the same direction.

so — using both methods is more powerful than either alone. Agreement validates the result. Disagreement is diagnostic: the direction of the discrepancy tells you which method to investigate. That is what experimental triangulation means in practice.

The point of the whole exercise

Density is never measured directly — you calculate it from mass and volume. Upthrust is not magic — it is the pressure gradient acting on surfaces. The Eureka can makes the physics tangible first; the pressure derivation explains why it works. Two methods, one quantity, one consistency check: that is experimental physics.
