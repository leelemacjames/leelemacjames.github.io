Overview

The perimeter-to-area law
P/A = 2/Δ

Pick's theorem connects area to lattice point counts. Reading it in reverse reveals a universal law about how boundary and interior relate — and how the geometry of the lattice determines the ratio.

Pick's theorem (general): A = Δ · (I + B/2 − 1)

Here Δ is the area of the fundamental domain of the lattice — the determinant of the basis matrix. For large polygons where the interior dominates, this law emerges:

P/A → 2/Δ

where P = B is the boundary point count, which serves as the lattice-metric perimeter under the Chebyshev (king-move) metric on each lattice.

Derivation in three lines

For large polygons: I ≈ A/Δ (interior fills the area) and B ≪ I, so Pick gives A ≈ Δ·I. Rearranging Pick exactly:

B = 2(A/Δ − I + 1)  →  B/A = 2/Δ · (1 − ΔI/A + Δ/A)

As the polygon grows: ΔI/A → 1 and Δ/A → 0, giving B/A = P/A → 2/Δ.

The law P/A = 2/Δ is Pick's theorem read as a statement about the lattice's intrinsic geometry.
Square lattice

Square lattice ℤ²
P/A → 2

On the square lattice, the Chebyshev (king-move) metric assigns cost 1 to each of the 8 neighbour directions. Every boundary lattice point contributes one step, so P = B.

Pick's theorem here is A = I + B/2 − 1. Rearranging with P = B:

A = I + P/2 − 1  ⟹  P = 2(A − I + 1)

The ratio P/A depends on how much of the interior is filled:

P/A = 2(1 − I/A + 1/A)

As the polygon grows and I/A → 1:

P/A → 2
What P = B means geometrically: on the square lattice, every adjacent pair of boundary lattice points is connected by exactly one king-move — a step of length 1 in the Chebyshev metric, regardless of direction (horizontal, vertical, or diagonal). So the boundary point count and the Chebyshev perimeter are the same number.
Eisenstein lattice

Eisenstein lattice ℤ[ω]
P/A → 4/√3

On the Eisenstein lattice the basis vectors meet at 60°, giving a triangular grid. Each lattice point has 6 nearest neighbours (not 8), and the hex metric assigns cost 1 to each of these six directions.

The fundamental domain has area Δ = √3/2. Pick's theorem becomes:

A = (√3/2) · (I + B/2 − 1)  ⟹  P/A = (4/√3)(1 − I·Δ/A + Δ/A)

As the polygon grows:

P/A → 4/√3 = 4√3/3 ≈ 2.309
Why 4/√3 and not 2/√3? The factor comes from 2/Δ = 2/(√3/2) = 4/√3. The denominator √3/2 is the fundamental domain area — the area of the rhombus spanned by the two Eisenstein basis vectors. Pick's formula divides by Δ, and P/A inherits that reciprocal.

P/A = (2/Δ)·(1 − ΔI/A + Δ/A). The interior fill ΔI/A drives everything. When ΔI/A → 1 (large, interior-dense polygon), P/A → 2/Δ from below. Small or boundary-heavy polygons have low ΔI/A and sit well below the limit — not because they are close to it asymptotically, but because their interior is sparse. Non-convex polygons waste boundary without enclosing interior, keeping ΔI/A low. To approach the limit: make the polygon large and convex.
Convex polygons

Convex polygons
the surprise reverses

For generic polygons P/A → 2/Δ is higher on the Eisenstein lattice. But restrict to convex polygons and optimise — the ordering reverses.

Why convex polygons are different

For a convex lattice polygon, boundary growth is constrained. Andrews (1963) proved:

B = O(A1/3) for convex lattice polygons

This means for convex polygons, B grows much slower than A. So P/A → 0 as the polygon grows on either lattice. The 2/Δ limit applies only to generic (possibly non-convex, boundary-heavy) polygons.

Optimal isoperimetric ratio P²/A

The right comparison for convex polygons is the isoperimetric ratio P²/A (lower is closer to a circle). The optimal convex lattice polygon uses all available boundary directions — 8 on the square lattice, 12 on the Eisenstein lattice.

The reversal explained: more boundary directions → better circle approximation → lower P²/A. The Eisenstein lattice has 12 available directions (6 unit vectors + 6 combinations) against the square lattice's 8. So the optimal Eisenstein polygon is actually closer to a circle, not further. The Eisenstein lattice is simultaneously worse on average (higher P/A generically) and better at best (lower P²/A for optimal convex shapes).

The triangular lattice is geometrically superior for enclosing area — but only when you restrict to convex shapes and optimise. Its density advantage in the generic case is outweighed by the proliferation of boundary-heavy non-convex configurations. The law P/A = 2/Δ penalises Eisenstein for the same density that makes it efficient, because density also multiplies the ways to waste boundary.
