Kepler, Bode, and Milankovitch

Three astronomy-rich task sequences designed to de-flatten knowledge: not just spotting patterns, but asking what kind of warrant the pattern carries, what assumptions the model hides, and how theory is reconstructed from difficult data.

Mathematics begins where pattern stops being enough. In science, models hide assumptions; in mathematics, patterns hide warrants.

Milankovitch

Cycles without a simple picture

Orbital eccentricity, obliquity, and precession vary on different timescales. Here the challenge is not to spot a single law but to understand how several cycles combine, interfere, and shift climatic forcing over long intervals.

A useful prompt: unlike Kepler's relation, this is not one clean dependency but a composite of interacting periodicities.

Task sequence A: Kepler from data

1. Pattern before proof Describe what seems to happen to period as distance increases. Why is this not yet a law?
2. Reconstruct the warrant Use the log-log plot to estimate the slope. Why does a straight line on log-log axes suggest a power law rather than a random trend?
3. Historical pressure Kepler did not have logarithms at the start. What does that tell you about the difference between discovery and later formalisation?
4. Epistemic hinge If the exponent came out as 1.48 rather than exactly 1.50, would that weaken the law, the data, or the model linking them?

Task sequence B: Bode under suspicion

1. Fit or fiction? Explain why a tidy numerical pattern can be persuasive even when it lacks deep theoretical support.
2. Historical reconstruction What would count as evidence that Bode's law is more than a numerical convenience?
3. Missing planet logic Why was the apparent gap near 2.8 AU historically exciting? Does later discovery of Ceres vindicate the law or merely tempt us to think so?
4. Epistemic hinge When does successful prediction count as explanation, and when is it only pattern-matching?

Task sequence C: Milankovitch and complexity

1. More than one rhythm Compare the three curves. Which varies most smoothly? Which looks most phase-shifted?
2. Composite causation Why is it harder to teach Milankovitch cycles as a single law than Kepler's third law?
3. Model under pressure If climatic data did not line up neatly with one orbital cycle, would you reject the theory, revise the forcing mechanism, or look for lag and feedback?
4. Epistemic hinge Does complexity weaken explanation, or does it simply force us to give up on one-variable stories?

Teacher bridge

These questions work because they move students from seeing pattern to asking what licenses the pattern. In Kepler, a relation emerges from messy celestial data and later becomes mathematically transparent. In Bode, an elegant regularity invites belief but may outrun its warrant. In Milankovitch, the difficulty is different again: not one law, but interacting cycles whose explanatory force depends on timing, feedback, and proxy interpretation.

Provocation: exercises fit rubrics; questions resist them. A good task here should leave the student unsure whether they have discovered a law, recognised a fit, or only learned how easily one can slide from one to the other.
