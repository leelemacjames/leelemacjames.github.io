Pattern, Warrant, and Law

Kepler, Bode, and Milankovitch — three cases where the data draws you in and the epistemology pulls you back
Mathematics begins where pattern stops being enough.

These tasks are not exercises. They are questions. Exercises fit rubrics; questions resist them. Each presents real astronomical data and asks not just what pattern emerges, but what kind of warrant the pattern carries, what assumptions the model hides, and at what point a fit becomes a law — or fails to.

Task 1 · Kepler
From planetary data to a claim
▶

Here is the data Kepler inherited. Describe what seems to happen to period as distance increases.

  1. Is the relationship linear? How can you tell from the shape of the curve?
  2. If you were Kepler — no logarithms, no computers — how would you test whether this is a power law?
  3. At what point does "it seems to grow faster" become a mathematical claim?
Distance from Sun (AU) vs orbital period (Earth years). Data from Tycho Brahe via Kepler (1619).
The epistemic point
The curve suggests a power law, but suggestion is not warrant. Kepler had to work by ratio — computing T/a, T²/a³, testing which combination held constant across all six planets. He did this entirely by hand, without logarithms (Napier published them too late for Kepler's key work). The modern student who sees this data on a screen and "knows" it's T² = a³ has retrieved a result without reconstructing the reasoning that licenses it.
Familiarity masquerades as understanding.
Task 2 · Kepler
The log-log reveal
▶

The same data, transformed. Both axes are now logarithmic.

  1. Why does a straight line on log-log axes indicate a power law?
  2. The slope comes out as 1.499. Is that 3/2, or merely close to it? What would you need to decide?
  3. If the slope were 1.48 instead, would that weaken the law, the data, or the model linking them?
ln(a) vs ln(T). Slope of best-fit line shown. Modern data including Pluto.
The epistemic point
The log-log transformation is not a neutral act — it is a theoretical commitment. You take logs because you suspect a power law; the straight line confirms the suspicion. But the warrant for claiming T² = a³ exactly (not T^1.998 = a^3) comes from Newton's derivation, not from the data alone. The data is consistent with the law; it does not prove the exponent is rational. Students should distinguish resemblance from rule.
Results are path-independent; meaning is not.
Task 3 · Bode
Pattern, fit, or law?
▶

Bode's rule: d = 0.4 + 0.3 × 2m. It predicts planetary distances with eerie accuracy — for some planets.

  1. Examine the table. Where does Bode's rule work well? Where does it fail?
  2. The gap at 2.8 AU predicted a missing planet. Ceres was later found there. Does this vindicate the law, or merely tempt us to think so?
  3. What would count as evidence that Bode's rule is more than a numerical convenience?
  4. Can a rule be useful without being true?

The epistemic point
Bode's rule is the epistemological opposite of Kepler's law. Kepler's law has a derivation — Newton showed it follows from the inverse-square law. Bode's rule has only a pattern. It fits the inner planets beautifully, predicted Ceres, and then fails catastrophically for Neptune and beyond. A student who sees only the successes will call it a law. A student who sees Neptune will call it a coincidence. The interesting question is: what kind of thing sits between law and coincidence?
Knowledge without provenance is indistinguishable from coincidence.
Task 4 · Kepler extended
Does the law travel?
▶

Kepler's law was discovered for planets orbiting the Sun. Here are Jupiter's four Galilean moons.

  1. If Kepler's law is truly a law, it should hold here too. Does it?
  2. What quantity changes between the solar system and the Jovian system?
  3. Newton used this to estimate Jupiter's mass. What assumption is he making?
  4. When does a pattern that "works again" strengthen a law, and when is it just the same fit wearing different clothes?

The epistemic point
A pattern that replicates in a new system is stronger than one that merely fits the original data — but only if the replication is independent. Newton's insight was that the same inverse-square law governs both systems, so T² ∝ a³ must hold for any central-force orbit. The Kepler constant changes (because the central mass changes), but the exponent is fixed by the physics. This is what separates Kepler from Bode: one has a derivation that predicts its universality; the other has only a pattern that happens to recur.
If your method cannot fail, your knowledge cannot grow.
Task 5 · Milankovitch
Cycles without a simple picture
▶

Earth's orbital eccentricity, axial tilt, and precession each vary on different timescales. Toggle the cycles below.

  1. Can you identify a single dominant period? Why or why not?
  2. Unlike Kepler's law, there is no single clean dependency here. What makes composite models harder to falsify?
  3. If the model has three free parameters (one per cycle), at what point does a fit become overfitting?
     
Normalised Milankovitch cycles. Not one law — interacting periodicities.
The epistemic point
Milankovitch cycles represent a fundamentally different epistemic challenge. Kepler gives you one clean relationship to test. Bode gives you a fit to evaluate. Milankovitch gives you a composite of interacting cycles whose explanatory force depends on timing, feedback, and proxy interpretation. A student who can handle Kepler and dismiss Bode may still be defeated by the honest complexity of climate forcing — where no single test confirms or refutes the model.
Good tasks withhold closure.
Provocation: exercises fit rubrics; questions resist them. A good task here should leave the student unsure whether they have discovered a law, recognised a fit, or only learned how easily one can slide from one to the other. They should have the scars of the practitioner, not just the vocabulary of the skeptic.
