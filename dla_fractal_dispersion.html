Diffusion-Limited Aggregation

Modeling Radioactive Particle Dispersion with Fractal Geometry

What is Diffusion-Limited Aggregation (DLA)?

DLA models how particles disperse when they undergo random walks until they encounter a cluster and stick to it. The key parameters that control the fractal structure are:

  • p (Sticking Probability): When a particle contacts the cluster, it sticks with probability p. Lower p means particles "explore" more before sticking, creating denser clusters (higher D). Higher p creates more branching (lower D, typically D ≈ 1.71).
  • ℓ (Step Size): The distance a particle moves in each random walk step. Larger ℓ simulates stronger turbulence, creating more ballistic trajectories and affecting the screening effect that produces fractality.
  • N (Number of Particles): Total particles to add. More particles give better statistical measurement of the fractal dimension D.
  • R₀ (Launch Radius): Distance from the source where particles begin their random walk. Must be larger than the cluster radius to allow proper diffusion.
  • λ (Decay Constant): Probability per step that a radioactive particle decays. The survival probability after n steps is e-λn. Models real nuclear decay during dispersion. Set to 0 to disable decay, or use small values like 0.0001 for realistic effects.

Unlike uniform circular dispersion (D = 2.0), DLA produces clusters with a fractal dimension of approximately D ≈ 1.71 in 2D, creating the irregular, branching patterns observed in real contamination events like Chernobyl and Fukushima.

The Mathematics

The fractal dimension is measured using the box-counting method:

\[D = \lim_{\epsilon \to 0} \frac{\log N(\epsilon)}{\log(1/\epsilon)}\]

where N(ε) is the number of boxes of size ε needed to cover the cluster.

For DLA clusters, the mass scales with radius as:

\[M(r) \propto r^D \quad \text{where } D \approx 1.71\]

This means particles are distributed much more sparsely than in a filled circle (D = 2), creating the characteristic "feathery" branching structure.

Real-World Applications

  • Chernobyl (1986): Atmospheric plume showed fractal clustering with D ≈ 1.7-1.9 due to turbulent mixing and particle aggregation
  • Fukushima (2011): Ocean dispersion exhibited branching patterns along current boundaries with D ≈ 1.6-1.8
  • Nuclear weapons testing: Stratospheric fallout created DLA-like patterns as particles aggregated during descent
  • Groundwater contamination: Flow through heterogeneous porous media produces fractal concentration patterns
