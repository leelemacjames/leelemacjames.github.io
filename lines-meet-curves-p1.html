Three Kinds of Number

Every number lives somewhere on a line, but not all numbers are built the same way.

Integers are the counting numbers (and their negatives). Rationals are every fraction of two integers. Irrationals are everything else: numbers whose decimal expansion never terminates, never repeats. How can one tell which type a geometric construction produces?

The Quadratic and Its Discriminant

When a line meets a curve of degree two (a conic section), the intersection points satisfy a quadratic equation. The general standard form is:

ax² + bx + c = 0

The solutions come from the quadratic formula:

x = (−b ± √(b² − 4ac)) / 2a

The expression under the square root is the discriminant:

Δ = b² − 4ac
When is the discriminant a perfect square? What kind of number does the formula produce when it is, and when it is not?

If Δ is a perfect square (0, 1, 4, 9, 16, ...), the square root is an integer, and the formula produces rational roots. If Δ is positive but not a perfect square, √Δ is irrational, and so are the roots. The discriminant is the gatekeeper between the rational and irrational worlds.

The Unit Circle: Rational Intersections

The unit circle is x² + y² = 1. The point (−1, 0) lies on it. A line of slope m through that point is:

y = m(x + 1)
One intersection is always (−1, 0). Where is the other? What kind of number is it?

Substitute into x² + y² = 1:

x² + m²(x+1)² = 1
(1+m²)x² + 2m²x + (m²−1) = 0

This factors as (x + 1) times a linear remainder, because x = −1 is guaranteed to be a root. The other root is:

x = (1 − m²) / (1 + m²)    y = 2m / (1 + m²)

If m = p/q is rational, then x and y are rational. Clear denominators: every Pythagorean triple emerges. The known root kills the quadratic, preventing any square root from appearing. Rational in, rational out.

The Rectangular Hyperbola: Irrational Intersections

Now replace the circle with the simplest rectangular hyperbola:

xy = 1

And cut it with the family of lines y = x − k, where k is a positive integer.

Substitute y = x − k into xy = 1. What quadratic appears? What does its discriminant look like?
x(x − k) = 1  ⟹  x² − kx − 1 = 0

Here a = 1, b = −k, c = −1. The discriminant is:

Δ = k² − 4(1)(−1) = k² + 4

Is k² + 4 ever a perfect square for integer k? Try it: 1+4=5, 4+4=8, 9+4=13, 16+4=20, 25+4=29... never a perfect square. So √Δ is always irrational, and the metallic means are born.

Every metallic mean Mk has a conjugate: the other root of the same quadratic. Their product is always −1 (from Vieta's formulas: product of roots = c/a = −1/1). The conjugate lives on the negative branch of the hyperbola, a mirror through the origin.
