The Language of Floating

Why the words we use about buoyancy shape — and sometimes distort — what we think we understand.

You open a textbook and read: "The upthrust acts on the object to make it float."
But does upthrust act? Does the object get made to do anything?
And where did the equation go? Is this physics, or is this a story?

SECTION 01But This Is Maths?

When you first meet a physics equation, something strange happens. You've been doing maths for years — rearranging, substituting, graphing. Then suddenly the letters change. Instead of x and y, you get ρ and g and V. The equals sign is the same. The algebra is the same. So why does it feel different?

What is the difference between x = 3y + 2 and F = ρVg?
Think about this before you reveal the answer...

Mathematically — nothing. Both are linear relationships. The algebra works identically.

The difference is that F = ρVg has words attached. Each symbol names something in the physical world: a force, a density, a volume, a gravitational field. And those words carry baggage — metaphors, everyday associations, implied stories about why things happen.

The equation is maths. The difficulty is the language wrapped around it.

The philosopher Ludwig Wittgenstein warned that confusion often comes not from the ideas themselves, but from misapplying words outside the contexts that give them meaning. In physics, words like "force," "push," "resist," and "act" feel familiar — but their meaning in equations is far narrower and more precise than in everyday speech.

Nancy Cartwright went further: the laws of physics, she argued, don't describe the raw world. They describe idealised models — simplified stages where the messy complexity of reality has been stripped away so the mathematics can get a foothold.

The Key Insight

Physics equations are not difficult because the maths is hard. They're difficult because the words surrounding them tell stories — stories of purpose, agency, and intent — that the equations themselves never claimed.

SECTION 02The Words We Use

Every word below has an everyday meaning and a physics meaning. They overlap just enough to be dangerous. Click each card to see Wittgenstein's warning about the gap.

Pressure
Everyday: stress, urgency, being squeezed
Physics: force per unit area, P = F/A
In physics, pressure is a ratio — how force is distributed across a surface. It has no opinion, no urgency. It doesn't "build up" with intention.
Wittgenstein: "Pressure" in everyday speech implies something being pressed — an agent doing the pressing. In physics, it's a calculated ratio. The word carries the ghost of a hand pushing.
tap
Upthrust
Everyday: being pushed upward, lifted
Physics: net upward force from pressure difference
Upthrust is a consequence of pressure increasing with depth. The bottom of an object experiences more pressure than the top. The net result: a force directed upwards. Nobody is pushing.
Wittgenstein: "Thrust" implies a thruster — something doing the thrusting. But upthrust is a pressure gradient, not an agent. The word smuggles in a hidden actor.
tap
Displacement
Everyday: being moved from your place, refugees
Physics: volume of fluid shifted by an immersed object
When an object enters a fluid, the fluid that was there has to go somewhere. The volume it vacates is the displacement. Archimedes connected this to the buoyant force.
Wittgenstein: "Displaced" implies the fluid has been wronged, moved against its will. Mechanistically, molecules simply redistribute according to the container geometry.
tap
Buoyancy
Everyday: cheerfulness, optimism, being "buoyed up"
Physics: the net upward force on an object in a fluid
Buoyancy is the name we give to the result of a pressure difference acting on a submerged surface. It is not a separate force with its own mechanism — it is the pressure difference.
Wittgenstein: We say "the water buoys the object" as if the water has a supportive personality. The mechanism is pressure arithmetic.
tap
Floating
Everyday: drifting, weightless, effortless
Physics: equilibrium when buoyant force = weight
Floating is not the absence of forces — it's their balance. The object doesn't "choose" to float. It sinks until it displaces enough fluid for the upward pressure force to equal the gravitational pull downward.
Wittgenstein: "It floats" sounds passive, as if the object is doing nothing. In fact, the object has settled into a dynamic equilibrium — a very particular depth where two quantities match.
tap
Density
Everyday: thick, crowded, "dense" as in slow-witted
Physics: mass per unit volume, ρ = m/V
Density is a ratio — how much mass is packed into a given volume. It doesn't describe a substance's essence or character. It's a model parameter that depends on temperature, pressure, and phase.
Wittgenstein: Calling something "dense" feels like naming a permanent property. But density changes with temperature. Cartwright would say: density is an effective parameter, not an ontological label.

SECTION 03The Equations — Just Maths, with Labels

Every equation below is algebra you already know how to do. The only new thing is what the letters stand for. Let's strip away the story and look at the structure.

P = ρ × g × h
Pressure at depth h in a fluid of density ρ, with gravitational field strength g
Why does pressure increase with depth?
Don't reach for "because the water pushes harder" — that's a story. What does the equation actually say?

The equation says P is proportional to h. Deeper means more fluid above, which means more weight of fluid per unit area pressing down. No agent is pushing — it's just gravity acting on the column of fluid above.

Cartwright would note: this equation "lies beautifully" — it assumes uniform density, incompressible fluid, and a uniform gravitational field. All idealisations. All useful. None literally true.

Fb = ρfluid × Vdisplaced × g
Archimedes' Principle — the buoyant force equals the weight of fluid displaced
Where does this "force" come from? Is it a new kind of force?
Is buoyancy something separate from pressure — or is it just pressure in disguise?

Buoyancy is not a separate force. It's the net result of pressure acting on all surfaces of the submerged object. The bottom face is deeper, so it experiences higher pressure than the top. The difference is a net upward force. That's all buoyancy is.

The equation Fb = ρVg is a shortcut — a compressed statement of the full pressure integral. It's the same maths, just tidied up with Archimedes' insight that the net effect equals the weight of displaced fluid.

Object floats when: ρobject < ρfluid
The floating condition — density comparison, nothing more
Common Examiner Error

Students write: "The object floats because it is light." This confuses mass with density. A steel ship is enormously heavy but floats because its average density (including the air inside) is less than water's. An iron nail is light but sinks because its density exceeds 1000 kg/m³. Floating is about density ratio, not weight.

SECTION 04Stripping the Intent

Following Dennett's distinction: when you hear a "why" question about buoyancy, ask yourself — am I being asked "how is that?" (mechanism) or "for what reason?" (purpose). Physics only answers the first. Here are common statements, with the teleological language crossed out and the mechanism offered instead.

Teleological Statement Mechanistic Translation
Water pushes the object upward Pressure on the lower surface exceeds pressure on the upper surface; the net force is directed upward.
The object wants to float The object's average density is less than the fluid's; it displaces enough fluid before full submersion for buoyant force to equal weight.
Upthrust acts on the object to keep it afloat The net upward force due to the pressure gradient matches the gravitational force downward — equilibrium.
Dense objects prefer to sink When object density exceeds fluid density, weight exceeds buoyant force at full submersion; net downward force accelerates the object.
The fluid resists the object entering it Immersion requires work against the pressure field; fluid molecules redistribute rather than "resist."
Archimedes discovered why things float Archimedes established a model: the buoyant force equals the weight of displaced fluid. This describes how floating works within that model, not why it happens in any ultimate sense.
Dennett's Lens

None of these objects have reasons. They have densities, volumes, and positions in a pressure field. Every "why does it float?" is really a "how is it that it floats?" — and the answer is always: because the numbers balance.

SECTION 06Check Your Language

Each question below tests whether you can distinguish mechanism from metaphor — the how is that? from the for what reason?

1. Which statement is mechanistic (not teleological)?
The correct answer avoids agency ("supports," "needs") and describes the force balance in terms of measurable quantities: pressure difference and gravitational force.
2. A student writes: "The steel ball sinks because it is heavy." What is wrong with this?
Mass alone doesn't determine floating. A 100,000-tonne aircraft carrier floats. A 5-gram iron ball sinks. The relevant quantity is density — mass per unit volume — compared to the fluid.
3. "Upthrust pushes the object up." What would Wittgenstein say about this?
Wittgenstein's point is that "push" implies an actor — a hand, a thruster, an agent with intent. But upthrust is an emergent consequence of pressure varying with depth. The word "push" tells a story the equation doesn't.
4. Cartwright would say that Archimedes' principle:
Cartwright doesn't say laws are wrong — she says they "lie beautifully." Archimedes' principle works within its model. But real fluids have viscosity, turbulence, temperature gradients, and compressibility. The law is an idealisation — powerful, useful, and not the whole truth.
5. "For what reason does ice float?" — which type of question is this (Dennett)?
Dennett would note: "For what reason does ice float?" invites an answer about purpose (e.g., "to insulate aquatic life"). The mechanistic rephrasing — "How is it that ice floats?" — gets the answer: hydrogen bonds form a hexagonal lattice less dense than liquid water. Both answers are useful, but only one is physics.
