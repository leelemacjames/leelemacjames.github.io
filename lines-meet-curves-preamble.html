What Does "Solving an Equation" Look Like?

Consider the parabola y = x² − 4x + 3. It is a curve in the plane. Every point on it satisfies that rule: plug in an x, compute y, plot the point.

Now ask: for which values of x does x² − 4x + 3 equal zero? That is, where does the curve cross the x-axis? The x-axis is just the horizontal line y = 0. So "solving x² − 4x + 3 = 0" is the same question as "where does the parabola meet the line y = 0?"

But there is nothing special about y = 0. The line could sit at any height.

If the parabola is y = x² + bx + c and the line is y = k, what equation do the crossing points satisfy?

At a crossing point, the parabola and the line give the same y-value. So:

x² + bx + c = k  ⟹  x² + bx + (c − k) = 0

Raising the line (increasing k) changes the constant term of the equation being solved. The shape of the parabola stays the same. Only the question "where does it cross?" changes.

What "monic" means

The quadratic x² + bx + c is called monic: the coefficient of x² is 1. This is not a restriction. Any quadratic ax² + bx + c with a ≠ 0 can be divided through by a to become monic: x² + (b/a)x + (c/a). Monic form simplifies everything that follows, because the leading coefficient disappears from every formula.

As k increases from below the vertex to above it, what happens to the crossings?

When the line sits below the vertex, there are no crossings: the discriminant is negative and the roots are complex. As the line rises to touch the vertex, the two crossings merge into one: the line is tangent to the parabola, and the discriminant is exactly zero. Lift the line above the vertex and two crossings appear, moving apart. The discriminant has become positive.

The vertical position of the line controls the discriminant. This is the geometric content of the quantity Δ = b² − 4(c − k) = b² − 4c + 4k. Raising k by 1 increases Δ by 4.

Vieta: Sum and Product as Geometry

Stay with the monic quadratic. If its two roots are x₁ and x₂, then the quadratic factors as (x − x₁)(x − x₂) = 0. Expand:

x² − (x₁ + x₂)x + x₁x₂ = 0

Compare with x² + Bx + C = 0. Reading off coefficients:

x₁ + x₂ = −B   (the sum of the roots)
x₁ · x₂ = C   (the product of the roots)

These are Vieta's formulas, discovered by François Viète in the 1590s. They say something remarkable: without knowing the individual roots, the coefficients of the quadratic already encode their sum and product.

Can the equations x₁ + x₂ = s and x₁ · x₂ = p be drawn as curves in their own right?

Yes. Use coordinates (u, v) to represent the two roots. Then u + v = s is a straight line (slope −1, intercept s). And uv = p is a rectangular hyperbola. The roots of x² − sx + p = 0 are the coordinates of the points where these two curves cross:

roots of x² − sx + p = 0  ⇔  line u + v = s meets hyperbola uv = p

The two intersection points are (x₁, x₂) and (x₂, x₁): the same pair of roots, just swapped between the axes. When the line misses the hyperbola entirely, the roots are complex.

The Discriminant Is a Distance Test

For the general quadratic ax² + bx + c = 0, the two roots sit symmetrically around the midpoint:

−b / 2a

The half-distance between them is controlled by the square root of the discriminant:

x = −b/2a ± √(b² − 4ac) / 2a
So what does Δ = b² − 4ac actually measure?

It measures separation:

  • Δ > 0: two real intersections, separated by 2·√Δ/(2|a|).
  • Δ = 0: one repeated intersection: the line is tangent to the curve.
  • Δ < 0: no real intersection; the roots are complex conjugates.
  • Δ a perfect square (and a, b, c rational): roots are rational.
  • Δ positive but not a perfect square: roots are irrational.

The discriminant is the gatekeeper. It decides not only whether intersections exist, but what kind of number they are.

Circle: A Known Intersection Collapses the Quadratic

The unit circle x² + y² = 1 meets the pencil of lines through (−1, 0):

y = m(x + 1)

The point (−1, 0) already sits on the circle. So one root of the intersection quadratic is known before any calculation begins: x = −1. The quadratic must contain the factor (x + 1). What remains after dividing it out is linear: a single equation in x with no square root needed.

Why does a rational slope m guarantee rational crossing coordinates?

Because the quadratic has been reduced to a linear equation, and linear equations with rational coefficients always have rational solutions. The formula is:

x = (1 − m²) / (1 + m²),   y = 2m / (1 + m²)

Rational input m gives rational output (x, y). Clear denominators: the homogeneous coordinates become a Pythagorean triple.

Hyperbola: Metallic Means Keep the Quadratic

For the rectangular hyperbola xy = 1, cut by the line y = x − k:

x(x − k) = 1  ⟹  x² − kx − 1 = 0

No known root this time. The line does not pass through any point already on the curve. The full quadratic survives:

x = k/2 ± √(k² + 4) / 2
For positive integer k, is k² + 4 ever a perfect square?

No. For k ≥ 2, the value k² + 4 sits strictly between k² and (k+1)² = k² + 2k + 1, and since 4 < 2k + 1 for k ≥ 2, there is no integer whose square equals k² + 4. For k = 1, Δ = 5: not a square. The roots are always irrational. These are the metallic means: golden (k=1), silver (k=2), bronze (k=3), and so on.

What to carry into Part I

Solving a quadratic is finding where a line crosses a curve. The discriminant decides whether the crossings exist, and what kind of number they produce. Vieta turns a quadratic into a line and a hyperbola. A known root on the curve collapses the quadratic to linear (circle → rational). No known root leaves the full quadratic alive (hyperbola → irrational). These two structures generate Pythagorean triples and metallic means respectively.
