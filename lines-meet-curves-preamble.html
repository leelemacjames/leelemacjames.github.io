The Basic Question

A quadratic equation is often taught as a formula. Geometrically, it is simpler: a straight line meets a degree-two curve. The two roots are the two intersection points seen from the x-axis.

Why should a straight line meeting a curve give an equation at all?

A curve imposes one rule. A line imposes another. A point of intersection must satisfy both at once. Substituting the line into the curve removes y and leaves a single equation in x. If the curve has degree two, that equation is quadratic.

Vieta: Sum and Product as Geometry

Write a monic quadratic as

x² + Bx + C = 0

If its roots are x₁ and x₂, then

x₁ + x₂ = −B,   x₁x₂ = C
Can the equations x₁ + x₂ = s and x₁x₂ = p be drawn?

Yes. Use coordinates (u,v) for the two roots. The condition u+v=s is a straight line. The condition uv=p is a rectangular hyperbola. So Vieta says:

roots of x² − sx + p = 0   ⇔   line u+v=s meets hyperbola uv=p

The two intersection coordinates are the two roots, just swapped: (x₁,x₂) and (x₂,x₁).

The Discriminant Is a Distance Test

For

ax² + bx + c = 0

the two roots sit symmetrically around the midpoint

−b/2a

The half-distance between the roots is controlled by the square root of the discriminant:

x = −b/2a ± √(b² − 4ac)/2a
So what does Δ = b² − 4ac measure?

It measures whether the two intersections are visible on the real plane, merged into a tangent point, or absent from the real plane.

  • Δ > 0: two real intersections.
  • Δ = 0: one repeated intersection: the line is tangent.
  • Δ < 0: no real intersection; the roots are complex.
  • Δ a perfect square: rational roots, provided a,b,c are integers and the division works rationally.

Circle: A Known Intersection Collapses the Quadratic

The unit circle meets the pencil of lines through (−1,0):

x² + y² = 1,   y = m(x+1)

Because (−1,0) is already on the circle, one root is known before calculation begins. The quadratic must contain the factor (x+1). What remains is linear.

Why does this produce rational points when m is rational?
x = (1−m²)/(1+m²),   y = 2m/(1+m²)

Rational input m gives rational output x,y. Clear denominators and the homogeneous coordinates become Pythagorean triples.

Hyperbola: Metallic Means Keep the Quadratic

For the rectangular hyperbola

xy = 1

cut by the line

y = x − k

substitution gives

x(x−k)=1   ⟹   x² − kx − 1 = 0

The roots are

x = k/2 ± √(k²+4)/2
Why does this usually give irrational roots?

For positive integer k, the discriminant is Δ = k²+4. It sits just above k² and below (k+1)² for k ≥ 2, so it cannot be a square. For k=1, Δ=5. Thus the roots are quadratic irrationals: the metallic mean and its conjugate.

What to carry into Part I

Finding roots is not merely algebraic manipulation. It is intersection geometry. Vieta turns a quadratic into a line and a hyperbola. The discriminant tells whether the intersections separate, merge, or disappear from the real plane. Perfect-square discriminants give rational arithmetic; non-square discriminants give quadratic irrational arithmetic.
