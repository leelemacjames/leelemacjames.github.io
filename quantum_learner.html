Nuclear Structure & Quantum Worlds

From Rutherford's gold foil to de Broglie's matter waves — the story of how physics learned that nature refuses to be only one thing at a time.

What this section is about

The quantum world is built on a handful of unsettling discoveries. Early 20th-century physicists found that energy comes in packets (quanta), that light behaves as both wave and particle, and that matter — even cricket balls — has an associated wavelength. None of this was expected. Classical physics didn't break gradually; it broke suddenly, and in specific, repeatable experiments.

Don't Forget: Conservation Laws in Collisions

Before you enter the quantum world, anchor yourself in what always holds. These conservation laws underpin nuclear scattering, Compton scattering, and every collision problem you'll meet:

Momentum is conserved in all collisions (elastic and inelastic), in every direction. This is non-negotiable: Σp_before = Σp_after.

Kinetic energy is conserved only in elastic collisions. In inelastic collisions, some KE converts to other forms (heat, deformation, radiation). Total energy is still conserved — it's just redistributed.

Elastic

Both momentum and KE conserved. Billiard balls (approximately), Compton photon–electron scattering, Rutherford scattering at large distances.

Inelastic

Momentum conserved, KE not conserved. Car crashes, nuclear reactions where mass-energy converts, particle creation/annihilation. Perfectly inelastic = objects stick together.

In Compton scattering (E12), you apply both conservation laws simultaneously: the photon transfers momentum and energy to the electron, and the wavelength shift is the measurable consequence. The maths only works because both laws hold.

SL E1 — Quantum Theory & Wave-Particle Duality

Quantum theory describes physics at the atomic and subatomic scale — where energy is quantised (discrete, not continuous) and particles show both wave-like and particle-like behaviour depending on the experiment.

Two foundational equations anchor everything:

ΔE = hf     (Planck equation)
c = fλ     (wave speed relation)

Where h = 6.626 × 10⁻³⁴ J·s (Planck's constant), f is frequency in Hz, c = 3.00 × 10⁸ m/s, and λ is wavelength. These two equations together let you move freely between energy, frequency, and wavelength.

Q1 What does it mean to say energy is "quantised"? ▶
Answer

Energy can only take specific, discrete values — not any arbitrary value on a continuous spectrum. An electron in an atom can have energy E₁ or E₂, but nothing in between. This was radical: classical physics assumed energy was continuous.

Q2 A photon has frequency 5.0 × 10¹⁴ Hz. What is its energy in eV? ▶
Answer

E = hf = 6.626 × 10⁻³⁴ × 5.0 × 10¹⁴ = 3.31 × 10⁻¹⁹ J.

Convert: 3.31 × 10⁻¹⁹ ÷ 1.602 × 10⁻¹⁹ ≈ 2.07 eV.

SL E2 — Geiger-Marsden-Rutherford Gold Foil Experiment

Alpha particles (⁴He²⁺ nuclei) were fired at thin gold foil. Most passed straight through — atoms are mostly empty space. A few deflected at large angles, and some bounced back entirely. This demanded a nuclear model: a tiny, dense, positively charged nucleus surrounded by orbiting electrons.

This demolished Thomson's "plum pudding" model, where positive charge was thought to be uniformly distributed. Follow-up experiments (deep inelastic scattering) later revealed that nucleons themselves are composite — made of quarks.

How do we know what we can't see? The Rutherford experiment is a paradigm case of indirect evidence. No one observed the nucleus directly. The inference came from scattering patterns — the same logic used in modern particle physics at CERN. What counts as "seeing" in physics?

Q1 Why did most alpha particles pass through the foil undeflected? ▶
Answer

Because atoms are overwhelmingly empty space. The nucleus is ~10⁻¹⁵ m across while the atom is ~10⁻¹⁰ m. Most alphas never came close to a nucleus.

Q2 What does a large-angle deflection tell us about the nucleus? ▶
Answer

The nucleus must be dense and carry concentrated positive charge. Only a massive, charged object could deflect an energetic alpha particle through a large angle or back the way it came (via Coulomb repulsion).

SL E3 — Distance of Closest Approach

When an alpha particle heads directly at a gold nucleus, it decelerates as electrostatic potential energy increases. At closest approach, all kinetic energy has converted to potential energy and the particle is momentarily at rest.

Using conservation of energy:

½mαv² = kqαQAu / rc

rc = 4kZe² / (mαv²)

With qα = 2e, QAu = Ze (Z = 79 for gold), and Eα = 7.68 MeV, this gives rc ≈ 3.0 × 10⁻¹⁴ m. This provides an upper bound on the nuclear radius — the nucleus must be smaller than this.

Q1 Why does rc give an upper bound, not the actual nuclear radius? ▶
Answer

Because the alpha particle was stopped by electrostatic repulsion before making contact with the nucleus. The actual nuclear surface is somewhere inside rc. A more energetic alpha would get closer, reducing the bound.

Q2 Which conservation law is used in this derivation? ▶
Answer

Conservation of energy. Initial kinetic energy equals electrostatic potential energy at closest approach (KE → 0 at that point). No energy is lost — this is treated as an elastic Coulomb interaction.

AHL E4 — Nuclear Density & the Fermi Radius

Nuclear volume is proportional to the number of nucleons A. Modelling the nucleus as a sphere of radius R:

R = R₀ · A1/3     (Fermi radius formula)

Where R₀ ≈ 1.2 × 10⁻¹⁵ m is the Fermi radius constant. Since V = ⁴⁄₃πR³ = ⁴⁄₃πR₀³A, the volume scales linearly with A. The density then becomes:

ρ = A·u / V = 3u / (4πR₀³) ≈ 2.4 × 10¹⁷ kg m⁻³

This is independent of A — all nuclei have roughly the same density. This is strong evidence that the nuclear force is short-range and saturating: each nucleon only interacts with its nearest neighbours.

Q1 Why does the R = R₀A1/3 relation imply constant nuclear density? ▶
Answer

If R ∝ A1/3, then V = ⁴⁄₃πR³ ∝ A. Mass also ∝ A (since each nucleon has mass ≈ u). So ρ = mass/volume ∝ A/A = constant. Density doesn't depend on which nucleus you're looking at.

SL E5 — Photon Emission & Energy Level Transitions

When an electron drops from a higher energy level to a lower one, the atom emits a photon whose energy equals the gap between levels:

ΔE = hf    and    c = fλ
Combined: λ = hc / ΔE

Useful shortcut: hc = 1.24 × 10⁻⁶ eV·m. So for ΔE = 1.88 eV → λ = 1.24 × 10⁻⁶ / 1.88 ≈ 660 nm (red light).

The conversion factor 1 eV = 1.602 × 10⁻¹⁹ J is essential — learn it or you'll waste time in exams.

Q1 An atom emits a photon of wavelength 486 nm. What is the energy gap in eV? ▶
Answer

ΔE = hc/λ = 1.24 × 10⁻⁶ / (486 × 10⁻⁹) = 2.55 eV. This is in the blue-green part of the visible spectrum (it's actually the H-β Balmer line).

Q2 Why do atoms emit line spectra rather than a continuous spectrum? ▶
Answer

Because energy levels are quantised. Electrons can only exist in specific energy states, so the gaps between them are fixed. Each transition produces a photon of one specific frequency — hence discrete spectral lines, not a continuous band.

AHL E6 — Rydberg Formula & Spectral Series

The Rydberg formula empirically describes the wavelengths of hydrogen spectral lines:

1/λ = RH (1/k² − 1/n²)

Where RH = 1.097 × 10⁷ m⁻¹ is the Rydberg constant, k is the lower energy level, and n > k is the upper level.

SeriesknRegion
Lyman1≥ 2Ultraviolet
Balmer2≥ 3Visible
Paschen3≥ 4Infrared

The ground state is k = 1; ionisation corresponds to n → ∞.

Q1 Which series produces the most energetic photons? Why? ▶
Answer

Lyman series (k = 1). Transitions to the ground state involve the largest energy gaps, producing UV photons. The series limit (n → ∞ to n = 1) gives the ionisation energy of hydrogen: 13.6 eV.

AHL E7 — Bohr Model: Assumptions

Bohr's model of the hydrogen atom rests on four postulates:

  • Electrons exist in discrete orbits (stationary states) around the nucleus.
  • Electrons in stationary states do not radiate energy — they're stable.
  • Atoms emit or absorb energy only when electrons transition between states.
  • Angular momentum is quantised in integer multiples of ℏ = h/(2π).

This was a hybrid: classical circular orbits plus a quantum selection rule. It worked brilliantly for hydrogen, but fails for multi-electron atoms.

When is a model "wrong" but still useful? Bohr's model gives the right energy levels for hydrogen despite being fundamentally incorrect about orbits (quantum mechanics replaces them with probability clouds). The model is empirically adequate in a limited domain. How do we decide when to abandon a model?

Q1 Why can't classical mechanics explain the stability of atoms? ▶
Answer

Classically, an accelerating charge (electron in circular orbit) must radiate electromagnetic energy. It would spiral inward and collapse into the nucleus in ~10⁻¹¹ s. Bohr's postulate that stationary states don't radiate was ad hoc — it worked, but he couldn't explain why. That explanation waited for Schrödinger.

AHL E8 — Bohr Energy Levels & Standing Waves

The total energy of an electron in the nth level of hydrogen:

En = −13.6 / n²   eV

Negative energies indicate bound states. The angular momentum is quantised:

Ln = mvr = nh/(2π) = nℏ

The connection to de Broglie: if the electron has wavelength λ = h/(mv), then the orbit circumference must fit an integer number of wavelengths: 2πr = nλ. Rearranging reproduces Bohr's angular momentum condition. Electron orbits are standing wave patterns.

Q1 What is the physical meaning of the negative sign in En = −13.6/n² eV? ▶
Answer

It means the electron is bound to the nucleus. You'd need to add energy to free it. The zero of energy is defined at n → ∞ (ionisation). At n = 1, the electron is 13.6 eV below the ionisation threshold.

Q2 Why must 2πr = nλ for a stable orbit? ▶
Answer

A standing wave requires constructive interference with itself after one full trip around the orbit. If the circumference isn't an integer number of wavelengths, the wave destructively interferes with itself and the state is not stable. Only specific radii (and hence energies) are allowed.

AHL E9 — Orbital Radii and Speeds

For hydrogen-like atoms, the orbital radius scales as:

rn = (n²/Z) · a₀

Where a₀ = 5.29 × 10⁻¹¹ m (Bohr radius) and Z is the atomic number. Orbital speed is inversely proportional to radius: vn ∝ 1/rn.

So comparing n = 3 to n = 1: the radius is 9× larger, the speed is 3× smaller, and the electron is much less tightly bound.

Q1 For a hydrogen atom, what is the ratio v₃/v₁? ▶
Answer

Since vn ∝ 1/n (for hydrogen, Z = 1), we get v₃/v₁ = 1/3. The electron in n = 3 moves at one-third the speed of the ground-state electron.

AHL E10 — Ionisation Energy & Emitted Photon Frequency

Energy to ionise from level ni:

Eion = −13.6 (1/nf² − 1/ni²) eV

For ionisation, nf → ∞, so 1/nf² → 0. From n = 2: Eion = 13.6/4 = 3.4 eV. From n = 1 (ground state): 13.6 eV.

When an electron falls back, the emitted photon has frequency f = ΔE / h. Convert eV to joules first (or use the shortcut hc = 1.24 × 10⁻⁶ eV·m).

Q1 Calculate the frequency of a photon emitted when hydrogen transitions from n = 3 to n = 1. ▶
Answer

ΔE = 13.6(1 − 1/9) = 13.6 × 8/9 = 12.09 eV = 1.937 × 10⁻¹⁸ J.

f = ΔE/h = 1.937 × 10⁻¹⁸ / 6.626 × 10⁻³⁴ ≈ 2.92 × 10¹⁵ Hz (UV — this is Lyman series).

AHL E11 — Einstein's Photoelectric Equation

Light hits a metal surface. Below a threshold frequency, no electrons are emitted — regardless of intensity. Above threshold, electrons are emitted immediately, with maximum KE that depends on frequency, not intensity.

Emax = hf − Φ

Where Φ is the work function — the minimum energy to release an electron from the surface. Intensity affects how many electrons are emitted (more photons → more electrons), but each photon's energy hf determines whether it can release one.

ObservationInterpretation
Higher frequencyGreater KE of emitted electrons
Higher intensityMore electrons emitted (same KE each)
Below threshold fNo emission at all

What makes an explanation "scientific"? The photoelectric effect could not be explained by Maxwell's wave theory of light, which predicted that any frequency should eject electrons if you waited long enough (energy accumulates). Einstein's particle model made a testable, falsifiable prediction: a sharp frequency cutoff. Experimental confirmation (Millikan, 1916) shifted the paradigm. This is Popper in action — or is it? Millikan set out to disprove Einstein and ended up confirming him.

Q1 Why can't a classical wave model explain the photoelectric threshold frequency? ▶
Answer

In the wave model, energy is spread continuously across the wavefront. Even low-frequency light should eventually deliver enough energy to release an electron if you wait. But experiments show emission is instantaneous above threshold and non-existent below it, regardless of exposure time. This requires energy to arrive in discrete packets (photons), each carrying hf.

Q2 A metal has work function 2.3 eV. Light of frequency 7.0 × 10¹⁴ Hz shines on it. What is Emax? ▶
Answer

hf = 6.626 × 10⁻³⁴ × 7.0 × 10¹⁴ = 4.64 × 10⁻¹⁹ J = 2.90 eV.

Emax = 2.90 − 2.3 = 0.60 eV.

AHL E12 — Compton Effect: X-ray Scattering by Electrons

When X-rays scatter off electrons, the scattered wavelength is longer than the incident wavelength. This cannot be explained by classical wave theory (which predicts no wavelength change). The Compton formula:

Δλ = λS − λi = (h / mec)(1 − cos θ)

The quantity h/(mec) = 2.43 × 10⁻¹² m is the Compton wavelength of the electron. The shift depends only on scattering angle θ, not on the incident wavelength.

This is a collision problem. You must conserve both momentum (vector: two components) and energy. The photon gives up some energy to the electron, so its frequency decreases and its wavelength increases. This was decisive evidence that photons carry both energy and momentum.

The Wave–Particle Duality Thread

Compton scattering is one link in a chain of experiments that forced physicists to accept that light behaves as particles and matter behaves as waves. Neither is exclusively one or the other — the behaviour you observe depends on the experiment you perform.

1905 Einstein Photoelectric effect — light as particles
1923 Compton X-ray scattering — photon momentum confirmed
1924 de Broglie Matter waves — particles have wavelength
1927 Davisson-Germer Electron diffraction — matter waves confirmed

Notice the symmetry: Einstein gave light particle properties, then de Broglie gave particles wave properties. Each time, the claim was experimentally verified — photoelectric effect and Compton for light; electron diffraction for matter. The dual nature isn't a metaphor; it's a measurable fact.

Q1 At what angle θ is the Compton wavelength shift maximum? ▶
Answer

At θ = 180° (backscatter), since (1 − cos 180°) = 2. Maximum shift = 2h/(mec) = 4.86 × 10⁻¹² m. At θ = 0°, Δλ = 0 (no scattering).

Q2 Which conservation laws are applied in Compton scattering, and why do you need both? ▶
Answer

Both conservation of energy and conservation of momentum (in two perpendicular directions, since it's a 2D scattering problem). You need both because there are three unknowns (scattered photon wavelength, electron speed, electron direction) and you need three equations — energy plus two momentum components.

AHL E13 — de Broglie Hypothesis: Matter Waves

De Broglie proposed that all particles — not just photons — have an associated wavelength:

λ = h / p = h / (mv)

For a cricket ball (0.015 kg at 160 km/h ≈ 44.4 m/s): λ = 6.626 × 10⁻³⁴ / (0.015 × 44.4) ≈ 10⁻³³ m. Undetectably small — wave behaviour is irrelevant at macroscopic scales.

For an electron (9.11 × 10⁻³¹ kg at 10⁶ m/s): λ ≈ 7.3 × 10⁻¹⁰ m, which is comparable to atomic spacing — wave effects (diffraction) become observable.

The full relativistic energy-momentum relation: E² = p²c² + m₀²c⁴. For photons (m₀ = 0), this gives E = pc → p = E/c = hf/c = h/λ, which is consistent with de Broglie.

Is the de Broglie wavelength "real"? A cricket ball has λ ≈ 10⁻³³ m — far below any conceivable measurement. Does it have a wavelength in any meaningful sense? The realist says yes (the physics applies universally). The instrumentalist says the concept is only meaningful where it makes measurable predictions. IB likes you to notice the tension.

Q1 Why don't we observe diffraction of everyday objects? ▶
Answer

Because λ = h/p, and macroscopic objects have enormous momentum (large m). Their de Broglie wavelength is incomprehensibly small — many orders of magnitude below the Planck length. Diffraction effects require the wavelength to be comparable to the gap or obstacle size, which never happens for macroscopic objects.

Q2 An electron is accelerated through 150 V. What is its de Broglie wavelength? ▶
Answer

KE = eV = 1.602 × 10⁻¹⁹ × 150 = 2.40 × 10⁻¹⁷ J.

p = √(2mKE) = √(2 × 9.11 × 10⁻³¹ × 2.40 × 10⁻¹⁷) = 6.61 × 10⁻²⁴ kg·m/s.

λ = h/p = 6.626 × 10⁻³⁴ / 6.61 × 10⁻²⁴ ≈ 1.0 × 10⁻¹⁰ m = 0.10 nm.

This is comparable to atomic spacing in crystals — hence electron diffraction is observable.

AHL E14 — Electron Diffraction & Davisson-Germer

Electrons emitted from a heated cathode are accelerated through voltage V, gaining kinetic energy eV. Their de Broglie wavelength:

λ = h / √(2meV)

Decreasing V → increasing λ → wider diffraction rings (same logic as light through a slit: longer wavelength diffracts more).

In 1927, Davisson and Germer fired electrons at a nickel crystal and observed a diffraction pattern matching the predicted de Broglie wavelength. This was the definitive confirmation of matter waves.

The experiment completes the symmetry: Einstein and Compton showed light has particle properties. Davisson-Germer showed particles have wave properties. Wave-particle duality is universal.

Q1 Why does decreasing the accelerating voltage produce wider diffraction rings? ▶
Answer

Lower V → lower KE → lower momentum → longer de Broglie wavelength (λ = h/p). Diffraction angle increases with wavelength (just as for light: sin θ ∝ λ/d). So the rings spread out.

Q2 Why was a crystal (rather than a single slit) used in the Davisson-Germer experiment? ▶
Answer

The de Broglie wavelength of accelerated electrons is ~10⁻¹⁰ m — roughly the spacing between atoms in a crystal lattice. A crystal provides a natural diffraction grating at exactly the right scale. Manufacturing a slit that small would have been impossible in 1927.
