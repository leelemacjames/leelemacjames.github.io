Relativity, Reference Frames & E = mc²

Space contracts, time dilates, mass is energy — and it all follows from one postulate: the speed of light is the same for everyone.

A9 A10 A11 CoM Frame E = mc²

What this section is about

Special relativity starts with two postulates: (1) the laws of physics are the same in all inertial frames, and (2) the speed of light in vacuum is the same for all observers. From these alone, everything else follows — time dilation, length contraction, the Lorentz transformations, the relativity of simultaneity, and the most famous equation in physics: E = mc².

This learner covers slides A9–A11 (Galilean and Lorentz transformations, spacetime intervals, world lines) and extends into two topics your handout treats elsewhere but that belong here conceptually: centre-of-mass reference frames (essential for collision analysis) and mass-energy equivalence (the foundation of nuclear physics).

The Lorentz Factor γ — The Key to Everything

Nearly every relativistic formula involves one quantity:

γ = 1 / √(1 − v²/c²)

At low speeds (v ≪ c): γ ≈ 1 and relativity reduces to Galilean mechanics. As v → c: γ → ∞ and relativistic effects dominate.

AHL A9 — Reference Frames & Galilean Transformations

A reference frame is a coordinate system from which measurements of position, velocity, and time are made. Different observers in different frames may disagree on velocities and positions — but (in Galilean physics) they agree on time and acceleration.

Galilean transformations (valid for v ≪ c):

t' = t    (universal time — same for everyone)
x' = x − vt    (positions shift)
u' = u − v    (velocities add linearly)

These work perfectly for everyday speeds. A ball thrown at 20 m/s from a train moving at 30 m/s is seen from the ground as moving at 50 m/s. Simple addition. But this implies that a light beam emitted from the train would be seen at c + 30 — which violates experiment. Light always travels at c regardless of the source's motion.

Q1Why can't Galilean velocity addition be correct at high speeds?▶
Answer

Because it predicts that the speed of light depends on the observer's motion: an observer chasing a light beam at v would see it moving at c − v. But the Michelson-Morley experiment (1887) and all subsequent experiments show that every observer measures light at exactly c, regardless of their motion. Galilean addition is experimentally wrong for light — and, more subtly, for all speeds when measured precisely enough. It's a low-speed approximation of the Lorentz transformation.

Q2Is acceleration absolute or relative in Galilean mechanics?▶
Answer

Absolute — all inertial observers agree on acceleration. If u' = u − v and v is constant (inertial frame), then du'/dt = du/dt. The acceleration is the same in both frames. This is why Newton's second law F = ma works in any inertial frame without modification. (In non-inertial frames, you need fictitious forces — centrifugal, Coriolis.)

When v/c → 0, γ → 1 and the Lorentz formulas reduce exactly to the Galilean ones. Relativity doesn't replace Newtonian mechanics — it contains it as a limiting case.

Time dilation — moving clocks run slow:

Δt = γΔt₀

Δt₀ is the proper time — the interval measured by a clock at rest relative to the events. Any other observer measures a longer interval. A muon created in the upper atmosphere with γ ≈ 7 lives 7× longer in our frame, allowing it to reach the ground despite its 2.2 μs rest-frame lifetime.

Length contraction — moving objects are shorter:

L = L₀/γ

L₀ is the proper length — measured in the rest frame of the object. Observers see the object compressed along the direction of motion. Perpendicular dimensions are unaffected.

"Proper" doesn't mean "correct". It means "measured in the rest frame". Proper time is measured by a clock present at both events. Proper length is measured by a ruler at rest relative to the object. These are the smallest time and longest length — other frames always measure more time and less length.

Time dilation and length contraction are not contradictory. Students ask: "if the muon's clock runs slow, how does it also see the atmosphere as thinner?" Both descriptions are consistent — they're the same physics described from two different frames. In the Earth frame, the muon lives longer (time dilation). In the muon's frame, the atmosphere is thinner (length contraction). Both give the same prediction: the muon reaches the ground.

Q1A spaceship travels at 0.80c. A clock on board ticks off 10 minutes. How long does this take according to Earth observers?▶
Answer

γ = 1/√(1 − 0.64) = 1/√0.36 = 1/0.6 = 5/3 ≈ 1.667.

Δt = γΔt₀ = 1.667 × 10 = 16.7 minutes. Earth observers see the ship's clock running slow — 10 minutes on the ship takes 16.7 minutes on Earth.

Q2A 100 m spaceship passes at 0.90c. How long does it appear to an Earth observer?▶
Answer

γ = 1/√(1 − 0.81) = 1/√0.19 = 1/0.4359 = 2.294.

L = L₀/γ = 100/2.294 = 43.6 m. The ship appears compressed to less than half its rest length in the direction of motion.

Q3Two spaceships approach each other, each at 0.80c relative to Earth. What is their speed relative to each other?▶
Answer

Galilean: 0.80 + 0.80 = 1.60c (impossible — exceeds c).

Lorentz velocity addition: u' = (u + v)/(1 + uv/c²) = (0.80c + 0.80c)/(1 + 0.80 × 0.80) = 1.60c/1.64 = 0.976c.

Always less than c, no matter how fast the individual speeds are. The denominator (1 + uv/c²) prevents the sum from ever reaching c.

AHL A11 — Spacetime Interval, Invariance & World Lines

The spacetime interval between two events is the relativistic replacement for "distance":

Δs² = (cΔt)² − Δx²

This is invariant — every inertial observer calculates the same Δs², even though they disagree on Δt and Δx individually. It's the fundamental "length" in spacetime, analogous to Pythagoras in ordinary space (but with a crucial minus sign).

  • Δs² > 0 (timelike): events can be causally connected. A signal slower than light can travel between them.
  • Δs² = 0 (lightlike/null): events connected by a light signal. This is the boundary.
  • Δs² < 0 (spacelike): events cannot be causally connected. No signal can travel between them. Different observers may even disagree on which happened first.

Relativity of simultaneity: two events simultaneous in one frame (Δt = 0) are generally NOT simultaneous in another frame if they're spatially separated (Δx ≠ 0). The Lorentz transformation mixes space and time coordinates.

Is time "real" or is it a coordinate? In Newtonian physics, time is absolute — everyone agrees on it. In relativity, time is frame-dependent, just like position. The only "real" (invariant) quantity is the spacetime interval. Does this mean time is merely a human construct — a way of parameterising experience — rather than a feature of the universe? The debate between "block universe" (all times equally real) and "presentism" (only now is real) is still live in philosophy of physics.

Q1Two events are separated by Δt = 5 μs and Δx = 1200 m. Is the interval timelike, spacelike, or lightlike?▶
Answer

Δs² = (cΔt)² − Δx² = (3 × 10⁸ × 5 × 10⁻⁶)² − (1200)² = (1500)² − (1200)² = 2 250 000 − 1 440 000 = 810 000 m² > 0.

Timelike. The events can be causally connected — a signal at c could cover 1500 m in 5 μs, which is more than the 1200 m separation. All observers agree on the ordering of these events.

Q2Why does the relativity of simultaneity matter for physics, not just philosophy?▶
Answer

Because it means cause and effect have frame-dependent ordering for spacelike-separated events. If two explosions are spacelike-separated, one observer sees A first, another sees B first, and a third sees them as simultaneous. This has real consequences: it's why faster-than-light communication would violate causality (you could send a message into your own past), and it constrains the structure of quantum entanglement (correlations without causation).

AHL Centre-of-Mass Reference Frame

The centre-of-mass (CoM) frame is the reference frame in which the total momentum of the system is zero: Σp = 0. This is also called the zero-momentum frame.

Why it matters: in the CoM frame, collision and decay problems become symmetric and much simpler. Particles approach head-on with equal and opposite momenta, and the analysis reduces to energy accounting.

vCoM = (m₁v₁ + m₂v₂) / (m₁ + m₂)    (classical)

In CoM frame: p₁' + p₂' = 0

In particle physics: the CoM frame determines what reactions are energetically possible. If two protons collide head-on (CoM frame), all their kinetic energy is available for creating new particles. If one proton is stationary (lab frame), much of the KE goes into moving the whole system and isn't available for particle creation. This is why colliders (head-on beams) are vastly more efficient than fixed-target experiments.

Available energy for particle creation: in the CoM frame, the total energy is the invariant mass of the system times c²:

ECoM² = (Σpc)² − (ΣE)²    ... wait, correctly:

(ECoM)² = (ΣE)² − (Σp⃗c)²

In the CoM frame, Σp⃗ = 0, so ECoM = ΣE = total energy. This is the maximum energy available for creating new particles. In any other frame, some energy is "wasted" on bulk motion.

The CoM frame is not always the lab frame. In fixed-target experiments (beam hits stationary target), the CoM is moving. In colliders (two beams hitting head-on with equal energy), the lab frame IS the CoM frame. The LHC is a collider precisely because this maximises the energy available for new physics.

Q1Two balls (2 kg at 6 m/s and 3 kg at −4 m/s) collide. What is the velocity of the CoM frame?▶
Answer

vCoM = (2 × 6 + 3 × (−4)) / (2 + 3) = (12 − 12) / 5 = 0 m/s.

The lab frame already IS the CoM frame — total momentum is zero. The balls approach each other with equal and opposite momenta (both 12 kg·m/s). In a perfectly elastic collision, they'd simply reverse directions.

Q2Why does the LHC use two counter-rotating beams rather than one beam hitting a fixed target?▶
Answer

In a fixed-target experiment, the CoM is moving, and conservation of momentum means much of the beam's energy goes into the kinetic energy of the products — it's not available for creating new particles. In a collider, the two beams have equal and opposite momenta: the CoM frame is the lab frame and all the kinetic energy is available for particle creation. For the same beam energy, a collider produces CoM energies proportional to E (total beam energy), while a fixed target only produces energies proportional to √E. The LHC's 13 TeV CoM energy would require a ~100 000 TeV fixed-target beam.

AHL E = mc² — Mass-Energy Equivalence

The most famous equation in physics. In full, the energy-momentum relation for a particle of rest mass m₀ and momentum p is:

E² = (pc)² + (m₀c²)²

This is the relativistic "Pythagoras" — energy, momentum, and rest mass form a right triangle. Two special cases:

  • At rest (p = 0): E = m₀c². Rest mass IS energy. A proton at rest has energy 938.3 MeV.
  • Massless particle (m₀ = 0): E = pc. Photons have no rest mass but carry momentum p = E/c = h/λ. This connects to de Broglie and Compton (see your quantum learner).

The total relativistic energy of a moving particle:

E = γm₀c²

This includes both rest energy (m₀c²) and kinetic energy (KE = (γ − 1)m₀c²). At low speeds, γ ≈ 1 + ½v²/c², so KE ≈ ½m₀v² — recovering the classical formula.

Where E = mc² appears in IB Physics:

  • Nuclear binding energy: mass defect Δm converts to binding energy ΔE = Δmc². (Covered in your binding energy learner.)
  • Pair production: a photon converts to a particle-antiparticle pair: Ephoton ≥ 2m₀c². Minimum photon energy to create an electron-positron pair: 2 × 0.511 MeV = 1.022 MeV.
  • Annihilation: particle meets antiparticle → photons. e⁻ + e⁺ → 2γ. Each photon has energy ≥ 0.511 MeV. This is how PET scans work (connects to your quantum learner, E39c).
  • Fission and fusion: the "missing mass" in nuclear reactions becomes kinetic energy of products.

Mass is not "converted" to energy like water to steam. Mass IS energy — they're the same physical quantity measured in different units (kg vs J), connected by c². A hot cup of tea has more mass than a cold one (by ~10⁻¹² kg — unmeasurably small). A compressed spring has more mass than a relaxed one. The distinction between "mass" and "energy" is a linguistic convenience, not a physical one. Does this mean our everyday categories (stuff vs activity) are fundamentally misleading?

Q1How much energy is contained in 1 kg of mass?▶
Answer

E = mc² = 1 × (3 × 10⁸)² = 9 × 10¹⁶ J = 90 petajoules.

This is roughly the annual energy consumption of a small country, or the yield of a ~21 megaton nuclear weapon. The reason nuclear weapons are so powerful is not that they're efficient — they convert less than 0.1% of their mass to energy. It's that c² is such an enormous number.

Q2A proton (rest mass 938.3 MeV/c²) has total energy 2000 MeV. What is its speed?▶
Answer

E = γm₀c² → γ = E/(m₀c²) = 2000/938.3 = 2.131.

γ = 1/√(1 − v²/c²) → v²/c² = 1 − 1/γ² = 1 − 1/4.541 = 1 − 0.2202 = 0.7798.

v = c√0.7798 = 0.883c ≈ 2.65 × 10⁸ m/s.

Q3What is the minimum photon energy needed to create a proton-antiproton pair?▶
Answer

Emin = 2mpc² = 2 × 938.3 = 1876.6 MeV ≈ 1.88 GeV.

This is a gamma ray with wavelength λ = hc/E = 1.24 × 10⁻⁶ eV·m / (1.877 × 10⁹ eV) = 6.6 × 10⁻¹⁶ m — about 1000× smaller than a proton. Such photons exist in cosmic ray interactions and near black holes.

Q4Why does a photon have momentum despite having zero mass?▶
Answer

Because the full relation is E² = (pc)² + (m₀c²)². For m₀ = 0: E = pc → p = E/c. The photon's momentum comes entirely from its energy, not from mass. Momentum is not "mass × velocity" in relativity — that's only the low-speed approximation. Compton scattering (your quantum learner, E12) is direct experimental proof that photons carry momentum: an X-ray photon bounces off an electron and transfers measurable momentum.
