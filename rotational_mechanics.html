Rotational Mechanics

From angular kinematics to rolling bodies
IB Physics HL — Engineering Physics (Option B) & Extended Mechanics
§1 Angular Kinematics
What are angular displacement, angular velocity, and angular acceleration — and how do they relate to their linear counterparts? ▾

Every rotational quantity has a linear twin, connected by the radius \(r\):

LinearRotationalLink
Displacement \(s\)Angular displacement \(\theta\) (rad)\(s = r\theta\)
Velocity \(v\)Angular velocity \(\omega\) (rad s⁻¹)\(v = r\omega\)
Acceleration \(a\)Angular acceleration \(\alpha\) (rad s⁻²)\(a = r\alpha\)
The radian is what makes these links clean — it's the ratio \(s/r\), so it carries no "unit baggage". One full revolution is \(2\pi\) rad, which is why \(\omega = 2\pi f\).

Angular velocity is a vector. Its direction is given by the right-hand rule: curl the fingers of your right hand in the direction of rotation, and your thumb points along \(\vec{\omega}\).

Do the SUVAT equations work for rotation? ▾

Yes — with a direct substitution. Wherever SUVAT uses \(s, u, v, a\), rotational kinematics uses \(\theta, \omega_0, \omega, \alpha\):

Linear SUVATRotational SUVAT
\(v = u + at\)\(\omega = \omega_0 + \alpha t\)
\(s = ut + \tfrac{1}{2}at^2\)\(\theta = \omega_0 t + \tfrac{1}{2}\alpha t^2\)
\(v^2 = u^2 + 2as\)\(\omega^2 = \omega_0^2 + 2\alpha\theta\)
\(s = \tfrac{1}{2}(u+v)t\)\(\theta = \tfrac{1}{2}(\omega_0 + \omega)t\)
These only work for constant angular acceleration — exactly the same caveat as linear SUVAT.

Worked example: A wheel accelerates from rest at \(\alpha = 4.0\) rad s⁻² for \(3.0\) s. Find the angular displacement.

\(\theta = \omega_0 t + \tfrac{1}{2}\alpha t^2 = 0 + \tfrac{1}{2}(4.0)(3.0)^2 = 18 \text{ rad}\)

That's \(18 / (2\pi) \approx 2.86\) revolutions.

How do you convert between rpm, Hz, and rad s⁻¹? ▾

Exam questions mix units freely — the conversion chain is:

\(\text{rpm} \xrightarrow{\div 60} \text{Hz} \xrightarrow{\times 2\pi} \text{rad s}^{-1}\)

Example: A motor spins at 3000 rpm.

\(f = \frac{3000}{60} = 50 \text{ Hz}, \qquad \omega = 2\pi(50) = 100\pi \approx 314 \text{ rad s}^{-1}\)
Always work in rad s⁻¹ inside equations. Convert back to rpm or Hz only at the end.
§2 Torque & Newton's Second Law for Rotation
What is torque, and why is it not the same as force? ▾

Torque \(\tau\) is the rotational effectiveness of a force. A force applied far from the pivot creates more rotational effect than the same force applied close to it:

\(\tau = Fr\sin\theta\)

where \(r\) is the distance from the pivot and \(\theta\) is the angle between \(\vec{F}\) and \(\vec{r}\). In vector form: \(\vec{\tau} = \vec{r} \times \vec{F}\).

Units: N m (not joules — even though they share the same dimensions, torque and energy are conceptually different).

Force changes linear momentum. Torque changes angular momentum. They are parallel ideas, not the same idea.
What is the rotational form of Newton's second law? ▾

The linear version says \(F = ma\), or more precisely \(F = \frac{dp}{dt}\). The rotational version is:

\(\tau = I\alpha \qquad \text{or equivalently} \qquad \tau = \frac{dL}{dt}\)

where \(I\) is the moment of inertia (how mass is distributed about the axis) and \(L = I\omega\) is angular momentum.

This is the master equation of rotational dynamics. Everything else — equilibrium, conservation of angular momentum, precession — follows from it.

§3 Moment of Inertia
Why does mass distribution matter, and what exactly is \(I\)? ▾

Moment of inertia is the rotational analogue of mass — it measures resistance to angular acceleration. But unlike mass, it depends on where the mass sits relative to the axis:

\(I = \sum_i m_i r_i^2 \qquad \text{(discrete masses)} \qquad I = \int r^2 \, dm \qquad \text{(continuous body)}\)

The \(r^2\) factor is crucial: moving mass twice as far from the axis quadruples its contribution to \(I\).

Standard results (IB data booklet):

ShapeAxis\(I\)
Point massDistance \(r\)\(mr^2\)
Thin rod (length \(L\))Through centre\(\tfrac{1}{12}mL^2\)
Thin rodThrough end\(\tfrac{1}{3}mL^2\)
Solid disc / cylinderCentral axis\(\tfrac{1}{2}mR^2\)
Hollow ring / hoopCentral axis\(mR^2\)
Solid sphereThrough centre\(\tfrac{2}{5}mR^2\)
Hollow sphereThrough centre\(\tfrac{2}{3}mR^2\)
Notice: the hoop has \(I = mR^2\) (all mass at distance \(R\)), while the disc has \(I = \tfrac{1}{2}mR^2\) (mass spread from 0 to \(R\)). Same mass, same radius — the disc is easier to spin because its mass is, on average, closer to the axis.
What is the parallel axis theorem, and when do you need it? ▾

The data booklet gives \(I\) about the centre-of-mass axis. If you need \(I\) about a different parallel axis a distance \(d\) away:

\(I_{\text{new}} = I_{\text{cm}} + Md^2\)

Example: A thin rod of mass \(M\), length \(L\), pivoted at one end:

\(I_{\text{end}} = I_{\text{cm}} + M\!\left(\frac{L}{2}\right)^2 = \frac{1}{12}ML^2 + \frac{1}{4}ML^2 = \frac{1}{3}ML^2 \quad \checkmark\)
The parallel axis theorem only works from the centre-of-mass axis outward. You cannot apply it between two arbitrary parallel axes without going via the centre first.
§4 Rotational Kinetic Energy
How does kinetic energy work for a spinning object? ▾

A spinning body has kinetic energy even if its centre of mass is stationary:

\(E_{\text{rot}} = \tfrac{1}{2}I\omega^2\)

This mirrors \(E_{\text{trans}} = \tfrac{1}{2}mv^2\) exactly. The derivation is just the sum of \(\tfrac{1}{2}m_i v_i^2\) for every particle, with \(v_i = r_i\omega\):

\(\sum \tfrac{1}{2}m_i(r_i\omega)^2 = \tfrac{1}{2}\!\left(\sum m_i r_i^2\right)\omega^2 = \tfrac{1}{2}I\omega^2\)

If the body is both translating and rotating (as in rolling), the total KE is:

\(E_{\text{total}} = \tfrac{1}{2}mv_{\text{cm}}^2 + \tfrac{1}{2}I_{\text{cm}}\omega^2\)
§5 Rolling Without Slipping
What does "rolling without slipping" actually mean, and what constraint does it impose? ▾

When a wheel rolls without slipping, the contact point is instantaneously at rest. This couples translation and rotation:

\(v_{\text{cm}} = R\omega \qquad \text{and} \qquad a_{\text{cm}} = R\alpha\)

This is a constraint, not a law. It reduces the degrees of freedom from two (translation + rotation) to one.

If the wheel skids, \(v_{\text{cm}} \neq R\omega\) and you need friction dynamics to solve the problem. Rolling without slipping is the idealisation where static friction is sufficient.
A solid sphere and a hollow sphere, same mass and radius, roll down the same incline from rest. Which arrives first? ▾

Use energy conservation. At the bottom, \(mgh = \tfrac{1}{2}mv^2 + \tfrac{1}{2}I\omega^2\). Substitute \(\omega = v/R\):

\(mgh = \tfrac{1}{2}mv^2 + \tfrac{1}{2}\!\left(cMR^2\right)\!\frac{v^2}{R^2} = \tfrac{1}{2}mv^2(1 + c)\)

where \(c\) is the shape factor (\(\tfrac{2}{5}\) for solid sphere, \(\tfrac{2}{3}\) for hollow sphere). Solving:

\(v = \sqrt{\frac{2gh}{1+c}}\)

Smaller \(c\) → larger \(v\) → the solid sphere wins. It diverts less energy into rotation because its mass sits closer to the axis.

Mass and radius cancel completely — only the geometry matters. A marble beats a ping-pong ball of any size, because \(\tfrac{2}{5} < \tfrac{2}{3}\).
What fraction of a rolling object's kinetic energy is rotational? ▾

Total KE \(= \tfrac{1}{2}mv^2(1 + c)\), and the rotational part is \(\tfrac{1}{2}cv \cdot mv^2\). So:

\(\frac{E_{\text{rot}}}{E_{\text{total}}} = \frac{c}{1+c}\)
Object\(c\)Rotational fraction
Solid sphere\(2/5\)\(2/7 \approx 29\%\)
Solid cylinder\(1/2\)\(1/3 \approx 33\%\)
Hollow sphere\(2/3\)\(2/5 = 40\%\)
Hoop\(1\)\(1/2 = 50\%\)
A hoop rolling at speed \(v\) carries twice the KE of one sliding at the same speed. Half its energy is locked into rotation.
§6 Angular Momentum & Conservation
When is angular momentum conserved, and what does that look like in practice? ▾

From \(\tau = dL/dt\): if the net external torque is zero, \(L = I\omega = \text{constant}\).

\(\tau_{\text{ext}} = 0 \implies I_1\omega_1 = I_2\omega_2\)

Classic examples:

• Ice skater: Arms out → large \(I\), slow \(\omega\). Arms in → small \(I\), fast \(\omega\). Angular momentum unchanged.

• Collapsing star: A star's core contracts from solar radius to neutron-star radius (\(\sim 10\) km). If \(R\) shrinks by factor \(10^5\), then \(I \propto R^2\) shrinks by \(10^{10}\), so \(\omega\) increases by \(10^{10}\). A star rotating once per month becomes a pulsar spinning hundreds of times per second.

Conservation of \(L\) is not a separate postulate — it follows directly from Newton's second law in rotational form when no torque acts. The depth is in recognising when torque vanishes.
A turntable (disc, mass 2.0 kg, radius 0.15 m) spins at 33 rpm. A 50 g putty ball is dropped vertically onto the rim. Find the new angular velocity. ▾

The putty lands vertically — no torque about the turntable axis. So \(L\) is conserved.

\(I_{\text{disc}} = \tfrac{1}{2}MR^2 = \tfrac{1}{2}(2.0)(0.15)^2 = 0.0225 \text{ kg m}^2\)
\(I_{\text{putty}} = mR^2 = (0.050)(0.15)^2 = 0.001125 \text{ kg m}^2\)

Initial: \(\omega_0 = 33 \times \frac{2\pi}{60} = 3.456\) rad s⁻¹

\(I_1\omega_1 = (I_1 + I_2)\omega_2\)
\(\omega_2 = \frac{0.0225 \times 3.456}{0.0225 + 0.001125} = \frac{0.07776}{0.023625} = 3.29 \text{ rad s}^{-1} \approx 31.4 \text{ rpm}\)
The turntable barely slows — the putty's \(I\) is only 5% of the disc's. But this is an inelastic collision: KE is lost even though \(L\) is conserved, just like a linear inelastic collision conserves momentum but loses KE.
§7 Combined Problems
A mass \(m\) hangs from a string wound around a cylinder of mass \(M\) and radius \(R\). Find the acceleration of the falling mass. ▾

This is the Atwood-with-a-pulley problem. The string exerts tension \(T\) on both the mass and the cylinder rim.

For the falling mass (Newton II, linear):

\(mg - T = ma \qquad \text{...(1)}\)

For the cylinder (Newton II, rotational, with \(\alpha = a/R\)):

\(TR = I\alpha = \tfrac{1}{2}MR^2 \cdot \frac{a}{R} \implies T = \tfrac{1}{2}Ma \qquad \text{...(2)}\)

Substitute (2) into (1):

\(mg = ma + \tfrac{1}{2}Ma \implies a = \frac{mg}{m + \tfrac{1}{2}M} = \frac{g}{1 + \frac{M}{2m}}\)
The cylinder's inertia slows the fall. If \(M \gg m\), the mass barely accelerates. If \(M = 0\) (massless pulley), we recover \(a = g\) — free fall, as expected.
How does gyroscopic precession work — and can you predict its rate? ▾

A spinning wheel tilted at angle from vertical doesn't fall — it precesses. Gravity exerts a torque \(\tau = mgd\) (where \(d\) is the horizontal distance from pivot to centre of mass), and this torque is perpendicular to \(\vec{L}\).

Since \(\vec{\tau} = d\vec{L}/dt\), the torque doesn't change the magnitude of \(L\) — it changes its direction. The spin axis sweeps out a cone:

\(\Omega_{\text{precession}} = \frac{\tau}{L} = \frac{mgd}{I\omega}\)

Faster spin → slower precession. This is why a bicycle is stable at speed: the wheel's large \(L\) resists tilting torques.

Precession is to angular momentum what circular motion is to linear momentum — the "force" (torque) is perpendicular to the "velocity" (\(\vec{L}\)), so the magnitude stays constant while the direction rotates.
