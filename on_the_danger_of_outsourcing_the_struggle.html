On the Danger of Outsourcing the Struggle

On thinking, tools, and those rare moments of recognition
Lee McCulloch-James. Dwight School London, 2026.

Before the Paint Goes On

Before we talk about thinking and tools, let me offer a simple metaphor from something far more ordinary than mathematics or physics: painting an external door.

When a door is painted properly, the glossy surface you see at the end is the least important part of the process. What matters is everything that happens before the gloss ever touches the door.

If the door has been painted before, the first step is not painting at all. It is sanding. The old surface must be roughened so that the new layer has something to grip. Painters call this giving the surface a key. Without that key, the new paint will sit beautifully for a while and then peel away.

After sanding comes the primer. The primer seals the surface and creates the foundation to which everything else adheres. This stage is slow and unglamorous, but it determines whether the paint will last for years or fail within months.

Then comes the undercoat. This is where the real colour begins to take shape. It is not yet the finished surface, but the form is now there. The undercoat carries the weight of the paint system. It is the layer that gives the finish its strength.

Only at the very end does the gloss go on. The gloss gives the surface its lustre. It is what people notice first. But the gloss is not what makes the paint durable. The durability lives underneath.

Thinking works in much the same way. Old ideas sometimes need sanding so that new understanding can attach to them. The primer stage is where the real intellectual work happens: the slow process of building the conceptual surface on which understanding can adhere. The undercoat is your own reasoning as it takes shape after hours of rumination and false starts. Only then should the gloss appear.

Large language models are very good at producing gloss. They can even help shape an undercoat once the structure already exists. But they cannot do the sanding, and they cannot lay the primer. Those layers must always be yours. If they are not, the finish may look convincing, but it will not last.

With matters allegorical off my chest, let us return to the question that prompted this letter.

I want to tell you about something that happened to me recently. I had an idea about a pattern in number theory. It was half-formed. I could see the shape of it but not the edges. I sat with a large language model and, over many hours of conversation, the idea became a short paper. The paper was rejected. One reason given was that the reviewer suspected much of it had been written by an AI.

They were partly right. The mathematics was mine. The direction was mine. The moment when the argument clicked was mine. But the prose, the connective tissue, the polished paragraphs that made the thing read like a finished paper: those were largely the machine's and my lack of attibution was telling. And a trained reader could tell.

That experience crystallised something I want to say to you about the relationship between thinking and tools, and about a danger that is specific to your generation. I teach you mathematics and physics. The danger touches both, but it touches them differently.

The Old Loops

In mathematics, the cognitive process has always looked something like this:

intuition → internal dialogue → clarity

You notice something. You argue with yourself about it. You write it down. You cross it out. You write it again. Slowly, painfully, the idea takes shape. The internal dialogue is where the work happens. It is slow because understanding is slow. You are not just producing sentences. You are testing whether you believe what the sentences say.

In physics, there is a parallel loop, but it passes through the world:

observation → model → prediction → test

You see something that does not fit your expectations. You build a model to explain it. The model makes a prediction. You test the prediction against reality. If the prediction fails, you revise the model. If it holds, you have learned something. The test is not optional. It is what separates physics from storytelling.

Both loops require a period of struggle. In mathematics, the struggle is internal: you sit with the algebra until the structure reveals itself. In physics, the struggle is also external: the universe is under no obligation to confirm your model, and the moment of failure, the experiment that does not go as predicted, is often more instructive than the moment of success.

The New Loops

Large language models offer a substitution in both disciplines:

intuition → dialogue with tool → clarity
observation → dialogue with tool → plausible model

The internal dialogue is replaced by an external one. You have a fragmentary idea. You describe it to the machine. The machine returns a candidate articulation: polished, structured, plausible. You read it and think, "Yes, that is what I meant."

In physics the danger has a specific shape. The machine can produce a model that is coherent, consistent with known results, and expressed in correct formalism. What it cannot do is test that model against reality. It has no laboratory. It has no apparatus. It cannot run the experiment. And a model that has not been tested against the world is not physics. It is a hypothesis dressed in the language of physics.

This is seductive in both disciplines. It is also, for someone at your stage, genuinely dangerous.

What the Machine Does and Does Not Do

The machine generates candidate structures. It is very good at this. Given fragments, it produces coherent wholes. Given a question, it produces a plausible answer. Given half a proof, it produces the other half in fluent mathematical prose. Given a set of experimental observations, it produces a model that sounds reasonable.

What it does not do is understand whether the structure is correct. It does not know if the proof is valid. It does not know if the model contradicts an experiment performed in 1987 that it was not trained on. It does not know if the result is interesting. It is generating text that is statistically consistent with the corpus of scientific and mathematical exposition it has seen. Sometimes that text is brilliant. Sometimes it is fluent nonsense. The machine cannot tell the difference. You must.

A historical perspective

Euler had human computers who performed calculations for him. Gauss had tables of primes. Ramanujan tested his conjectures by hand-computing hundreds of terms. None of them were diminished by the mechanical step. They were defined by what happened after it: the recognition of structure, the judgment of significance, the decision about what deserved to become a theorem.

In physics the same is true. Tycho Brahe spent decades collecting positional data on Mars. He did not understand it. Kepler took Brahe's data and, after years of failed models, recognised that the orbits were ellipses. The data was the mechanical step. The recognition was the science. Later, Eddington sailed to an island off West Africa to photograph a solar eclipse and test whether starlight bent as Einstein predicted. He could have stayed home and declared the theory beautiful. He did not. He went and measured. That is the difference between physics and plausible narrative.

Recognition as the Source of Authority

When I look at a candidate structure produced during dialogue with a LLM tool and say, "that is wrong," or "that is right but uninteresting," or "that is the point," I am performing the same act of judgment I would perform if the candidate had surfaced from my own thinking at three in the morning.

But here is the crucial difference between you and me. I have spent thirty years building the internal machinery that lets me make that judgment. I have assimilated and accommodated, in Piaget's language, a body of mathematical and physical knowledge. I have made enough mistakes, followed enough dead ends, run the course of a nascent thought to some logical conclusion that gave the wrong answer, and sat with enough confusion, that I have a sense of when an argument is (actually!) load-bearing and when it merely appears load-bearing. That sense is hard-won. It cannot be shortcut.

The danger is this. If you outsource the internal dialogue before you have built the internal machinery, you lose the capacity to perform the recognition step. The tool gives you clarity before you have earned it. The result looks like understanding. It is not understanding. It is fluency without foundation. And you will not know the difference until the moment someone asks you to defend what you have written, and you find that you cannot.

Piaget's Ladder

On assimilation and accommodation

Piaget described two processes by which we build knowledge. Assimilation is fitting new information into existing mental structures. Accommodation is restructuring those mental frameworks when the new information does not fit. The second process is the hard one. It requires discomfort. It requires sitting with the fact that your current understanding is inadequate and needs to be rebuilt.

A large language model will never make you accommodate. It will produce a smooth, coherent account that you can assimilate immediately, because it is designed to produce text that reads as already-understood. The struggle, the confusion, the rebuilding: those are precisely the experiences that the tool eliminates. And they are precisely the experiences that build the machinery of recognition.

In mathematics, accommodation looks like the moment when you realise that your intuition about infinity is wrong and you have to rebuild your concept of number. Or the moment when a proof you believed for a week turns out to have a gap and you have to start again.

In physics, accommodation looks like the moment when you drop two objects of different mass and they hit the ground at the same time, and your Aristotelian expectation is shattered. Or the moment when you calculate the ultraviolet catastrophe from classical thermodynamics and realise the theory must be wrong, that energy must be quantised, and that the universe does not care what you find elegant. The experiment has told you that your mental model needs to break and reform.

A language model will never give you that experience. It will give you Planck's answer. It will not give you Planck's confusion. And the confusion was where the physics happened.

If you skip that period, you do not arrive at the same destination faster. You arrive somewhere else entirely. You arrive at fluency. Fluency is not understanding. You can be fluent in the language of quantum mechanics without having any sense of where the weight-bearing walls are.

The Scientific Method, As It Was

The scientific method, as you were taught it, runs in a cycle:

observe → hypothesise → predict → experiment → revise

Each step requires a different kind of thinking. Observation requires attention. Hypothesis requires imagination. Prediction requires mathematical rigour: you must derive a specific, falsifiable consequence from your model. Experiment requires honesty: you must accept the result even when it is not what you hoped. Revision requires humility: you must be willing to abandon an idea you liked.

The method is slow. It is meant to be slow. The slowness is not a bug. It is the mechanism by which false ideas are filtered out. Speed is the enemy of this process. Every shortcut that allows a plausible-sounding hypothesis to skip the experimental test is a corruption of the method.

The Scientific Method, As It Is Becoming

The temptation now is to let the tool handle the middle steps. You observe something. You ask the machine to hypothesise. It produces a model. You ask it to predict. It produces a formula. You skip the experiment because the formula looks right, and the prose surrounding it sounds authoritative.

This is not the scientific method. It is the scientific aesthetic. It has the shape of science without the substance. The substance is in the test. The test is the moment where the universe gets a vote. If you remove that vote, what remains is literature.

I say this with some self-awareness. The paper I wrote with the help of a language model was a piece of mathematics, not physics. But the reviewer's criticism, "you do not explain why this is important or what problem it solves," is the scientific method's question applied to a mathematical result: so what? What does this observation let you do that you could not do before? If the answer is nothing, the observation is a curiosity. The machine produced a polished account of a curiosity. It could not tell me that the curiosity was not enough.

Two Disciplines for Your Writing

Because these tools produce polished language so readily, they introduce a particular temptation: your work can begin to sound finished before the thinking is finished. Guard against this. The disciplines are simple.

In mathematics:

claim → equation → consequence

A claim should be tied to an explicit mathematical object. An equation should make the claim precise. A consequence should show what follows and why anyone should care. If you cannot state the claim without the machine's help, you do not yet understand the claim. If the equation does not appear naturally from the claim, the claim may be hollow.

In physics:

observation → model → prediction → measurement

An observation should be tied to something you have actually seen or measured. A model should be the simplest structure that accounts for it. A prediction should be a specific number or relation that the model produces and that you did not put in by hand. And a measurement should be something you or someone else can actually perform. If the prediction cannot be tested, it is not yet physics. It is a sketch.

Strip everything back to these skeletons before you show your work to anyone. If the skeleton stands, you have something. If it does not, no amount of polished prose will save it.

On Honest Disclosure

When I use these tools in my own work, I say so. Not dramatically. Not evasively. In the same spirit as a physicist who writes "numerical simulations were performed using MATLAB" or a mathematician who writes "symbolic computations were performed using Mathematica." The sentence I use is:

Expository structuring was assisted by Claude (Anthropic). The mathematical content and direction of argument are the author's.

This is honest. It does not pretend the tool was absent. It does not pretend the tool had mathematical or physical judgment. It describes a workflow. You should do the same whenever you use these tools. Not because you are required to, but because the habit of honesty about your own process is itself a form of intellectual discipline. If you cannot say clearly which parts are yours and which parts were generated, that is a signal that you do not yet understand the work well enough to put your name on it.

What I Am Asking of You

I am not asking you to avoid these tools. They exist. They are useful. I use them myself. I am asking you to understand what they are and what they are not.

They are generators of candidate structures. They are not judges of truth. They are combinatorial assistants. They do not have comprehension. They do not take responsibility for claims. They cannot reject incorrect reasoning on mathematical grounds. They cannot run an experiment. They cannot tell you when a model contradicts nature. That is your job.

And to do that job, you must first build the internal machinery. There is no shortcut. The confusion is not an obstacle to understanding. The confusion is the process of understanding. Sit with it. Endure it. Let your mental structures break and reform. That is how the ladder gets climbed.

In mathematics, the struggle is with the proof that will not close, the conjecture that turns out to be false, the definition that needs to be rewritten for the third time. In physics, the struggle is with the experiment that gives the wrong answer, the model that is beautiful but predicts a number that is off by a factor of two, the graph that does not look like the theory said it should. Both struggles are necessary. Both build the same thing: the capacity to know, when you see a result, whether it is real.

The tools will get better. They will produce more convincing mathematics, more plausible physics, more fluent exposition. The gap between "generated" and "understood" will become harder to see from the outside. But it will remain. It will always remain. Because understanding is not a property of text. It is a property of the person who can stand behind the text, defend it, extend it, and know when it is wrong. In mathematics, that means knowing when a proof has a hole. In physics, that means knowing when a prediction needs to be tested against the world rather than accepted because it sounds right. That is what I want you to become. The tool cannot want it for you.
