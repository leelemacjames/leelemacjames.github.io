Essay · On Thinking and Tools

Thinking With,
Not For

On workload, LLMs, and why ideas still have to be earned.

Lee McCulloch-James  ·  Dwight School London, 2026

There is a real danger in handing too much intellectual workload to a large language model. The danger is not only error. It is also the temptation to skip the slow formation of thought. Yet the opposite mistake is now equally possible: to reject the tool because one confuses acceleration with replacement. The question is not whether the machine can produce polished algebra faster than we can. The question is where ideas come from, and what role a tool should play once an idea has begun.

A small mathematical episode makes the point. The episode is modest in content but revealing in method. It begins, as many ideas do, with something already known well enough to be seen structurally rather than merely remembered as a fact.

§ 1The first recognition

Odd numbers can be written as differences of two consecutive squares. That is a familiar observation. But once one has spent enough time with determinants, the observation no longer sits alone. It changes aspect. One sees that

det[[a,b],[b,a]] = a² − b²

and so the difference of two squares is not merely a schoolroom identity. It is a determinant of a symmetric 2×2 matrix. At that point, "oddness" is no longer just a parity fact. It becomes the image of a structured map.

This is the sort of shift that domain knowledge makes possible. Nothing spectacular has happened. No theorem has dropped from the sky. But the object has changed category in the mind. One has gone from remembering a result to seeing a structure.

§ 2The second recognition

Another piece of prior knowledge then presses in. Determinants do not only package static identities. They also generate sequences. Fibonacci numbers, for instance, can emerge from tridiagonal determinant recurrences. So determinants are not one trick. They are a family of machines. They produce values, identities, and recurrences according to the symmetry imposed upon them.

That prompts the next question almost automatically: if the 2×2 symmetric determinant gives the difference of two squares, what happens in 3×3?

One natural candidate is the circulant determinant

det[[a,b,c],[c,a,b],[b,c,a]] = a³ + b³ + c³ − 3abc

At first sight this looks like failure. The analogy with the quadratic case does not survive intact. The extra term, −3abc, appears to spoil the hoped-for clean generalisation. A possible idea appears, then stalls. This is ordinary intellectual life. Most ideas do not collapse dramatically. They simply sit unresolved and begin to wither.

§ 3The narrative problem

This is where the comparison with Working Girl becomes useful — and it is a comparison I have drawn before, in a different context, in these pages. The memorable tension in that story is not just between intelligence and hierarchy. It is between having a live idea and lacking the institutional machinery to move it. An idea can be present before it is legible. It can exist in a state of partial formation, waiting for the right language, or the right structure, or simply the right confidence, to be carried forward.

Much of thinking is like that. One notices something. One cannot yet prove it, place it, or fully trust it. The idea remains suspended between intuition and abandonment. That suspended state matters. It is where knowledge either deepens or evaporates.

§ 4Time passes; knowledge accumulates

What changed in this case was not the determinant itself but the background against which it was seen. Later, after other reading and other work, the expression

a² + b² + c² − ab − bc − ca

ceased to look accidental. It looked familiar. More precisely, it looked structurally adjacent to the Eisenstein norm. The quadratic dispersion term was no longer debris left over from a failed cubic analogy. It was recognisable mathematical matter. The vague idea began to crystallise.

The important point is that the idea was not generated by the LLM. It was generated by prior knowledge encountering a new configuration.

That is the thesis in miniature. Ideation is an outgrowth of domain-specific knowledge. One sees only what one has become capable of seeing. Without earlier acquaintance with determinants, recurrence structure, and norm forms, there would have been nothing to connect. No tool can substitute for that substrate.

§ 5What the LLM actually did

Once the structural resemblance had been noticed, however, the language model became useful in a very specific way. It did not supply the originating insight. It removed friction. It expanded the cubic expression quickly. It verified the factorisation. It checked the modular obstruction. It let the algebraic consequences be tested at speed.

Symbiosis, not substitution

The human contribution was pattern recognition, selection, suspicion, and direction. The machine contribution was rapid completion of routine symbolic work, available quickly enough that the half-formed idea did not die before being examined.

This is precisely the relationship worth defending. Without the LLM, the algebra might have taken long enough, or felt cumbersome enough, that the idea would have been dropped. With the LLM, the idea could be pursued while still warm. That matters. Many insights are not lost because they are false; they are lost because they are fragile.

§ 6The educational difficulty

This brings us to the real pedagogical problem. If students are permitted to offload all symbolic effort immediately, then they may never build the stock of internal knowledge from which pattern recognition emerges. But if they are denied all computational assistance, many embryonic ideas will never survive the distance between intuition and formalisation.

In an earlier essay I described this in terms of the painter's preparatory layers — the sanding, the primer, the undercoat that no one ever sees but upon which everything depends. A language model is very good at producing gloss. It can help shape an undercoat once the structure already exists. But it cannot do the sanding, and it cannot lay the primer. Those layers must always be yours. If they are not, the finish may look convincing, but it will not last.

So the question is not whether LLM use is good or bad in the abstract. The question is which parts of intellectual work must remain slow if understanding is to form, and which parts may be accelerated once that understanding has begun to take shape.

  • Knowledge must be laid down through effort, repetition, and mulling over.
  • Pattern recognition depends on that accumulated substrate.
  • Ideation is what happens when the substrate becomes mobile.
  • An LLM can help at the stage of verification and elaboration, but it cannot replace the prior laying-down of knowledge without emptying the process of its source.

§ 7A practical principle

The aim in teaching should therefore not be to ban such tools, nor to normalise total dependence on them. It should be to stage their use properly. First, the learner must labour enough to acquire internal structure. Then, once a real question or conjecture emerges, the tool may serve as a fast algebraic partner. Used too early, it prevents thought from forming. Used at the right moment, it helps thought to survive.

This is a staging problem, not a permission problem. The error in most current institutional responses to AI — whether panicked prohibition or blithe permissiveness — is to treat the tool as uniformly dangerous or uniformly helpful. It is neither. Its value depends entirely on when, in the sequence of thinking, it enters the process. Before the structure forms, it short-circuits understanding. After the structure forms, it is an accelerant of genuine inquiry.

∎

That is the distinction worth keeping. The machine is excellent at finishing work. But the beginning of work — the noticing, the analogy, the sense that one expression belongs to the family of another — that still comes from a mind shaped by prior effort. In that sense, the old truth remains. Knowledge has to be worked for. It has to be mulled over. Only then can a small, vague idea become something one can recognise, test, and keep alive long enough to know whether it is worth the keeping.
