Preamble I · Foundations

Bases & Powers
The grammar of scale

Before logarithms, you need to be fluent in powers. A power is just repeated multiplication — and two bases dominate science: base 10 (our counting system) and base 2 (computers, binary, doublings).

Logarithms look frightening. They are not. A logarithm answers one simple question: "What power do I raise this base to, to get that number?" That is all. Everything else follows.

Base 10: the decimal ladder

Each rung multiplies by 10. The exponent just counts the rungs.

Base 2: the binary ladder

Each rung doubles. Computers think in base 2. So do populations doubling, cells dividing, and crumple hierarchies.

210 = 1024. This is close to 103 = 1000. Computer scientists exploit this — "1 kilobyte" is actually 1024 bytes. What is 210 in terms of approximate powers of 10?
Correct. 210 = 1024 and 103 = 1000 — a 2.4% difference. This means log10(2) is very close to 3/10 = 0.3. (The exact value is 0.30103.) This approximation is used constantly in physics: every 10 binary doublings is approximately 3 decimal orders of magnitude.
210 = 1024, which is within 2.4% of 103 = 1000. They are approximately equal. This is why log10(2) is approximately 0.3 — a handy number in physics.

Hover to illuminate any rung

Notice the pattern: as the exponent increases by 1, the value multiplies by the base. Decreasing by 1 divides. Going negative means you are below 1 — fractions, not mysteries.

Preamble II · The Logarithm

What Is a Logarithm?

The logarithm is the inverse of a power. If raising base b to power n gives you x, then the log base b of x is n.

bn = x   ↔   logb(x) = n

Read it as: "log base b of x" = the power you raise b to, to get x.

Preamble III · The Three Rules

Three Rules That Do Everything

All logarithm manipulation reduces to three rules. Memorise these and you can handle any power law.

Rule 1 · Product

log(A · B) = log A + log B

Logs turn multiplication into addition. This is why slide rules and log tables made 18th century astronomy possible — multiplication becomes addition of lengths.

Rule 2 · Power

log(xn) = n · log(x)

Exponents become multipliers. This is the key rule for power laws — the exponent n slides in front of the log.

Rule 3 · Quotient

log(A/B) = log A − log B

Division becomes subtraction. Follows directly from Rules 1 and 2: log(A/B) = log(A · B−1) = log A − log B.

Special Values

logb(b) = 1   logb(1) = 0

log of the base = 1 (b1=b). Log of 1 = 0 (b0=1 for any base). Log of numbers <1 is negative.

Preamble IV · Worked Examples

Build Your Fluency

Work through these yourself before clicking. Each one is a step toward reading power laws naturally.

1.   log10(10 000) = ?
10 000 = 104, so log10(10 000) = 4. Each zero after the 1 is one unit of log10.
10 000 = 104 (ten thousand = ten to the four). Count the zeros after the 1: four zeros, log = 4.
2.   log2(64) = ?
64 = 26 (2,4,8,16,32,64 — six doublings). So log2(64) = 6.
Count doublings: 2,4,8,16,32,64 — that is six steps. 64 = 26, so log2(64) = 6.
3.   log2(1/32) = ?
1/32 = 1/25 = 2−5. Power rule: log2(2−5) = −5. Fractions give negative logs.
32 = 25, so 1/32 = 2−5. Then log2(2−5) = −5 × log2(2) = −5.
4.   log10(0.0001) = ?
0.0001 = 10−4 (four decimal places, four moves left). log10(10−4) = −4.
0.0001 = 1/10 000 = 1/104 = 10−4. So log10(0.0001) = −4. Each decimal place shifts the log by −1.
5.   log10(5 × 103) = ?   (use log10(5) ≈ 0.699)
Product rule: log(5 × 103) = log(5) + log(103) = 0.699 + 3 = 3.699.
Notice: the integer part (3) tells you the order of magnitude; the decimal part (0.699) encodes the coefficient within that order.
Product rule: log(5 × 103) = log(5) + log(103) = 0.699 + 3 = 3.699.
In scientific notation x = a × 10n, log x = log(a) + n. The integer part is the power of ten; the decimal part encodes the leading digits.

Preamble V · Log-Log Plots

The Log-Log Graph

When both axes are logarithmic, a power law y = k · xn becomes a straight line. This is the central visual tool of all the science that follows.

Why? Apply logs to both sides:

Derivation: power law to straight line
y = k · xnStart: a power law relation
log(y) = log(k · xn)Take log of both sides (any base)
log(y) = log(k) + log(xn)Product rule: log(AB) = log A + log B
log(y) = log(k) + n·log(x)Power rule: log(xn) = n·log(x)
Y = C + n·XWhere Y=log(y), X=log(x), C=log(k). A straight line!

Preamble VI · The Map

Y = MX + C: the complete map

You already know Y = MX + C. Now you can see that every power law is secretly this — once you have the right eyes (logarithmic eyes).

The gradient M on a log-log plot is the exponent n in the power law. The intercept C is log(k), where k is the proportionality constant. Read off slope and intercept, write down the law. That is the whole procedure.
