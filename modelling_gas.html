Modelling a Gas

From bouncing molecules to engines — the ideal gas and its laws
IB Physics SL/HL — Topic 3.2 (Legacy) / Topic B.3 (2025+)

Two levels of description

A gas is made of an enormous number of molecules (\(\sim 10^{23}\)) each with its own position and velocity. Tracking every molecule is impossible and unnecessary. Instead, physics offers two complementary descriptions:

Microscopic (kinetic theory): Model the molecules as tiny elastic billiard balls and derive bulk properties from their average behaviour. This gives you the why — why pressure exists, why temperature means kinetic energy.

Macroscopic (thermodynamics): Describe the gas using just four state variables — pressure \(p\), volume \(V\), temperature \(T\), and amount \(n\) — linked by an equation of state: \(pV = nRT\). This gives you the what — what happens when you heat, compress, or expand a gas.

The bridge between the two is the ideal gas model: a set of assumptions about molecules that makes the microscopic description tractable and the macroscopic law exact.

§1 Pressure
What is pressure, and where does it come from at the molecular level?▾

Pressure is force per unit area:

\(p = \dfrac{F}{A}\)

Units: pascal (Pa) = N m⁻². Standard atmospheric pressure ≈ \(1.01 \times 10^5\) Pa.

At the molecular level: gas molecules bounce off the walls of their container. Each collision delivers a tiny impulse. The combined effect of billions of collisions per second per cm² is a smooth, measurable force — which, divided by the wall area, gives pressure.

Pressure is not caused by molecules "pushing" against each other — it's caused by molecules hitting the walls. More collisions per second (higher temperature) or more collisions per unit area (smaller volume) → higher pressure.
A 150 kg person stands on one foot (9.0 cm × 10 cm). Find the pressure on the ground. Then find the snowshoe area needed to stay below \(1.0 \times 10^5\) Pa.▾

Part 1 — Pressure on ground:

\(F = mg = 150 \times 10 = 1500\text{ N}\)
\(A = 0.090 \times 0.10 = 0.0090\text{ m}^2\)
\(p = F/A = 1500/0.0090 = 1.7 \times 10^5\text{ Pa}\)

Part 2 — Snowshoe area:

\(A = F/p = 1500 / (1.0 \times 10^5) = 0.015\text{ m}^2 = 150\text{ cm}^2\)
The snowshoe is only about 70% larger in area than the bare foot, but it drops the pressure below atmospheric — enough to prevent breaking through thin ice. Pressure depends on area, not weight.
§2 The Ideal Gas Model
What are the assumptions of the kinetic theory of an ideal gas?▾

The molecules of an ideal gas:

1. Are identical perfect spheres (no internal structure or rotation).

2. Undergo perfectly elastic collisions with each other and with the walls — no kinetic energy is lost.

3. Have no intermolecular forces — they don't attract or repel each other. Consequently, their potential energy is zero; all internal energy is kinetic.

4. Are so small that their total volume is negligible compared to the container volume.

5. Move randomly in all directions — no preferred direction of motion.

IB exam phrasing: You need to state these assumptions precisely. "Molecules are small" is not enough — say "the volume of the molecules is negligible compared to the volume of the container." "No forces" must specify "no intermolecular forces except during collisions."
How does the kinetic model explain the relationships between \(p\), \(V\), and \(T\)?▾

Temperature ↑ → Pressure ↑ (at constant volume): Higher temperature means higher average molecular speed → molecules hit walls harder and more often → more force per unit area.

Volume ↓ → Pressure ↑ (at constant temperature): Smaller container means less distance between walls → molecules hit each wall more frequently → more force per unit area.

Volume ↓ (rapidly) → Temperature ↑: If you compress a gas quickly (push a piston), you do work on it. By the work-energy theorem, the gas gains kinetic energy → its temperature rises. This is adiabatic heating — it's why a bicycle pump gets hot.

§3 Moles, Molar Mass & the Avogadro Constant
What is a mole, and what is \(N_A\)?▾

One mole of any substance contains exactly \(N_A = 6.02 \times 10^{23}\) particles (atoms or molecules). This is the Avogadro constant.

The molar mass \(M\) of an element is its gram atomic weight — the mass of one mole, in grams. Carbon: \(M = 12.0\) g mol⁻¹. Oxygen: \(M = 16.0\) g mol⁻¹. Water (H₂O): \(M = 2(1.0) + 16.0 = 18.0\) g mol⁻¹.

The number of moles \(n\) in a sample of \(N\) particles:

\(n = \dfrac{N}{N_A}\)

Worked example: How many molecules in 12.25 g of water?

\(n = \dfrac{12.25}{18.0} = 0.680\text{ mol}\)
\(N = nN_A = 0.680 \times 6.02 \times 10^{23} = 4.10 \times 10^{23}\text{ molecules}\)
The mole is the bridge between the lab scale (grams, litres) and the molecular scale (individual atoms). Every gas law calculation passes through it: mass → moles → use \(pV = nRT\).
§4 The Equation of State: \(pV = nRT\)
What is the ideal gas law, and what does each symbol mean?▾
\(pV = nRT\)
SymbolQuantitySI unit
\(p\)PressurePa (= N m⁻²)
\(V\)Volumem³
\(n\)Number of molesmol
\(R\)Universal gas constant8.31 J mol⁻¹ K⁻¹
\(T\)Absolute temperatureK (kelvin)
Temperature must be in kelvin. Convert from Celsius: \(T(\text{K}) = T(°\text{C}) + 273\). Forgetting this is the single most common gas law error.
What is the difference between an equation of state and a dynamical law?▾

\(pV = nRT\) is an equation of state — a thermodynamic constraint that says which combinations of \(p\), \(V\), \(T\), \(n\) are physically possible at equilibrium. It tells you what states exist, but not how the system moves between them.

\(F = ma\) is a dynamical law — a time-evolution rule. Given the current state and forces, it tells you the future state.

Thermodynamics describes the macroscopic world (\(p, V, T, n\)). Dynamics describes the microscopic world (\(x_i, v_i, m_i\) for each molecule). The equation of state is the summary that emerges when you average over \(10^{23}\) molecules.

Many different microscopic configurations (positions and velocities of all molecules) give rise to the same macroscopic state (\(p, V, T\)). The thermodynamic state is an ensemble — a summary of the dynamics, not a replacement for it.
A gas cylinder (volume \(2.0 \times 10^{-2}\) m³) is filled to a pressure of 20 MPa at 17°C. Find (a) the number of moles, (b) the number of atoms.▾

\(T = 17 + 273 = 290\) K. \(p = 20 \times 10^6\) Pa.

(a)

\(n = \dfrac{pV}{RT} = \dfrac{20 \times 10^6 \times 2.0 \times 10^{-2}}{8.31 \times 290} = \dfrac{4.0 \times 10^5}{2410} = 170\text{ mol}\)

(b)

\(N = nN_A = 170 \times 6.02 \times 10^{23} = 1.0 \times 10^{26}\text{ atoms}\)
An ideal gas at 30°C and 6.0 atm is heated at constant volume to 330°C. Find the new pressure.▾

Constant volume and fixed mass: \(n\) and \(V\) are constant. So \(p_1V = nRT_1\) and \(p_2V = nRT_2\). Dividing:

\(\dfrac{p_2}{p_1} = \dfrac{T_2}{T_1}\)

\(T_1 = 30 + 273 = 303\) K. \(T_2 = 330 + 273 = 603\) K.

\(p_2 = p_1 \times \dfrac{T_2}{T_1} = 6.0 \times \dfrac{603}{303} = 12\text{ atm}\)
The temperature doubled (in kelvin), so the pressure doubled. This only works because we used kelvin — in Celsius the ratio 330/30 = 11, which gives nonsense. Always convert first.
Two containers (6 dm³ at 12 atm and 3 dm³ at 6 atm) are connected by a valve at the same temperature. The valve is opened. Find the final pressure.▾

Total moles are conserved. At constant \(T\), \(n = pV/(RT)\), so:

\(n_1 + n_2 = \dfrac{p_1 V_1}{RT} + \dfrac{p_2 V_2}{RT} = \dfrac{p_f(V_1 + V_2)}{RT}\)

The \(RT\) cancels:

\(p_f = \dfrac{p_1 V_1 + p_2 V_2}{V_1 + V_2} = \dfrac{12 \times 6 + 6 \times 3}{6 + 3} = \dfrac{72 + 18}{9} = 10\text{ atm}\)
This is a weighted average of pressures, weighted by volume. It's not simply \((12+6)/2 = 9\) because the containers have different volumes.
§5 Real Gases vs Ideal Gases
How does a real gas differ from an ideal gas, and when does the ideal model break down?▾
PropertyIdeal gasReal gas
Molecular shapeIdentical perfect spheresVarious shapes (N₂, H₂O, NH₃...)
CollisionsPerfectly elasticNot perfectly elastic
Intermolecular forcesNoneAttractive and repulsive
Molecular volumeNegligibleNon-negligible at high density
LiquefactionImpossiblePossible

Real gases approximate ideal behaviour when:

• Low pressure (molecules far apart → forces negligible, molecular volume small compared to container).

• High temperature (high KE → intermolecular forces are a small fraction of kinetic energy).

• Low density (same physical reasoning as low pressure).

Ideal gas behaviour fails dramatically near the liquefaction point — where molecules are close together and moving slowly. Helium at 4 K is as non-ideal as it gets. Nitrogen at room temperature and 1 atm is nearly ideal.
§6 Gas Processes: Isobaric, Isochoric & Isothermal
What are the three named gas processes, and what does each hold constant?▾
ProcessHeld constantResulting lawGraph shape (p-V)
IsobaricPressure \(p\)\(V \propto T\) (Charles's law)Horizontal line
Isochoric (isovolumetric)Volume \(V\)\(p \propto T\) (Gay-Lussac's law)Vertical line
IsothermalTemperature \(T\)\(p_1V_1 = p_2V_2\) (Boyle's law)Hyperbola (\(p \propto 1/V\))

All three are special cases of \(pV = nRT\) with one variable frozen and \(n\) fixed.

The names are Greek: iso = equal; baros = weight/pressure; choros = space/volume; thermos = heat. If you know the Greek roots, the names decode themselves.
Show that \(V \propto T\) for an isobaric process with a fixed amount of gas.▾

Start from \(pV = nRT\). Rearrange: \(V = \dfrac{nR}{p} \cdot T\).

If \(n\) and \(p\) are constant, then \(nR/p\) is a constant. So:

\(V = (\text{const}) \times T \implies V \propto T\)

This is Charles's law. On a \(V\)-\(T\) graph (kelvin), it's a straight line through the origin. On a \(V\)-\(t\) graph (Celsius), it's a straight line with a negative \(x\)-intercept at \(-273°\)C — absolute zero.

Show that \(p \propto T\) for an isochoric process, and explain the graphical significance.▾

From \(pV = nRT\): \(p = \dfrac{nR}{V} \cdot T\).

If \(n\) and \(V\) are constant: \(p = (\text{const}) \times T \implies p \propto T\).

On a \(p\) vs \(T\) graph (kelvin): straight line through the origin with gradient \(nR/V\). On a \(p\) vs \(t\) graph (Celsius): straight line hitting the \(t\)-axis at \(-273°\)C.

This is how absolute zero was first estimated experimentally — by extrapolating a pressure-vs-Celsius graph for a gas at constant volume until \(p = 0\). Every gas gives the same intercept: \(-273.15°\)C.
A graduated syringe in an ice bath shows \(p_1 = 15\), \(V_1 = 10\). After compression at constant temperature: \(p_2 = 30\), \(V_2 = 5\). Verify Boyle's law.▾
\(p_1 V_1 = 15 \times 10 = 150\)
\(p_2 V_2 = 30 \times 5 = 150\)
\(p_1 V_1 = p_2 V_2 \quad \checkmark\)

Why we wait before recording: The compression heats the gas (work done on it). We wait for it to cool back to the ice-bath temperature so the process is truly isothermal.

§7 p-V Diagrams, Work & Thermodynamic Cycles
How do you read a p-V diagram — what do the axes, curves, and areas mean?▾

A p-V diagram is the fundamental tool of thermodynamics. Each point is a state (\(p, V\)). A curve is a process — a sequence of states.

• Vertical line = isochoric (constant \(V\), no work done).

• Horizontal line = isobaric (constant \(p\)).

• Hyperbola = isothermal (constant \(T\)).

• Area under the curve = work done by the gas during that process.

How do you calculate the work done by a gas?▾

For a constant-pressure process:

\(W = p\Delta V\)

If \(\Delta V > 0\) (gas expands), \(W > 0\) — the gas does work on the surroundings. If \(\Delta V < 0\) (gas is compressed), \(W < 0\) — the surroundings do work on the gas.

For a process where \(p\) changes, the work is the area under the p-V curve. This must be calculated geometrically (rectangles + triangles) or by integration.

For an isochoric process (\(\Delta V = 0\)): \(W = 0\). No volume change → no work.

Work = area under the curve is a general principle. For a straight-line process from \((V_1, p_1)\) to \((V_2, p_2)\), the area is a trapezium: \(W = \tfrac{1}{2}(p_1 + p_2)(V_2 - V_1)\).
A gas undergoes the cycle A→B→C→A where A = (10 m³, 2 Pa), B = (10 m³, 8 Pa), C = (25 m³, 8 Pa). Find the work done in each leg and the total cycle work.▾

A→B: Isochoric (\(\Delta V = 0\)): \(W = 0\).

B→C: Isobaric (\(p = 8\) Pa): \(W = p\Delta V = 8 \times (25 - 10) = +120\) J.

C→A: \(p\) changes along a straight line from (25, 8) to (10, 2). The area under this line is a trapezium:

\(W = \tfrac{1}{2}(8 + 2)(10 - 25) = \tfrac{1}{2}(10)(-15) = -75\text{ J}\)

(Negative because the gas is compressed.)

Total cycle work:

\(W_{\text{cycle}} = 0 + 120 + (-75) = +45\text{ J}\)
The net work done in a closed cycle is the area enclosed by the cycle on the p-V diagram. Clockwise cycle → positive work (gas does net work on surroundings = heat engine). Anticlockwise → negative (surroundings do net work on gas = heat pump/refrigerator). Reversing the cycle gives \(W = -45\) J.
§8 Average Kinetic Energy & Internal Energy
What is the average kinetic energy of an ideal gas molecule, and what does it depend on?▾
\(\bar{E}_K = \tfrac{3}{2}k_B T = \dfrac{3RT}{2N_A}\)

where \(k_B = 1.38 \times 10^{-23}\) J K⁻¹ is the Boltzmann constant and \(T\) is in kelvin.

Key observations:

• The average KE depends only on temperature — not on the type of gas, not on its mass, not on the pressure.

• Heavier molecules move more slowly at the same temperature (to have the same average KE).

• At \(T = 0\) K, \(\bar{E}_K = 0\) — all molecular motion stops. This is the microscopic meaning of absolute zero.

\(k_B\) is "the gas constant per molecule" — literally \(k_B = R/N_A\). The equation \(\bar{E}_K = \tfrac{3}{2}k_B T\) is the most direct statement of what temperature is: a measure of average molecular kinetic energy.
2.50 mol of hydrogen gas at 175°C. Find (a) the average KE per atom, (b) the total internal energy, (c) the pressure if the volume is 1.25 m³.▾

\(T = 175 + 273 = 448\) K.

(a) Average KE per atom:

\(\bar{E}_K = \tfrac{3}{2}k_B T = \tfrac{3}{2}(1.38 \times 10^{-23})(448) = 9.27 \times 10^{-21}\text{ J}\)

(b) Total internal energy: For an ideal gas, all internal energy is kinetic. Number of atoms:

\(N = nN_A = 2.50 \times 6.02 \times 10^{23} = 1.51 \times 10^{24}\)
\(U = N\bar{E}_K = 1.51 \times 10^{24} \times 9.27 \times 10^{-21} = 14\,000\text{ J} = 14\text{ kJ}\)

(c) Pressure:

\(p = \dfrac{nRT}{V} = \dfrac{2.50 \times 8.31 \times 448}{1.25} = 7450\text{ Pa}\)
Note: hydrogen gas is H₂ (diatomic), but the Boltzmann KE formula gives the energy per molecule, not per atom. For monoatomic gases the distinction doesn't arise. For diatomic gases in IB, the formula is still applied per molecule — the \(\tfrac{3}{2}\) factor counts translational degrees of freedom only.
What is the relationship between \(R\), \(k_B\), and \(N_A\)?▾
\(R = k_B N_A\)

The gas constant \(R = 8.31\) J mol⁻¹ K⁻¹ is for one mole of particles. The Boltzmann constant \(k_B = 1.38 \times 10^{-23}\) J K⁻¹ is for one particle. They are the same quantity, just scaled by Avogadro's number.

This means \(pV = nRT\) and \(pV = Nk_B T\) are the same equation written in different units — moles vs molecules.

Every gas law equation has a "molar" version (using \(n\) and \(R\)) and a "molecular" version (using \(N\) and \(k_B\)). Use whichever matches what the question gives you.

The entire subject is a negotiation between two descriptions of the same reality. Kinetic theory tells you that pressure is molecules hitting walls and temperature is average kinetic energy. Thermodynamics tells you that \(pV = nRT\) and that processes on a p-V diagram encode the work a gas can do. Neither description is complete without the other — and the ideal gas model is the bridge that connects them.
