Stage 2

The same machine, 109 times bigger

A pumped-storage hydroelectric station is your two experiments run back-to-back. At night — when demand is low and wind or nuclear generation is high — surplus electricity pumps water uphill into a reservoir. During the day — when demand peaks — water flows back down through turbines to regenerate electricity.

The reservoir is a gravitational battery. The physics is identical to your bench experiment: W = ρghV. The only differences are scale and round-trip efficiency.

Stage 4 · Criterion D

Is it a green battery?

Pumped hydro is often described as a "green battery" for the grid. Your experiment gives you the physics to interrogate that claim precisely — not just agree or disagree with it, but ask under what conditions it is or isn't true.

The reservoir stores energy losslessly in the form of gravitational potential energy of water. The losses happen at the pump and turbine stages, not in storage itself. So the 25% round-trip loss is a machine problem, not a physics problem — in principle it could be reduced further.

But conservation of energy also means the electricity used to pump has to come from somewhere. If the grid at 2 a.m. is still partly running on gas, then "charging" the reservoir is partly a gas process — and the 25% round-trip loss makes it less efficient than simply burning the gas directly at peak time.

The case for

Enables large-scale storage of genuinely renewable energy (wind, nuclear). Responds to grid demand within 16 seconds. No carbon emitted during discharge. Dinorwig's upper lake was an existing natural feature — minimal new flooding. Stores energy for hours, unlike batteries.

The case against

25% round-trip loss means 1 kWh pumped returns only ~0.75 kWh. Greenness depends entirely on the energy source used for pumping. New sites require flooding valleys — habitat destruction, community displacement. Geology constrains where it can be built.

The local / global tension

Dinorwig sits inside Snowdonia National Park. The tunnels were bored through the mountain; the infrastructure is hidden. Globally, large dams (Three Gorges, Kariba, Aswan) displaced hundreds of thousands. The scale of the solution changes who bears the cost.

What your numbers say

Your pump reached — efficiency. Dinorwig achieves ~75%. The gap exists because of better engineering — larger pipes, smoother impellers, higher head. Conservation of energy sets the ceiling at 100%; friction sets the real limit.
