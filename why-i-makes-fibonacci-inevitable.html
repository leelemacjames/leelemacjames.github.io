Why i Makes Fibonacci Inevitable

Determinants, Norms, and Recurrence

The imaginary unit isn't decoration. It is the algebraic move that turns a determinant's built-in subtraction into the addition that defines Fibonacci.

But that move only matters because determinants already encode number systems. A 2×2 symmetric determinant produces the difference of two squares. A 3×3 circulant produces a cubic norm form. Tridiagonal chains produce recurrences. The patterns are already there.

The role of \(i\) is simply this: it selects the additive branch of that structure. Fibonacci, Pell, metallic means, Cassini, and cubic norm identities are not separate phenomena. They are different shadows cast by the same determinant machinery.

The determinant already subtracts

A determinant is an alternating machine. Feed it a 2×2 matrix and it hands back one product minus another. That's the whole of it. No extra structure needed:

\[\det\!\begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{pmatrix} = ad - bc\]

Now impose a single symmetry constraint: make the matrix symmetric with equal diagonal entries. That raw subtraction crystallises into something familiar:

\[\det\!\begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ b & a \end{pmatrix} = a^2 - b^2\]

The difference of two squares isn't an isolated trick. It is the simplest structured determinant. That framing matters, because it means we can generalise by changing the matrix rather than guessing at algebraic patterns.

First rung of the ladder

Every odd integer is a structured determinant:

\[n \;=\; a^2 - b^2 \;=\; \det\!\begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ b & a \end{pmatrix}, \qquad a = \tfrac{n+1}{2},\quad b = \tfrac{n-1}{2}\]

Both \(a\) and \(b\) are integers precisely when \(n\) is odd. So the odd integers are exactly the values taken by a symmetric 2×2 determinant over the integers. This is not a trick. It is the simplest instance of a general phenomenon: structured matrices generate structured subsets of the integers. The matrix symmetry type determines which subset. Everything that follows is a consequence of asking what happens when you change the structure.

From a single identity to a whole sequence

One determinant gives one number. But stack identical structure along a tridiagonal spine and the determinants form a sequence, each term determined by the previous two.

Consider the \(n \times n\) tridiagonal matrix with 1s on the diagonal and \(x\) on every neighbouring off-diagonal:

\[K_n(x) \;=\; \det\!\begin{pmatrix} 1 & x & 0 & \cdots \\ x & 1 & x & \cdots \\ 0 & x & 1 & \ddots \\ \vdots & & \ddots & \ddots \end{pmatrix}\]

Expanding along the first row (the standard cofactor move) gives the continuant recurrence:

\[K_n(x) \;=\; K_{n-1}(x) \;-\; x^2\, K_{n-2}(x), \qquad K_0 = 1,\;\; K_1 = 1.\]

That minus sign is structural. It comes from the alternating character of the determinant itself. Every tridiagonal continuant subtracts.

The substitution that changes everything

Now set \(x = i\).

That's it. The entire Fibonacci connection is this one move. Watch what happens to the coefficient of \(K_{n-2}\):

\[-x^2 \;=\; -i^2 \;=\; -(-1) \;=\; +1\]
The key insight

The imaginary unit flips the determinant's built-in minus into a plus. The continuant recurrence \(K_n = K_{n-1} - x^2 K_{n-2}\) becomes \(K_n = K_{n-1} + K_{n-2}\).

That is the Fibonacci recurrence. Not by analogy. Exactly.

So \(i\) isn't a decorative phase factor. It is the precise algebraic choice that converts structured determinant subtraction into the additive recurrence that defines the Fibonacci numbers. With \(K_0 = 1\) and \(K_1 = 1\) we get:

n1234567
Kn(i) 123581321

Fibonacci numbers, shifted by one index. Falling out of determinants of tridiagonal matrices whose off-diagonals happen to be imaginary.

The metallic family: same trick, different amplitude

If \(x = i\) gives Fibonacci, what does \(x = \sqrt{m}\, i\) give? The coefficient becomes:

\[-x^2 \;=\; -(\sqrt{m}\,i)^2 \;=\; -(-m) \;=\; +m\]

So the recurrence generalises to:

\[D_n \;=\; D_{n-1} \;+\; m\, D_{n-2}\]

This is the metallic mean family. Each value of \(m\) picks out a classical sequence:

mOff-diagonalRecurrenceSequence
1 \(i\) \(D_n = D_{n-1} + D_{n-2}\) Fibonacci (golden)
2 \(\sqrt{2}\,i\) \(D_n = D_{n-1} + 2D_{n-2}\) Pell (silver)
3 \(\sqrt{3}\,i\) \(D_n = D_{n-1} + 3D_{n-2}\) Bronze

The whole metallic zoo lives inside one parametric family of tridiagonal determinants. The parameter isn't the ratio; it's the off-diagonal entry. And the imaginary unit is what makes every member additive rather than subtractive.

Cassini as a determinant shadow

Once Fibonacci numbers live inside determinants, Cassini's identity stops being a surprise and starts being a corollary. Take three consecutive Fibonacci numbers \(f_{n-1}, f_n, f_{n+1}\) and build a 2×2 matrix:

\[\det\!\begin{pmatrix} f_n & f_{n-1} \\ f_{n+1} & f_n \end{pmatrix} \;=\; f_n^2 - f_{n-1}\, f_{n+1} \;=\; (-1)^{n+1}\]

The determinant of consecutive Fibonacci numbers never wanders. It oscillates between \(+1\) and \(-1\), forever. Geometrically this is telling you something sharp: the two column vectors of the matrix span a parallelogram of area exactly 1, and the sign records orientation. As \(n\) increases the parallelogram stretches and shears dramatically as Fibonacci numbers grow, yet its signed area flips between \(\pm 1\) at each step. The determinant is counting handedness: each successive Fibonacci pair reverses the orientation of the frame they define.

The bridge. Difference of two squares is a static 2×2 determinant identity. Cassini is a dynamic one: the entries are sequence terms, and the identity holds for every index. The tridiagonal continuant is the engine that makes the dynamic version work.

Two directions of generalisation

A: Same shape, new parameters

Stay tridiagonal but vary the off-diagonal. This gives the metallic family (above), Cassini-type identities for each member, and a clean taxonomy of second-order recurrences parametrised by a single number \(m\).

What about Tribonacci?

Does \(x = i\) in a larger tridiagonal matrix give \(T_n = T_{n-1} + T_{n-2} + T_{n-3}\)?

No. Tridiagonal matrices only couple nearest neighbours, so cofactor expansion always produces a second-order recurrence regardless of size. To encode Tribonacci you need the companion matrix of its characteristic polynomial \(\lambda^3 - \lambda^2 - \lambda - 1 = 0\):

\[C = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}, \qquad C\begin{pmatrix}T_{n-1}\\T_{n-2}\\T_{n-3}\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}T_{n-1}+T_{n-2}+T_{n-3}\\T_{n-1}\\T_{n-2}\end{pmatrix}\]

It is called a companion because it accompanies a polynomial: the top row reads off the recurrence coefficients, and the lower rows shift the state. The recurrence order equals the matrix dimension. Tribonacci needs a genuinely three-dimensional state space; a tridiagonal tweak cannot reach it.

B: Circulant matrices and the Eisenstein norm

A circulant matrix has each row equal to the previous one shifted one place right, wrapping cyclically. The 3×3 case with entries \(a, b, c\):

\[\begin{pmatrix} a & b & c \\ c & a & b \\ b & c & a \end{pmatrix}, \qquad \det = a^3 + b^3 + c^3 - 3abc\]

Over the integers this factors. Expanding \((a+b+c)(a^2+b^2+c^2-ab-bc-ca)\) term by term:

\[\begin{aligned} &a(a^2+b^2+c^2-ab-bc-ca)\\ +\;&b(a^2+b^2+c^2-ab-bc-ca)\\ +\;&c(a^2+b^2+c^2-ab-bc-ca) \end{aligned}\]

Collecting: the \(a^3, b^3, c^3\) terms survive; every cross term \(a^2b\) appears once with a plus sign (from the \(a \cdot a^2\) row of \(b\)'s bracket) and once with a minus sign (from \(b \cdot (-ab)\)), so all six cross-terms cancel, and the \(abc\) terms add to \(-3abc\):

\[(a+b+c)(a^2+b^2+c^2-ab-bc-ca) = a^3+b^3+c^3-3abc \quad \checkmark\]

The quadratic factor \(a^2+b^2+c^2-ab-bc-ca\) is the Eisenstein norm in disguise. Let \(\omega = e^{2\pi i/3}\), the primitive cube root of unity satisfying \(\omega^2+\omega+1=0\), so \(\omega^2 = -\omega-1\). Define \(z = a + \omega b + \omega^2 c\). Using \(\omega^2 = -\omega-1\) this reduces to a two-term element of the Eisenstein integers \(\mathbb{Z}[\omega]\):

\[z = a + \omega b + (-\omega-1)c = (a-c) + (b-c)\,\omega\]

The norm of \(p + q\omega \in \mathbb{Z}[\omega]\) is \(N(p+q\omega) = p^2 - pq + q^2\). With \(p = a-c\), \(q = b-c\):

\[N(z) = (a-c)^2 - (a-c)(b-c) + (b-c)^2 = a^2+b^2+c^2-ab-bc-ca\]

The form \(p^2-pq+q^2\) is the norm form of \(\mathbb{Z}[\omega]\): positive definite, vanishing only at \(p=q=0\), its level sets the hexagonal geometry of the Eisenstein lattice.

What integers does this determinant produce?

The 3×3 circulant determinant \(a^3+b^3+c^3-3abc\) produces a specific arithmetic slice of the integers, exactly as the 2×2 symmetric determinant produces the odd integers. Take three consecutive integers \(a = n+1,\ b = n,\ c = n-1\). Direct expansion gives:

\[(n+1)^3 + n^3 + (n-1)^3 - 3(n+1)n(n-1) = 9n\]

So every multiple of 9 is realised by this determinant. More is true. Using \(s = a+b+c\) and \(q = a^2+b^2+c^2-ab-bc-ca\), we have \(\det = sq\) and the identity \(q = s^2 - 3(ab+bc+ca) \equiv s^2 \pmod{3}\). This forces a rigidity: if the determinant is divisible by 3, then \(3 \mid s\), so \(q \equiv s^2 \equiv 0 \pmod{3}\), and hence \(9 \mid sq\). The determinant can never be congruent to 3 or 6 mod 9.

The representability gap is exact: \(a^3+b^3+c^3-3abc\) takes precisely those integer values not divisible by 3, together with all multiples of 9. Numbers divisible by 3 but not by 9 (such as 3, 6, 12, 15, …) never occur. The prime 3 plays in the cubic setting the role that 2 plays one dimension down.

Why consecutive integers give 9n

Verify \((n+1)^3 + n^3 + (n-1)^3 - 3(n+1)n(n-1) = 9n\) by direct expansion.

Expand the cubes separately:

\[\begin{aligned} (n+1)^3 &= n^3+3n^2+3n+1\\ n^3 &= n^3\\ (n-1)^3 &= n^3-3n^2+3n-1 \end{aligned}\]

Sum: \(3n^3 + 6n\). For the product term:

\[3(n+1)n(n-1) = 3n(n^2-1) = 3n^3 - 3n\]

Subtracting: \((3n^3+6n)-(3n^3-3n) = 9n\). \(\square\)

Why 3, 6, 12, 15, … never occur

Prove that if \(3 \mid (a^3+b^3+c^3-3abc)\) then \(9 \mid (a^3+b^3+c^3-3abc)\).

Write \(\det = sq\) where \(s = a+b+c\) and \(q = a^2+b^2+c^2-ab-bc-ca\). The key identity: \(q = s^2 - 3(ab+bc+ca)\), so \(q \equiv s^2 \pmod{3}\).

Suppose \(3 \mid sq\). Two cases:

Case A: \(3 \mid s\). Then \(q \equiv s^2 \equiv 0 \pmod{3}\), so \(3 \mid q\) as well. Hence \(9 \mid sq\).

Case B: \(3 \nmid s\). Then \(s \equiv \pm 1 \pmod{3}\), so \(s^2 \equiv 1 \pmod{3}\), giving \(q \equiv 1 \pmod{3}\). So \(3 \nmid q\), which means \(3 \nmid sq\). This contradicts our assumption.

Case B is impossible. Therefore \(3 \mid \det\) implies \(9 \mid \det\). \(\square\)

Every multiple of 9 is reachable

Show that the construction \((a,b,c) = (n+1,n,n-1)\) realises every multiple of 9, and give the first three examples.

By the expansion above, this triple gives determinant \(9n\). As \(n\) ranges over all integers, \(9n\) ranges over all multiples of 9. The first three positive examples:

\[(2,1,0) \mapsto 9 \qquad (3,2,1) \mapsto 18 \qquad (4,3,2) \mapsto 27\]

Combined with the previous result, the integers represented by \(a^3+b^3+c^3-3abc\) are exactly those not congruent to 3 or 6 mod 9. \(\square\)

The ladder of norms

\(a^2+b^2\) is the norm in \(\mathbb{Z}[i]\) (Gaussian integers, \(i^2=-1\)). \(a^2-ab+b^2\) is the norm in \(\mathbb{Z}[\omega]\) (Eisenstein integers, \(\omega^2+\omega+1=0\)). The 3×3 circulant determinant, after extracting \((a+b+c)\), delivers the Eisenstein norm with \(p = a-c\), \(q = b-c\). The matrix symmetry type selects the ring.

The principle, stated precisely

Determinant–recurrence principle

Let \(D_n\) be the determinant of an \(n \times n\) band matrix with fixed diagonals. Then \((D_n)\) satisfies a linear recurrence, and the order of that recurrence equals the bandwidth:

  • Tridiagonal → second order
  • Pentadiagonal → third order
  • Circulant → homogeneous polynomial (norm-type) forms

The determinant encodes the recurrence automatically via cofactor expansion. No further argument is needed: the structure of the matrix determines the structure of the sequence.

Imaginary substitution

Setting \(x = \sqrt{m}\,i\) converts the intrinsic subtraction of the tridiagonal determinant into addition:

\[D_n = D_{n-1} - x^2 D_{n-2} \;\longrightarrow\; D_n = D_{n-1} + m\,D_{n-2}\]

This single move generates Fibonacci (\(m=1\)), Pell (\(m=2\)), and the entire metallic family from one structural source. The imaginary unit does not create the recurrence. It selects the additive branch of a structure that was already there.

Exercises

Exercise A · the gap

The symmetric 2×2 determinant produces all odd integers. What does it produce for even \(n\)? Show that \(a^2 - b^2 = n\) has no integer solution when \(n \equiv 2 \pmod{4}\), and find integer solutions for all \(n \equiv 0 \pmod{4}\).

\(a^2 - b^2 = (a+b)(a-b)\). If \(a,b\) are both integers, then \(a+b\) and \(a-b\) have the same parity. So their product is either odd×odd = odd, or even×even = multiple of 4. It can never be \(\equiv 2 \pmod 4\). For \(n = 4k\), take \(a = k+1\), \(b = k-1\): then \((a+b)(a-b) = 2k \cdot 2 = 4k\). So the symmetric 2×2 determinant over the integers produces exactly the integers not congruent to 2 mod 4: all odd numbers and all multiples of 4.

Exercise B · discover continuants

For the tridiagonal matrix with diagonal 1 and off-diagonal \(x\), compute \(K_n\) for \(n = 1, 2, 3, 4\). Guess and prove the recurrence.

\(K_1 = 1\), \(K_2 = 1 - x^2\), \(K_3 = 1 - 2x^2\), \(K_4 = 1 - 3x^2 + x^4\). The pattern is \(K_n = K_{n-1} - x^2 K_{n-2}\), proved by cofactor expansion along the first row: the \((1,1)\) minor gives \(K_{n-1}\), the \((1,2)\) minor gives \(x \cdot x \cdot K_{n-2}\) with a sign from the alternating determinant, yielding \(-x^2 K_{n-2}\).

Exercise C · the substitution

Set \(x = i\). Explain algebraically why the continuant recurrence becomes the Fibonacci recurrence.

The recurrence \(K_n = K_{n-1} - x^2 K_{n-2}\) has coefficient \(-x^2\). With \(x = i\): \(-x^2 = -i^2 = -(-1) = +1\). So the recurrence becomes \(K_n = K_{n-1} + K_{n-2}\), the Fibonacci recurrence exactly. The imaginary unit doesn't change the matrix structure; it flips the sign of \(x^2\) from positive to negative, converting subtraction into addition.

Exercise D · metallic family

Choose \(x = \sqrt{m}\, i\) and prove \(D_n = D_{n-1} + m D_{n-2}\). Compute the first eight terms for \(m = 2\) (Pell).

\(-x^2 = -(\sqrt{m}\,i)^2 = -(-m) = +m\), so the recurrence becomes \(D_n = D_{n-1} + m\,D_{n-2}\). For \(m = 2\) with \(D_0 = 1, D_1 = 1\): the first eight terms are \(1, 1, 3, 5, 11, 21, 43, 85\). These are the Pell numbers (shifted). The ratio \(D_n / D_{n-1}\) converges to \(1 + \sqrt{2}\), the silver mean.

Exercise E · Eisenstein norm

Let \(\omega = e^{2\pi i/3}\) and \(z = a + \omega b + \omega^2 c\). Compute \(z\bar{z}\) directly and verify it equals \(a^2+b^2+c^2-ab-bc-ca\). Then show \((a+b+c)\cdot z\bar{z} = a^3+b^3+c^3-3abc\).

\(\bar{z} = a + \omega^2 b + \omega c\). Expanding \(z\bar{z}\) using \(\omega^3=1\) and \(1+\omega+\omega^2=0\): \(z\bar{z} = a^2 + b^2 + c^2 + ab(\omega^2+\omega) + bc(\omega+\omega^2) + ca(\omega^2+\omega) = a^2+b^2+c^2 - ab - bc - ca\), since \(\omega+\omega^2 = -1\). For the second part: \((a+b+c)(a^2+b^2+c^2-ab-bc-ca)\). Expand: collect \(a^3\) terms to get \(a^3+b^3+c^3\), then cross terms cancel to leave \(-3abc\). Done.

Exercise F · challenge

The Eisenstein norm \(a^2 - ab + b^2\) is the norm in \(\mathbb{Z}[\omega]\). The Gaussian norm is \(a^2 + b^2\), the norm in \(\mathbb{Z}[i]\). Which 2×2 structured matrix has \(a^2 + b^2\) as its determinant, and what does its entry structure imply about the underlying ring?

\(\det\!\begin{pmatrix}a & -b \\ b & a\end{pmatrix} = a^2 + b^2\). The matrix represents multiplication by \(a + bi\) in \(\mathbb{Z}[i]\): its columns are the images of \(1\) and \(i\) under that map. The determinant is the Gaussian norm \(N(a+bi) = a^2+b^2\). Comparing: the symmetric matrix \(\begin{pmatrix}a&b\\b&a\end{pmatrix}\) gives \(a^2-b^2\) (split integers \(\mathbb{Z}[\sqrt{1}]\)); the skew matrix gives \(a^2+b^2\) (Gaussian integers \(\mathbb{Z}[i]\)). The off-diagonal sign encodes whether the extension is real or imaginary.

Coda

Determinants do not merely compute values. They classify arithmetic structures. Quadratic norms, cubic norms, and linear recurrences all arise from symmetry choices in matrices: the symmetry type of the matrix determines which number system it inhabits.

This is the Erlangen programme applied to arithmetic. Just as Klein classified geometries by their symmetry groups, we can classify integer sequences and norm forms by the matrix structures that generate them. The 2×2 symmetric determinant corresponds to \(\mathbb{Z}[i]\) and \(\mathbb{Z}[\sqrt{m}]\). The 3×3 circulant corresponds to \(\mathbb{Z}[\omega]\), the Eisenstein integers. The tridiagonal family corresponds to metallic recurrences.

The imaginary unit \(i\), or more generally the cyclotomic elements \(i\) and \(\omega\), do not create these structures. They select which branch behaves additively. Setting \(x = \sqrt{m}\,i\) in a tridiagonal determinant does not invent Fibonacci: it reveals that Fibonacci was always sitting inside the subtractive structure, waiting for the sign to flip.

That is what "inevitable" means.
