IB Physics — Topic C

Refraction, Diffraction & Interference

What happens when waves change medium, pass through slits, and overlap — the experiments that proved light is a wave.

C9 C10 C11 C12 C13

What this section is about

This section covers three wave behaviours that only make sense if light is a wave: refraction (bending at boundaries), diffraction (spreading through apertures), and interference (superposition producing bright and dark fringes). These phenomena were historically decisive — Young's double-slit experiment in 1801 effectively settled the wave-vs-particle debate for a century, until Einstein reopened it with the photoelectric effect.

Slides C9–C13: Snell's law and refractive index, critical angle and total internal reflection, single-slit diffraction (AHL), Young's double-slit interference, and diffraction gratings. Each topic has one or two key equations — learn what they mean physically, not just how to plug numbers in.

The Equation Map

Five equations cover this entire section. Know which applies where:

n₁ sin θ₁ = n₂ sin θ₂ — Snell's law (refraction at any boundary)

sin θc = n₂/n₁ — critical angle (TIR condition, n₁ > n₂)

sin θ = 1.22λ/a — first minimum, single-slit diffraction (AHL)

s = λD/d — fringe spacing, double-slit interference

nλ = d sin θ — diffraction grating (maxima positions)

SL C9 — Refraction & Snell's Law

Wavefronts are surfaces connecting points of equal phase. Rays are perpendicular to wavefronts, showing the direction of propagation.

Refraction occurs when a wave crosses a boundary between media with different speeds. The wave bends because one side of the wavefront changes speed before the other.

n₁ sin θ₁ = n₂ sin θ₂    (Snell's Law)

sin θ₁ / sin θ₂ = v₁/v₂ = n₂/n₁

The absolute refractive index of a medium: n = c/v, where c is the speed of light in vacuum and v is the speed in the medium. Higher n → slower light → more bending toward the normal.

Mediumnv (×10⁸ m/s)
Vacuum1.0003.00
Air1.0003~3.00
Water1.332.26
Glass (typical)1.502.00
Diamond2.421.24

Angles are measured from the NORMAL, not the surface. θ₁ and θ₂ are both angles between the ray and the line perpendicular to the boundary. Students who measure from the surface get the complement and everything goes wrong.

"Toward the normal" means the angle gets smaller. Light going from fast → slow (low n → high n) bends toward the normal (θ₂ < θ₁). Light going from slow → fast bends away from the normal (θ₂ > θ₁). Memory: think of walking from pavement into mud at an angle — the foot that hits the mud first slows down, and you pivot toward the normal.

Q1Light hits a glass surface (n = 1.50) at 40° to the normal from air. What is the angle of refraction?▶
Answer

n₁ sin θ₁ = n₂ sin θ₂ → 1.00 × sin 40° = 1.50 × sin θ₂

sin θ₂ = 0.6428/1.50 = 0.4285 → θ₂ = 25.4°

Light bends toward the normal (entering a denser medium).

Q2Does refraction change the frequency, wavelength, or speed of the wave?▶
Answer

Speed changes (v = c/n). Wavelength changes (λ = v/f, so λ decreases in denser medium). Frequency does NOT change — it's determined by the source, not the medium. This is critical: frequency is preserved across all boundaries. If it weren't, energy conservation would be violated at the interface.

SL C10 — Critical Angle & Total Internal Reflection

When light travels from a denser medium to a less dense medium (n₁ > n₂), the refracted ray bends away from the normal. At a specific angle — the critical angle θc — the refracted ray runs along the boundary (θ₂ = 90°). Beyond θc, no refraction occurs: all light reflects back. This is total internal reflection (TIR).

sin θc = n₂/n₁    (n₁ > n₂)
Three cases at a dense-to-less-dense boundary. Below θc: partial refraction + partial reflection. At θc: refracted ray grazes the surface. Above θc: total internal reflection — no transmitted ray.
Worked Example — Glass to Water

Given: nglass = 1.60, nwater = 1.33.

sin θc = nwater/nglass = 1.33/1.60 = 0.8313 θc = arcsin(0.8313) = 56.2°

Any light inside the glass hitting the glass-water boundary at > 56.2° will be totally internally reflected.

TIR only occurs when going from denser → less dense (n₁ > n₂). It can NEVER happen when light enters a denser medium. Students sometimes try to apply the critical angle formula the wrong way round — if n₁ < n₂, sin θc > 1, which has no solution.

Q1Why does optical fibre use TIR rather than mirrors to guide light?▶
Answer

TIR is 100% efficient — no light is lost at each reflection (assuming the angle exceeds θc). Mirrors always absorb a small fraction. Over thousands of reflections across kilometres of fibre, even tiny losses per reflection would be devastating. TIR also works in a flexible cable — the geometry doesn't need to be precise, just ensure all angles exceed θc.

Q2What is the critical angle for diamond in air?▶
Answer

sin θc = nair/ndiamond = 1.00/2.42 = 0.413. θc = 24.4°. This very small critical angle is why diamonds sparkle — light entering the top is almost always totally internally reflected at the back facets, bouncing around inside before exiting the top face. The cutting geometry is designed to exploit this.

AHL C11 — Single-Slit Diffraction

When a wave passes through a single aperture of width a, it diffracts — spreads out. Different parts of the wavefront interfere, producing a central bright maximum flanked by alternating dark and bright fringes.

The angular position of the first minimum:

sin θ = 1.22λ/a    (circular aperture)

sin θ = λ/a    (rectangular slit — the IB sometimes uses this simpler form)

The 1.22 factor applies specifically to circular apertures (the Airy pattern). For a rectangular slit, the first minimum is at sin θ = λ/a. Check which your question specifies.

Key features of the pattern:

  • Central maximum is twice as wide as the secondary maxima.
  • Central maximum contains most of the light intensity (~84% for a rectangular slit).
  • Narrower slit → wider diffraction pattern (more spreading). Wider slit → narrower pattern.
  • Longer wavelength → wider pattern. Shorter wavelength → narrower pattern.

Diffraction ≠ interference (though they're related). Diffraction is the spreading of a wave through a single aperture. Interference is the superposition of waves from two or more sources. In a double-slit experiment, both happen simultaneously: each slit diffracts, and the two diffracted waves interfere. The observed pattern is an interference pattern modulated by a diffraction envelope.

Q1Light of wavelength 550 nm passes through a 0.10 mm slit. What is the angular width of the central maximum?▶
Answer

First minimum at sin θ = λ/a = 550 × 10⁻⁹ / 1.0 × 10⁻⁴ = 5.5 × 10⁻³.

θ = 0.315°. The central maximum extends from −θ to +θ, so the full angular width = 0.63° ≈ 11 mrad.

Q2If you make the slit narrower, what happens to the diffraction pattern?▶
Answer

The pattern spreads out (wider central maximum). sin θ = λ/a — smaller a means larger θ. This is counterintuitive: a smaller opening produces a wider spread. In the limit a → 0, the wave spreads in all directions (like a point source). In the limit a → ∞, no spreading occurs (geometric shadow). Diffraction is most pronounced when a ≈ λ.

SL C12 — Double-Slit Interference (Young's Experiment)

Two coherent sources (same frequency, constant phase relationship) produce an interference pattern of equally-spaced bright and dark fringes on a screen.

Fringe spacing: s = λD/d

Where λ = wavelength, D = slit-to-screen distance, d = slit separation.

Conditions for fringes:

  • Bright (constructive): path difference = nλ (n = 0, 1, 2…)
  • Dark (destructive): path difference = (n + ½)λ

The sources must be coherent — this is why Young used a single source illuminating both slits. A laser is the modern coherent source of choice. Two separate light bulbs would not produce a stable pattern.

Young's experiment (1801) was arguably the most important physics experiment of the 19th century. It demonstrated that light produces interference fringes — behaviour that only waves can exhibit. This effectively killed Newton's corpuscular theory for 100 years, until Einstein showed that light also behaves as particles (photons). Can a single experiment settle a scientific debate? Or does the history of wave-particle duality show that "settled" questions can reopen?

Q1Slits 0.50 mm apart, screen 2.0 m away, λ = 630 nm. What is the fringe spacing?▶
Answer

s = λD/d = (630 × 10⁻⁹ × 2.0) / (0.50 × 10⁻³) = 1.26 × 10⁻⁶ / 5.0 × 10⁻⁴ = 2.52 × 10⁻³ m = 2.5 mm

Q2What happens to the fringe pattern if you use white light instead of monochromatic?▶
Answer

The central fringe (path difference = 0) is white (all wavelengths interfere constructively at the centre). Moving outward, each wavelength has a different fringe spacing (s ∝ λ), so the fringes spread into a spectrum: blue fringes closer to the centre, red fringes further out. After a few orders, the colours overlap and the pattern becomes a washed-out white — no more distinct fringes.

Q3Why must the sources be coherent?▶
Answer

Interference requires a stable phase relationship between the two waves. If the sources are incoherent (random phase fluctuations), the bright and dark fringes shift position randomly — much faster than your eye or detector can follow. The time-averaged result is uniform illumination: no visible pattern. Coherent sources maintain a fixed phase difference, so the fringe positions are stable and observable.

SL C13 — Diffraction Grating

A diffraction grating is many parallel slits (hundreds or thousands per mm). It produces the same interference pattern as a double slit, but with much sharper, brighter maxima — the more slits, the narrower and more intense each bright fringe.

nλ = d sin θn

Where n = order (0, 1, 2…), d = slit spacing, θn = angle to the nth maximum. The zero-order maximum (n = 0) is always at θ = 0 (straight through).

Finding d from the number of lines: A grating with 600 lines/mm has d = 1/600 mm = 1.67 × 10⁻⁶ m = 1.67 μm.

Worked Example — Maximum Order

Question: A grating with 600 lines/mm is used with 550 nm light. What is the highest order visible?

d = 1.67 × 10⁻⁶ m. For maximum order: sin θ ≤ 1. nλ ≤ d → n ≤ d/λ = 1.67 × 10⁻⁶ / 550 × 10⁻⁹ = 3.04 nmax = 3 (must be an integer)

The grating equation nλ = d sin θ looks identical to the double-slit condition mλ = d sin θ. The physics is the same — constructive interference from path differences of whole wavelengths. The difference is practical: a grating has hundreds of slits, so the maxima are extremely sharp (narrow peaks) and the minima between them are very dark. A double slit has broad, fuzzy fringes. The grating is far superior for measuring wavelengths precisely, which is why spectroscopy uses gratings, not double slits.

Q1A grating with 300 lines/mm shows the 2nd-order maximum at 19.5°. What is the wavelength?▶
Answer

d = 1/300 mm = 3.33 × 10⁻⁶ m.

λ = d sin θ / n = 3.33 × 10⁻⁶ × sin 19.5° / 2 = 3.33 × 10⁻⁶ × 0.3338 / 2

λ = 556 nm (green light).

Q2Why do gratings produce sharper maxima than double slits?▶
Answer

With N slits, the condition for constructive interference becomes extremely specific — all N waves must arrive in phase. If the angle deviates even slightly from the exact maximum, some of the N waves start cancelling. The more slits, the more sensitive the cancellation, and the narrower the peak. Mathematically, the angular width of each maximum is proportional to 1/N. A grating with 1000 slits produces peaks 500× narrower than a double slit.

Q3Why are higher-order maxima (n = 2, 3…) further apart when using white light?▶
Answer

sin θ = nλ/d. For higher n, the spread of angles between red (long λ) and violet (short λ) is larger: Δ(sin θ) = nΔλ/d. At n = 1, the spread is small — the spectrum is compact. At n = 2, the spread doubles. At n = 3, the spectra from adjacent orders can overlap (red of order 3 overlaps with violet of order 4). This limits the useful number of orders.
