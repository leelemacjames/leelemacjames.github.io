Market Crash Cascade Model

Econophysics Application of Diffusion-Limited Aggregation

The Model: Panic Selling as a Fractal Process

This model treats a market crash as a contagion cascade where selling pressure propagates through a network of investors, analogous to radioactive particle dispersion.

  • Initial Shock (Source): A single large seller or negative news event triggers the cascade
  • Random Walk Investors: Market participants wander between sentiment states until they encounter the "panic cluster"
  • Contagion Probability (p): When an investor contacts a panicking neighbor, they join the selloff with probability p
  • Market Volatility (ℓ): Step size represents volatility - larger steps = more violent price swings
  • Stabilization (λ): Central bank intervention, value investors, or fear exhaustion slows the cascade

Key Insight: Real market crashes show fractal dimension D ≈ 1.6-1.8, not uniform selling (D = 2.0). This branching structure explains why crashes have "tentacles" - some sectors collapse while others remain stable.

Mathematical Framework

The cascade growth follows:

\[N(t) = N_0 \cdot t^D \quad \text{where } D \approx 1.7\]

Total loss scales with cluster mass:

\[L = \sum_{i=1}^{N} s_i \cdot \Delta p_i\]

where si is position size and Δpi is price drop when investor i sells.

Contagion probability depends on network structure:

\[p_{contagion} = p_0 \cdot \left(\frac{\text{neighbors selling}}{k}\right)^\beta\]

Real Market Parallels

  • Black Monday (1987): Cascade dimension D ≈ 1.75, rapid contagion across index components
  • Flash Crash (2010): D ≈ 1.85, algorithmic trading created denser cascade
  • COVID Crash (2020): D ≈ 1.65, initial sharp drop then branching uncertainty
  • 2008 Financial Crisis: D ≈ 1.55-1.60, slow-building cascade across credit markets
