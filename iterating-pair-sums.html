What happens when you keep pair-summing

Take a sorted triple. Replace it with the three pair-sums, sorted. Repeat. The span never changes. The shape flips. The whole orbit collapses to a two-parameter closed form. Toggle the corners and watch.

Why the rule looks like it does

Before any sequence, the geometry. A triangle has three vertices and three edges. Label the vertices with our three numbers a, b, c. Each edge naturally carries the sum of its two endpoints: the edge between a and b carries a+b, the edge between a and c carries a+c, the edge between b and c carries b+c.

Now perform the dual operation: make the edges into the new vertices. The triangle flips. Its three edges become its three new vertices, carrying the pair-sums as their values. The triangle has eaten its own boundary, and the new triangle is upside-down with respect to the first.

The middle pair-sum (gold) a + c · always becomes the middle vertex of the next triangle
The largest pair-sum (red) b + c · always becomes the largest vertex of the next triangle

Look at the diagram for a moment without me narrating. The three things to notice are these.

One. Each triangle is upside-down relative to the previous one. This is the geometric content of the operation: vertices have become edges, edges have become vertices. The triangle is its own dual in this sense, which is why iteration is possible at all. K3 is the unique complete graph for which vertices and edges have the same cardinality (3 each), so the dual lives in the same space as the original.

Two. The colour of each new vertex tells you its parentage. A blue vertex came from the smallest edge of the previous triangle. A gold vertex came from the middle edge. A red vertex came from the largest edge. The lineage is unambiguous because the ordering a + b < a + c < b + c is forced (it always holds when a < b < c), so "smallest", "middle", "largest" are well-defined at every step.

Three. Trace any one colour through the diagram. Follow the gold path from step 0 to step 3: the edge labelled 15 becomes the vertex labelled 15 becomes the edge labelled 31 becomes the vertex labelled 31 becomes the edge labelled 61 becomes the vertex labelled 61 becomes the edge labelled 123. This is the middle sequence, in geometric form. Each value spends one step as an edge of one triangle and the next step as a vertex of the next.

The rule (a, b, c) → (a+b, a+c, b+c) looks arbitrary when written algebraically. The flipping triangles show it is not arbitrary at all. It is the inevitable consequence of saying: edges become vertices, vertices become edges, repeat. The values just come along for the ride.

The set-up

Pick three numbers a < b < c. The three pair-sums in sorted order are (a+b, a+c, b+c) — smallest = small + middle, middle = small + large, largest = middle + large. So pair-summing is a self-map on sorted triples. You can do it again. And again.

Notice what happens. The first differences of the minima sequence are exactly the values of the middle sequence. The first differences of the maxima sequence are also exactly the middle sequence. This is because the new minimum is old min + old middle, and the new maximum is old max + old middle — both grow by exactly the middle value at each step. It is the cleanest possible algebra, falling straight out of the definition of "sorted pair-sums".

What survives the iteration

If you stare at the table long enough, the same number stares back at you from the right-most column. The span — max minus min — is fixed forever. Whatever the starting triple, whatever the step, the span is invariant.

The proof is one line. If the triple is (a, b, c), the new triple in sort order is (a+b, a+c, b+c). The new span is (b+c) − (a+b) = c − a, the old span. The middle vertex cancels.

cn − an  =  c0 − a0   for all n
an invariant of the iteration

This is the simplest kind of invariant: a quantity preserved under the dynamics. In mathematics the search for invariants is the search for what is real about a system — what survives every transformation. Here, the span survives. Everything else changes.

The other invariant: the gap multiset

Look at the left gap (mid − min) and the right gap (max − mid). In the (5, 8, 10) example they are 3 and 2. After one step they are 2 and 3. They have swapped.

Algebra: the new gaps are (a+c) − (a+b) = c − b = R_old and (b+c) − (a+c) = b − a = L_old. The iteration permutes the two gaps. So the multiset {L, R} is invariant, but the assignment of which-is-which has period 2.

This is your first encounter with a structure that has two flavours of invariance: a strictly-conserved quantity (the span) and a quantity whose value alternates but whose multiset is conserved (the gaps). In physics this distinction is the difference between a strict conservation law and a discrete symmetry. The triple iteration has both, in miniature.

The sign of the gap difference

Define gn = Ln − Rn at step n — the gap difference, signed. From the swap rule (the iteration permutes L and R) we have gn+1 = −gn. So the sign alternates every step. If you started with L > R it flips to L < R, then back, then forward, forever.

Why does this matter? Because g shows up in the recursion for the middle sequence. The new middle is a + c. Using g = (b − a) − (c − b) = 2b − a − c, we get a + c = 2b − g. So:

bn+1 = 2 · bn − gn
gn alternates between +g0 and −g0

This is "almost doubling, with a sign-flipping wobble." The current sign-of-gap-difference flag for your triple is:

The middle sequence almost doubles each step — the ratio bn+1 / bn converges to 2 — but the wobble of ±g0 prevents it from being a pure power-of-two scaling. That wobble is the entire content of the sign analysis, and it is what makes the closed form interesting rather than trivial.

The closed form

Solving the recursion bn+1 = 2 bn − gn, with gn = g0 · (−1)n, gives a clean closed form. Let T = a + b + c be the sum of the original triple, and g = L − R = 2b − a − c be the initial gap difference (positive if L > R, negative if L < R). Then for all n ≥ 0:

bn = (T · 2n + g · (−1)n) / 3
middle of the n-th triple, in closed form

Two parameters, T and g, pin the entire orbit. Different triples that share the same T and g have identical middle sequences. The triple has been compressed to two numbers.

The Jacobsthal connection

The Jacobsthal numbers are 0, 1, 1, 3, 5, 11, 21, 43, 85, 171, ..., defined by Jn+1 = Jn + 2 Jn−1 with J0 = 0, J1 = 1. Their closed form is

Jn = (2n − (−1)n) / 3
the Jacobsthal numbers, in closed form

This is exactly our formula with T = 1, g = −1. So Jacobsthal is the canonical case of our family. (No actual integer triple realises T = 1 and g = −1 simultaneously — the Jacobsthal sequence is the algebraic generator, not a literal orbit.)

Compare to ours. Our middle sequence is

bn = (T / 3) · 2n  +  (g / 3) · (−1)n
an affine combination of 2n and (−1)n

Both sequences live in the same two-dimensional space spanned by 2n and (−1)n. The orbit of every triple under pair-sum iteration is a linear combination of these two basic sequences. The two coefficients T/3 and g/3 determine which member of this family you've landed on.

When the orbit hits a shifted Jacobsthal sequence

For the orbit to match shifted Jacobsthal numbers Jn+k, we need

bn = Jn+k = (2n+k − (−1)n+k) / 3 = (2k · 2n − (−1)k · (−1)n) / 3
the condition for a Jacobsthal alignment

Matching coefficients with our formula: this happens precisely when T = 2k and g = −(−1)k, i.e. when T is a power of 2 and g has the right sign parity. The preset (1, 5, 10) has T = 16 = 24 and g = −1, so it lands on Jn+4 = 5, 11, 21, 43, 85, 171, … — the middles match J4, J5, J6, J7, J8, J9 precisely.

So Jacobsthal alignments are not rare. Whenever the triple sum is a power of 2 and the gap difference is ±1 with the right parity, the orbit lands on shifted Jacobsthal numbers. The other small case worth knowing: (2, 3, 5) has T = 10, g = −1, so its middle sequence is (10 · 2n − (−1)n) / 3 = 3, 7, 13, 27, 53, 107, … which is not Jacobsthal but lives in the same family — same g but T ≠ 2k.

The two-parameter family — what T and g actually control

Every orbit is pinned by two numbers, T and g. They play distinct roles. T sets the exponential growth: the sequence grows like (T/3) · 2n. g sets the amplitude of the wobble: the oscillation around the trend has amplitude |g/3|.

The ratio |g|/T tells you how visible the wobble is relative to the trend. When |g|/T is tiny, the sequence looks like clean doubling. When |g|/T is large, the oscillation is conspicuous.

The boundary: when does |g| approach T?

For a triple with strictly positive values and a < b < c, the inequality |g| < T always holds. The proof is direct: |g| = |2b − a − c| ≤ max(2b − a − c, a + c − 2b). For positive a, b, c with a < b < c, both quantities are smaller than a + b + c = T. Equality would require some of a, b, c to be zero or negative.

This is the algebraic statement that the trend always dominates the wobble. The middle sequence bn = (T · 2n + g · (−1)n) / 3 stays positive for all n precisely because |g| < T. The positivity of the triple and the dominance of the exponential trend are the same constraint. Step outside that boundary — by allowing negative or zero values — and the sequence can develop strange behaviour like passing through zero.

So the (T, g) parameter plane has a clean structure: the strip |g| < T contains every realisable orbit from a positive ordered triple. The line g = 0 is the spine of pure doubling. Lines of constant T or constant g are families of orbits with shared growth rate or shared wobble. The Jacobsthal numbers and their shifts are an arithmetic progression of distinguished points at T = 2k, g = ±1.

All three sequences grow roughly as 2n, but they are separated by the span. On a log scale they would be three parallel lines getting asymptotically closer. The proportional convergence — span / mean → 0 — is the iteration's way of saying "the gaps stay fixed in absolute size but become negligible compared to the magnitudes."

Why this only works for triples

The pair-sum operation on n vertices produces C(n, 2) = n(n−1)/2 edges. For this to be a self-map — for the output to be the same size as the input, so we can iterate — we need n(n−1)/2 = n, which forces n = 3.

Triples are the unique fixed point of the cardinality count. K3 is the only complete graph whose edges and vertices balance. Pair-summing is therefore a self-map only on triples, and this is the structural reason that triples have such a clean iteration theory while quadruples and higher do not.

If you wanted to iterate on a quadruple, you would need to first define a way of cutting six pair-sums back down to four numbers. There are many natural choices — take the four smallest, take the four edges of a Hamiltonian cycle, take the four sums adjacent to each vertex — and each gives its own dynamical system, none of which is as clean as the triple case.

This is a small but real example of a deep mathematical principle: self-similarity requires balance. Iterating a transformation needs the output to be of the same type as the input, and that is a hard combinatorial constraint. The reason fractals exist is that the scaling map is a self-map. The reason renormalisation works is that the renormalisation step is a self-map. Triple iteration works for exactly the same reason: pair-sum balances against itself only at n = 3.

The three eigenmodes of the iteration

So far we have done arithmetic on triples. Now let us look at the iteration as a single object — a matrix — and see what it really is.

The rule (a, b, c) → (a+b, a+c, b+c) can be written as a multiplication. Each new value is a sum of the old values, with a 1 wherever the old value contributes and a 0 wherever it does not. Reading off the coefficients gives a 3×3 grid of 1s and 0s:

Hunting for eigenvectors

Here is the new question. We know what M does to any triple — it produces the next one. Are there some special triples that M handles particularly cleanly? Triples that come out looking like themselves, only scaled?

A vector v is called an eigenvector of M if applying M to v gives back a multiple of v — not a brand-new direction, but the same direction with a scaling factor. The scaling factor is called the eigenvalue and is usually written λ ("lambda"). The defining equation is:

M · v  =  λ · v
M leaves the direction of v alone, only stretches or flips it

How do you find eigenvectors of a matrix? For a beginner there is a beautiful method: guess symmetric directions and check. The matrix M has a lot of symmetry — its rows and columns are permutations of each other — so any direction that respects that symmetry has a good chance of being an eigenvector. Let us try the three most symmetric guesses on a sorted triple.

Three guesses, three hits. We have found all three eigenvectors of M, with eigenvalues 2, 1, and −1.

This is what eigenvectors are: special directions a matrix leaves alone, only scaling. For our matrix M, those directions correspond exactly to the three things the iteration does — grow (λ=2), preserve (λ=1), and flip (λ=−1). The matrix has no fourth thing to do. Any starting triple is some blend of these three directions, and the iteration acts on each blend independently.

Every starting triple (a, b, c) decomposes uniquely as a sum of components along these three eigenvectors. After n steps, each component is multiplied by its eigenvalue raised to the n-th power. Since the eigenvalues are 2, 1, and −1, this gives three terms with growth factors 2n, 1, (−1)n — and these are precisely the building blocks of our closed form.

Each step decomposed: λ=2 component (gold) doubles · λ=1 component (green) sits still · λ=−1 component (red) flips

The closed form, properly understood

Expanding the eigendecomposition for the middle coordinate b alone gives the formula we already derived. The (1,1,1) component contributes (T/3) · 2n. The (1,0,−1) component contributes nothing to b (its middle entry is 0). The (1,−2,1) component contributes (g/3) · (−1)n with the right sign convention. Adding:

bn = (T · 2n + g · (−1)n) / 3
the closed form, now in its proper home as an eigenmode sum

The same exercise gives the minimum and maximum sequences. The decomposition of (a, b, c) in the eigenbasis has coefficients (T/3, −S/2, −g/6) where S = c − a, and each coordinate of the triple picks up a different combination:

an = T·2n/3  −  S/2  −  g·(−1)n/6
bn = T·2n/3  +  g·(−1)n/3
cn = T·2n/3  +  S/2  −  g·(−1)n/6
all three sequences, decomposed into the three eigenmodes

Notice the structure. The growth term T·2n/3 is the same in all three — they share the doubling. The constant ±S/2 separates the minima and maxima from the middle (which has no constant term because the eigenvector (1, 0, −1) has a zero middle entry). The oscillation term hits the middle harder than the extremes — coefficient 1/3 versus 1/6 — because the eigenvector (1, −2, 1) places double weight on the middle entry.

Subtract cn − an and the growth and oscillation terms vanish, leaving exactly S. The span invariance falls out of the algebra immediately, because the eigenvector (1, 0, −1) is the only one with a non-zero c − a component, and its eigenvalue is 1.

Three eigenvalues. Three independent stories. λ=2 doubles, λ=1 fixes, λ=−1 flips. The orbit of any triple is the sum of three motions along three fixed axes in ℝ³, each evolving according to its own simple rule. The closed form is not a clever algebraic trick — it is the eigendecomposition of a 3×3 matrix written out long. Every triple iteration ever performed is a particular trajectory in this three-axis decomposition. The triple just sets the initial coefficients.

This is the simplest possible non-trivial discrete dynamical system on ℝ³ with three distinct real eigenvalues. It lives natively inside the Year 7 Sums in Pairs investigation, waiting for any pupil with the curiosity to ask "what if I just keep going?"
