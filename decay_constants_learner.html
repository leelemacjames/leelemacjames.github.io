01 — The differential equation

One Equation to Rule Them All

Before we begin: what property must a function have so that its rate of change is proportional to its current value? Sketch what you think that curve looks like.

The decay equation is not a fact handed down from physics — it is a logical consequence of one assumption: the rate at which something changes is proportional to how much of it currently exists. Write that in the language of calculus:

\[ \frac{dN}{dt} = -\lambda N \]

The minus sign encodes decay rather than growth. Separate variables and integrate:

\[ \int \frac{dN}{N} = -\lambda \int dt \quad \Rightarrow \quad \ln N = -\lambda t + C \quad \Rightarrow \quad N(t) = N_0\, e^{-\lambda t} \]

The exponential is the unique function whose derivative is itself (scaled). It is not a coincidence that it appears everywhere — it is the only possible solution to this class of problem.

Because linearity is the simplest possible relationship between a quantity and its rate of change. Whenever a system has no memory — when the future evolution depends only on the present state, not on history — it satisfies this equation. This is the Markov property in disguise. It appears in radioactive decay, drug pharmacokinetics, population dynamics, electrical circuits, Newton's law of cooling, and information theory, because all of these are memoryless linear processes.

Two parameters parametrise this single curve. They are not different things — they are reciprocals of each other, with different historical etymologies from different fields:

λ (decay constant) ⟷ reciprocal ⟷ τ (time constant)  so  λ = 1/τ

02 — RC circuits & the time constant

τ = RC: Reading the Circuit

A capacitor discharges through a resistor. Bigger R — does it discharge faster or slower? What about bigger C? Before reading on, commit to a prediction.

Kirchhoff's voltage law for a series RC circuit gives:

\[ V_R + V_C = 0 \quad \Rightarrow \quad RC\,\frac{dV}{dt} + V = 0 \]

Rearranging: \(\frac{dV}{dt} = -\frac{1}{RC}\,V\). This is exactly our template ODE with λ = 1/RC, so the solution is:

\[ V(t) = V_0\, e^{-t/RC} \equiv V_0\, e^{-t/\tau} \quad \text{where } \tau = RC \]

Larger R

More resistance → current flows more slowly → capacitor drains more slowly → larger τ → slower decay. Prediction verified: bigger R, slower discharge.

Larger C

More capacitance → more charge stored per volt → same current drains proportionally more slowly → larger τ. Both R and C slow the decay symmetrically.

The units of τ = RC are ohms × farads = seconds. Check: Ω·F = (V/A)·(C/V) = C/A = s. The unit analysis was already telling you what τ measures: a characteristic time.

τ is not the time to fully discharge — it is the time for V to fall to V₀/e ≈ 0.368 V₀. After 5τ, V ≈ 0.7% of V₀, which is when engineers call the circuit "discharged" in practice.

What τ means geometrically

The tangent to the decay curve at t = 0 intersects the time axis at exactly t = τ. This is a beautiful geometric fact: if the decay continued at its initial rate, it would reach zero at precisely one time-constant. The curve "remembers" τ in its initial slope.

\[ \left.\frac{dV}{dt}\right|_{t=0} = -\frac{V_0}{\tau} \quad \Rightarrow \quad \text{tangent hits zero at } t = \tau \]

03 — Damped harmonic oscillators

When Decay Meets Oscillation

A pendulum in oil. A shock absorber. A plucked guitar string. What do these have in common, and how does exponential decay enter a system that is also oscillating?

The damped harmonic oscillator satisfies:

\[ m\ddot{x} + b\dot{x} + kx = 0 \]

Dividing by m and writing γ = b/2m, ω₀² = k/m, the trial solution x = est yields the characteristic equation s² + 2γs + ω₀² = 0, giving:

\[ s = -\gamma \pm \sqrt{\gamma^2 - \omega_0^2} \]

Three regimes emerge, controlled by the ratio γ/ω₀:

γ < ω₀
Underdamped
x(t) = A e⁻ᵞᵗ cos(ω_d t + φ)
Oscillation wrapped in exponential envelope. The decay constant γ = b/2m sets the envelope. Guitar strings, RLC circuits below resonance.
γ = ω₀
Critically Damped
x(t) = (A + Bt)e⁻ᵞᵗ
Returns to equilibrium fastest without oscillation. Car suspension design target, galvanometer damping.
γ > ω₀
Overdamped
x(t) = A e^{s₊t} + B e^{s₋t}
Two real negative exponents — slow return without oscillation. Door closers, heavy shock absorbers.

The exponential decay constant τ = 1/γ = 2m/b directly parallels the RC circuit: replace m↔C (inertia/capacitance), b↔1/R (damping/conductance), k↔1/L (restoring/inductance). The analogy is exact — the same ODE, different physical labels.

Damped oscillation — three regimes

The quality factor Q = ω₀/2γ = ω₀τ/2 measures how many oscillation cycles occur in one decay time. Q ≫ 1 means many oscillations before the amplitude drops to 1/e. Quartz crystal oscillators have Q ~ 10⁶; acoustic cavities, Q ~ 10³; a car suspension, Q ~ 0.5 (deliberately underdamped toward critical). The decay time and oscillation time are coupled through τ = 2Q/ω₀.

04 — The decay constant λ and the magic of 1/e

Why 36.8% Is the Universe's Favourite Number

After one time-constant τ, what fraction of the original quantity remains? Express it as an exact value before computing its decimal — then decide whether 36.8% seems "natural" or arbitrary.

At t = τ = 1/λ:

\[ N(\tau) = N_0\, e^{-\lambda \cdot (1/\lambda)} = N_0\, e^{-1} = \frac{N_0}{e} \approx 0.3679\, N_0 \]

The number e⁻¹ ≈ 0.36788 is not arbitrary. It is the fixed point where the decay function crosses the level 1/e — and this crossing defines the time-constant. The 36.8% is not a choice; it is what τ means by construction.

Why this particular fraction?

Consider: the exponential e is defined such that e^1 = e ≈ 2.718. So e^{−1} = 1/2.718... ≈ 0.368. The reason e appears (rather than, say, 2) is that e is the base of natural logarithms — the unique base for which the derivative of bˣ is exactly bˣ itself, with no extra constant factor. Any other base would introduce a multiplicative constant into our ODE and break its clean self-referential structure.

Invest £1 at 100% annual interest. Compound n times per year:

\[ A = \left(1 + \frac{1}{n}\right)^n \]

As n → ∞ (continuous compounding), this converges to e ≈ 2.71828... Equivalently, e is the amount £1 grows to under continuous 100% compounding for 1 year. Its reciprocal e⁻¹ is the fraction remaining after one unit of continuous decay — the "natural" decay residual.

05 — Optimal stopping & the secretary problem

1/e Is Also the Secretary's Survival Ratio

You must hire one secretary from N candidates, interviewed sequentially in random order. You can only accept or reject each candidate on the spot. What strategy maximises your probability of choosing the best candidate overall?

This is the Secretary Problem (also called the Marriage Problem, or the 37% Rule). The optimal strategy has a beautiful structure:

  1. Explore phase: reject the first r−1 candidates unconditionally. Record the best seen so far.
  2. Exploit phase: accept the first candidate who beats all previously seen.

The optimal threshold r* that maximises the probability of success is:

\[ r^* = \left\lfloor \frac{N}{e} \right\rfloor \approx \frac{N}{e} \approx 0.368\, N \]

Reject the first 36.8% of candidates. Then hire the next who beats all previous. With this strategy, the probability of success converges to:

\[ P^*_{\text{success}} \to \frac{1}{e} \approx 36.8\% \quad \text{as } N \to \infty \]

Both the threshold and the optimal success probability converge to 1/e. The same transcendental constant governs both the timing of exploration and the ceiling of achievable success. This is not a coincidence — it is the same underlying integral \( \int_0^1 -\ln(x)\, dx = 1 \).

The Mountain Metaphor. In high-altitude mountaineering, the "Death Zone" above ~8,000 m is where oxygen density falls below the threshold for sustained human life. The atmospheric pressure at 8,000 m is approximately e⁻¹ ≈ 37% of sea-level pressure (the scale height of the atmosphere is ~8.5 km). So the Death Zone begins almost exactly at one atmospheric scale height — the altitude at which you have "used up" your first time-constant of air density. Climbers have an implicit 37% rule: spend any significant time above 8 km and you are in the exponential tail, where every additional metre costs disproportionately. The 1/e fraction is not just a mathematical nicety — above this altitude, your margin is gone.

The secretary problem's optimal threshold comes from maximising the integral:

\[ P(r) = \frac{r-1}{N} \sum_{k=r}^{N} \frac{1}{k-1} \approx -x_0 \ln(x_0) \quad \text{where } x_0 = r/N \]

Differentiating and setting equal to zero: d/dx[−x ln x] = −ln x − 1 = 0, so x = e⁻¹. The exponential function and its derivative are the same function — so any optimisation of a quantity defined via logarithms will generically hit e⁻¹ as a critical point. The same calculus that gives us the decay time-constant gives us the optimal stopping fraction.

06 — Half-life and ln 2

Why ln 2 Is the Physicist's Other Favourite

The half-life t½ is the time for a quantity to fall to exactly half its value. Express t½ in terms of λ and τ before reading on. How does it relate to ln 2 ≈ 0.693?

Set N(t½) = N₀/2:

\[ N_0 e^{-\lambda t_{1/2}} = \frac{N_0}{2} \quad \Rightarrow \quad e^{-\lambda t_{1/2}} = \frac{1}{2} \quad \Rightarrow \quad t_{1/2} = \frac{\ln 2}{\lambda} = \tau \ln 2 \]

So t½ = τ · ln 2 ≈ 0.693 τ. The half-life is always shorter than the time-constant by a factor of ln 2. This makes intuitive sense: halving is less dramatic than falling to 1/e ≈ 36.8%, because 50% > 36.8%, so halving happens earlier than τ.

Why ln 2?

Because we want the exponent that makes e to that power equal 1/2. That is precisely the definition of ln(1/2) = −ln 2. Half-lives naturally invoke logarithms base e, not base 2, because the underlying process runs on natural exponentials.

Rule of 72

In finance: doubling time ≈ 72/r% for compound growth rate r. This is 100·ln2/r ≈ 69.3/r ≈ 72/r (rounded for mental arithmetic). The same logarithm, same equation — growth instead of decay.

The relationship between the three key numbers:

t½ = τ · ln 2 → τ = t½ / ln 2 ≈ 1.4427 · t½ → λ = ln 2 / t½
Half-life and time-constant are often confused in biology vs. physics. Pharmacologists use t½ (drug half-life) while engineers use τ (circuit time-constant). They describe the same curve, just anchored to different characteristic fractions. Know which convention your field uses.

07 — The general ODE in context: Heat & Atmosphere

The Atmosphere Is Solving Our ODE

If air is in hydrostatic equilibrium — pressure at each height supporting the weight of air above — write a differential equation for pressure P as a function of height z. What do you expect the solution to look like?

Hydrostatic balance

Consider a thin slab of atmosphere at height z, thickness dz. Pressure decreases upward because there is less atmosphere above to push down:

\[ \frac{dP}{dz} = -\rho g \]

Using the ideal gas law P = ρRT/M (R gas constant, T temperature, M molar mass of air), we can substitute ρ = PM/RT:

\[ \frac{dP}{dz} = -\frac{Mg}{RT}\,P \equiv -\frac{P}{H} \quad \text{where } H = \frac{RT}{Mg} \approx 8.5\,\text{km} \]

This is exactly our ODE, with z replacing t and H replacing τ. The solution:

\[ P(z) = P_0\, e^{-z/H} \]

H ≈ 8.5 km is the scale height of the atmosphere — the atmospheric τ. At z = H ≈ 8.5 km, pressure is P₀/e ≈ 37% of sea level. Mount Everest (8.849 km) sits almost exactly at one scale height — a fact that is not coincidental but reflects why it is at the absolute biological limit of unassisted climbing.

Newton's Law of Cooling — the same structure

The rate of heat loss is proportional to the temperature difference from the environment:

\[ \frac{d\theta}{dt} = -\frac{1}{\tau}\,\theta \quad \text{where } \theta = T - T_{\text{env}}, \quad \tau = \frac{mC_p}{hA} \]

Here m is mass, Cₚ specific heat capacity, h the heat transfer coefficient, A surface area. The thermal time-constant τ = mCₚ/hA has identical mathematical status to RC. A coffee cup's τ ≈ 20–30 minutes; an ocean basin's τ ≈ decades.

Newton's law describes the excess temperature θ = T − T_env decaying to zero, not T itself decaying to zero. Temperature cannot decay to zero — it decays toward ambient. The exponential decay applies to the deviation from equilibrium. This is a general principle: it is the perturbation from the equilibrium state that follows exponential decay, not the absolute quantity. The same applies to RC circuits (voltage decays toward 0V, the equilibrium) and damped oscillators (displacement decays toward equilibrium position).

08 — Breadth of application

The Exponential Decay Multiverse

Every domain below reduces to dN/dt = −λN or its close relatives. The parameters change; the mathematics does not.

Nuclear Physics
Radioactive Decay
N(t) = N₀ e^{−λt}, t½ = ln2/λ
The archetypal example. Carbon-14 t½ = 5,730 yr used for dating; uranium-238 t½ = 4.5 Gyr comparable to Earth's age.
Electronics
RC / RL Circuits
τ = RC or τ = L/R
Filter design, signal smoothing, timing circuits. The RC time constant directly sets the −3dB frequency: f_c = 1/(2πRC).
Pharmacokinetics
Drug Elimination
C(t) = C₀ e^{−(ln2/t½)t}
Drug concentration in blood. Ibuprofen t½ ≈ 2h; warfarin t½ ≈ 40h. Dosing schedules are engineered around t½.
Fluid Dynamics
Atmospheric Pressure
P(z) = P₀ e^{−z/H}, H ≈ 8.5 km
Barometric formula. Valid for isothermal atmosphere; real atmosphere deviates due to temperature variation with altitude.
Acoustics / Optics
Beer-Lambert Law
I(x) = I₀ e^{−αx}
Light intensity through absorbing medium; sound in a cavity. Absorption coefficient α plays the role of λ; penetration depth 1/α plays τ.
Information Theory
Poisson Waiting Times
P(T > t) = e^{−λt}
Time between events in a Poisson process is exponentially distributed. Memoryless: the probability of waiting τ more seconds is always the same, regardless of how long you've already waited.
Finance
Continuous Discounting
PV = FV · e^{−rt}
Present value of future cashflow. The discount rate r plays λ. The "doubling time" of money at rate r is ln2/r ≈ 0.693/r — the Rule of 72 in disguise.
Epidemiology
SIR Model Recovery
I(t) ~ I₀ e^{(β−γ)t}
In the SIR model, infected individuals recover at rate γ. Recovery time 1/γ is the infectious period time-constant — the same τ, now biological.
Geology
Radiometric Dating
t = (1/λ) ln(1 + D/P)
Ratio of daughter to parent isotope gives elapsed time. Requires knowing λ (measured in lab) and assuming system closure — no daughter escaped.

10 — Self-assessment

Test Your Understanding

1. A capacitor discharges through a 10 kΩ resistor. After one time-constant τ, what fraction of the original voltage remains?

Correct. After one τ, V = V₀ · e⁻¹ ≈ 0.368 V₀. Note 63.2% is the fraction that has decayed away — a common sign error. The capacitor has 36.8% left, not 63.2%.

2. Carbon-14 has a half-life of 5,730 years. What is its decay constant λ in yr⁻¹?

Correct. λ = ln 2 / t½. The factor 1/t½ would be the reciprocal of the time-constant τ, not the half-life: τ = t½/ln2 ≈ 8,267 yr, so λ = 1/τ = ln2/t½.

3. In the Secretary Problem with N = 100 candidates, how many should you observe-and-reject in the exploration phase?

Correct. Reject the first ⌊N/e⌋ = ⌊36.79⌋ = 36 candidates (sometimes stated as 37 including the boundary). The success probability converges to 1/e ≈ 36.8% — the same constant twice.

4. The atmospheric scale height H ≈ 8.5 km is analogous to which quantity in an RC circuit?

Correct. H is the characteristic length over which pressure falls to 1/e — the spatial analogue of τ. The pressure ODE dP/dz = −P/H exactly mirrors dV/dt = −V/τ, with z↔t and H↔τ.

5. A damped harmonic oscillator has b/m = 4 s⁻¹ and ω₀ = 3 rad/s. Is this underdamped, critically damped, or overdamped?

Correct. γ = b/2m = 4/2 = 2 s⁻¹. Compare γ = 2 to ω₀ = 3: γ < ω₀, so this is actually underdamped — wait, recheck: γ = b/2m = 2, ω₀ = 3, so γ < ω₀ → underdamped. The system oscillates. The envelope decays with time-constant τ = 1/γ = 0.5 s.
