Criterion D

Reflecting on the impacts of science

/10

This criterion asks you to move between the physics and its consequences — for people, environments, and the decisions that societies must make. The best answers use calculations to anchor their reasoning, rather than treating the science and the ethics as separate things.

The analogy — battery, capacitor, or neither?

From a physics perspective, the three devices store energy in fundamentally different ways:

  • A capacitor stores energy directly in an electric field — it charges and discharges in milliseconds, but holds very little total energy.
  • A battery stores energy as chemical potential energy and converts it back to electricity — slower than a capacitor, but can store hours of energy.
  • Dinorwig stores energy as gravitational PE — slower than a battery, but stores 9.1 GWh and can deliver it for hours. It buys electricity when prices are low and sells when prices are high. It is the grid's rechargeable battery.
  • The tidal lagoon does not store energy at all — it is a generator driven by the Moon's gravitational pull on the ocean. It cannot choose when to generate.

D1 2 marks

Explain, using the physics of energy storage, why Dinorwig is better described as a battery than as a capacitor. Your answer must refer to the form in which energy is stored and the time scale of response.
