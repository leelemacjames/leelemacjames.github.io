Prime differences and a Sierpiński-like triangle

Start with the primes, form successive rows of first differences, and colour each entry by its residue class. With absolute differences and a binary-style colouring, a familiar triangular void pattern begins to appear. It is not literally Pascal mod 2, but it is visually close enough to make the comparison mathematically suggestive.

Let pn be the prime sequence. Define the difference rows by

Δ1pn = |pn+1 - pn|,    Δk+1pn = |Δkpn+1 - Δkpn|.

Then colour Δkpn by its value modulo m.

What to look for

  • Most prime gaps are even after 2, so parity dominates quickly.
  • Absolute differencing keeps values non-negative and sharpens repeated local structure.
  • Black/white parity-style colouring often reveals the strongest triangular lacunae.
  • Changing the seed sequence changes the texture: primes, semiprimes, and reptend primes do not behave alike.

Interpretation

Once the initial prime 2 has passed, consecutive prime gaps are mostly even. That means parity information gets inherited down the table, and repeated differencing can create self-similar voids. In that restricted sense, the picture is reminiscent of a Sierpiński triangle: not because the primes satisfy Pascal's recurrence, but because modular cancellation produces triangular patches of uniform residue.

With absolute differences, the rule 

currentDiff[i] = Math.abs(previousRow[i + 1] - previousRow[i]);

removes sign and emphasises magnitude classes. Under mod 8 colouring this often gives a comparatively “dull” but structured field for primes: many entries collapse into a small set of even residues. By contrast, Fibonacci or signed-difference variants usually show stronger alternation or mirror effects.

A good exercise is to compare three seeds:

  • Primes: sparse, gap-driven, dominated by even behaviour after the first term.
  • Semiprimes: denser composite structure, often rougher and less uniform.
  • Full reptend primes: a thinner subsequence, useful if you want to test whether special multiplicative order conditions leave a visible signature in the difference triangle.
