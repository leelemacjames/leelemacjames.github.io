Just Enough Education to Perform

Knowledge · Provenance · Epistemic Flattening
The archive has been flattened into reach. Depth is no longer hidden; it is bypassed.

Rumsfeld warned that the gravest danger lay in unknown unknowns: threats one cannot even frame as questions. But in education a different configuration now presents itself. The danger is not what cannot be seen. It is what is everywhere seen and nowhere rebuilt. Known knowns have become hollow being so publicly available, endlessly repeatable, detached from the struggle that once made them intelligible. The question is no longer what must be known, but what must be understood in order to evaluate what is known.

Knowledge without provenance is indistinguishable from coincidence.

The Stereophonics title names three things at once. In column I it is invisible in which education simply provides, and performance is assumed to follow. In column II it becomes the diagnosis: retrieval without reconstruction produces competence without comprehension. In column III it becomes the thing the ansatz explicitly resists: those tasks designed so that "just enough" is never enough, because the task withholds closure until the student has rebuilt what they claim to know.

But a question remains. What happens when the ansatz succeeds? When students learn to reconstruct fluently, to generate errors on cue, to state assumptions without prompting? When friction itself becomes frictionless?

If a student cannot be wrong, they cannot be thinking.
The recursion
Each remedy produces a new form of the disease it cures.

Once the epistemic goods of column III are made explicit as once schema, reasoning, error detection, and epistemic awareness are operationalised into assessable dimensions they themselves become targets. Schema can be performed. Reasoning can be stylised. Error detection can be rehearsed. Epistemic awareness can become a new surface. The fifth column cannot be defeated by a fifth rubric dimension. Any criterion for authentic uncertainty becomes a performance target the moment it is named. This is Goodhart's Law applied to epistemology: when a measure of understanding becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure of understanding.

The ansatz of friction remains the best available response. Deliberately designed constraint is better than no constraint. But it operates under a permanent condition: its own success will produce fluent performers who have learned to reconstruct ladders without ever needing one.

What remains, then, are footholds or load-bearing sentences that mark where compression has occurred, and resist flattening not by withholding access but by insisting on depth.

Knowledge without provenance is indistinguishable from coincidence.
Retrieval is not reconstruction.
The ladder disappears precisely when it has been climbed.
Familiarity masquerades as understanding.
What is made assessable becomes optimisable.
Knowing that knowledge is uncertain is not the same as being uncertain while knowing.
If a student cannot be wrong, they cannot be thinking.
Without exposure, critique becomes theatre.

These are not conclusions. They are warnings reminding us that the problem is no longer ignorance but surplus certainty. Nor is it no longer inaccessible depth but bypassed depth, no longer the absence of knowledge but its frictionless availability in forms too compressed to inhabit.

To teach is not to deliver pronouncements, but to lower the drawbridge.
