Surds & Exact Values

Why √2 is not 1.41421… · the mute number · simplifying by square factors
S

Rational comes from the Latin ratio — "made to speak" — a number expressible exactly as a fraction p/q. Its opposite, irrational, means mute: a number that cannot speak itself as a ratio. The Arabic mathematician al-Khwarizmi used the term surd (from Arabic aṣamm, deaf) in the 9th century for roots that stay forever mute — they never terminate, never repeat.

A surd is precisely a root that is irrational. The radical sign √ is the instruction to take the root; the number beneath it is the radicand. When the radicand is a perfect square, the root speaks (√4 = 2). When it is not, the root is mute — and we write the surd itself as the exact value, rather than any approximation.

Rational root — not a surd
= 2 exactly

The radicand 4 = 2². It is a perfect square, so the root speaks clearly. √4 is not a surd.

Irrational root — a surd
= 1.41421356… (mute)

The radicand 2 has no rational square root. The decimal never terminates, never repeats. Writing "√2" — equivalently 2½ — is the exact name. Any decimal is merely an approximation.

Why decimals cannot reach √10 — the infinite squeeze

We need x such that x² = 10 exactly. Each decimal approximation traps the answer in a shrinking interval — but never catches it. This is what "irrational" means structurally: the rational numbers have a hole at exactly this point, and only the surd √10 names it.

The process continues forever. The surd √10 is the exact name — no decimal ever is.

Simplifying surds — tap each to reveal the working

Key rule: √(a×b) = √a × √b. To simplify, find the largest square factor of the radicand and pull it out. The remaining radicand should have no square factors — it is then fully simplified.

Niven's theorem & the rational parametrisation

By Niven's theorem (1956), the only angles θ ∈ [0°, 90°] where both cos θ and sin θ are rational are 0° and 90°. This means the only inscribed regular polygon with all rational vertices is the square — its vertices are exactly the four axis intercepts (±1, 0) and (0, ±1).

To generate all rational points on the unit circle systematically, set t = tan(θ/2). Then: cos θ = (1−t²)/(1+t²) and sin θ = 2t/(1+t²). Every rational t yields a rational point. This is the rational parametrisation of the circle — and it is exactly the Pythagorean triple construction in disguise: the triple (a, b, c) with a²+b²=c² corresponds to t = b/(a+c).
