Roots of Unity

The N-th roots of unity are the solutions to zN = 1. They sit equally spaced on the unit circle. Two cases define entire number systems: N = 4 gives us i, and N = 3 gives us ω.

The roots are ωNk = e2πik/N for k = 0, 1, …, N−1. Drag the slider to see how the polygon changes. N = 4 is the Gaussian case (square). N = 3 is the Eisenstein case (triangle).

Why these two are special

N = 4: The Gaussian world

The primitive 4th root is i = eiπ/2. The four roots {1, i, −1, −i} form the unit group of the Gaussian integers ℤ[i]. The lattice they generate tiles the plane with squares.

N = 3: The Eisenstein world

The primitive 3rd root is ω = e2πi/3 = −½ + (√3/2)i. The six units {±1, ±ω, ±ω²} form the unit group of the Eisenstein integers ℤ[ω]. The lattice tiles with equilateral triangles.

Only N = 1, 2, 3, 4, 6 give roots of unity that generate lattice tilings of the plane. This is the crystallographic restriction: only 2-, 3-, 4-, and 6-fold rotational symmetries tile ℝ².

ii vs ωω

What happens when a complex number is raised to the power of itself? The surprising answer: you land on the real line. The derivation for ii is classical. The ωω case is its natural companion.

The asymmetry: ii is purely real because i sits at angle π/2, and multiplying the exponent by i rotates it onto the real axis (i² = −1). But ω sits at angle 2π/3, so ω·(2πi/3) has both real and imaginary parts. The self-exponentiation of ω does not collapse to ℝ.

The Integer Rings

Adjoining a root of unity to the ordinary integers ℤ creates a new number ring. ℤ[i] and ℤ[ω] are the two most important examples: both are unique factorisation domains, Euclidean domains, and principal ideal domains.

Gaussian Integers ℤ[i]

a + bi, where a, b ∈ ℤ

Minimal polynomial of i: x² + 1 = 0

Units (invertible elements): {1, i, −1, −i}. Four units. The unit group is cyclic of order 4: ℤ/4ℤ.

Norm: N(a + bi) = a² + b². The norm is multiplicative: N(αβ) = N(α)N(β).

Euclidean function: the norm itself. Division algorithm works because ℤ[i] tiles the plane with unit squares, so any complex number is within distance √2/2 < 1 of a Gaussian integer.

Eisenstein Integers ℤ[ω]

a + bω, where a, b ∈ ℤ

Minimal polynomial of ω: x² + x + 1 = 0

Units: {±1, ±ω, ±ω²}. Six units. The unit group is cyclic of order 6: ℤ/6ℤ.

Norm: N(a + bω) = a² − ab + b². Also multiplicative.

Euclidean function: the norm. Division works because ℤ[ω] tiles the plane with equilateral triangles, and any point is within distance 1/√3 < 1 of an Eisenstein integer.

Lattice Geometry

Each integer ring generates a lattice in the complex plane. The Gaussian lattice is square; the Eisenstein lattice is triangular. These are the only two "maximally symmetric" planar lattices (besides the trivial rectangular and oblique ones).

Square lattice · ℤ[i]

Basis vectors: {1, i}. Each fundamental domain is a unit square. Four-fold rotational symmetry.

Triangular lattice · ℤ[ω]

Basis vectors: {1, ω}. Each fundamental domain splits into two equilateral triangles. Six-fold rotational symmetry.

How rational primes split in ℤ[i]

p = 2: ramifies. 2 = −i(1+i)². The prime (1+i) has norm 2.

p ≡ 1 mod 4: splits. Can be written as a² + b². Example: 5 = (2+i)(2−i).

p ≡ 3 mod 4: stays prime (inert). Example: 3 remains prime in ℤ[i].
