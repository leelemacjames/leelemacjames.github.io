Aperiodic Tilings from Digital Fibonacci Sequences and Ulam Spirals

The Digital Fibonacci Sequence

The classical Fibonacci model assumes immortal rabbits: each pair breeds forever. The Digital Fibonacci sequence introduces mortality via a generational limit g, producing a binary string at each generation whose concatenation reveals strikingly different tiling textures when wrapped into an Ulam spiral.

The sequence is constructed from three algorithmic rules applied to binary states — bunnies (0) and rabbits (1):

  1. A bunny (0) matures into a rabbit (1).
  2. A rabbit (1) gives birth to a bunny (0) and persists — yielding the pair 10.
  3. After g generations, a rabbit expires and is removed from the population.

The parameter g controls the depth of generational memory. With g = 3, only grandparents survive alongside their offspring. With g = 4, great-grandparents persist. The limiting case g = ∞ recovers the classical Fibonacci sequence — no death, only growth — and the binary string converges to the infinite Fibonacci word.

Algorithmically, each entity carries its birth generation as metadata. At generation t, a rabbit born at generation b has age t − b. If this age reaches g, the rabbit is silently removed. This is a direct encoding of mortality into the recurrence — the population at each step is not simply Fn but a filtered sum over surviving lineages.

Each generation's population is recorded as a binary string, then all generations are concatenated (reversed, reading from most recent generation back to origin) and truncated to k digits for display on the Ulam spiral.

Ulam Spiral Wrapping

The Ulam spiral walks outward from a central point in the right–up–left–down pattern, placing one digit per cell. With prime numbers the spiral famously reveals diagonal alignments; here, the binary digits of the Digital Fibonacci sequence produce aperiodic tilings whose visual texture depends sensitively on g.

The comparison against a random draw of {0, 1} with matched density is instructive. The Fibonacci-derived spirals show structured banding and quasi-periodic motifs absent in the stochastic case — evidence that the mortality rules imprint long-range correlations on the binary string.

Zeckendorf Representations of Primes

Zeckendorf's Theorem

Every positive integer can be represented uniquely as the sum of one or more distinct non-consecutive Fibonacci numbers:

N = Σ Fi where no two Fi are consecutive.

For example, 100 = 89 + 8 + 3 → 101001000F

The greedy algorithm walks backward through the Fibonacci sequence, subtracting the largest number that fits and skipping one index to enforce non-consecutiveness. Concatenating the Zeckendorf representations of successive primes and wrapping the result into an Ulam spiral produces tilings that "chime rather than rhyme" — structured, but in a different register than the lifecycle model.
