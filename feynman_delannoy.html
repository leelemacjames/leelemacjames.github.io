The Weighted King — a Feynman ensemble on the Delannoy lattice

Each path carries a Boltzmann weight in its Euclidean length. Tune β and watch the ensemble reorganise.

Every E/N/NE walk from (0,0) to (n,n) is assigned weight exp(−β · L), where L is its Euclidean length. The diagonal step costs √2 in length but advances both axes at once. The slider varies β — the inverse temperature. At β=0 all paths weigh equally (Delannoy counting). As β→∞ the weight concentrates on the geodesic (all-diagonal). The colour map shows the visit probability of each cell under the chosen weighting.

What you're seeing

The heat map on each cell is its visit probability — the chance that a path drawn from the Boltzmann ensemble passes through that cell. Brighter gold means more visits; faded grey means rare. The ensemble is computed exactly by dynamic programming: weighted forward sums to each cell, weighted backward sums from each cell, multiplied and normalised.

Three regimes

At β = 0 every path counts equally. The dominant contribution comes from paths near the antidiagonal of the visit grid — the saddle-point of the Delannoy sum sits there, with about 1 − 1/√2 ≈ 0.293 of the path's "coordinate work" done by diagonal steps. This is the uniform-measure asymptotic we derived analytically.

As β increases, longer paths are exponentially suppressed. Diagonal steps are favoured (they're a single move of length √2 ≈ 1.41 rather than two moves of total length 2). The visit map sharpens onto the diagonal ridge. In the limit β → ∞ only the unique geodesic — all- diagonal — survives.

At β < 0 (negative temperature, by physicist convention) longer paths are rewarded. The ensemble pushes outward to the lattice corners and the diagonal steps become rare. The picture flattens against the boundary — the king prefers to walk the long way round.

The critical β

At β = ln(2)/(2−√2) ≈ 1.183 the ensemble enters a notable regime. This is the value where the Boltzmann weight of a diagonal step e^(−β√2) exactly cancels the combinatorial bonus of 2 that diagonals carry in the closed formula D(n,n) = Σ C(n,j)² 2^j. Below it, the closed formula behaves like Pascal-squared; above, the diagonal dominates. The slider preset button lands you on this value.

The expected-length curve

Watch the ⟨L⟩ readout as you sweep β from negative to large positive. At very negative β it approaches 2n (pure orthogonal, Manhattan distance). At β = 0 it sits at the Delannoy average (2 − 1/√2) · n ≈ 1.293 n. At very large β it descends toward the geodesic n√2 ≈ 1.414 n. The crossover is smooth and the curve is monotonically decreasing in β — a textbook thermodynamic response.

In statistical-mechanics language, this is the canonical ensemble of a one- dimensional non-interacting "gas" of step-choices on a triangular constraint surface (the path must reach (n,n)). The Delannoy generating function is its partition function at β = 0, and the saddle-point analysis is its equation of state.
