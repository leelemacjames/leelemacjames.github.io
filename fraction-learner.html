§ 01 — Start HereWhat does a fraction mean?

A fraction is your answer to: "how much of the whole thing do I have?" The bottom number says how many equal pieces the whole is cut into. The top number says how many of those pieces are yours.

⟡ Before anything else — agree on this

A chocolate bar has 12 squares. You eat 4. What fraction did you eat? Write it down, then simplify it. What do you notice?

4 out of 12 = 412. Both 4 and 12 divide by 4, giving 13. Same amount, simpler name. The fraction hasn't changed — only how we write it has. This is called simplifying or cancelling.

Now: adding fractions

Try each card below. Think about the actual food, not the numbers. What does your gut say?

You eat ½ of a pizza, then eat another ¼ of the same pizza. How much have you eaten altogether?
¾ of the pizza. Half = 2 quarters, plus 1 quarter = 3 quarters. You can see it: the denominator stays the same when the slice size doesn't change.
You eat ⅓ of a cake. Your friend eats another ⅓ of the same cake. What fraction of the cake is gone?
2/3 of the cake. When the slice size (denominator) is the same, you just add the number of slices (numerators). Same denominator → just add the tops.
You drink ½ a bottle of juice. Your friend drinks ⅓ of the same bottle. How much juice is gone?
Trickier — the slices don't match up. Halves and thirds are different sizes. We need to find a common slice size. We'll work this out properly in §2.
Someone claims: ½ + ⅓ = 2/5. They added the tops (1+1=2) and the bottoms (2+3=5). Does this feel right?
It should feel wrong. ½ = 0.5 and 2/5 = 0.4. So the "answer" 2/5 is actually less than ½ alone. How can eating a piece of tart leave you with less than you started with? Something is wrong here.
"Does that feel right?" is always a good question in mathematics. If your answer is smaller than what you started with after adding something positive — something has gone wrong.
⟡ The central question for this learner

We've shown that 12 + 13 ≠ 25 as an amount of tart. But we should ask: is there any situation where 2/5 would be the right answer to something involving ½ and ⅓? And what is the correct answer to ½ + ⅓?

We'll answer both. Keep reading.

Halves and thirds don't match up — the slices are different sizes. We need to cut the tart into pieces that are the same size so we can count them together. The smallest number of pieces that works for both halves and thirds is 6 (because 6 ÷ 2 = 3 and 6 ÷ 3 = 2 — no remainder either time).

So: ½ becomes 3/6, and ⅓ becomes 2/6. Now we can add: 3/6 + 2/6 = 5/6. This is bigger than ¾ = 4.5/6 but less than 1 — exactly as your gut probably said.

The number 6 is called the lowest common multiple of 2 and 3. Finding it is the key step.

⚠ Why "add tops and bottoms" fails

The "add tops and bottoms" answer is 2/5 = 0.4. The correct answer is 5/6 ≈ 0.833. These are very different! The mistake is treating the denominator (the bottom number) like a plain number you can just add. But the denominator isn't a count — it's a slice size. And slice sizes don't add: if you cut a tart into 2 pieces and another into 3 pieces, you don't suddenly have a tart cut into 5 pieces.

§ 03 — Different Tart SizesIs 2/5 ever the right answer?

We've dismissed 2/5. But let's not throw it away without understanding it. Here's a new scenario.

⟡ New scenario — think before reading on

You eat 1 slice out of 2 from a small tart. Your friend eats 1 slice out of 3 from a large tart. Together you've eaten 2 slices out of the 5 slices that existed in total. Is 25 a fair description of what happened?

It depends on the slice sizes! If the small tart has small slices and the large tart has large slices, then one of your 2 slices is worth much more than the other. Counting both slices as equal — "2 out of 5" — is only fair if they're actually the same size.

So 2/5 is a count of slices, not an amount of tart. It correctly counts what fraction of the total number of slices were eaten — but only makes sense as an amount if all slices are equal.

⟡ So what is the mediant (add tops and bottoms) actually doing?

The operation ab ⊕ cd = a+cb+d is called the mediant. It correctly answers: "if I pooled all equal-sized pieces, what fraction of the total count did we eat?" It is also always guaranteed to land between the two original fractions — which makes it incredibly useful for a different purpose: filling the number line with fractions. That's the Farey tree in §4.

⅓ ≈ 0.333  ·  2/5 = 0.400  ·  ½ = 0.500. Yes — the mediant sits strictly between them. This is a theorem: if a/b < c/d, then a/b < (a+c)/(b+d) < c/d, always. The mediant is always an "in-between" number.

§ 04 — The Farey TreeUsing the mediant to find every fraction

Here's the remarkable thing: starting from 0/1 on the left and 1/1 on the right, the mediant generates every fraction between 0 and 1, in lowest terms, exactly once — arranged as a beautiful binary tree.

⟡ Build the first two rows yourself

Row 1: mediant of 0/1 and 1/1 = ?   Row 2: mediant of 0/1 with your row-1 answer, and mediant of your row-1 answer with 1/1. Write all three fractions down.

Row 1: (0+1)/(1+1) = 1/2.

Row 2 left: (0+1)/(1+2) = 1/3.   Row 2 right: (1+1)/(2+1) = 2/3.

Row 2 is: 1/3, 1/2, 2/3. In order. Keep going and you generate all fractions — 1/4, 1/3, 2/5, 1/2, 3/5, 2/3, 3/4, … — each exactly once.

Every fraction appears exactly once — this is a theorem. And every adjacent pair in the tree satisfies |ad − bc| = 1 (cross-multiply and subtract: you always get 1). This guarantees two things: the mediant is automatically in lowest terms (no simplifying needed), and the mediant genuinely falls strictly between its two parents.

This condition — |ad − bc| = 1 — connects the Farey tree to one of the most important groups in mathematics (SL(2,ℤ)), but that's for another day.

§ 05 — The Correct RuleCommon slice sizes — a step-by-step method

We've seen from the tarts what needs to happen: convert both fractions to the same denominator (same slice size), then add the numerators (count the slices). Let's see it with numbers, before the formula.

Example: ¼ + ⅙

Quarters and sixths don't match. What is the smallest number that both 4 and 6 divide into cleanly? It's 12.

14 = 312   ·   16 = 212   ·   312 + 212 = 512

⟡ Try before the calculator

Use the same method: 23 + 34. What is the lowest common multiple of 3 and 4? What is the answer? Is it bigger than 1?

LCM(3,4) = 12.   2/3 = 8/12.   3/4 = 9/12.   Sum = 17/12. Yes — bigger than 1, which means more than a whole tart. That's perfectly fine: 17/12 = 1 and 5/12.

⟡ The general formula (once you're comfortable)

ab + cd = ad + bcbd. The bottom (bd) is always a common multiple of b and d. The top (ad + bc) converts each fraction into that common denominator and counts the total slices. This always gives the right answer. You may then need to simplify.

§ 06 — Egyptian FractionsAncient sums of unit fractions

Around 1650 BCE, ancient Egyptians wrote every fraction as a sum of different unit fractions — fractions with 1 on top. No repeats. So 23 became 12 + 16 — you can check: 3/6 + 1/6 = 4/6 = 2/3. ✓

⟡ Try it yourself first

Can you write 34 as a sum of two different unit fractions? What about 56?

3/4 = 1/2 + 1/4. Check: 2/4 + 1/4 = 3/4 ✓

5/6 = 1/2 + 1/3. Check: 3/6 + 2/6 = 5/6 ✓

§ 07 — Comparing FractionsWhich is bigger?

Now we know how to add fractions, comparing them is easy: subtract and check the sign. Even easier: cross-multiply.

To compare ab and cd :   compare   a × d   with   b × c

⟡ Try mentally first

Which is bigger: 35 or 47? Compute 3×7 and 5×4. Whichever is bigger tells you which fraction is bigger.

3×7 = 21.   5×4 = 20.   21 > 20, so 3/5 > 4/7. Quick decimal check: 3/5 = 0.6, 4/7 ≈ 0.571. Confirmed.

Why does cross-multiplication work? Because comparing a/b and c/d is the same as comparing ad/bd and bc/bd — same denominator, so you just compare the tops: ad vs bc.
