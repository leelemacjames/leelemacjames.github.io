Homogeneous Coordinates

Ordinary coordinates use two numbers (x, y) to fix a point. Projective geometry uses three: [X : Y : Z]. The rule is that scaling all three by the same factor gives the same point:

[X : Y : Z] = [2X : 2Y : 2Z] = [−X : −Y : −Z]

To recover ordinary coordinates, divide through by Z:

x = X/Z,   y = Y/Z   (provided Z ≠ 0)
What happens when Z = 0? Where does that "point" live?

When Z = 0, the ratios X/Z and Y/Z blow up. These are points at infinity. They represent directions, not locations. Every family of parallel lines meets at a shared point at infinity. In projective geometry there are no parallel lines: every pair meets somewhere, perhaps at infinity.

The full projective plane = the ordinary plane + a "line at infinity" (all points [X : Y : 0]).

The Circle in Projective Dress

The unit circle x² + y² = 1 becomes, after substituting x = X/Z and y = Y/Z and clearing denominators:

X² + Y² = Z²
This looks familiar. Where else have those three letters appeared in that arrangement?

It is the Pythagorean equation itself: a² + b² = c². In homogeneous coordinates, the unit circle is the Pythagorean equation. Finding rational points on the circle is identical to finding integer solutions of a² + b² = c². The projective viewpoint reveals that Pythagorean triples are not a curiosity of number theory but the coordinate geometry of a conic in the projective plane.

Stereographic Projection from (−1, 0)

The pencil of lines y = m(x + 1) through (−1, 0) is a projective parameterisation of the circle. In homogeneous coordinates, the fixed point is [−1 : 0 : 1]. The line Y = m(X + Z) intersects X² + Y² = Z² at [−1 : 0 : 1] and one other point. The slope m parameterises the entire circle (minus the base point) via a rational map. This is stereographic projection.

The key property: projecting from a rational point on the curve always produces a rational parameterisation. This is a theorem of algebraic geometry, not an accident.

m = 3/4

The Pencil Construction for Metallic Means

Part I used the elegant formulation: cut xy = 1 with y = x − k. But there is another route, one that mirrors the circle's projective pencil structure more closely.

Consider the same pencil of lines y = m(x + 1) through (−1, 0), but now intersecting a different hyperbola:

xy − kx − y = 0
What curve is this? Rearrange to isolate y.

Factor y out of two terms: y(x − 1) = kx, so:

y = kx / (x − 1)

This is a rectangular hyperbola with vertical asymptote x = 1 and horizontal asymptote y = k. Unlike xy = 1, this hyperbola depends on the parameter k, absorbing it into the curve rather than the line.

The Elimination

Substitute y = m(x + 1) into xy − kx − y = 0:

m(x+1)·x − kx − m(x+1) = 0
mx² + mx − kx − mx − m = 0
mx² − kx − m = 0
x² − (k/m)x − 1 = 0

Setting k/m = n (an integer) recovers the metallic mean quadratic x² − nx − 1 = 0. But notice the redundancy: two parameters (k and m) do the work of one (n = k/m). The construction has a gauge freedom that the simpler xy = 1 version eliminates.

Projective Comparison

Circle construction: The base point (−1, 0) lies on the circle. One root of the intersection quadratic is always x = −1, collapsing the quadratic to linear. The pencil gives a rational parameterisation.

Hyperbola construction: The base point (−1, 0) does not lie on xy − kx − y = 0 (substituting gives k ≠ 0). Both roots of the quadratic survive. The pencil produces an irrational involution: a Möbius map x ↦ −1/x with fixed points at x = ±i (the circular points at infinity).

m = 1

Homogeneous Form of the Hyperbola

In projective coordinates [X : Y : Z], the hyperbola xy − kx − y = 0 becomes:

XY − kXZ − YZ = 0

The line y = m(x + 1) becomes Y = m(X + Z), or equivalently Y − mX − mZ = 0. The intersection of these two projective conics and lines is the natural habitat of the metallic mean construction.

Where does the hyperbola XY − kXZ − YZ = 0 meet the line at infinity (Z = 0)?

Set Z = 0: the equation becomes XY = 0. So the hyperbola meets infinity at [1 : 0 : 0] and [0 : 1 : 0], which are the "ends" of the x-axis and y-axis respectively. Every rectangular hyperbola with axes parallel to the coordinate axes passes through these same two points at infinity. This is why the asymptotes are always horizontal and vertical.

Two Constructions, One Pencil

Pythagorean (Circle)

Affine: x² + y² = 1

Projective: X² + Y² = Z²

Base point: on the curve

Pencil: y = m(x+1)

Result: linear residual

Output: rational

Elegant: the right tool for the job

Metallic (Hyperbola)

Simple: xy = 1, y = x − k

Pencil form: xy − kx − y = 0

Base point: off the curve

Pencil: y = m(x+1), n = k/m

Result: genuine quadratic

Output: irrational

Convoluted: gauge redundancy in k/m

The pencil construction through (−1, 0) is the shared projective scaffolding. The circle construction is elegant precisely because it exploits the base point lying on the curve. The metallic construction, in pencil form, carries an extra parameter that must be quotiented out. The simpler xy = 1 formulation is more honest: it uses one parameter (k) for one degree of freedom, and lets the geometry speak without projective overhead.

Both constructions live in the projective plane. But the circle earns its projective treatment; the metallic means merely tolerate it.
