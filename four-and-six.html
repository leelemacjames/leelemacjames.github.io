McMurmerings · on the arithmetic of shape

Four and Six

Why only two lattices in the plane carry a symmetry richer than the half-turn, and why they live at the two corners of the space of all shapes.

A sequel to “The Costume of the Star.”

The previous essay left a debt unpaid. Having argued that a lattice supplies a unit of area for nothing, and that the choice of a polygonal norm to flatter that lattice is a decoration laid over an affine floor rather than a piece of its structure, it closed by admitting that it had not said why the square and the hexagon should be the two shapes around which this decoration arranges itself so willingly, nor why these two lattices among the uncountable many should be the ones their arithmetic singles out. The honest answer asks us to stop staring at any single lattice and to look instead at the space of all of them at once, where the two exceptional shapes reveal themselves as the only two addresses at which anything special can occur, and where the reason there are exactly two, and exactly these two, turns out to be a single fact about whole numbers wearing several disguises.

I The shape of a lattice

A lattice in the plane is the set of integer combinations of two independent vectors, and its shape, as something distinct from its size and its orientation, is what survives once we agree to forget those two. Reading the plane as the complex numbers, a lattice is \(L = \mathbb{Z}\,\omega_1 + \mathbb{Z}\,\omega_2\), and dividing every point of it by \(\omega_1\) rotates and rescales the whole figure without disturbing its shape, since multiplication by a complex number is a similarity, so that every lattice is similar to one of the standard form \(\mathbb{Z} + \mathbb{Z}\tau\) governed by a single complex parameter \(\tau = \omega_2/\omega_1\), which we may always take to lie in the upper half-plane by ordering the two generators to fix the orientation. The shape of a lattice is in this way a single point \(\tau\), and two lattices have the same shape precisely when their parameters agree.

II The space of all shapes

This description is almost faithful, failing only because a lattice carries no preferred pair of generators, so that a different choice of basis for the same lattice yields a different parameter for the very same shape. The bases of a fixed lattice are related by the integer matrices of determinant one, the group written \(\mathrm{SL}(2,\mathbb{Z})\), and these act on the parameter through the fractional transformation \(\tau \mapsto (a\tau+b)/(c\tau+d)\), so that the true space of shapes is the upper half-plane with all such transformations divided out, the object number theorists call the modular curve. One may draw a single representative region for it, a tall vertical strip rising from a circular floor, with every possible shape appearing exactly once inside it, and so lay out the whole moduli of lattices as a gallery one can walk.

Walking that gallery, almost every shape one passes carries only the most meagre symmetry, the half-turn that sends each lattice point to its negative, present everywhere because the negative of a lattice is always the same lattice. This rotation through a straight angle is the common inheritance of every shape, the symmetry no lattice can lack and the generic lattice cannot exceed, so that the typical point of the gallery is a shape whose sole rotational self-coincidence is the turn through \(\pi\).

III Two places it swells

At two points of the gallery, and only two, the symmetry swells beyond this inheritance. The first is the shape \(\tau = i\), the square lattice of the Gaussian integers \(\mathbb{Z}[i]\), where multiplication by \(i\) carries the lattice exactly onto itself, since \(i(\mathbb{Z}+\mathbb{Z}i) = \mathbb{Z}i + \mathbb{Z}i^2 = \mathbb{Z} + \mathbb{Z}i\), and this quarter-turn, repeated, generates a rotational symmetry of order four whose members are the four powers of \(i\). The second is the shape \(\tau = \omega = e^{2\pi i/3}\), the hexagonal lattice of the Eisenstein integers \(\mathbb{Z}[\omega]\), where multiplication by a primitive sixth root of unity preserves the lattice and generates a rotational symmetry of order six, its members the six sixth-roots of unity that are exactly the units of the Eisenstein ring.

These two shapes sit at the two corners of the gallery's circular floor, and they are the fixed points of the only transformations of finite order in the modular group, the inversion \(\tau \mapsto -1/\tau\) that holds \(i\) still and an order-three transformation that holds \(\omega\) still. Setting aside the universal half-turn that every shape already carries, what survives at these two addresses is an extra twofold symmetry standing above the square and an extra threefold symmetry standing above the hexagon, and these are the two cone points at which the gallery, smooth in every other place, draws to a gentle point.

IV Why exactly these

That there should be two such points, bearing symmetries of orders four and six and no others, is no accident of where we happened to look but a theorem about whole numbers, and it is the same integrality that organised the gallery in the first place. A rotation that maps a lattice to itself, expressed in a basis of that lattice, must have integer entries, since it carries basis vectors to other lattice points, and an integer matrix has an integer trace; the trace of a rotation through an angle \(\theta\) is \(2\cos\theta\), so a rotation can preserve a lattice only when \(2\cos\theta\) is a whole number, which pins \(\cos\theta\) to the five values \(\{-1,-\tfrac12,0,\tfrac12,1\}\) and the rotation to the orders one, two, three, four, and six. The familiar absence of any fivefold or sevenfold symmetry in a crystal is this same arithmetic spoken aloud, and the largest rotational symmetries a planar lattice can carry are therefore the fourfold of the square and the sixfold of the hexagon, with the threefold of the triangle already contained inside the sixfold. The whole numbers that forbid a fivefold lattice are the very entries filling the matrices of the modular group, so that the crystallographic restriction and the two cone points of the moduli space are one fact regarded from two sides.

V The arithmetic face

There is a third side to the same fact, and it is the arithmetic one the earlier essay had only gestured toward. The complex numbers that map a lattice into itself form a ring, and for almost every shape that ring is no larger than the ordinary integers, since only the whole-number multiples of a generic lattice land back inside it; at the two exceptional shapes the ring grows, to the Gaussian integers \(\mathbb{Z}[i]\) above the square and to the Eisenstein integers \(\mathbb{Z}[\omega]\) above the hexagon, the two lattices acquiring what number theorists call complex multiplication. The rotational symmetry of a lattice is precisely the group of units of this ring, the elements able to rotate the lattice rather than merely to stretch it, and the swelling of symmetry from twofold to fourfold or sixfold is exactly the swelling of the unit group from \(\{\pm 1\}\) to the fourth or the sixth roots of unity.

Among all the imaginary quadratic fields there are only two, \(\mathbb{Q}(i)\) and \(\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{-3})\), whose integers admit any unit beyond \(\pm 1\), and this is the deepest way to say why the privileged shapes number exactly two. The same pair sits at the only two exceptional values of the coordinate that names a shape, the hexagon at \(j = 0\) and the square at \(j = 1728\), the two values over which the modular curve is branched, so that wherever one looks in the arithmetic the count comes back the same.

VI Back to the norm

Return now to the decoration we had set aside, the polygonal norm chosen to flatter a lattice, and its two natural forms fall out with nothing more to prove. A norm whose unit ball is to share the full symmetry of a lattice must own a ball invariant under that lattice's rotations, and for the generic shape, whose only rotation is the half-turn, no polygon will serve, since any centrally symmetric polygon carries more symmetry than the bare half-turn and so would impose on the lattice a structure it does not possess; the single body matching a generic lattice's poverty of symmetry is the circle, which is to say the Euclidean ball, the shape that flatters every lattice by favouring none. At the square the fourfold symmetry welcomes a square unit ball with its dihedral symmetry of order eight, and at the hexagon the sixfold symmetry welcomes a hexagonal ball with its dihedral symmetry of order twelve, and these are the two polyhedral norms whose symmetry a lattice is able to return in full. The compatible Finsler refinement is therefore a privilege a lattice must earn by its shape rather than a free decoration laid over it, and the privilege falls due at exactly two addresses in the whole moduli of lattices.

None of this disturbs what the first essay claimed, that the lattice supplies its unit of area for nothing and that Pick's count belongs to the affine floor beneath all metrics. What the moduli picture adds is the reason the refinement above that floor is so nearly always idle, the circle serving as the only symmetric measure a generic lattice can host, and the reason that on two occasions, and only two, a lattice may trade its circle for a polygon without surrendering any symmetry. The square and the hexagon, far from being illustrations chosen for convenience, are the two cone points of the space of all shapes, the two imaginary quadratic fields carrying surplus units, the two crystallographic maxima, and the two branch points of the coordinate that names a lattice, which are four descriptions of a single pair of points that the arithmetic of the plane was never going to permit to be three, or five.
