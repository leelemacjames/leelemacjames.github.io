Dimensional Analysis

What an equation must look like before you even solve it
IB Physics SL/HL — Mathematical Tools & Measurement

What is dimensional analysis?

Every physical quantity has a dimension — the type of thing it measures. There are seven base dimensions in SI physics. In IB, you will meet four constantly and three occasionally:

DimensionSymbolSI unitIB frequency
Length[L] or mmetre (m)everywhere
Mass[M] or kgkilogram (kg)everywhere
Time[T] or ssecond (s)everywhere
Temperature[Θ] or Kkelvin (K)thermal physics
Electric current[I] or Aampere (A)electricity
Amount of substance[N] or molmole (mol)gas laws
Luminous intensity[J] or cdcandela (cd)rarely

Every derived unit is built from these. Velocity is \(\text{m/s} = \text{m} \cdot \text{s}^{-1}\), so its dimension is \([L\,T^{-1}]\). Force is \(\text{kg} \cdot \text{m/s}^2\), so its dimension is \([M\,L\,T^{-2}]\). Energy is \(\text{kg} \cdot \text{m}^2/\text{s}^2\), so its dimension is \([M\,L^2\,T^{-2}]\).

The core principle is simple: both sides of any valid physics equation must have the same dimensions. If the left side is a time, the right side must also be a time — not a length, not a speed, not a dimensionless number. This is not a convention; it follows from the fact that you cannot equate physically different types of quantity.

Dimensional analysis exploits this rule in two powerful ways:

1. Checking equations. If you derive a result and the dimensions don't match, you've made an error — guaranteed. This catches algebraic mistakes before you waste time substituting numbers.

2. Discovering equations. If you know which variables a quantity depends on, dimensional analysis can tell you how it depends on them — often uniquely, up to a dimensionless constant. This is the method we explore below.

§1 The Method
How does dimensional analysis actually work as a problem-solving tool? ▾

The recipe has four steps:

Step 1. Identify the quantity you want (e.g. period \(T\)) and the variables it could depend on (e.g. length \(\ell\), gravitational acceleration \(g\)).

Step 2. Write the relationship as a proportionality with unknown exponents:

\(T \propto \ell^{\,x} \cdot g^{\,y}\)

Step 3. Replace each variable with its dimensions and equate the dimensions of both sides:

\([\,T\,] = [\,L\,]^x \cdot [\,L\,T^{-2}\,]^y = L^{x+y}\,T^{-2y}\)

Step 4. Match powers of each base dimension to get simultaneous equations. Solve for the exponents.

You are not solving a physics problem — you are solving an algebra problem that the physics must obey. The answer is the only combination of variables that has the correct units.
§2 Worked Example: Pendulum Period
The period \(T\) of a simple pendulum depends on length \(\ell\) and gravitational field strength \(g\). Find the relationship. ▾

Write the ansatz:

\(T \propto \ell^{\,x} \cdot g^{\,y}\)

Dimensions: \([T] = \text{s}\), \([\ell] = \text{m}\), \([g] = \text{m}\,\text{s}^{-2}\).

Equate:

\(\text{s}^1 = \text{m}^x \cdot (\text{m}\,\text{s}^{-2})^y = \text{m}^{x+y} \cdot \text{s}^{-2y}\)

Now match each base dimension separately:

\(\text{For m}: \quad x + y = 0\)
\(\text{For s}: \quad -2y = 1\)

From the second equation: \(y = -\tfrac{1}{2}\). Substituting: \(x = \tfrac{1}{2}\).

\(T \propto \ell^{1/2} \cdot g^{-1/2} = \sqrt{\frac{\ell}{g}}\)

The full result (from a proper derivation) is \(T = 2\pi\sqrt{\ell/g}\). Dimensional analysis gives you everything except the \(2\pi\).

Notice what didn't appear: the mass of the bob. There is no way to include mass \(m\) (dimension \([M]\)) because neither \(\ell\) nor \(g\) contains \([M]\), and neither does \(T\). Mass is dimensionally excluded — which is why all pendulums of the same length have the same period, regardless of mass.
What if I wrongly guessed that \(T\) depends on mass as well? Would the method catch it? ▾

Try it. Set \(T \propto m^a \cdot \ell^{\,x} \cdot g^{\,y}\). Now:

\(\text{s} = \text{kg}^a \cdot \text{m}^{x+y} \cdot \text{s}^{-2y}\)

Matching \(\text{kg}\): \(a = 0\). The method forces the mass exponent to zero — it cannot participate. You didn't need to know the physics; the dimensions told you.

This works only because the period genuinely has no mass dependence. If the physics did involve mass (e.g. gravitational potential energy \(E = mgh\)), the method would correctly assign a non-zero exponent to \(m\).
§3 Worked Example: Surface Wave Speed
The speed \(v\) of a surface water wave might depend on wavelength \(\lambda\), depth \(D\), gravitational acceleration \(g\), and water temperature \(T_w\). Find the relationship. ▾

Write the ansatz with all four variables:

\(v \propto \lambda^x \cdot D^y \cdot g^z \cdot T_w^{\,w}\)

Dimensions: \([v] = \text{m}\,\text{s}^{-1}\), \([\lambda] = \text{m}\), \([D] = \text{m}\), \([g] = \text{m}\,\text{s}^{-2}\), \([T_w] = \text{K}\).

Equate:

\(\text{m}\,\text{s}^{-1} = \text{m}^{x+y+z} \cdot \text{s}^{-2z} \cdot \text{K}^w\)

Match each base dimension:

\(\text{For K}: \quad w = 0 \quad \text{(temperature drops out!)}\)
\(\text{For s}: \quad -2z = -1 \implies z = \tfrac{1}{2}\)
\(\text{For m}: \quad x + y + \tfrac{1}{2} = 1 \implies x + y = \tfrac{1}{2}\)

We get \(w = 0\) and \(z = \tfrac{1}{2}\), but we cannot separate \(x\) and \(y\) — both \(\lambda\) and \(D\) have the same dimension (length), so the method cannot distinguish them.

\(v \propto \lambda^x \cdot D^{1/2 - x} \cdot g^{1/2} = \sqrt{g} \cdot \lambda^x \cdot D^{1/2-x}\)

The simplest case is \(x = 0\), giving \(v \propto \sqrt{gD}\) — the shallow-water wave speed. Or \(y = 0\), giving \(v \propto \sqrt{g\lambda}\) — the deep-water wave speed. Both are real physical limits.

Temperature was excluded for the same reason mass was excluded from the pendulum: velocity is \([M^0\,L^1\,T^{-1}\,\Theta^0]\), so any variable with dimension \([\Theta]\) must enter with exponent zero. Dimensional analysis can't tell you why temperature doesn't matter — only that it can't appear in this combination.
Why couldn't dimensional analysis separate \(\lambda\) from \(D\)? ▾

Because \(\lambda\) and \(D\) have identical dimensions — both are \([\text{m}]\). The method matches powers of base dimensions, and two variables with the same dimension are invisible to it. You get one equation (\(x + y = \tfrac{1}{2}\)) for two unknowns — an underdetermined system.

This is a genuine limitation. Dimensional analysis tells you the form of the answer but not the function of dimensionless ratios. The full solution involves a function \(f(D/\lambda)\) that the method cannot determine:

\(v = \sqrt{g\lambda \cdot f\!\left(\frac{D}{\lambda}\right)}\)

In the shallow limit (\(D \ll \lambda\)), \(f \to D/\lambda\), giving \(v \propto \sqrt{gD}\). In the deep limit (\(D \gg \lambda\)), \(f \to \text{const}\), giving \(v \propto \sqrt{g\lambda}\).

Whenever two or more variables share the same dimension, dimensional analysis gives a family of solutions rather than a unique one. You need physics (or experiment) to pin down the rest.
§4 Fundamental Constants & Prefixes
What are the dimensions of the fundamental constants \(c\), \(h\), \(e\), \(k_B\), and \(N_A\)? ▾

Each constant connects different physical quantities, and its dimensions encode that connection:

ConstantValueUnitDimension
Speed of light \(c\)\(2.998 \times 10^8\)m s⁻¹\([L\,T^{-1}]\)
Planck's constant \(h\)\(6.626 \times 10^{-34}\)J·s\([M\,L^2\,T^{-1}]\)
Elementary charge \(e\)\(1.602 \times 10^{-19}\)C\([I\,T]\)
Boltzmann constant \(k_B\)\(1.381 \times 10^{-23}\)J K⁻¹\([M\,L^2\,T^{-2}\,\Theta^{-1}]\)
Avogadro's number \(N_A\)\(6.022 \times 10^{23}\)mol⁻¹\([N^{-1}]\)
\(h\) has units of J·s = energy × time, which is also the unit of action. This is not coincidence — Planck's constant is the fundamental quantum of action, the smallest "packet" of energy×time that nature permits.

The trap: when a prefixed unit is squared or cubed, the prefix is also raised to that power. \(1\text{ cm}^2 = (10^{-2}\text{ m})^2 = 10^{-4}\text{ m}^2\), not \(10^{-2}\text{ m}^2\). This is the single most common unit-conversion error in IB exams.

Example: A density is given as \(7800\text{ kg m}^{-3}\). Express it in \(\text{g cm}^{-3}\):

\(7800 \;\frac{\text{kg}}{\text{m}^3} = 7800 \;\frac{10^3\,\text{g}}{(10^2\,\text{cm})^3} = 7800 \;\frac{10^3\,\text{g}}{10^6\,\text{cm}^3} = 7.8 \;\frac{\text{g}}{\text{cm}^3}\)
§5 Further Examples
Derive the form of the drag force \(F\) on a sphere moving through a fluid, assuming it depends on radius \(r\), speed \(v\), and fluid density \(\rho\). ▾

Ansatz: \(F \propto r^a \cdot v^b \cdot \rho^c\).

Dimensions: \([F] = \text{kg}\,\text{m}\,\text{s}^{-2}\), \([r] = \text{m}\), \([v] = \text{m}\,\text{s}^{-1}\), \([\rho] = \text{kg}\,\text{m}^{-3}\).

\(\text{kg}\,\text{m}\,\text{s}^{-2} = \text{m}^a \cdot (\text{m}\,\text{s}^{-1})^b \cdot (\text{kg}\,\text{m}^{-3})^c\)
\(= \text{kg}^c \cdot \text{m}^{a+b-3c} \cdot \text{s}^{-b}\)

Three equations, three unknowns:

\(\text{kg}: \quad c = 1\)
\(\text{s}: \quad -b = -2 \implies b = 2\)
\(\text{m}: \quad a + 2 - 3 = 1 \implies a = 2\)
\(F \propto r^2 v^2 \rho = \rho\,r^2\,v^2\)

The actual formula is \(F = \tfrac{1}{2}C_D \rho A v^2\), where \(A = \pi r^2\) is the cross-sectional area and \(C_D\) is a dimensionless drag coefficient. Dimensional analysis gets the \(\rho r^2 v^2\) structure exactly right — the \(\tfrac{1}{2}C_D\pi\) factor is dimensionless and invisible to the method.

This is the high-speed (inertial) drag regime. At very low speeds, viscosity \(\mu\) (dimension \([\text{kg}\,\text{m}^{-1}\,\text{s}^{-1}]\)) replaces density as the relevant fluid property, and the same method gives Stokes' law: \(F \propto \mu r v\).
Check that \(E = mc^2\) is dimensionally consistent. ▾

Left side: \([E] = \text{kg}\,\text{m}^2\,\text{s}^{-2}\).

Right side: \([m][c]^2 = \text{kg} \cdot (\text{m}\,\text{s}^{-1})^2 = \text{kg}\,\text{m}^2\,\text{s}^{-2}\).

\(\text{LHS} = \text{RHS} \quad \checkmark\)

This doesn't prove the equation is true — dimensional consistency is necessary but not sufficient. The equation \(E = 7mc^2\) is also dimensionally consistent. But dimensional inconsistency proves an equation is false.

Dimensional analysis is an asymmetric tool: it can prove you're wrong but never prove you're right. This makes it a perfect error-catching mechanism.
Can you use dimensional analysis to estimate the Planck length — a scale built from \(G\), \(\hbar\), and \(c\)? ▾

We want a length \(\ell_P \propto G^a \cdot \hbar^b \cdot c^d\).

Dimensions: \([G] = \text{m}^3\,\text{kg}^{-1}\,\text{s}^{-2}\), \([\hbar] = \text{kg}\,\text{m}^2\,\text{s}^{-1}\), \([c] = \text{m}\,\text{s}^{-1}\).

\(\text{m} = (\text{m}^3\,\text{kg}^{-1}\,\text{s}^{-2})^a \cdot (\text{kg}\,\text{m}^2\,\text{s}^{-1})^b \cdot (\text{m}\,\text{s}^{-1})^d\)
\(\text{kg}: \quad -a + b = 0 \implies a = b\)
\(\text{s}: \quad -2a - b - d = 0\)
\(\text{m}: \quad 3a + 2b + d = 1\)

From \(a = b\): \(-2a - a - d = 0 \implies d = -3a\). Substituting into the m-equation: \(3a + 2a - 3a = 1 \implies 2a = 1 \implies a = \tfrac{1}{2}\).

So \(a = b = \tfrac{1}{2}\), \(d = -\tfrac{3}{2}\):

\(\ell_P = \sqrt{\frac{G\hbar}{c^3}} \approx 1.6 \times 10^{-35}\text{ m}\)
The Planck length is \(10^{-20}\) times smaller than a proton. It is the scale where quantum mechanics and general relativity collide — built entirely from the three constants that govern gravity, quantum theory, and the speed limit of the universe. Dimensional analysis doesn't just find it; it tells you these are the only three ingredients you need.
§6 Dimensional Analysis as an Error Detector
A student derives the centripetal acceleration as \(a = v^2 r\). Is this correct? ▾

Check dimensions:

\([v^2 r] = (\text{m}\,\text{s}^{-1})^2 \cdot \text{m} = \text{m}^3\,\text{s}^{-2}\)

But \([a] = \text{m}\,\text{s}^{-2}\).

\(\text{m}^3\,\text{s}^{-2} \neq \text{m}\,\text{s}^{-2}\). The formula is wrong.

The correct formula is \(a = v^2/r\), which gives:

\([v^2/r] = \text{m}^2\,\text{s}^{-2} / \text{m} = \text{m}\,\text{s}^{-2} \quad \checkmark\)
This takes five seconds and catches the most common algebraic error — inverting a variable. Build the habit: after deriving any result, check its dimensions before moving on.
What about dimensionless quantities — can they break the method? ▾

Dimensionless quantities (angles in radians, refractive index, drag coefficients, \(\pi\), \(2\pi\), \(\tfrac{1}{2}\), logarithms, exponentials) are invisible to dimensional analysis. The method cannot detect them, produce them, or verify them.

This means:

• Any pure number can appear as a multiplicative factor, and the method won't notice. That's why \(T = 2\pi\sqrt{\ell/g}\) and \(T = \sqrt{\ell/g}\) are both dimensionally valid.

• You cannot use dimensional analysis to derive the argument of a trig function, exponential, or logarithm — but you can check that the argument is dimensionless. If you write \(\sin(mg\ell)\), the argument has dimensions of energy (J), which is meaningless — \(\sin\) requires a pure number.

Rule: the argument of \(\sin\), \(\cos\), \(\exp\), \(\ln\), and any other transcendental function must be dimensionless. If it isn't, the equation is wrong.
Σ The Toolkit

To discover a relationship: list the variables → write \(Q \propto A^a B^b C^c\) → equate dimensions → solve for exponents → accept that dimensionless constants are unknown.

To check an equation: compute the dimensions of both sides independently → compare. If they disagree, the equation is wrong.

To check a function argument: the argument of \(\sin\), \(\cos\), \(\exp\), \(\ln\) must be dimensionless. If it has units, the equation is wrong.

Dimensional analysis is the closest physics gets to a free lunch. It converts ignorance about the detailed mechanism into knowledge about the structural form of the answer — and it costs nothing but five minutes of algebra.
