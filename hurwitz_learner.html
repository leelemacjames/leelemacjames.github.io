From Eisenstein to Hurwitz

A guided extension. Starting from the unit group of $\mathbb{Z}[\omega]$ and asking what happens when we want richer representation theory, the path leads through Hamilton's quaternions to the unique maximal $\mathbb{Z}$-order with $24$ units — and to the binary tetrahedral group that sits inside it.

L. McCulloch-James · Working Note · v1
§ 1

Starting point: the unit group of $\mathbb{Z}[\omega]$

The Eisenstein integers $\mathbb{Z}[\omega]$, with $\omega = e^{2\pi i/3}$ a primitive cube root of unity, form a Euclidean domain inside $\mathbb{C}$. Their group of units is

$$ \mathbb{Z}[\omega]^\times \;=\; \{\pm 1,\, \pm\omega,\, \pm\omega^2\} \;\cong\; \mathbb{Z}_6 \;=\; \mathbb{Z}_2 \times \mathbb{Z}_3. $$

The two factors are independent: the $\mathbb{Z}_2$ is generated by $-1$ (sign flip), the $\mathbb{Z}_3$ by $\omega$ (rotation by $2\pi/3$). Multiplication by $\omega$ partitions the six units into three orbits of size two: $\{1, -1\}$, $\{\omega, -\omega\}$, $\{\omega^2, -\omega^2\}$. Equivalently, the quotient $\mathbb{Z}[\omega]^\times / \{\pm 1\}$ is a cyclic group of order three.

This is the structure to keep in view: an abelian group with three classes that come for free from the arithmetic of a particular imaginary quadratic ring, before any further input. The interactive figure below lets you click each unit and see which $\mathbb{Z}_3$ class it belongs to.

Aside · why one looks at $\mathbb{Z}[\omega]^\times$ in the first place

A reader coming from spinor calculus may want to know what motivates the focus on unit groups of arithmetic rings. The short version: the Lie algebra $\mathfrak{gl}(2,\mathbb{C})$ decomposes as $\mathfrak{sl}(2,\mathbb{C}) \oplus \mathbb{C}$, and the abelian summand is exactly the trace one-form $a = \tfrac{1}{2}\,\omega^A{}_A$ that lifts ordinary spinors to spinor densities via the determinant: $\psi \mapsto (\det A)^w A\psi$. So the $\mathbb{C}$ in the second summand is doing real work — it is the carrier of the complex density weight, magnitude and phase together.

Once one notices this, two questions become natural. First: what arithmetic does the abelian summand actually want to carry? Imaginary-quadratic rings of class number one are the natural shortlist, and Eisenstein is the only one whose unit group has a $\mathbb{Z}_3$. Second: what happens if we try to enlarge $\mathbb{C}$ further? That second question turns out to require leaving commutative arithmetic altogether (§2), and the question of what plays the role of the determinant in the non-commutative setting is the subject of §5.

A natural next question, once one has the Eisenstein $\mathbb{Z}_3$ in hand: can larger and more interesting groups be obtained from the units of similarly natural number systems? The answer constrains us in surprising ways, and this is the substance of §2 onward.

One feature of the Eisenstein $\mathbb{Z}_3$ to note before we leave it: at present it lives inside $\mathbb{C}$, where it acts as a planar rotation by $2\pi/3$. It cycles three points in a single complex line. We will see, much later, that this same $\mathbb{Z}_3$ reappears inside the Hurwitz quaternion arithmetic — but not as a planar rotation. There it acts as a $120°$ rotation cycling the three quaternionic directions $i \to j \to k \to i$. The cyclic-three structure is internalised as an automorphism of an honest 3-dimensional space. That promotion from planar to spatial is one of the structural payoffs of the move, and it gives the precise reason why $|2T| = 24 = 8 \times 3$: the eight Lipschitz quaternionic directions, acted on by the three-fold Eisenstein cycle.

§ 2

The obstruction: abelian unit groups have only 1-d irreps

$\mathbb{Z}[\omega]^\times \cong \mathbb{Z}_6$ is abelian. By a basic result in representation theory, every irreducible complex representation of an abelian group is $1$-dimensional — they are characters $\mathbb{Z}_6 \to \mathbb{C}^\times$, and there are exactly six of them, one for each group element when one identifies $\mathbb{Z}_6$ with its own dual.

This is a hard ceiling. No matter how cleverly one assembles the units of any subring of $\mathbb{C}$, the unit group is contained in $\mathbb{C}^\times$ and is therefore abelian. So we cannot produce a $2$-dimensional or $3$-dimensional irreducible representation — let alone a non-trivial collection of them — from any subring of $\mathbb{C}$. The Eisenstein hexagon is, in this sense, the end of the line.

To get higher-dimensional irreducibles, we have to leave commutativity behind. The next natural number system must be a non-commutative algebra over $\mathbb{Q}$ in which $\mathbb{Z}[\omega]$ embeds.

Aside · why one might care to extend Several independent threads from physics and from arithmetic geometry converge on the same question. In the chiral $\mathrm{GL}(2,\mathbb{C})$ formalism for gravity, the abelian summand of $\mathfrak{gl}(2,\mathbb{C})$ is geometrically inert and its arithmetic is free to be chosen; the natural reading is Eisenstein, and the natural question is whether the choice extends. Independently, the classification of finite subgroups of $\mathrm{SU}(2)$ (the McKay correspondence) shows that the binary tetrahedral group $2T$ — which we are about to encounter — corresponds to the $E_6$ Dynkin diagram. Both contexts point to the same mathematical object. The remainder of this learner stays inside the arithmetic and lets the rest follow.
§ 3

What the next number system must satisfy

Before constructing it, we list the constraints. The new number system $\mathbb{X}$ must:

R1. Contain $\mathbb{Z}[\omega]$ — or at least a subring isomorphic to it. The Eisenstein $\mathbb{Z}_3$ is the structure we already have; an extension that discards it is not an extension. Equivalently: the new arithmetic must contain a primitive cube root of unity, i.e.\ an element $\alpha$ with $\alpha^3 = 1$ and $\alpha \neq 1$.

R2. Be non-commutative. By §2, otherwise no irreducibles of dimension $> 1$.

R3. Have a unit group with rich enough representation theory. We will aim, concretely, for a unit group $G$ admitting at least three non-isomorphic 1-d irreps, a 2-d irrep, and a 3-d irrep. By Burnside's relation $\sum d_i^2 = |G|$, this forces $|G| \geq 1+1+1+4+9 = 16$, and in practice (since irrep dimensions divide $|G|$) the smallest natural option turns out to be $|G| = 24$.

R4. Be an order (see definition below) in some natural division algebra over $\mathbb{Q}$. We want a parent ring whose arithmetic is well-defined; we do not want to assemble $\mathbb{X}$ by hand from a list of generators with no host structure.

R5. Be a maximal order. If a non-maximal order satisfies all the requirements, the larger order containing it does too. There is no principled way to stop short of the maximum, so we want the canonical object.

Aside · two meanings of "order" We are about to use the word order in two completely different technical senses, and it is worth nailing both down before the next paragraph.

Order of a group (and of an element): the number of elements. $|2T| = 24$. The element $\rho$ has order $6$ because $\rho^6 = 1$ and no smaller power equals $1$. This is the sense used in R3.

Order in an algebra (the sense used in R4, R5, and everywhere $\mathbb{H}_{\mathrm{H}}$ is called "the Hurwitz order"): a subring $\mathcal{O} \subset A$ of a finite-dimensional $\mathbb{Q}$-algebra $A$ which is a finitely-generated $\mathbb{Z}$-module spanning $A$ over $\mathbb{Q}$. Concretely: $\mathcal{O}$ is closed under addition and multiplication, contains $1$, sits inside $A$ as a lattice (a $\mathbb{Z}$-span of an $A$-basis), and every element of $\mathcal{O}$ satisfies a monic polynomial with integer coefficients.

The integer-analogue is the cleanest example. $\mathbb{Z}$ is an order in $\mathbb{Q}$: a subring, $\mathbb{Z}$-spanning, every element a root of $x - n$. The Eisenstein integers $\mathbb{Z}[\omega]$ are an order in $\mathbb{Q}(\omega)$. A maximal order is one not properly contained in any larger order; for $\mathbb{Z}[\omega]$ it is the ring of integers of the field, and it is maximal. The Lipschitz integers $L$ are an order in $\mathbb{Q} \otimes \mathbb{H}$ but are not maximal — they sit properly inside $\mathbb{H}_{\mathrm{H}}$, which is.

The smallest division algebra over $\mathbb{Q}$ that strictly contains $\mathbb{C}$ and is non-commutative is the Hamilton quaternion algebra $\mathbb{H} = \mathbb{R}\langle 1, i, j, k \rangle$ with relations $i^2 = j^2 = k^2 = ijk = -1$. There is essentially nothing smaller. R1–R5 collectively push us toward a maximal order in $\mathbb{Q} \otimes \mathbb{H}$. The naive guess is the Lipschitz order $L = \mathbb{Z}\langle 1,i,j,k\rangle$. The next subsection rules it out — not because it fails to be an order (it is one), but because it fails to be maximal and fails to contain the Eisenstein cube root.

§ 4

Why Lipschitz fails, why Hurwitz works

The Lipschitz order $L = \mathbb{Z}\langle 1,i,j,k\rangle$ has eight units: $\{\pm 1, \pm i, \pm j, \pm k\}$, forming the quaternion group $Q_8$. This satisfies R2 (non-commutative) and R4 (an order). It fails the others.

RequirementLipschitz $L$ ($Q_8$, order 8)Hurwitz $\mathbb{H}_{\mathrm{H}}$ ($2T$, order 24)
R1 contains $\mathbb{Z}[\omega]$No — Eisenstein needs a cube root, $L$ has noneYes — $\rho = \tfrac{1}{2}(1+i+j+k)$ has $\rho^3 = -1$
R3 enough irreps5 irreps of dim $(1,1,1,1,2)$ — only one 2d, no 3d7 irreps of dim $(1,1,1,2,2,2,3)$ — exactly what we need
R5 maximal orderNo — $L \subsetneq \mathbb{H}_{\mathrm{H}}$Yes — unique maximal order up to conjugacy

The fix is to adjoin one half-integer point. Define the Hurwitz order

$$ \mathbb{H}_{\mathrm{H}} \;=\; L + \mathbb{Z}\rho, \qquad \rho \;=\; \tfrac{1}{2}(1 + i + j + k). $$

The element $\rho$ has norm $\rho \bar{\rho} = \tfrac{1}{4}(1+1+1+1) = 1$, so it is a unit. Direct calculation: $\rho^2 = \tfrac{1}{2}(-1 + i + j + k)$, and $\rho^3 = -1$. So $\rho$ has order $6$ in the unit group, and $\rho^2$ generates a $\mathbb{Z}_3$ — precisely the Eisenstein cube root, now living as $\rho^2$ inside a non-commutative parent (R1 satisfied).

Squaring first, using $i^2 = j^2 = k^2 = -1$ and $ij = k$, $jk = i$, $ki = j$, $ji = -k$ etc.:

$$ (1+i+j+k)^2 \;=\; -2 + 2i + 2j + 2k, \qquad \rho^2 \;=\; \tfrac{1}{2}(-1 + i + j + k). $$

Then $\rho^3 = \rho \cdot \rho^2$. Multiplying $(1+i+j+k)(-1+i+j+k)$ and collecting terms, every imaginary contribution cancels and the real parts sum to $-4$:

$$ \rho^3 \;=\; \tfrac{1}{4} \cdot (-4) \;=\; -1. $$

Hence $\rho^6 = 1$. The element $\rho$ has order 6, and $\rho^2$ has order 3 — exactly the Eisenstein cube root, now living inside a non-commutative arithmetic.

The full unit group of $\mathbb{H}_{\mathrm{H}}$ has order 24. Eight of the units are the Lipschitz units; the other sixteen are $\tfrac{1}{2}(\pm 1 \pm i \pm j \pm k)$. This 24-element group is the binary tetrahedral group $2T$, isomorphic to $\mathrm{SL}(2, \mathbb{F}_3)$, fitting in the non-split extension

$$ 1 \to \mathbb{Z}_2 \to 2T \to A_4 \to 1, $$

where $A_4$ is the rotation group of the regular tetrahedron and the central $\mathbb{Z}_2$ is $\{\pm 1\}$ — the universal charge conjugation.

Forced, not chosen Voight's Quaternion Algebras (Springer GTM 288, §11) establishes that $\mathbb{H}_{\mathrm{H}}$ is the unique maximal order in $\mathbb{Q}\otimes\mathbb{H}$ up to conjugacy. We do not choose $\mathbb{H}_{\mathrm{H}}$ from a menu; once we accept R1–R5, it is the only option.
§ 5

What replaces the determinant

A point we have skirted: when we move from $\mathfrak{gl}(2,\mathbb{C})$ to a setting where the trace summand is enlarged from $\mathbb{C}$ to a quaternionic order, we are no longer working over a commutative field. The determinant — the structural object that distinguishes $\mathrm{GL}$ from $\mathrm{SL}$ — does not survive the move unchanged. Before going further it is worth asking what it gets replaced by, because the answer is not a formal substitution of one symbol for another. The replacement involves a genuine split of the data the determinant was carrying.

What the determinant was doing

For $A \in M_2(\mathbb{C})$, the determinant has three properties that pull together a great deal of structure: it is multiplicative, $\det(AB) = \det A \det B$; it detects invertibility, $A$ invertible iff $\det A \neq 0$; and it lands in a commutative field, $\det A \in \mathbb{C}$. The first two are algebraic; the third is what makes the construction useful for tracking density weights, since a scalar can be raised to a power.

In $\mathrm{SL}(2,\mathbb{C})$ the determinant is fixed at $1$, and ordinary spinors transform without any density-weight factor. In $\mathrm{GL}(2,\mathbb{C})$ the determinant $\det A = r e^{i\theta} \in \mathbb{C}^\times$ is non-trivial, and spinors lift to spinor densities transforming as $\psi \mapsto (\det A)^w A \psi$. The complex determinant carries two pieces of information at once: a real magnitude $r$, and a $\mathrm{U}(1)$ phase $\theta$. Both are needed for the density-weight construction; both are part of what $\det$ is.

Why this breaks over $\mathbb{H}$

Over a quaternionic matrix ring $M_2(\mathbb{H})$, the naive formula $ad - bc$ is ambiguous because multiplication in $\mathbb{H}$ does not commute. There is no determinant satisfying all three of the properties above with quaternionic-valued entries. The standard remedy is the reduced norm $\mathrm{Nrd}(A)$, defined by embedding $\mathbb{H} \hookrightarrow M_2(\mathbb{C})$ as

$$ a + bi + cj + dk \;\longmapsto\; \begin{pmatrix} a + bi & c + di \\ -c + di & a - bi \end{pmatrix}, $$

extending entrywise to $M_2(\mathbb{H}) \hookrightarrow M_4(\mathbb{C})$, and taking the ordinary complex determinant of the $4 \times 4$ image:

$$ \mathrm{Nrd}(A) \;=\; \det_{\mathbb{C}}\bigl(\Phi(A)\bigr) \;\in\; \mathbb{R}_{\geq 0}. $$

On a single quaternion $q = a + bi + cj + dk$, the reduced norm specialises to $\mathrm{Nrd}(q) = q\bar{q} = a^2 + b^2 + c^2 + d^2$, which is the same $N(q)$ we will meet in §6. Multiplicativity and invertibility-detection are restored. But the output is now real, not complex.

What is lost, and what comes back as something else

The complex determinant $\det A = r e^{i\theta}$ packed magnitude and phase into a single $\mathbb{C}^\times$-valued object. The reduced norm keeps only the magnitude:

SettingObjectLives inCarries
$M_2(\mathbb{C})$$\det A = r e^{i\theta}$$\mathbb{C}^\times$scale + phase
$M_2(\mathbb{H})$$\mathrm{Nrd}(A) = r^2$$\mathbb{R}_{>0}$scale only

So the phase has gone missing. Where does it go? The answer is that on quaternion entries it does not vanish — it is just no longer scalar. Every non-zero quaternion factors as

$$ q \;=\; |q| \cdot u, \qquad |q| = \sqrt{N(q)} \in \mathbb{R}_{>0}, \qquad u \in \mathrm{SU}(2),\quad N(u) = 1. $$

The magnitude $|q|$ is what the reduced norm sees. The unit factor $u$ is a unit quaternion acting on the matrix by left-multiplication, and it is generally not a scalar — it is an internal $\mathrm{SU}(2)$ rotation. So the single complex determinant has split into two structurally different pieces: a real scaling factor, and a non-commutative internal symmetry.

The Hurwitz layer on top

Restricting to Hurwitz arithmetic, the unit factor $u$ is forced to lie in the maximal-order unit group:

$$ u \;\in\; \mathbb{H}_{\mathrm{H}}^\times \;=\; 2T, \qquad |2T| = 24. $$

So the enriched transformation a spinor density would undergo, in this Hurwitz-extended setting, is schematically

$$ \psi \;\longmapsto\; \mathrm{Nrd}(A)^w \cdot \rho(u) \cdot A\psi, \qquad u \in 2T, $$

where $\rho$ is some representation of $2T$. The continuous density weight $\mathrm{Nrd}(A)^w$ is the surviving scalar piece; $\rho(u)$ is a finite internal sector, not a density weight at all. Hurwitz units cannot be treated as ordinary scalar weights unless one restricts to the centre $Z(2T) = \{\pm 1\}$, in which case $\rho(u) = \pm 1$ and the construction collapses back to a sign — exactly the discrete data that survives at the end of the breaking chain in §8.

The corrected slogan

Hurwitz enrichment gives density weights plus discrete internal sectors, not "Hurwitz units are determinants". The complex determinant carried magnitude and phase as one $\mathbb{C}^\times$-valued object. The quaternionic story splits these into a real magnitude (the reduced norm, continuous) and a finite group element (the unit factor in $2T$, discrete). The split is the actual content of the move $\mathbb{C} \rightsquigarrow \mathbb{H}_{\mathrm{H}}$ on the trace sector, and it is the reason the Hurwitz refinement adds genuinely new structure rather than just changing the arithmetic of an existing one.

§ 6

Why this refinement: a quadratic invariant the units preserve

Before enumerating the units of $\mathbb{H}_{\mathrm{H}}$ and their representations, it is worth pausing to say what kind of mathematical object the Hurwitz order actually is, in language a physicist already lives in. The picture is short and clean.

The quaternion algebra $\mathbb{H}$, viewed as a real vector space, is just $\mathbb{R}^4$ with coordinates $(a, b, c, d)$ corresponding to $a + bi + cj + dk$. It carries a natural quadratic form — the norm

$$ N(q) \;=\; q\bar{q} \;=\; a^2 + b^2 + c^2 + d^2, $$

which is positive-definite. The unit quaternions — elements with $N(q) = 1$ — form the group $\mathrm{Sp}(1) \cong \mathrm{SU}(2)$, and they act on $\mathbb{H}$ by conjugation $q \mapsto u q u^{-1}$. This action preserves $N$ (norm is multiplicative, $N(uqu^{-1}) = N(q)$) and fixes the real axis pointwise, so on $\mathrm{Im}(\mathbb{H}) = \mathbb{R}^3$ it acts as the double cover $\mathrm{SU}(2) \twoheadrightarrow \mathrm{SO}(3)$.

Foothold · the analogy worth keeping in view

A physicist will recognise the structure. On the relativistic side, Minkowski space carries the quadratic invariant $P^\mu P_\mu$, preserved by the Lorentz group $\mathrm{SO}(1,3)$ (or its cover $\mathrm{SL}(2,\mathbb{C})$). Irreducible representations of the Poincaré group are organised by the value of this Casimir: mass shells are level sets of $P^\mu P_\mu$.

On the quaternionic side, $\mathbb{R}^4 = \mathbb{H}$ carries the quadratic invariant $N(q)$, preserved by $\mathrm{SU}(2)$ acting by conjugation. The "level sets" $N(q) = 1$ are the unit sphere $S^3$, and the finite subgroups of $\mathrm{SU}(2)$ — among them $2T$ — sit on this sphere as discrete configurations of points that the symmetry preserves.

Quaternion norm $N(q) \;\longleftrightarrow\;$ Casimir $P^\mu P_\mu$. Both are quadratic invariants. Both organise the structure of the space and its representations. The Hurwitz order is to $N$ what a well-adapted representation basis is to a Casimir: it exposes the invariant cleanly and aligns the discrete structure with the symmetry that preserves it.

What the Hurwitz refinement actually does

Now the question of why we needed to go beyond Lipschitz becomes clearer. The Lipschitz units $\{\pm 1, \pm i, \pm j, \pm k\}$ are eight points on $S^3$, and they are preserved by conjugation, but they form only the $D_4$ root system in $\mathbb{R}^4$ — a comparatively small finite configuration. The 24 Hurwitz units are the vertices of the regular 24-cell, the short roots of the $F_4$ root system. They are the largest finite set of points on $S^3$ that forms a regular polytope, and they are the discrete subgroup of $\mathrm{SU}(2)$ that is maximal among those whose action on $\mathrm{Im}(\mathbb{H})$ projects to a tetrahedral rotation group.

The transition

$$ L \;=\; \mathbb{Z}\langle 1,i,j,k\rangle \;\longrightarrow\; \mathbb{H}_{\mathrm{H}} \;=\; L + \mathbb{Z}\rho $$

does not change the underlying invariant: it is still $N(q) = a^2 + b^2 + c^2 + d^2$. What changes is which lattice points land on the unit sphere $S^3 = \{q : N(q) = 1\}$. The Lipschitz lattice has $8$ such points: $\pm 1, \pm i, \pm j, \pm k$, the integer vectors of squared norm $1$. The Hurwitz lattice has $24$ such points: the $8$ Lipschitz units, plus the $16$ half-integer vectors $\tfrac{1}{2}(\pm 1 \pm i \pm j \pm k)$, each of squared norm $\tfrac{1}{4}(1+1+1+1) = 1$. Adjoining $\rho$ adds $16$ new norm-$1$ elements to the lattice without changing the ambient quadratic form.

These norm-$1$ elements are the units of the order. They act on the lattice by left-multiplication, and that action is exactly the part of the continuous $\mathrm{SU}(2)$ norm-preserving symmetry that also preserves the discrete lattice. So the unit group of the order is the discrete intersection

$$ G \;=\; \{u \in \mathrm{SU}(2) : u \cdot \mathcal{O} = \mathcal{O}\} \;=\; \{u \in \mathcal{O} : N(u) = 1\}. $$

For Lipschitz $\mathcal{O} = L$, this is $Q_8$ of order $8$. For Hurwitz $\mathcal{O} = \mathbb{H}_{\mathrm{H}}$, this is $2T$ of order $24$. The Hurwitz refinement makes more of $\mathrm{SU}(2)$ visible to the arithmetic.

What the lattices look like

The two lattices live in $\mathbb{R}^4$, which we cannot directly see. But the structural distinction can be exhibited cleanly by slicing along the real-imaginary plane and along the imaginary 3-space, and looking at the points on the unit sphere in each slice. This is the structural answer to "why Hurwitz, not Lipschitz". Both are $\mathbb{Z}$-orders in $\mathbb{Q}\otimes\mathbb{H}$. Both inherit the same norm $N$. But $\mathbb{H}_{\mathrm{H}}$ contains $24$ unit-norm lattice points where $L$ contains only $8$, and the additional $16$ are exactly what is needed to close the discrete arithmetic under the action of $\rho$. From this viewpoint, the maximality theorem (Voight §11) is no longer an opaque fact: it is the statement that no further unit-norm lattice points can be added by enlarging the order, and the alignment between unit-set and lattice has reached its limit.

One consequence: the units form a root system

A small bonus, useful later. The 24 Hurwitz units, viewed as vectors in $\mathbb{R}^4$, are the short roots of the $F_4$ root system. The full $F_4$ root system has $48$ vectors — the $24$ units plus their dual lattice $24$-cell, which consists of vectors of squared norm $2$. The Weyl group $W(F_4)$ has order $1152$ and acts on this configuration; restricted to the $24$ short roots alone, it factors through the symmetry group of the 24-cell. We will not need this in what follows, but it is the bridge between the arithmetic story (Hurwitz order) and the Lie-theoretic story ($F_4$ as an exceptional Lie group), and it is the place where the recent $24$-cell programmes connect.

The representation count

The character table of $2T$ has seven conjugacy classes. The dimensions of the seven complex irreducible representations are

$$ (d_1, d_2, d_3, d_4, d_5, d_6, d_7) \;=\; (1,1,1,2,2,2,3), \qquad \sum d_i^2 \;=\; 1+1+1+4+4+4+9 \;=\; 24. $$

The right-hand side equals $|2T|$, as Burnside's relation requires. There is no leftover representation.

The element $\rho \in 2T$ acts on $(i, j, k)$ by cyclically permuting them: $i \mapsto j \mapsto k \mapsto i$.

The representation theory does the work; the physics is a re-labelling. The remainder of this learner will not lean on the correspondence — it stays inside the arithmetic.
§ 8

Stabiliser chains: subgroups fixing a complex sub-line

Inside $\mathbb{H}$, the real subspace $\mathbb{R}\cdot 1$ together with any chosen unit imaginary $u$ (with $u^2 = -1$) spans a copy of $\mathbb{C}$:

$$ \mathbb{C}_u \;=\; \mathbb{R}\cdot 1 \,+\, \mathbb{R}\cdot u \;\subset\; \mathbb{H}, \qquad u^2 = -1. $$

This is a 2-dimensional real subspace, and a copy of the complex numbers as a subring. The question we ask: which elements of $2T$ preserve $\mathbb{C}_u$ under the conjugation action $v \mapsto g v g^{-1}$? Different precise readings of "preserve" give different answers, and following the chain of these answers reveals a sequence of nested subgroups of $2T$ — a breaking chain forced purely by linear algebra.

The two distinct stabiliser computations

A point worth being careful about: there are two reasonable things to mean by "the stabiliser of $\mathbb{C}_u$ in $2T$", and they give genuinely different answers. Take $u = i$ for concreteness, so $\mathbb{C}_u = \mathbb{C}_i = \mathbb{R}\cdot 1 + \mathbb{R}\cdot i$.

Type of stabiliserDefinitionResult
Setwise $g \in 2T$ such that $g \mathbb{C}_i g^{-1} \subseteq \mathbb{C}_i$ as a set (orientation may flip) $Q_8 = \{\pm 1, \pm i, \pm j, \pm k\}$, order 8
Pointwise $g \in 2T$ such that $g v g^{-1} = v$ for every $v \in \mathbb{C}_i$ $\langle i \rangle = \{\pm 1, \pm i\} \cong \mathbb{Z}_4$, order 4

Direct check on the pointwise stabiliser: $g v g^{-1} = v$ for all $v \in \mathbb{C}_i$ is equivalent to $g$ commuting with $i$. Inside $2T$, the centraliser of $i$ is exactly $\{\pm 1, \pm i\}$ — a cyclic group of order $4$. The setwise stabiliser additionally includes elements that send $i$ to $-i$; conjugation by $j$ does this ($j i j^{-1} = -i$), and the four elements $\{\pm j, \pm k\}$ all act this way, doubling the count to $8$.

Aside · where this matters in physics In the chiral $\mathrm{GL}(2,\mathbb{C})$ formalism for gravity, the soldering bivector $\Sigma^{AB}$ is the metric-defining object; on shell, $\Sigma$ selects an almost-complex structure on the internal space, which is exactly the choice of a $\mathbb{C}_u$ inside $\mathbb{H}$. The breaking chain above then becomes the residual-symmetry chain after the metric has acquired its on-shell value, with the setwise version being the relevant one (charge conjugation flips $u$ but preserves $\mathbb{C}_u$ as a set). This learner does not develop the application; it is mentioned only to point at where the structure matters outside arithmetic.
§ 9

Three layers: geometry, Clifford, Hurwitz

We have now seen three structural moves involving quadratic forms and lattices, and they are easy to conflate. A reader coming from physics in particular may suspect that what we have done is a kind of "Clifford-like" linearisation, since spinors and Pauli matrices operate in territory that looks superficially similar. They do not. The Hurwitz move is a distinct third operation, and it is worth saying clearly what each of the three layers does and where they sit relative to each other.

Geometry: the quadratic form alone

The starting datum is a real vector space equipped with a quadratic form $g$. Examples: $\mathbb{R}^n$ with the Euclidean metric; Minkowski space with the Lorentzian metric; $\mathbb{H} \cong \mathbb{R}^4$ with the norm $N(q) = a^2 + b^2 + c^2 + d^2$. At this layer one has a vector space and a quadratic invariant. Symmetry is the orthogonal group $\mathrm{O}(g)$ that preserves it. Nothing more is said; in particular, no vectors are multiplied together.

Clifford: linearising the quadratic form

Clifford's construction takes the quadratic form $g$ and produces an associative algebra $\mathrm{Cl}(g)$ in which vectors satisfy $v^2 = g(v,v)$, equivalently $vw + wv = 2 g(v,w)$. The quadratic form is not literally made linear; rather, the quadratic constraint is realised as the multiplication law of a new algebra in which $v$ appears linearly. The payoffs are concrete:

  • Reflections and rotations become products of vectors inside $\mathrm{Cl}(g)$.
  • The spin group sits inside $\mathrm{Cl}(g)$ as a subset of the multiplicative group.
  • Spinors arise as modules over $\mathrm{Cl}(g)$. Pauli matrices are a representation of $\mathrm{Cl}_3$; Dirac matrices are a representation of $\mathrm{Cl}_{1,3}$.

So Clifford's move is to package the quadratic form into an algebra and let the algebra act. It is a construction that introduces multiplication where there was none.

Hurwitz: discrete arithmetic compatible with an existing invariant

The Hurwitz move is structurally different. Quaternions already satisfy $q\bar{q} = a^2 + b^2 + c^2 + d^2$ as a built-in feature of multiplication. There is no Clifford-style algebra to construct; the multiplication and the quadratic form are present together from the start. What Hurwitz does is ask, of the algebra that already has both: which lattice sits cleanly inside it, in the sense made precise in §6 — which lattice has the largest set of unit-norm points?

The answer is the Hurwitz order $\mathbb{H}_{\mathrm{H}}$ with $24$ unit-norm points, maximal among orders in $\mathbb{Q} \otimes \mathbb{H}$. This is not a linearisation, an algebra construction, or anything resembling Clifford's move. It is a refinement of the arithmetic data inside an algebra that already has all the structure Clifford would have built. The unit group $2T$ that emerges from the refinement is a finite symmetry of the lattice — discrete, not continuous, and not realised through products of vectors.

The layers compared

LayerOperationOutcomeWhat is added
Geometry Quadratic form $g$ on a vector space Metric structure, orthogonal group $\mathrm{O}(g)$ An invariant; no algebra yet
Clifford Encode $g$ into an associative algebra: $v^2 = g(v,v)$ Spin group, spinors as modules Multiplication and a linear action
Hurwitz Refine to a maximal lattice in an algebra already carrying $g$ Discrete unit group $2T$, $24$-cell, $F_4$ root system Arithmetic and a finite symmetry

The three layers are independent in the following sense: one can do geometry without Clifford, Clifford without Hurwitz, and Hurwitz without Clifford (which is what we have done in this learner). The construction in $\mathbb{H}$ skips the Clifford layer entirely, because the algebra $\mathbb{H}$ is a Clifford algebra in disguise (specifically $\mathrm{Cl}^+_3 \cong \mathbb{H}$, the even part of the Clifford algebra of $\mathbb{R}^3$), so Clifford's job is already done. What remains is the arithmetic refinement, which is the Hurwitz layer.

Aside · why this disambiguation matters

Popular accounts of "octonions and the Standard Model" or "Clifford algebra unification" can blur the distinction between the Clifford layer (which gives you spinors) and the arithmetic layer (which gives you discrete symmetries from unit groups of orders). The two are doing different work. The Hurwitz programme is about the third layer alone, on top of an algebra that already provides the second. Conflating them risks claiming too much for the construction or attributing the wrong structural role to the unit group.

The Hurwitz order is forced. Once we decide we want a $\mathbb{Z}$-order in $\mathbb{Q}\otimes\mathbb{H}$ that is maximal and contains an embedded copy of $\mathbb{Z}[\omega]$, Voight's classification (Quaternion Algebras, §11) leaves no other candidate up to conjugacy. Aside · the application that motivated this The arc traced here was originally arrived at from a question in chiral $\mathrm{GL}(2,\mathbb{C})$ gravity: the abelian summand of $\mathfrak{gl}(2,\mathbb{C})$ admits an arithmetic refinement, and pushing it as far as a maximal order takes one straight to $\mathbb{H}_{\mathrm{H}}$ and $2T$. Whether the map is structurally significant or coincidental is a question this learner does not settle. What it does settle is that the arithmetic is forced.
