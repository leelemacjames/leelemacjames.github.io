The Noun Behind the Verbs

In which the proliferation of methods threatens to make the question of a best explanation meaningless, and then resolves it, by revealing that all the methods are presentations of a single equation that none of them can vary.

Architect Carbon · Lee McCulloch-James  ·  third of three, after Pascal's Triangle is Not a Noun and The Second Verb

The first piece in this sequence argued that a triangle is the orbit of an operator, that the diagram on the page is downstream of a verb, and that teaching the verb gives the pupil a structure where teaching the diagram gives them only a thing to remember. The second piece showed that the spines of the triangle, the famous sequences hidden along its diagonals, have verbs of their own: a small companion matrix that walks the sequence forward, and a large continuant whose determinant produces it in one shot. I closed by naming two further verbs without developing them, the continued fraction and the generating function, and promising they would have their own treatment.

This is that treatment, and it arrives with a problem.

The problem is that the verbs have multiplied. We began with one diagram, Pascal's triangle, and one operator producing it. We now have, for a single sequence such as Fibonacci, at least five distinct ways of generating it: the iterative recurrence, the companion matrix, the continuant determinant, the continued fraction, and the generating function. Each produces the same numbers. Each is correct. Each illuminates a different facet. And this abundance, far from settling matters, seems to dissolve a question I have been circling since the first piece. If there are five good methods, which is the explanation? Or has the very idea of the explanation become meaningless, drowned in a sea of equally valid descriptions?

The Deutsch criterion, and the threat to it

David Deutsch, in The Beginning of Infinity, offers a criterion for what makes an explanation good. We seek, he writes, explanations that are hard to vary in the sense that changing the details would ruin them.

"We do so by seeking good explanations — explanations that are hard to vary in the sense that changing the details would ruin the explanation."

David Deutsch, The Beginning of Infinity (Allen Lane, 2011), ch. 1

A bad explanation can be adjusted freely to fit any outcome, because its parts do no real work. A good explanation is rigid: change any piece and it stops working. The rigidity is the mark of contact with reality. The myth that winter comes because a goddess grieves can be reshaped to fit any seasonal pattern on any planet, so it explains nothing. The axial-tilt account cannot be reshaped at all without making false predictions, so it explains everything it touches.

Now here is the threat. The five verbs for Fibonacci are all easy to swap for one another. If I do not like the continued fraction I can use the generating function. If the determinant is inconvenient I can iterate the companion matrix. They are interchangeable. By the Deutsch criterion, interchangeability is the signature of a bad explanation, of details that do no functional work. So have we shown, by accumulating methods, that none of them explains Fibonacci? Has the multiplicity of verbs argued us into the conclusion that the sequence has no good explanation at all, only a heap of equivalent recipes?

If there are five good methods, which is the explanation? Or has the idea of the explanation dissolved?

The resolution

The resolution is that the five verbs are not five explanations. They are five presentations of one explanation, and the one explanation is hard to vary in exactly Deutsch's sense.

Watch what happens when we ask each verb the same question: what number, raised to successive powers, would satisfy you?

The iterative recurrence says Fn = Fn−1 + Fn−2. Try a pure power, Fn = xn. Substituting and dividing through by xn−2 gives x² = x + 1.

The companion matrix is A = [[1,1],[1,0]]. Its characteristic polynomial, the determinant of A − xI, is x² − x − 1. Setting it to zero gives x² = x + 1.

The continuant determinant satisfies the same recurrence as the iteration, so it yields the same equation by the same substitution.

The continued fraction for the golden ratio is [1; 1, 1, 1, …], which is the fixed point of the map x ↦ 1 + 1/x. A fixed point satisfies x = 1 + 1/x, and multiplying through by x gives x² = x + 1.

The generating function is G(x) = x / (1 − x − x²). Its denominator is 1 − x − x², the reciprocal polynomial of x² − x − 1; the poles of G sit at the reciprocals of the roots of x² = x + 1.

x² = x + 1
The characteristic equation. Every verb is a presentation of it.

Five verbs, one equation. The iteration produces it by substitution. The companion matrix is it, in the literal sense that the term "characteristic equation" was coined for the characteristic polynomial of a matrix. The continuant inherits it from the recurrence. The continued fraction reaches it as a fixed-point condition. The generating function carries it as the polynomial in its denominator. The equation sits underneath all five, unchanged, while the methods above it vary freely.

The methods are easy to vary. The equation is not. That is the whole point.

Where the rigidity lives

This relocates the Deutsch criterion and saves it. The interchangeability of the verbs is not the interchangeability of competing explanations. It is the interchangeability of competing notations for the same explanation. You can write the number three as 3, as III, as 0b11, as the successor of two, as the cardinality of a particular set. These are not five theories of three. They are five names for it. The freedom to choose among them is the freedom of notation, not the looseness of a bad explanation.

And the thing they all name, the characteristic equation x² = x + 1, is hard to vary in precisely Deutsch's sense. Change the equation and you change the sequence. Move the coefficient on the linear term and Fibonacci becomes the Pell numbers or the Jacobsthal numbers. Change the constant and you alter the growth rate, the eigenvalues, the closed form, the poles of the generating function, all at once and in lockstep, because they are all reflections of the same roots. There is no way to perturb the characteristic equation that leaves the sequence intact. Every detail of the equation plays a functional role. The roots are the golden ratio and its conjugate, and those roots propagate into every verb: they are the eigenvalues of the companion matrix, the limit of the continued fraction convergents, the reciprocals of the generating function's poles, the bases in the closed-form expression. Pull on the equation and the whole apparatus moves.

So the Deutschian good explanation of Fibonacci is not the recurrence, nor the matrix, nor any single verb. It is the characteristic equation, the invariant that every verb presents and none can alter. The verbs are how we look at it. The equation is what we are looking at.

Deutsch makes a remark that fits this exactly. Good explanations, being hard to vary, tend to be unique:

"It is in the nature of good explanations — being hard to vary — that there is only one of them."

David Deutsch, The Beginning of Infinity (Allen Lane, 2011), ch. 13

He is talking about rival scientific theories, of which the good one drives out the others. But the remark lands with unexpected force here, in a setting he was not addressing. There really is only one explanation of Fibonacci, the characteristic equation, and the five verbs are not five rivals to it but five routes to it. The uniqueness Deutsch describes is the uniqueness of the equation. The plurality we were worried about is only the plurality of the roads.

  • Iteration Substitute xn into the recurrence. The characteristic equation appears by cancellation.
  • Companion matrix The characteristic equation is the characteristic polynomial det(A − xI). The name is not a coincidence; it is the origin of the term.
  • Continuant determinant Inherits the recurrence, and with it the same characteristic equation. The matrix shape adds spectral information but does not change the equation.
  • Continued fraction The fixed point of x ↦ 1 + 1/x satisfies x = 1 + 1/x, which is the characteristic equation rearranged.
  • Generating function The denominator is the reciprocal of the characteristic polynomial. Its poles are the reciprocals of the equation's roots.

Why this is the lesson, not the methods

Return to the pupil. The noun-pedagogy hands them Fibonacci as a list. The verb-pedagogy hands them a method, or several, for generating it. Both are now visibly incomplete. The list is incomplete because it hides the rule. But the methods are also incomplete, each on its own, because each presents the underlying equation from a single angle and a pupil who learns only one method mistakes the angle for the object.

The deeper lesson, the one worth the trilogy, is that the methods converge. A pupil who has seen the recurrence give x² = x + 1 by substitution, and then the companion matrix give the same equation as its characteristic polynomial, and then the continued fraction give it again as a fixed-point condition, has learned something that no single method could teach. They have learned that the equation is the invariant and the methods are its shadows. They have learned to look through the verb at the noun behind it. And the noun behind it is not the diagram we started by criticising, the finished triangle handed over to be remembered. It is a reconstructed noun, an equation arrived at from five directions and found to be the same from all of them.

The verbs are how we look. The equation is what we are looking at.

This is, I think, the resolution of the worry that opened the first piece, the worry that animated the whole sequence: the fear that playing with these objects, recasting them, building tools to view them, was an indulgence, a path-dependent polishing of one's own priors with no contact with anything real. The contact with the real is the characteristic equation. It is what does not move when everything else does. It is what every method is forced to agree about. The multiplicity of methods is not evidence that the mathematics is arbitrary. It is evidence that the mathematics is rigid, because so many different roads are compelled to arrive at the same place.

Penrose took his classmate's answer seriously because there is no single privileged description of a fraction; there is the family of descriptions and the object they jointly pin down. Deutsch tells us a good explanation is hard to vary. Put the two together and you get the orientation this trilogy has been building toward. The descriptions are many and easy to vary. The object they pin down is one and hard to vary. Teaching mathematics well means teaching the pupil to hold both at once: to move fluently among the descriptions, and to see, through all of them, the single hard-to-vary thing they are descriptions of.

That thing is the noun. Not the noun we were handed at the start, the diagram to be memorised, but the noun we reconstruct at the end, the equation that every verb is compelled to honour. The triangle was never the noun. The operator was never quite the noun either. The noun is the characteristic equation, and the whole apparatus of triangles and operators and determinants and continued fractions and generating functions exists to let us approach it from every side and confirm that it does not move.

  1. The five presentations of the Fibonacci characteristic equation x² − x − 1 = 0: (1) substitution of xn into the recurrence; (2) the characteristic polynomial of the companion matrix [[1,1],[1,0]]; (3) the recurrence inherited by the continuant determinants; (4) the fixed-point equation of the continued-fraction map x ↦ 1 + 1/x; (5) the reciprocal of the generating-function denominator 1 − x − x². All five were verified numerically to share the roots φ = (1+√5)/2 and ψ = (1−√5)/2.
  2. The term "characteristic equation" originates in linear algebra, as the equation det(A − λI) = 0 whose solutions are the eigenvalues of A. Its appearance in the theory of linear recurrences is not an analogy but the same object: a linear recurrence of order d is equivalent to a first-order vector recurrence driven by a d-by-d companion matrix, and the recurrence's characteristic equation is that matrix's characteristic polynomial set to zero.
  3. The claim that perturbing the characteristic equation perturbs every verb in lockstep is exact. The roots of the equation are the eigenvalues of the companion matrix, the bases of the closed-form (Binet) expression, the limit of the continued-fraction convergents, and the reciprocals of the generating-function poles. A change to either coefficient of x² − px − q moves the roots and therefore moves all of these simultaneously. This is the precise sense in which the explanation is hard to vary: its parts are not independent, so no part can be changed alone.
  4. On Deutsch: the formulation that good explanations are "hard to vary" is the central thesis of The Beginning of Infinity: Explanations That Transform the World (Allen Lane, 2011), introduced in chapter 1. The two quotations in the body are verbatim: "explanations that are hard to vary in the sense that changing the details would ruin the explanation" (ch. 1), and "It is in the nature of good explanations — being hard to vary — that there is only one of them" (ch. 13, in the context of rational decision-making and the uniqueness of good explanations). The application to the convergence of multiple generating methods on a single characteristic equation is mine, not Deutsch's; he was addressing rival empirical theories, not equivalent mathematical presentations. The transfer is offered as illumination, not as a claim about his intent.
  5. The same synthesis applies to every spine of every triangle in the first two pieces, with the appropriate characteristic equation. For sequences with position-dependent coefficients, such as the central Delannoy numbers, the "characteristic equation" generalises to an indicial or hypergeometric relation rather than a fixed polynomial, and the unification is correspondingly subtler; the clean five-verb convergence shown here is exact for constant-coefficient recurrences such as Fibonacci.
