Standing Waves, Harmonics & the Doppler Effect

Waves that stay put, tubes that sing at specific frequencies, and why an ambulance changes pitch as it passes you.

C14 C15 C16 C17 C18 C19 C20

What this section is about

Standing waves are what happen when a wave meets its own reflection: instead of travelling, the energy stays trapped between boundaries, and only specific frequencies are allowed. This discreteness — only certain wavelengths "fit" — is the same idea that reappears in quantum mechanics (electron standing waves in atoms).

The Doppler effect is what happens when a wave source and observer are in relative motion: frequencies shift. For sound, this is the ambulance siren. For light, it's how we know the universe is expanding.

This learner covers slides C14–C20: standing wave formation, harmonics of strings and tubes, resonance, the classical Doppler effect, and the AHL extension to light (including redshift). The harmonics tables are the exam workhorse — if you know the pattern, you can reconstruct any case from scratch.

The One Rule That Generates All Harmonics

Every harmonics problem reduces to one question: how many half-wavelengths (or quarter-wavelengths) fit in the length L?

At a fixed end (string clamped, tube closed) → node. At a free end (string free, tube open) → antinode.

Both ends fixed (or both open): the string/tube fits n × (λ/2) = L → λn = 2L/n → fn = nv/(2L). All harmonics present (n = 1, 2, 3…).

One end fixed, one open: fits n × (λ/4) = L (odd n only) → λn = 4L/n → fn = nv/(4L). Only odd harmonics (n = 1, 3, 5…).

If you remember these two patterns, you can derive every row of every harmonics table in the handout.

SL C14 — Formation of Standing Waves

A standing wave forms when two waves of the same frequency and amplitude travel in opposite directions and superpose. This typically happens when a wave reflects back on itself from a boundary.

  • Nodes: Points of zero displacement (always). Complete destructive interference.
  • Antinodes: Points of maximum displacement. Complete constructive interference.
  • Between adjacent nodes: all particles oscillate in phase with each other.
  • Particles on opposite sides of a node: oscillate 180° out of phase (antiphase).
  • Energy is NOT transmitted — it remains trapped between nodes. This is the key difference from a progressive (travelling) wave.
3rd harmonic: 3 half-wavelengths fit in length L. Nodes (N) at fixed ends and at every λ/2 interval. Antinodes (A) midway between nodes. Dashed line shows the other extreme of oscillation.

Standing waves do NOT transfer energy. Students often say "the wave carries energy from node to node." No — energy oscillates back and forth between kinetic and potential forms within each segment, but there is no net transport along the string/tube. A progressive wave transfers energy; a standing wave stores it.

"In phase" within a segment, "antiphase" across a node. All particles between two adjacent nodes move together (same direction, same phase). Particles on opposite sides of a node move in opposite directions (180° phase difference). This is different from a progressive wave where phase changes continuously along the wave.

Q1How does a standing wave differ from a progressive wave? Give three differences.▶
Answer

(1) Energy: progressive waves transfer energy; standing waves do not.

(2) Amplitude: progressive waves have the same amplitude at every point; standing waves have amplitude that varies from zero (nodes) to maximum (antinodes).

(3) Phase: in a progressive wave, phase changes continuously along the direction of travel. In a standing wave, all points between adjacent nodes are in phase, and points across a node are in antiphase. There is no continuous phase progression.

Q2A guitar string vibrates at its 2nd harmonic. How many nodes and antinodes are there?▶
Answer

2nd harmonic = 2 half-wavelengths in L. That gives 3 nodes (both fixed ends + one in the middle) and 2 antinodes (one in each segment). General rule for the nth harmonic (both ends fixed): n + 1 nodes and n antinodes.

Q1A string of length 0.80 m is fixed at both ends. The wave speed is 320 m/s. What is the fundamental frequency and the frequency of the 3rd harmonic?▶
Answer

f₁ = v/(2L) = 320/(2 × 0.80) = 200 Hz

f₃ = 3f₁ = 600 Hz

Q2Why are only odd harmonics present when one end is free?▶
Answer

The boundary conditions require a node at the fixed end and an antinode at the free end. The distance from node to antinode is λ/4. So the total length L must be an odd number of quarter-wavelengths: L = λ/4, 3λ/4, 5λ/4… This means only n = 1, 3, 5… are allowed. Even harmonics would require a node at the free end or an antinode at the fixed end — neither is physically possible.

SL C16 — Harmonics in Tubes (Pipes)

The same logic as strings, but now: closed end → node (air can't move), open end → antinode (air moves freely).

Closed one end, open the other (clarinet, bottle): odd harmonics only.

Harmonicnλf
1st14Lv/(4L)
3rd34L/33v/(4L)
5th54L/55v/(4L)

Open at both ends (flute, organ pipe open): all harmonics present.

Harmonicnλf
1st12Lv/(2L)
2nd2Lv/L
3rd32L/33v/(2L)

Closed at both ends (rare, but asked): mathematically identical to open-at-both-ends (nodes at both boundaries instead of antinodes — same spacing). All harmonics, same formula.

Open-both-ends and closed-both-ends give the same frequencies. This surprises students. The reason: both configurations have the same type of boundary at each end (both antinodes, or both nodes), so the same number of half-wavelengths fit. The physics is different (pressure nodes vs displacement nodes) but the maths is identical: fn = nv/(2L).

"Closed one end" = odd harmonics only. This is the case students forget. The missing even harmonics give closed pipes a characteristically "hollow" tone — it's why a clarinet sounds different from a flute even at the same pitch.

Q1A closed pipe is 0.50 m long. Speed of sound = 340 m/s. What are the first three resonant frequencies?▶
Answer

Closed one end → odd harmonics only: fn = nv/(4L).

f₁ = 340/(4 × 0.50) = 170 Hz

f₃ = 3 × 170 = 510 Hz

f₅ = 5 × 170 = 850 Hz

No 2nd or 4th harmonic exists for this pipe.

Q2An open pipe has fundamental frequency 440 Hz. What length is it? (v = 340 m/s)▶
Answer

Open both ends: f₁ = v/(2L) → L = v/(2f₁) = 340/(2 × 440) = 0.386 m ≈ 39 cm. This is concert A — roughly the length of a flute's effective air column.

SL C17 — Resonance

Resonance occurs when a system is driven at its natural frequency — the frequency at which it would oscillate freely. At resonance, energy transfer from driver to system is maximised and amplitude grows dramatically.

Natural frequencies for common IB systems:

Mass-spring: f₀ = (1/2π)√(k/m)
Pendulum: f₀ = (1/2π)√(g/L)
General: f₀ = (1/2π)√(restoring term / inertial term)

Damping reduces the amplitude of oscillations over time (friction, air resistance). With damping, the resonance peak broadens and shifts slightly below the natural frequency. Heavy damping suppresses resonance almost entirely.

Resonance connects music, engineering, and molecular physics. A wine glass shatters at its resonant frequency. The Tacoma Narrows Bridge collapsed from wind-driven resonance. Greenhouse gas molecules absorb IR radiation at their resonant vibrational frequencies. Is it the same phenomenon in each case, or are we using one word to describe structurally similar but physically different things? This is the question of whether mathematical structure constitutes physical explanation.

Q1A mass of 0.25 kg on a spring (k = 100 N/m) is driven by an oscillator. At what frequency will resonance occur?▶
Answer

f₀ = (1/2π)√(k/m) = (1/2π)√(100/0.25) = (1/2π)√400 = 20/(2π) = 3.18 Hz

Q2What effect does increasing damping have on a resonance curve?▶
Answer

The peak amplitude decreases, the peak broadens (wider range of frequencies produce a response), and the peak frequency shifts slightly lower than f₀. With very heavy damping, the peak disappears entirely — the system barely responds at any frequency. Light damping: tall sharp peak. Heavy damping: low broad hump.

SL C18 — The Doppler Effect

The Doppler effect is the change in observed frequency (and wavelength) when there is relative motion between a wave source and an observer. The actual frequency emitted doesn't change — what changes is the frequency received.

Sign Convention & Equations

Define v = wave speed in medium, us = source speed, uo = observer speed. Positive direction = source toward observer.

Approaching

Source moving toward observer: wavefronts bunch up.

λapp = (v − us) / f → shorter wavelength.

fapp = fv / (v − us) → higher frequency.

Observer moving toward source: encounters wavefronts faster.

fapp = f(v + uo) / v → higher frequency.

Receding

Source moving away: wavefronts spread out.

λapp = (v + us) / f → longer wavelength.

fapp = fv / (v + us) → lower frequency.

Observer moving away: encounters wavefronts more slowly.

fapp = f(v − uo) / v → lower frequency.

General formula (both moving):

fapp = f × (v ± uo) / (v ∓ us)

Memory aid: approaching → frequency up → numerator gets bigger (+ uo) and denominator gets smaller (− us). Receding → frequency down → reverse the signs.

A moving source changes wavelength. A moving observer does not. When the source moves, it physically compresses or stretches the wavefronts in the medium — the wavelength genuinely changes. When the observer moves, the wavelength in the medium is unchanged; the observer simply encounters wavefronts at a different rate. This distinction matters: the two effects give slightly different formulas, and they're NOT symmetric (unlike for light in special relativity).

The Doppler effect applies to all waves — sound, water, light. But for sound, you must use the medium-based formulas. For light (no medium), only relative velocity matters, and special relativity modifies the result.

Q1A siren emits 800 Hz and moves toward a stationary observer at 30 m/s. Speed of sound = 340 m/s. What frequency does the observer hear?▶
Answer

Moving source approaching: fapp = fv/(v − us) = 800 × 340/(340 − 30) = 800 × 340/310

fapp = 877 Hz. The pitch rises — consistent with everyday experience.

Q2Same siren (800 Hz, 30 m/s) but now moving AWAY from the observer. What frequency?▶
Answer

fapp = fv/(v + us) = 800 × 340/(340 + 30) = 800 × 340/370 = 735 Hz.

Notice the asymmetry: approaching raised f by 77 Hz, but receding only lowered it by 65 Hz. The Doppler effect is not symmetric for a moving source.

Q3An observer runs toward a stationary 500 Hz source at 20 m/s (v = 340 m/s). What frequency do they hear?▶
Answer

Moving observer approaching: fapp = f(v + uo)/v = 500 × (340 + 20)/340 = 500 × 360/340 = 529 Hz.

Compare: if the source were moving at 20 m/s instead, f = 500 × 340/320 = 531 Hz. Close but not identical — the two scenarios are physically different.

AHL C19 — Doppler Shift of Light & Redshift

For light (or any EM wave), the Doppler shift at low velocities (v ≪ c) simplifies to:

Δf/f ≈ Δλ/λ ≈ v/c

Where v is the relative velocity between source and observer. This approximation works well for most astrophysical applications (galaxy recession speeds, stellar motion).

The full relativistic formula includes time dilation effects and is symmetric (only relative velocity matters, unlike sound).

Types of redshift:

  • Doppler redshift: source moving away → longer wavelengths → "red" end of spectrum.
  • Gravitational redshift: light climbing out of a gravitational well loses energy → lower frequency.
  • Cosmological redshift: space itself expands → wavelengths stretch while light is in transit. This is the redshift Hubble measured, and it's not a Doppler effect — it's expansion of the metric.

Three different physical mechanisms, one observable result (redshift). How do astronomers distinguish them? Cosmological redshift correlates with distance (Hubble's law). Gravitational redshift depends on the gravitational field at the source (predictable from mass and radius). Doppler redshift shows up as individual spectral line shifts, often with periodic variation (binary stars). When the same observation can have multiple causes, what role does theory play in choosing between them?

Q1A galaxy shows a hydrogen spectral line at 660 nm instead of its rest wavelength of 656 nm. What is the galaxy's recession speed?▶
Answer

Δλ = 660 − 656 = 4 nm. Δλ/λ = 4/656 = 0.00610.

v = c × Δλ/λ = 3.0 × 10⁸ × 0.00610 = 1.83 × 10⁶ m/s ≈ 1830 km/s.

This is about 0.6% of c — well within the low-velocity approximation.

Q2Why is the Doppler effect for light symmetric (only relative velocity matters) while for sound it is not?▶
Answer

Sound propagates through a medium (air). The source's motion relative to the air physically changes the wavelength; the observer's motion relative to the air changes the rate of encountering wavefronts. These are different effects. Light has no medium — there is no preferred frame. Special relativity guarantees that only the relative velocity between source and observer matters. The time dilation factor (Lorentz) combines with the classical Doppler formula to produce a single, symmetric result.

AHL C20 — Wave Equations Summary (Double-Slit & Doppler)

A compact reference for the key AHL wave equations:

Double-slit interference:

Path difference: Δx = d sin θ
Bright fringes: mλ = d sin θ   (m = 0, 1, 2…)

Doppler (moving source):

f' = fv / (v − us)   (approaching)
f' = fv / (v + us)   (receding)

Doppler (moving observer):

f' = f(v + uo) / v   (approaching)
f' = f(v − uo) / v   (receding)

All these are given in the IB data booklet, but you need to know which formula applies to which scenario. The sign convention is the biggest source of errors — work out which way approach/recession goes before substituting numbers.

Q1Light passes through slits 0.25 mm apart. The 2nd-order bright fringe is at angle θ = 0.28°. What is the wavelength?▶
Answer

mλ = d sin θ → λ = d sin θ / m = (0.25 × 10⁻³ × sin 0.28°) / 2

sin 0.28° = 0.004887. λ = (0.25 × 10⁻³ × 0.004887) / 2 = 1.222 × 10⁻⁶ / 2 = 611 nm (orange-red light).

Q2A source and observer approach each other, each at 25 m/s (v = 340 m/s). Source emits 1000 Hz. What does the observer hear?▶
Answer

Both moving toward each other: f' = f(v + uo)/(v − us) = 1000 × (340 + 25)/(340 − 25)

f' = 1000 × 365/315 = 1159 Hz.

The combined approach gives a larger shift than either moving alone.
