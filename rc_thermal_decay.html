RC Thermal Decay

Temperature · Resistance · Time Constant

The discharge of a capacitor through a resistor follows an exponential decay. The time constant τ = RC controls how quickly that decay occurs. Any thermal effect must act through R or C.

V(t) = V₀ · e−t/(RC)  ≡  V₀ · e−t/τ
Hypothesis · Before you continue

If you heat the resistor in an RC circuit, does the discharge curve become faster or slower? Commit to an answer, then work through the section below.

Rewrite as a pure exponential:

y = A · e−kt    where   k = 1/τ = 1/(RC)

Increasing τ decreases k, which flattens the curve. The key question is: does heating increase τ or decrease it?

What Changes with Temperature?

Resistance — the dominant effect

For a typical metal resistor, resistance increases linearly with temperature:

R(T) = R₀(1 + α·ΔT)

where α ≈ 0.003–0.005 K⁻¹ for common metals (copper, nichrome). Heating the resistor raises R, which raises τ = RC, which slows the decay.

Common confusion

"Surely the capacitor temperature coefficient is measurable?" — In principle yes, but at school level the noise, drift, and class-dependence of real capacitors make it unreliable. The resistor gives a clean, reproducible signal.

In a metal, electrons carry current through a lattice of positive ions. At higher temperatures, those ions vibrate more vigorously. This increases the rate at which conducting electrons scatter off the lattice — effectively making it harder for electrons to flow.

Result: higher temperature → more scattering → higher resistivity → higher resistance.

This is the Drude model mechanism, valid for most pure metals and alloy resistors.

Extension: the NTC Thermistor

A Negative Temperature Coefficient (NTC) thermistor behaves oppositely to a metal resistor: heating reduces resistance. This gives a striking contrast for discussion.

Metal resistor

Heating → R↑ → τ↑

Decay becomes slower

Lattice vibration scattering

NTC thermistor

Heating → R↓ → τ↓

Decay becomes faster

Thermally activated charge carriers

The NTC thermistor follows approximately:

R(T) = R₀ · exp[B(1/T − 1/T₀)]

where B ≈ 3000–5000 K is the material constant. The exponential sensitivity makes the effect dramatically visible with even modest heating — a good choice if you want a large, reversible effect.

In a semiconductor (which is what most NTC thermistors are), charge carriers (electrons and holes) are thermally promoted across a band gap. More thermal energy → more carriers promoted → higher conductivity → lower resistance.

This is the opposite of metals, where the number of carriers is fixed but scattering increases. Thermistors are dominated by carrier generation; metals by carrier scattering.

Practical Classroom Setup

Components

Fixed resistor 10–50 kΩ (or NTC thermistor for exaggerated effect) · Capacitor ~100 µF electrolytic · DC supply 6–9 V · Switch · Voltmeter / data logger / oscilloscope

Heating methods

Hairdryer at controlled distance · Warm water bath (if resistor is insulated) · Hand warmth (small effect, good for demo) · NOT an open flame.

What students should compare

Record two discharge curves: once with the resistor at room temperature, once after heating. Plot V(t) for both. Fit to extract τ. Confirm τhot > τcold for a metal resistor.

Examiner trap

"The capacitor discharges faster when hot." — Only if you have replaced R with an NTC thermistor. For a metal resistor the opposite is true. Know which component you heated and which model applies.
