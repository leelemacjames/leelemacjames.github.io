From Eisenstein triples to cuboids and Pell

The same theme appears three times in different clothes: a norm on a lattice, a rational parametrisation of a conic, and a Pell recursion that stabilises integrality. The cuboid story is not separate from the Eisenstein story. It is a 3D lift of the same arithmetic instinct.

Gaussian: x²+y²=z²
Eisenstein: a²-ab+b²=c²
Cuboid lift: d²=a²+b²+(ab)²
Pell backbone: u²-2v²=±1
Main idea. In the Gaussian world, squaring a lattice point gives a right triangle. In the Eisenstein world, squaring a hexagonal-lattice point gives an Eisenstein triangle. In your cuboid construction, a classical Pythagorean pair (a,b) is lifted again by attaching the third edge ab, and the Pell orbit is exactly what keeps the new space diagonal integral.

1. The classical side: Gaussian integers

The standard parametrisation of primitive Pythagorean triples comes from squaring a Gaussian integer.

(p+qi)^2 = (p^2-q^2) + 2pq \, i
so
a = p^2-q^2, \quad b = 2pq, \quad c = p^2+q^2.

This is the familiar circle story in arithmetic form: rational points on X²+Y²=1 become integer triples after clearing denominators.

Structural slogan. A right triangle is the shadow of a Gaussian square.

2. The Eisenstein side: the hexagonal analogue

On the hexagonal lattice, the natural norm changes because the basis angle changes. The Eisenstein norm is

N(a+b\omega) = a^2-ab+b^2.

Squaring an Eisenstein integer gives

(m+n\omega)^2 = (m^2-n^2) + n(2m-n)\,\omega,
with
c = m^2-mn+n^2.

Hence

a^2-ab+b^2 = c^2.
Structural slogan. An Eisenstein triple is the shadow of an Eisenstein square.

3. Why your cuboid sits between them

You start from a classical Pythagorean pair

a = p^2-q^2, \quad b = 2pq, \quad c = p^2+q^2

and then build a cuboid with edges a, b, and ab. Its space diagonal is

j(p,q)^2 = a^2+b^2+(ab)^2 = c^2 + (ab)^2.

That last rewrite is the key. It says the cuboid condition is not random: it asks whether the pair (c,ab) is itself the leg pair of a second right triangle.

This is the bridge. The classical Gaussian square gives you (a,b,c). Then the cuboid construction asks for a second squareness condition one level up:
(c,ab,j) \text{ should also be Pythagorean.}
So the cuboid is a lifted norm problem: first on the square lattice, then into three dimensions by multiplying the two legs together.

4. The hidden Pell mechanism

The striking list

(p,q)=(2,1),(5,2),(12,5),(29,12),(70,29),\dots

is not accidental. It is generated by

p_n = 2p_{n-1}+p_{n-2}, \qquad q_n = 2q_{n-1}+q_{n-2},

whose characteristic root is 1+√2, the silver ratio. These are the standard Pell-type convergents to 1+√2.

\frac{p_n}{q_n} \to 1+\sqrt{2}

So the cuboid integrality condition is being stabilised by the same arithmetic that drives Pell equations.

Interpretation. The sequence is an orbit under multiplication by 1+√2 in ℤ[√2].

So the cuboid diagonal sits inside a second Pythagorean triple (5,12,13).

Gaussian → circle

The geometry is Euclidean in the square basis.

X^2+Y^2=1

Primitive triples come from a binary parity sieve:

p \not\equiv q \pmod 2.

Eisenstein → ellipse

The geometry is Euclidean in the hexagonal basis.

X^2-XY+Y^2=1

Primitive triples come from the ramified-prime sieve:

m+n \not\equiv 0 \pmod 3.

Cuboid → lifted circle

The geometry is no longer a plane conic, but a lifted square condition.

j^2 = c^2 + (ab)^2

Integrality is no longer controlled by mod 2 or mod 3 alone, but by a Pell orbit.

6. A clean conceptual chain

Stage Arithmetic object Quadratic form What integrality means
Gaussian ℤ[i] x²+y² ordinary Pythagorean triple
Eisenstein ℤ[ω] a²-ab+b² hexagonal Pythagorean triple
Cuboid lift ℤ[√2] lurking in recurrence a²+b²+(ab)² space diagonal also integral
Best way to say it. The cuboid family is not an Eisenstein norm directly. It is better viewed as a second-stage lift of a Gaussian triple, whose integrality pattern is organised by Pell recurrences. The link to the Eisenstein story is therefore structural rather than literal: both are norm-based parametrised geometries, but the cuboid case climbs from a 2D norm to a 3D multiplicative lift.

7. The semi-prime cuboid aside

If you instead take prime edges p, q, and pq, then

d^2 = p^2 + q^2 + (pq)^2.

When one prime is 2, this reduces to

d^2 - 5q^2 = 4,

which is genuinely Pell-type. The example (p,q,d)=(2,3,7) works because

7^2 - 5\cdot 3^2 = 4.

For general odd primes, the equation no longer has this simple Pell form, so computational search becomes natural.

8. A precise takeaway for your write-up

You can present the relationship in one sentence like this:

\text{Gaussian squaring} \longrightarrow \text{Pythagorean pair} \longrightarrow \text{cuboid lift} \longrightarrow \text{Pell-controlled integrality}.

And the Eisenstein side runs in parallel:

\text{Eisenstein squaring} \longrightarrow \text{hexagonal triple} \longrightarrow \text{mod-3 parity sieve}.
So the connection is this: Eisenstein gives the right 2D comparison class; the cuboid family is the 3D descendant of the Gaussian mechanism; Pell is the hidden engine that explains why only a thin recursive family survives the extra diagonal condition.

9. Suggested wording for the opening paragraph

You can lift this directly into your paper or adapt it.

The classical Pythagorean parametrisation arises by squaring a primitive Gaussian integer, while the Eisenstein analogue arises by squaring a primitive Eisenstein integer on the hexagonal lattice. The integral cuboid construction considered here belongs to the same general family of norm-driven problems, but with an additional multiplicative lift: starting from a classical Pythagorean pair \((a,b)\), we form a cuboid with third edge \(ab\), so that the space diagonal satisfies \(j^2=a^2+b^2+(ab)^2=c^2+(ab)^2\). The integrality of this lifted diagonal is not generic; it is organised by a Pell-type recurrence whose characteristic root is \(1+\sqrt2\). Thus the cuboid family is best understood as a Pell-governed 3-dimensional lift of the Gaussian theory, standing in structural parallel to the Eisenstein parametrisation.

This explainer is designed as a bridge document: not claiming that the cuboid form is itself an Eisenstein norm, but showing clearly how the three stories line up as lattice norms, rational parametrisations, and integrality sieves.
