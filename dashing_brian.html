The Ballad of Dashing Brian

Being a True and Faithful Account of Heroism, Villainy and Deliverance

Brian of Ashwood, Ranger and Rescuer

In the kingdom of Fernhallow, where the ancient oaks grew so thick the sun was but a rumour, there lived a man of uncommon valour and exceedingly good cheekbones. Brian of Ashwood, known in every tavern from Millhaven to the Moors as Dashing Brian, strode through life with a quiver of arrows, a roguish moustache, and a smile that had, on three separate occasions, caused a duchess to faint clean away.

Now it came to pass that Lady Isolde of the Silverbrook, a maiden of great wit and unfortunate circumstance, had fallen into the clutches of Father Malachar. Malachar was a priest whose piety was a costume and whose cruelty was quite genuine. He had imprisoned Isolde in the bell tower of his gloomy chapel, claiming she owed a tithe of soul and silence. She owed him nothing of the sort, but he had very thick walls and a surprisingly good lawyer.

Word reached Brian on a Tuesday, which he considered an undignified day for villainy. He consulted his arrows, all seventeen of them and each one named, saddled no horse (he preferred to run; it kept the thighs excellent), and set out through the Greenwood at a pace that alarmed the squirrels.

He arrived at midnight, which is the correct time for such arrivals. The chapel loomed, gargoyles leering, a single torch guttering at the gate. Father Malachar stood in the courtyard, flanked by two enormous monks who had taken their vows of silence only in the matter of conversation, not of menace. Leave now, vagabond, Malachar intoned, or I shall have you excommunicated, fined, and possibly tonsured.

Brian smiled. He reached over his shoulder, drew an arrow (Beatrice, his most persuasive) and loosed it in a single unhurried motion. It split the torch clean in two, plunging the yard into darkness. There followed a great deal of shouting, one very loud collision, and the sound of two large monks discovering a muddy ditch with their faces. Brian was already climbing.

He found Lady Isolde at the top of the tower, not weeping (she had given that up after the first afternoon) but reading a stolen psalter she found philosophically interesting. She looked Brian up and down with the appraising eye of a woman who has had time to consider exactly what sort of rescuer she requires. You took your time, she said. It is Wednesday. Brian had the grace to look mildly abashed. He offered his hand. She took it. They descended into the Greenwood, leaving Father Malachar tangled in his own ecclesiastical robes in the ditch, contemplating the wages of wickedness and the astonishing velocity of a well-aimed arrow.
