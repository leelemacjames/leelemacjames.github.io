What is an order?

From the integers to the Gaussian integers to the Lipschitz quaternions: what changes when we move from rings inside fields to rings inside non-commutative algebras, and why the obvious quaternion lattice turns out to be the wrong one.

L. McCulloch-James · Working Note · v1

§ 1

Why this matters before we start

We will eventually want to know what the Hurwitz order is, and why it is forced rather than chosen. To get there we need to understand a word that gets thrown around casually in this subject: order. An order is not a number, not a group, not a vector space exactly. It is a special kind of subring sitting inside a larger algebra, and the question of which order one picks turns out to control everything downstream.

This learner builds the idea in three steps. We start with the commutative case (the integers $\mathbb{Z}$ inside $\mathbb{Q}$, then the Gaussian integers $\mathbb{Z}[i]$ inside $\mathbb{Q}(i)$, then the Eisenstein integers $\mathbb{Z}[\omega]$ inside $\mathbb{Q}(\omega)$) because there the picture is clean and the answers are old. Then we move to the non-commutative case (the Lipschitz quaternions $\mathbb{Z}\langle i,j,k\rangle$ inside Hamilton's $\mathbb{Q} \otimes \mathbb{H}$) where something surprising happens: the obvious lattice is no longer maximal. That surprise is what Hurwitz fixes, but we will not get to the fix in this learner. The fix lives in Part II.

The question to keep in view: what is the right discrete arithmetic to put inside a continuous algebra? In commutative settings, the answer is usually obvious. In non-commutative settings, it is usually not.

§ 2

Rings of integers in number fields

The integers $\mathbb{Z}$ sit inside the rational numbers $\mathbb{Q}$ as the natural discrete subring: closed under addition, subtraction, and multiplication, with $1 \in \mathbb{Z}$. Every element of $\mathbb{Z}$ is a root of a monic polynomial with integer coefficients (the polynomial $x - n$ has root $n$), so the elements of $\mathbb{Z}$ are algebraic integers. Equivalently: $\mathbb{Z}$ is exactly the set of algebraic integers inside $\mathbb{Q}$.

Generalising to other number fields: a number field $K$ is a finite-degree field extension of $\mathbb{Q}$. The simplest examples:

$$ \mathbb{Q}(i) \;=\; \{a + bi : a, b \in \mathbb{Q}\}, \qquad \mathbb{Q}(\omega) \;=\; \{a + b\omega : a, b \in \mathbb{Q}\}, \qquad \omega = e^{2\pi i/3}. $$

Inside each, the ring of integers $\mathcal{O}_K$ is the set of algebraic integers of $K$ — elements that satisfy some monic polynomial with $\mathbb{Z}$-coefficients. For these two fields, the ring of integers turns out to be:

$$ \mathcal{O}_{\mathbb{Q}(i)} \;=\; \mathbb{Z}[i] \;=\; \{a + bi : a, b \in \mathbb{Z}\}, \qquad \mathcal{O}_{\mathbb{Q}(\omega)} \;=\; \mathbb{Z}[\omega] \;=\; \{a + b\omega : a, b \in \mathbb{Z}\}. $$

Both are obtained by taking the "obvious" lattice generated by $1$ and a chosen imaginary unit. Both happen to be maximal in their fields, meaning no strictly larger subring of $\mathbb{Q}(i)$ or $\mathbb{Q}(\omega)$ has the same structure. Both are principal ideal domains and Euclidean, so they have nice factorisation properties.

A subtlety worth flagging — Z[ω] vs Z[i√3]

The choice $\omega = e^{2\pi i/3} = \tfrac{1}{2}(-1 + i\sqrt{3})$ matters. One might naively think that $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{-3}) = \mathbb{Q}(i\sqrt{3})$ has ring of integers $\mathbb{Z}[i\sqrt{3}]$, but it does not. The ring $\mathbb{Z}[i\sqrt{3}]$ is a proper subring of $\mathbb{Z}[\omega]$, missing exactly the half-integer point $\omega = \tfrac{1}{2}(-1 + i\sqrt{3})$. This already foreshadows the quaternion story: "the obvious lattice with integer coordinates" is not always the right one; sometimes one must adjoin a half-integer point to obtain the maximal order.

§ 3

Visualising the lattices

Both $\mathbb{Z}[i]$ and $\mathbb{Z}[\omega]$ are discrete subsets of $\mathbb{C}$. Drawing them as points in the plane makes the geometry of the arithmetic visible. The Gaussian integers form a square lattice; the Eisenstein integers form a triangular (hexagonal) lattice. Each has a finite group of units sitting on the unit circle: the points at distance $1$ from the origin.

The unit group of $\mathbb{Z}[i]$ is the cyclic group of order $4$; the unit group of $\mathbb{Z}[\omega]$ is the cyclic group of order $6$. The Eisenstein case has one extra structural feature: its unit group contains a $\mathbb{Z}_3$ subgroup (the cube roots of unity), generated by $\omega$ itself. This $\mathbb{Z}_3$ is the arithmetic seed of everything that follows in Parts II and III; for now, just record that $\mathbb{Z}[\omega]$ has $6$ units while $\mathbb{Z}[i]$ has $4$.

§ 4

Orders, abstractly

Generalising further: let $A$ be a finite-dimensional algebra over $\mathbb{Q}$. An order in $A$ is a subring $\mathcal{O} \subset A$ satisfying:

ConditionWhat it means
$1 \in \mathcal{O}$ The multiplicative identity is in $\mathcal{O}$.
$\mathcal{O}$ is a $\mathbb{Z}$-lattice in $A$ It is finitely generated as an additive abelian group, and spans $A$ over $\mathbb{Q}$ ($\mathbb{Q}\mathcal{O} = A$).
Closed under multiplication If $x, y \in \mathcal{O}$ then $xy \in \mathcal{O}$.
Every $x \in \mathcal{O}$ is integral over $\mathbb{Z}$ $x$ satisfies a monic polynomial with $\mathbb{Z}$-coefficients.

The first two conditions say $\mathcal{O}$ is the right shape — a lattice spanning the whole algebra. The third says it is closed under the algebra operation. The fourth — integrality — rules out pathological choices like $\tfrac{1}{n}\mathbb{Z}[i]$ which would be a closed subring but not an order, because its elements are not algebraic integers.

A maximal order is one not strictly contained in any larger order. In the commutative case, the ring of integers $\mathcal{O}_K$ is always the unique maximal order, so the picture is clean. We will see in §7 that non-commutative algebras can have many maximal orders, and the question of which to use is no longer automatic.

Examples and non-examples

Examples of orders in $\mathbb{Q}(i)$: $\mathbb{Z}[i]$ itself (the maximal one); $\mathbb{Z}[2i]$ (a proper suborder, missing the element $i$); $\mathbb{Z} + 3i\mathbb{Z}$ (an even smaller suborder). Non-examples in $\mathbb{Q}(i)$: $\mathbb{Z}[\tfrac{1}{2}]$ (not an algebraic-integer lattice; fails condition 4); $\mathbb{Q}[i]$ (not a lattice; not finitely generated over $\mathbb{Z}$).

§ 5

The quaternionic setting

Hamilton's quaternions $\mathbb{H} = \mathbb{R}\langle 1, i, j, k\rangle$ form an associative algebra with multiplication

$$ i^2 = j^2 = k^2 = ijk = -1, \qquad ij = -ji = k, \quad jk = -kj = i, \quad ki = -ik = j. $$

The crucial difference from $\mathbb{C}$ is that this multiplication is non-commutative: $ij = k$ but $ji = -k$. Algebraically, $\mathbb{H}$ is a four-dimensional associative algebra over $\mathbb{R}$, a division algebra (every non-zero element has a two-sided inverse), and the unique such non-commutative algebra over $\mathbb{R}$ up to isomorphism.

The rational quaternions are $\mathbb{Q} \otimes_{\mathbb{R}} \mathbb{H} = \mathbb{Q}\langle 1,i,j,k\rangle$, the quaternions with rational coordinates. This is the non-commutative analogue of $\mathbb{Q}(i)$ or $\mathbb{Q}(\omega)$. Inside it, we want to find the natural discrete subring — the analogue of $\mathbb{Z}[i]$ or $\mathbb{Z}[\omega]$.

The norm and conjugation extend from $\mathbb{C}$ to $\mathbb{H}$ in the expected way. For $q = a + bi + cj + dk$:

$$ \bar{q} \;=\; a - bi - cj - dk, \qquad N(q) \;=\; q\bar{q} \;=\; a^2 + b^2 + c^2 + d^2. $$

The norm $N(q)$ is real-valued (no imaginary part survives), multiplicative ($N(q_1 q_2) = N(q_1) N(q_2)$), and zero only at $q = 0$. Note that even though $q_1 q_2 \neq q_2 q_1$ in general, their norms agree: $N(q_1 q_2) = N(q_2 q_1)$.

§ 6

The Lipschitz order

The naive quaternion analogue of $\mathbb{Z}[i]$ is the Lipschitz order:

$$ L \;=\; \mathbb{Z}\langle 1, i, j, k\rangle \;=\; \{a + bi + cj + dk : a, b, c, d \in \mathbb{Z}\}. $$

This is the lattice of "integer-coordinate quaternions" — the obvious analogue of taking the unit lattice in $\mathbb{R}^4$ that respects the quaternionic basis. Lipschitz studied this ring in 1886, before Hurwitz's refinement.

Verification that $L$ is an order:

ConditionFor $L = \mathbb{Z}\langle 1,i,j,k\rangle$
$1 \in L$ Yes, $1 = 1 + 0i + 0j + 0k$.
$\mathbb{Z}$-lattice spanning $L$ is the free $\mathbb{Z}$-module on $\{1,i,j,k\}$, and $\mathbb{Q}L = \mathbb{Q}\langle 1,i,j,k\rangle$.
Closed under multiplication Yes — multiplying any two basis elements gives $\pm$ another basis element, so $L \cdot L \subseteq L$.
Every $x \in L$ integral over $\mathbb{Z}$ Yes — $q = a + bi + cj + dk$ satisfies $q^2 - 2a\,q + N(q) = 0$, monic with $\mathbb{Z}$-coefficients when $a, N(q) \in \mathbb{Z}$.

So $L$ is a perfectly good order. Its unit group consists of the norm-$1$ elements with integer coordinates: these are $\pm 1, \pm i, \pm j, \pm k$, a group of order $8$ known as the quaternion group $Q_8$. This is the natural quaternionic analogue of the Gaussian unit group $\{\pm 1, \pm i\}$.

Everything so far is reassuring. The Lipschitz quaternions are to $\mathbb{H}$ what the Gaussian integers are to $\mathbb{C}$: a clean discrete lattice closed under arithmetic, with a tidy finite unit group. We could stop here, declare $L$ the answer, and proceed. The surprise is that this would be wrong.

§ 7

What "maximal" means in non-commutative settings

In a commutative number field, the ring of integers $\mathcal{O}_K$ is both unique and maximal: every other order is strictly contained in it. In a non-commutative quaternion algebra, maximality still makes sense (an order is maximal if it is not contained in any strictly larger order), but uniqueness no longer holds.

For the rational quaternion algebra $\mathbb{Q} \otimes \mathbb{H}$, there are infinitely many maximal orders, all conjugate to one another. What is unique up to conjugacy is the type of the maximal order. The Lipschitz order $L$ is one candidate, but as we will see, it fails to be maximal — it sits inside a strictly larger order. The larger order is the Hurwitz order, and Part II of this learner constructs it explicitly.

Why uniqueness fails non-commutatively

In a commutative field, "the integral closure of $\mathbb{Z}$" makes unambiguous sense as the set of all algebraic integers. In a non-commutative algebra, the set of all integral elements need not be closed under multiplication — $x$ and $y$ may each be integral while $xy$ is not, because the relevant polynomial for $xy$ depends on whether $x$ and $y$ commute. So "the integral closure" is not a ring, and maximal orders have to be specified more carefully. This is purely a feature of non-commutativity; the commutative case is spared because integral closure is always a ring there.

§ 8

The element $L$ is missing

We can detect the failure of $L$ to be maximal by exhibiting an element of $\mathbb{Q} \otimes \mathbb{H}$ that is:

  1. a) not in $L$,
  2. b) integral over $\mathbb{Z}$,
  3. c) such that $L$ together with this element generates a strictly larger order.

The element is

$$ \rho \;=\; \tfrac{1}{2}(1 + i + j + k). $$

This is not in $L$ because its coordinates are halves, not integers. But it has norm $N(\rho) = \tfrac{1}{4}(1 + 1 + 1 + 1) = 1$, and its trace $\rho + \bar{\rho} = 1$ is an integer. The minimal polynomial of $\rho$ is therefore

$$ x^2 - x + 1 \;=\; 0, $$

which is monic with $\mathbb{Z}$-coefficients. So $\rho$ is an algebraic integer of $\mathbb{Q} \otimes \mathbb{H}$, but it lies outside $L$. The maximal order containing both $L$ and $\rho$ is the Hurwitz order, but constructing it carefully — verifying closure, counting units, identifying the unit group as the binary tetrahedral group — is the work of Part II.

Note the structural parallel to §2's remark about $\mathbb{Z}[i\sqrt{3}] \subsetneq \mathbb{Z}[\omega]$. The commutative case foreshadowed this: there too, the obvious-looking lattice with integer-only coordinates was missing a half-integer point that turned out to be an algebraic integer of the field. The non-commutative case just makes the same phenomenon four-dimensional instead of two.

A preview of why $\rho$ matters

The element $\rho$ has order $6$ in the multiplicative group of $\mathbb{Q} \otimes \mathbb{H}$ (direct calculation: $\rho^2 = \tfrac{1}{2}(-1 + i + j + k)$, $\rho^3 = -1$, $\rho^6 = 1$). So $\langle \rho \rangle$ is the cyclic group of order $6$, which is precisely the unit group of the Eisenstein integers $\mathbb{Z}[\omega]$. Adjoining $\rho$ to $L$ is therefore the quaternionic act of carrying the Eisenstein $\mathbb{Z}_3$ structure into non-commutative arithmetic. Part III makes this comparison explicit.

Looking ahead

This learner has established what an order is, has constructed the Lipschitz order $L$ as the obvious quaternion analogue of $\mathbb{Z}[i]$, and has flagged that $L$ is not maximal because a half-integer point $\rho$ lies outside it while being an algebraic integer of the rational quaternion algebra. We have not yet built the Hurwitz order, verified its closure, or counted its units. Part II of this learner takes up that construction directly: what does it mean to adjoin $\rho$ to $L$, what does the resulting lattice look like, and why does it satisfy the order conditions of §4 with $24$ units rather than $8$.
