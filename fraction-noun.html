§ 01 — The TrapWhat happens when you press ÷

When a younger student sees 69 they often reach for the calculator. They type 6 ÷ 9 and get back something like 0.6666667.

Now their fraction is gone. In its place: a smeared decimal with a rounding error at the end. What has been lost?

⟡ Think first

Write down three things you can do easily with 69 that become hard or impossible once it's been turned into 0.6666667.

1. Simplify it — you can spot that 6 and 9 share a factor of 3. With 0.6666667 you can't see that at all.

2. Add it to another fraction — adding 69 + 13 = 69 + 39 = 99 = 1 is clean. Try doing that with 0.6666667 + 0.3333333.

3. Use it in algebra — the fraction keeps its structure. 0.6666667 is already broken.

⊗ The calculator approach
6 ÷ 9 = 0.6666667
structure lost
rounding error introduced
cannot simplify
hard to add to other fractions
useless in algebra
✓ Keep the fraction
6/9 is exact
structure preserved
no rounding error
simplifies to 2/3
adds cleanly to other fractions
works perfectly in algebra
⚠ The ÷ symbol is a verb

The symbol ÷ means "now perform the division". It is an instruction, like a command. Pressing it destroys the fraction and gives you a decimal instead. The slash or vinculum in 69 does not mean that — it means "the thing that is 6 out of 9". It names a number without calculating it. This distinction sounds small. It is enormous.

§ 02 — PerFractions hiding in plain sight

The Latin word per means "out of" or "for each". It is buried inside some of the most common symbols and words in everyday mathematics — usually without anyone pointing it out.

⟡ Before reading on

The symbol % is called "percent". What does cent mean? (Hint: think of a century, a centimetre, a cent coin.) So what does per cent literally say?

Cent comes from Latin for one hundred. So per cent = "per hundred" = "out of one hundred". The symbol % literally means ÷ 100, or equivalently, the fraction 1100. When you write 37%, you are writing 37100. The fraction was there the whole time.

%
per cent = per 100
37% means 37 out of every 100. It is the fraction 37/100. The % sign is shorthand for "/100".
‰
per mille = per 1000
Used in blood alcohol levels, salinity. The three zeros in ‰ hint at 1000. It means "/1000".
km/h
kilometres per hour
The slash is a fraction bar. 60 km/h means 60 km1 hour — a rate, which is a fraction.

Used in finance (basis points). Each extra zero circle adds another factor of 10. Same structure.
37100  =  37%  =  37 per cent three different notations for the same fraction — none of them require a calculator
The slash / in a fraction is not telling you to do anything. It is naming a relationship: "top out of bottom". That is the entire job of the fraction bar.
⟡ The % symbol unpacked

Look at the % symbol carefully. It is sometimes said to have evolved from the abbreviation p° c° for per cento, then written as p/c, and eventually stylised into the modern %. The two circles literally represent the two zeros in 100. The slash between them is the fraction bar.

So % is a fraction in disguise. Once you see it, you cannot unsee it.

§ 03 — Noun vs VerbIs the fraction a thing or a command?

In English, a noun is a thing. A verb is an action. "Run" is a verb. "Runner" is a noun. The same word can be both — but in any sentence, it plays one role.

The same is true in mathematics. 69 can be read two ways:

Verb reading (dangerous)
"Six divided by nine"
→ instruction to calculate
→ reach for calculator
→ get 0.6666667
→ structure destroyed
fraction is gone
Noun reading (powerful)
"The number six-ninths"
→ a name for a point on the number line
→ keep as written
→ look for structure
→ simplify, compare, add
fraction lives — and works
⟡ Test the idea

Which of these is being used as a noun, which as a verb?
(a)   "What is 3 ÷ 4?"     (b)   "The probability of heads is 12."

(a) Verb — the ÷ is a command to compute. The expected answer is 0.75 or "three quarters".

(b) Noun — the fraction names a proportion. No calculation is expected. We are not being asked to do 1 ÷ 2; we are being told that a certain thing is one-half. The fraction is a label.

In algebra and beyond, nearly every fraction you encounter is a noun. The calculator habit of turning nouns into decimals is what makes algebra feel harder than it needs to be.

Numbers have many names — fractions are one naming system

The number we call "two thirds" can be written:

23  =  46  =  69  =  1015  =  0.6̄ infinitely many names, all for the same point on the number line — the fraction form is exact, the decimal is not

A fraction is not just one name — it is a whole family of names. Choosing which name to use is a skill. The skill of cancelling is choosing the simplest name in the family.

§ 04 — CancellingFinding the simplest name

If a fraction is a noun — a name for a number — then cancelling is just finding a shorter, cleaner name for the same thing. You are not changing the number. You are renaming it.

⟡ The key idea

Look at 69. Both 6 and 9 are in the "times 3" table. What happens if you divide both the top and bottom by 3?

6 ÷ 3 = 2.   9 ÷ 3 = 3.   So 69 = 23.

Why is this allowed? Because dividing both top and bottom by the same number is the same as multiplying by 33 = 1 — and multiplying by 1 never changes a number.

We say 3 is a common factor of 6 and 9. Cancelling means dividing out common factors.

The factor view of a fraction

Write the numerator and denominator as products of their factors. Anything that appears in both can be cancelled — it divides to 1.

69  =  2 × 33 × 3  =  2 × 33 × 3  =  23 the shared factor 3 appears top and bottom — it cancels, leaving 2/3
⚠ What cancelling is NOT

Cancelling is not crossing things out at random. You can only cancel a factor that is multiplied into both the numerator and denominator. You cannot cancel a number that is added in. For example:

WRONG:   2 + 32 + 5 ≠ 35  (you cannot cancel the 2)

RIGHT:   2 × 32 × 5 = 35  (the 2 is a shared factor — cancel it)

⟡ The payoff

Once you see 69 = 23, you know something exact and structural about this number. You know it is a little less than 1. You know it is two thirds of the way to 1. You can add it, multiply it, put it in an equation. The decimal 0.6666667 tells you almost nothing — and it is wrong anyway (the true value never ends).

§ 05 — Why It MattersFractions in algebra — nouns win every time

Here is where the noun/verb distinction really pays off. Consider a simple algebraic simplification. Watch what happens in each approach.

⟡ The task

Simplify:   3x6   as much as possible.

Try it yourself before looking at both approaches below.

⊗ Decimal/verb approach
try to "do" the division immediately 3x ÷ 6 = ?
x is unknown — calculator can't help stuck. Cannot proceed.
the fraction needed to stay as a fraction
✓ Noun/fraction approach
keep it as a fraction — look for common factors 3x6 = 3 × x3 × 2
3 is a shared factor — cancel it = 3 × x3 × 2
result: = x2

The fraction approach works because we never tried to calculate — we manipulated the fraction as a noun, spotting structure. The decimal approach fails immediately because there is nothing to calculate without knowing x.

A slightly bigger example

Simplify:   1218   then use it in the fraction   1218 × 34.

⊗ Calculator first
12 ÷ 18 ≈ 0.6667
then multiply by 3/4: 0.6667 × 0.75
≈ 0.5000
could be exactly ½ — but how would you know?
✓ Noun approach
1218 = 23
now multiply: 23 × 34 = 2 × 33 × 4
cancel the shared 3: = 24 = 12

The fraction route gives an exact answer — exactly one half — and shows why it is one half. The decimal route gives "0.5000" with no understanding, and the rounding error might have given 0.4999 or 0.5001 on another calculation, leaving you unsure.

⟡ Reflect

Can you think of an everyday situation where knowing the answer is exactly ½ matters more than knowing it is approximately 0.5? (Hint: think of sharing something between two people, or a probability.)

If the probability of winning a game is exactly 12, the game is fair. If it were 0.4999, it would be slightly unfair. The fraction tells you something the decimal hides. In cryptography, engineering tolerances, and legal contracts, the difference between exactly ½ and approximately ½ can be enormous.

⟡ The one thing to remember

A fraction with a horizontal bar — ab — is a noun. It names a number. It does not ask you to do anything. The ÷ symbol is a verb: it commands you to calculate. In algebra and beyond, you almost always want the noun. Resist the calculator. Keep the fraction.

6 ÷ 9 ≈ 0.666667 destroys information.
69 = 23 reveals it.

§ 06 — Solving an Equation3x/18 = 1/12 — the noun way

Here is a classic equation that trips up students who reach for the calculator too early. We will solve it two ways — and see exactly where the decimal approach collapses, and why the fraction approach glides.

⟡ Your attempt first

Try to solve 3x18 = 112   before reading on. Write down whatever steps feel natural. What do you notice about the left-hand side before you even think about x?

Look at the left side: 3x18. The 3 in the numerator and the 18 in the denominator share a factor of 3. The fraction can be simplified before you do anything else — exactly because you are treating it as a noun, not rushing to calculate.

3x18 = 3 × x3 × 6 = x6. Now the equation is just x6 = 112.

⟡ Strategy: simplify first, then isolate x

Treat every fraction as a noun. Cancel before touching x. Multiply both sides by whatever clears the denominator under x — keeping everything as exact fractions throughout.

⚠ What happens when you convert too early

Watch what happens if you press ÷ on both sides before spotting any structure. Every step accumulates approximation.

Start 3x18 = 112 fine so far — both sides are fractions
Press ÷ 3x ÷ 18 = 1 ÷ 12 right side: 1 ÷ 12 ≈ 0.08333333 — already approximate, already no structure visible
Left side 3x ÷ 18 ≈ 0.1667x 3 ÷ 18 ≈ 0.1667 — the shared factor of 3 is now invisible. The connection between 3 and 18 is gone.
Isolate x x ≈ 0.08333 ÷ 0.1667 dividing two approximations — error compounds. Calculator gives ≈ 0.4999700...
Answer? x ≈ 0.5? Is it exactly ½? Is it exactly 1/2? The decimal doesn't tell you. You'd have to guess and check. The structure that made the answer obvious has been destroyed at step 2.
The answer is exactly x = 1/2. The decimal route hides this completely. The fraction route makes it obvious in three lines.

⟡ The pattern to remember

Every time you see axb = cd, the moves are always the same:

1. Simplify the left fraction (cancel gcd(a, b) if > 1)
2. Multiply both sides by the remaining denominator
3. Simplify the right side
4. Read off x as an exact fraction

None of these steps require a calculator. All of them require treating the fraction as a noun.
