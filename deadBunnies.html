The Digital Fibonacci Sequence

The classical Fibonacci model assumes immortal rabbits. This explorer extends the lifecycle model with a spectral, projective, and matrix-level companion view: the spiral remains the visible texture, but a second layer now reveals the dynamics that produce it.

The mortality parameter g controls the memory depth. The new panels treat this depth as a finite companion system, so the visual tiling can be read at three levels at once: as a binary sequence, as a recurrence with shifting memory, and as a spectral object with a dominant root and subordinate modes.

  1. A bunny (0) matures into a rabbit (1).
  2. A rabbit (1) gives birth to a bunny and persists, yielding 10.
  3. After g generations, a rabbit is removed from the population.

What the extension adds

Spectral Decay Visualizer. The eigenvalues of the order-g companion matrix are plotted on the complex plane against the unit circle, so the spectral gap can be watched directly as g changes.

Projective Monitor. A live cross-ratio is computed from successive population sizes x_(n-3), x_(n-2), x_(n-1), x_n, with a sparkline showing convergence toward its asymptotic target.

Matrix Memory View. The current transition matrix is shown either numerically or as a heatmap, making the recurrence’s “memory shift” visible.

Scaling Mode. A logarithmic plot of |ξ_k − ξ_(k−1)| gives a direct window onto geometric decay, in the spirit of the scaling comparison mentioned in the paper’s conclusion.

Projective monitor

For four consecutive terms, the live projective statistic is taken as

ξn = \( (x_{n-3} - x_{n-1})(x_{n-2} - x_n) / ((x_{n-3} - x_n)(x_{n-2} - x_{n-1})) \)

and is compared with an asymptotic estimate derived from the dominant spectral ratio r = λ₂ / λ₁ via (1+r)²/(1+r+r²).
