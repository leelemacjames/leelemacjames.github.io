Urn Spectral Regime Simulator

Move the dial, watch the derivation hold. The charts confirm the eigenvalue-ratio theorem; they do not discover it.

A two-colour urn draws a ball with probability equal to that colour's current share, then adds balls by a fixed rule. The single parameter \(p\) is the probability that the added ball matches the colour drawn. That one number sets whether the urn has momentum or anti-momentum:

momentum  High \(p\) is positive reinforcement: the colour you drew is the colour you add, so a transient lead feeds itself. The urn remembers its early history and can freeze into it. Pólya (\(p=1\)) is the pure case.

anti-momentum  Low \(p\) is negative reinforcement: the added ball opposes the drawn one, so any lead is actively pushed back. The urn forgets its history and reverts. Friedman (\(p=0\)) is the pure case.

The whole point of the companion explainer is that this momentum/anti-momentum split is not a difference of two unrelated models — it is one family read on opposite sides of a single eigenvalue threshold, \(\rho=\tfrac12\). This page lets you sweep the dial across that threshold and see the predicted behaviour appear. For the derivation of why \(\rho=\tfrac12\) is the dividing line, read the explainer first: leelemacjames.github.io/urn-spectral-conditions.html.

1 · Ensemble proportion fan
Each faint line is one run; the bright line is the ensemble mean. y-axis = fraction of balls that are red after \(n\) draws; x-axis = \(n\) on a log scale. The dashed green line marks the ½ split. Read it this way: if the lines converge onto ½ as \(n\) grows, the urn forgets its history (anti-momentum). If they stay fanned out at the right edge, each run has locked into its own terminal proportion (momentum). The width of the fan at the right edge is the fluctuation that chart 3 measures.
