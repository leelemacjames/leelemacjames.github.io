Thermodynamics: Laws, Cycles & Entropy

Energy can be converted but never created or destroyed — and every conversion comes at a cost. The two laws that govern everything.

B14 B15 B16 B17

What this section is about

Thermodynamics is the physics of energy conversion. The first law is energy conservation applied to thermal systems — heat in = internal energy change + work done. The second law adds the constraint that not all energy conversions are possible: entropy always increases, and no engine is perfectly efficient.

These four slides (all AHL) cover: the first law and its application to four types of gas process, the adiabatic equation, heat engines and the Carnot cycle, and entropy in both its macroscopic and microscopic definitions. The Q&A tests the conceptual errors the IB exploits most ruthlessly — sign conventions, which process allows what, and why you can never build a perfect engine.

The First Law: One Equation, Four Special Cases

Everything starts here:

Q = ΔU + W

Q = heat added to the system. ΔU = change in internal energy. W = work done by the system (W = PΔV for isobaric). This is energy conservation: the heat you put in either stays inside (raising U) or comes out as work (pushing a piston).

Each of the four named processes sets one of these quantities to zero or holds one variable constant — the first law then tells you how the other two relate.

AHL B14 — The First Law & Gas Processes

Isobaric (constant P)

Pressure held constant. V ∝ T (Charles's law). The gas expands or contracts.

Q = ΔU + PΔV

Heat goes into both internal energy and work. On a PV diagram: horizontal line.

Isovolumetric (constant V)

Volume held constant. P ∝ T. No expansion → no work done.

Q = ΔU   (W = 0)

All heat goes directly into internal energy. On a PV diagram: vertical line.

Isothermal (constant T)

Temperature held constant. PV = const (Boyle's law). For ideal gas: ΔU = 0.

Q = W   (ΔU = 0)

All heat input becomes work output. On a PV diagram: hyperbola (PV = const).

Adiabatic (Q = 0)

No heat transfer. Fast processes, or perfectly insulated systems.

0 = ΔU + W   →   W = −ΔU

Work comes from internal energy. Gas cools as it expands, heats as compressed. On a PV diagram: steeper than isothermal.

Sign convention is the biggest source of errors. In the IB convention: Q > 0 means heat added to the system. W > 0 means work done by the system (expansion). ΔU > 0 means internal energy increases (temperature rises). Some textbooks define W as work done on the system, which flips the sign: Q = ΔU − W. Check which convention your question uses before substituting.

Isothermal ≠ adiabatic. Students confuse these constantly. Isothermal: temperature constant, heat flows in/out to maintain T. Adiabatic: no heat flows at all, temperature changes. An isothermal expansion needs a heat source; an adiabatic expansion needs nothing but slows the gas down (T drops).

Q1500 J of heat is added to a gas at constant pressure. The gas does 200 J of work expanding. What is ΔU?▶
Answer

Q = ΔU + W → 500 = ΔU + 200 → ΔU = 300 J.

Of the 500 J heat input, 200 J went into pushing the piston (work) and 300 J went into raising the gas temperature (internal energy). This is the essence of the first law: energy is partitioned, not lost.

Q2A gas undergoes adiabatic compression. Does its temperature increase, decrease, or stay the same?▶
Answer

Increases. Adiabatic: Q = 0, so 0 = ΔU + W. Compression means W < 0 (work done on the gas). Therefore ΔU = −W > 0: internal energy increases, temperature rises. This is why diesel engines ignite fuel without a spark — the air-fuel mixture is compressed adiabatically until it's hot enough to combust.

Q3During an isothermal expansion of an ideal gas, where does the work energy come from?▶
Answer

From the heat reservoir the gas is in contact with. Since T is constant, ΔU = 0 for an ideal gas. So Q = W: every joule of work the gas does on the piston is replaced by a joule of heat flowing in from the reservoir. The gas acts as a conduit, converting heat into work with no change in its own state. This is the isothermal expansion stage of the Carnot cycle.

The adiabatic equation for an ideal gas:

PVγ = constant

Where γ (gamma) is the heat capacity ratio: γ = Cp/Cv. For monatomic gases (He, Ar): γ = 5/3 ≈ 1.67. For diatomic gases (N₂, O₂): γ = 7/5 = 1.40.

On a PV diagram, an adiabat is steeper than an isotherm (PVγ vs PV = const, and γ > 1). This means adiabatic compression raises pressure faster than isothermal compression for the same volume change — because the temperature is also rising.

Q1Why is γ different for monatomic and diatomic gases?▶
Answer

γ = Cp/Cv depends on the degrees of freedom (DOF). Monatomic: 3 translational DOF → Cv = (3/2)R, Cp = (5/2)R → γ = 5/3. Diatomic: 3 translational + 2 rotational = 5 DOF → Cv = (5/2)R, Cp = (7/2)R → γ = 7/5. More DOF means the gas absorbs more heat per degree of temperature rise, making γ smaller. The extra "slots" for energy storage make the gas "softer" under compression.

Q2Air (γ = 1.4) at 100 kPa is adiabatically compressed to 1/10 of its volume. What is the final pressure?▶
Answer

PVγ = const → P₂ = P₁(V₁/V₂)γ = 100 × 101.4 = 100 × 25.12 = 2512 kPa ≈ 25 atm.

Compare isothermal: P₂ = 100 × 10 = 1000 kPa. Adiabatic gives 2.5× higher pressure because the temperature also rises during compression.

AHL B16 — Heat Engines & the Carnot Cycle

A heat engine converts thermal energy into mechanical work. It operates between a hot reservoir (Th) and a cold reservoir (Tc). It absorbs Qh, does work W, and rejects Qc to the cold sink.

η = W/Qh = (Qh − Qc)/Qh = 1 − Qc/Qh

The Carnot cycle is the theoretical maximum-efficiency cycle: two isothermal stages (heat exchange) and two adiabatic stages (no heat exchange, temperature changes). All processes are reversible.

ηCarnot = 1 − Tc/Th    (temperatures in kelvin)

This is the upper bound on efficiency for any engine operating between Th and Tc. No real engine can reach it because all real processes involve some irreversibility (friction, turbulence, finite-time heat transfer).

η = 1 (100% efficiency) is impossible. It would require Tc = 0 K, which violates the third law of thermodynamics (you can't reach absolute zero). Even with Th = 10 000 K and Tc = 300 K, ηCarnot = 1 − 300/10000 = 97% — still not 100%.

Temperatures MUST be in kelvin. η = 1 − Tc/Th with °C gives nonsense. An engine between 200°C (473 K) and 50°C (323 K) has η = 1 − 323/473 = 31.7%, not 1 − 50/200 = 75%.

Q1A power station operates between steam at 540°C and cooling water at 30°C. What is its maximum theoretical efficiency?▶
Answer

Th = 540 + 273 = 813 K. Tc = 30 + 273 = 303 K.

ηCarnot = 1 − 303/813 = 1 − 0.373 = 62.7%.

Real thermal power stations achieve ~35–45% — well below Carnot because of irreversibilities. The gap is not engineering failure; it's physics.

Q2Why does a heat engine NEED a cold reservoir? Can't it just convert all the heat into work?▶
Answer

No — this is the content of the second law of thermodynamics (Kelvin-Planck statement): it is impossible to build a cyclic engine that converts heat completely into work with no other effect. Some heat must be rejected to a cold sink. The reason is fundamentally about entropy: converting heat to work without rejecting heat would decrease the total entropy of the universe, which is forbidden. The cold reservoir is the "entropy dump".

Q3An engine absorbs 800 J from the hot reservoir and rejects 500 J to the cold. What are W and η?▶
Answer

W = Qh − Qc = 800 − 500 = 300 J.

η = W/Qh = 300/800 = 37.5%.

AHL B17 — The Second Law & Entropy

Entropy has two definitions — one macroscopic, one microscopic. Both are needed.

Macroscopic (Clausius):

ΔS = ΔQ / T    [J/K]

Entropy change equals heat transferred divided by the temperature at which it was transferred. Adding heat to a cold object increases entropy more than adding the same heat to a hot object — the same energy is more "disordering" when the system is already cold.

Microscopic (Boltzmann):

S = kB ln Ω

Where Ω is the number of microstates — the number of distinct microscopic arrangements compatible with the macroscopic state. More microstates = higher entropy = more "disorder" (though "disorder" is a loose metaphor).

The Second Law: The total entropy of an isolated system can never decrease. For any spontaneous process: ΔSuniverse ≥ 0. Equality holds only for reversible processes.

Consequences and applications:

  • Heat flows spontaneously from hot to cold — never the reverse (without work input). This increases total entropy.
  • Heat death of the universe: as entropy increases toward a maximum, all temperature differences equalise. No energy gradients remain → no work possible → no processes → thermodynamic equilibrium everywhere.
  • Life doesn't violate the second law. Living organisms maintain low internal entropy by exporting higher entropy to their surroundings (heat, waste). The total entropy of organism + surroundings increases. You stay ordered; the universe becomes more disordered by more than enough to compensate.

The second law is the only law of physics with a preferred direction of time. Newton's laws, Maxwell's equations, Schrödinger's equation — all work equally well forwards and backwards in time. The second law breaks this symmetry: entropy increases toward the future. This gives time its "arrow." Is the second law fundamental, or does it emerge from the statistics of large numbers of particles obeying time-symmetric laws? Most physicists say the latter — but then the question becomes: why was entropy low in the past? That's still an open problem in cosmology.

Q1500 J of heat flows from a body at 400 K to a body at 300 K. What is the total entropy change?▶
Answer

ΔShot = −Q/Th = −500/400 = −1.25 J/K (entropy decreases — heat leaves).

ΔScold = +Q/Tc = +500/300 = +1.67 J/K (entropy increases — heat arrives).

ΔStotal = −1.25 + 1.67 = +0.42 J/K.

Total entropy increases — this is an irreversible process, as the second law demands. The cold body gains more entropy than the hot body loses, because the same heat is more "disordering" at a lower temperature.

Q2A refrigerator moves heat from cold to hot. Does this violate the second law?▶
Answer

No. The second law says heat can't flow from cold to hot spontaneously (without work input). A refrigerator uses external work (electrical energy driving the compressor) to move heat against the natural gradient. The total entropy of the universe still increases: the entropy decrease inside the fridge is more than offset by the entropy increase in the warm room + the entropy generated by the compressor motor. You're paying for the entropy reduction with electricity.

Q3What does Boltzmann's S = kB ln Ω tell us that Clausius's ΔS = Q/T doesn't?▶
Answer

Boltzmann's formula gives entropy an absolute value and connects it to the microscopic state count. Clausius only gives changes in entropy and is purely macroscopic — it says nothing about molecules. Boltzmann explains why entropy increases: there are vastly more disordered microstates than ordered ones, so the system overwhelmingly evolves toward higher Ω. It also makes the third law quantitative: at T = 0 K for a perfect crystal, Ω = 1 (one microstate), so S = kB ln 1 = 0.

Q4Why does the IB call entropy the "quality of energy"?▶
Answer

High-quality energy (low entropy) is concentrated and can do work: electrical energy, chemical energy, kinetic energy of a moving object. Low-quality energy (high entropy) is dispersed and cannot easily do work: thermal energy spread uniformly through a room. Every energy conversion increases total entropy, degrading energy from high quality to low quality. Eventually, all energy becomes uniformly dispersed thermal energy — this is the heat death. The first law says energy is conserved; the second law says its usefulness always decreases.
