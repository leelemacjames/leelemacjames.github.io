Why this section exists

IB Physics uses fewer than 30 Latin and Greek letters to describe hundreds of quantities. The same symbol k means the Coulomb constant, the Boltzmann constant, and the spring constant — three completely different physical ideas with different units. If you don't track which k you're working with, you will write nonsense and not realise it.

This learner has two parts. First, the symbol overloading tables — a field guide to which letter means what in which context. Second, a topic-by-topic conceptual map listing the fundamental objects, laws, and distinctions you need to have internalised for each area of the syllabus. Each section ends with Q&A that targets the confusions IB examiners actually test.

This is not a revision tool for content — it's a revision tool for clarity. If you can't cleanly distinguish a scalar from a vector, a system from its surroundings, or a force from a field, the content won't save you.

T = period vs T = temperature is the single most common symbol collision in IB exams. In SHM or wave problems, T is always period (seconds). In gas law or thermal problems, T is always temperature (kelvin). If both appear in the same problem (e.g. thermal expansion affecting a pendulum), you must be explicit about which is which.

P = pressure vs P = power: context resolves this. In gas law equations (PV = nRT), it's pressure. In energy/circuit problems, it's power. The IB data booklet uses the same letter for both.

Q1 In the equation PV = nRT, identify every symbol and state whether it's a scalar or vector. ▶
Answer

P = pressure (scalar, Pa). V = volume (scalar, m³). n = number of moles (scalar, dimensionless count). R = universal gas constant (scalar, 8.31 J/(mol·K)). T = temperature (scalar, K). Every quantity in the ideal gas law is a scalar. There are no vector quantities in thermodynamics at IB level.

Q2 A question mentions both "the period of oscillation" and "the temperature of the gas". How do you avoid ambiguity? ▶
Answer

Use subscripts: Tosc for period and Tgas (or θ) for temperature. Alternatively, use τ for period if your teacher accepts it. The IB mark scheme will accept any unambiguous labelling — the key is that your reader never has to guess which T you mean. State your convention at the start of your working.

N (number) vs N (newtons) vs N⃗ (normal force): Three completely different things sharing one letter. The number N is a count (dimensionless). The newton N is a unit of force. The normal force N⃗ is a specific contact force perpendicular to a surface. In free-body diagrams, always write the normal force as FN or N⃗ (with the arrow) to avoid confusion with "the number of particles is N".

"Reaction force" is a misnomer your handout rightly flags. The normal force is not a Newton's Third Law pair with weight — it's the surface's response to compression. N3L pairs act on different objects; weight and normal force act on the same object.

Q1 A block sits on a table. Is the normal force the Newton's Third Law pair of the weight? ▶
Answer

No. This is one of the most common errors in IB mechanics. Weight (mg, downward) is the gravitational pull of the Earth on the block. Its N3L pair is the gravitational pull of the block on the Earth (same magnitude, opposite direction, acting on a different object). The normal force (N, upward) is the table pushing up on the block — its N3L pair is the block pushing down on the table. Weight and normal happen to be equal in this case only because the block is in equilibrium (Newton's Second Law with a = 0), not because of N3L.

Q2 What's the difference between n (moles) and n (refractive index)? ▶
Answer

Completely unrelated quantities sharing a letter. Moles (n) = amount of substance, used in PV = nRT. Refractive index (n) = c/v, the ratio of the speed of light in vacuum to its speed in the medium, used in Snell's law (n₁ sin θ₁ = n₂ sin θ₂). Context always tells you which one. If a question involves both gas laws and optics (unlikely but not impossible), use subscripts.

V (potential) vs V (volume) vs V (volts): In PV = nRT, V is volume. In E = QV or V = kQ/r, V is electric potential (measured in volts). In "the potential difference is 12 V", V is the unit (volts). Same letter, three meanings. The IB data booklet uses V for all three.

E (energy) vs E⃗ (electric field): Energy is a scalar (no direction). Electric field is a vector (direction = force on a positive test charge). If a question says "E = 500", you need to check the units: J means energy; N/C means field strength.

Q (heat) vs Q (charge): In Q = mcΔT, it's heat. In Q = It or F = kQq/r², it's charge. Never in the same equation — but they can appear in the same problem (e.g. electrical heating).

Q1 In the equation W = QV, identify each symbol. Is this work, heat, or something else? ▶
Answer

W = work done (J). Q = electric charge (C). V = potential difference (V, volts). This is the work done in moving charge Q through potential difference V. Here Q is not heat — the context is electrical. This equation is equivalent to the definition of voltage: V = W/Q.

Q2 A heater draws current I for time t, raising the temperature of water. Write the relevant equations, being careful with Q. ▶
Answer

Electrical energy supplied: W = VIt (or equivalently, charge delivered Qelec = It, energy = QelecV).

Heat absorbed by water: Qheat = mcΔT.

Energy conservation (assuming no losses): W = Qheat, so VIt = mcΔT.

Using subscripts (Qelec, Qheat) prevents the disastrous confusion of writing "Q = Q" and thinking you've proved something.

What are "ontological notions"? Ontology asks: what exists? In each physics topic, there's a set of fundamental objects (mass, charge, field), properties (scalar, vector, extensive, intensive), and relations (laws, equations of state). Knowing the content means nothing if you can't say what kind of thing each quantity is. The sections below list the conceptual furniture of each topic — the things you need to be able to name, classify, and distinguish under exam pressure.

MECHANICS Classical Mechanics — Objects & Confusions

Scalars

Mass (m): resistance to acceleration. Speed (|v|): magnitude only. KE (½mv²): energy of motion. PE (U): energy of configuration.

Vectors

Displacement (r⃗): position change. Velocity (v⃗ = dr⃗/dt). Acceleration (a⃗ = dv⃗/dt). Force (F⃗): interaction between objects. Momentum (p⃗ = mv⃗).

Laws

N1: No net force → no acceleration. N2: F⃗ = ma⃗. N3: Equal and opposite forces on different objects.

Key Distinctions

Mass ≠ weight. Speed ≠ velocity. Distance ≠ displacement. Force ≠ momentum. Equilibrium ≠ rest.

N3L pairs: The IB's favourite trap. "The table pushes up on the book (normal force), and the book pushes down on the table (reaction)" — this IS a valid N3L pair (same type, same magnitude, opposite direction, different objects). But "weight down, normal up" on the same object is NOT N3L — it's N2L equilibrium.

QCan an object be accelerating and have constant speed simultaneously?▶
Answer

Yes. Uniform circular motion: speed is constant but velocity changes direction continuously, so acceleration (centripetal) is non-zero. Acceleration is the rate of change of velocity (a vector), not speed (a scalar).

THERMO Thermodynamics — Objects & Confusions

System Types

Open: exchanges matter + energy. Closed: exchanges energy only. Isolated: exchanges neither. Most IB problems use closed systems.

Properties

Extensive (depend on amount): mass, volume, internal energy. Intensive (don't): temperature, pressure, density.

Laws

0th: Thermal equilibrium defines temperature. 1st: ΔU = Q − W (energy conservation). 2nd: Entropy of isolated system never decreases.

Key Distinctions

Heat ≠ temperature. Internal energy ≠ heat. Work (gas) = PΔV. Phase change: temperature stays constant while latent heat is absorbed/released.

Heat is NOT a property of an object. An object has internal energy and temperature. Heat (Q) is energy in transit due to a temperature difference. Saying "the object contains heat" is technically wrong — it contains internal energy. Heat is a process, not a state.

QDuring a phase change, energy is being added but temperature doesn't rise. Where does the energy go?▶
Answer

Into breaking intermolecular bonds (increasing potential energy), not increasing kinetic energy. Temperature is proportional to average KE of molecules. Since KE doesn't change during a phase transition, temperature is constant. The energy is stored as latent heat: Q = mL.

WAVES Wave Phenomena — Objects & Confusions

Wave Types

Transverse: oscillation ⊥ propagation (EM waves, strings). Longitudinal: oscillation ∥ propagation (sound, compression springs).

Vibration Types

Free: natural frequency, no driving. Forced: external periodic force. Damped: amplitude decreases over time. Resonance: driving frequency = natural frequency.

Parameters

A = amplitude. λ = wavelength. f = frequency. c = wave speed (c = fλ). T = period (T = 1/f).

Key Distinctions

Progressive (travelling) vs standing waves. Rays vs wavefronts. Phase vs path difference. Diffraction vs interference.

SHM: x(t) = A sin(ωt + φ). Students confuse ω (angular frequency, rad/s) with f (frequency, Hz). They're related by ω = 2πf, but they're not interchangeable. Using f in place of ω (or vice versa) in SHM equations produces answers wrong by a factor of 2π.

Standing waves don't "travel". They're the superposition of two progressive waves going in opposite directions. Nodes never move. Antinodes oscillate in place. Energy is not transported — it's stored between nodes.

QIn a double-slit experiment, what determines whether you get a bright or dark fringe at a given point?▶
Answer

The path difference between the two slits to that point. Bright fringe: path difference = nλ (constructive interference, waves arrive in phase). Dark fringe: path difference = (n + ½)λ (destructive interference, waves arrive in antiphase). The fringe pattern depends on wavelength and slit separation, not amplitude.

ELECTRICITY Electricity & Magnetism — Objects & Confusions

Fundamentals

Current (I): flow of charge, A. Potential (V): energy per unit charge. Resistance (R): V/I, Ω. Charge (Q): coulombs.

Fields

E⃗ = electric field (force per unit positive charge). B⃗ = magnetic field (force on moving charge). Φ = flux (field × area).

Kirchhoff's Laws

K1: ΣI at junction = 0 (charge conservation). K2: ΣV around loop = 0 (energy conservation).

Key Distinctions

EMF ≠ terminal pd (EMF = ε = V + Ir). Static charge vs current. Conventional current vs electron flow. Cell vs battery.

Conventional current flows from + to −. Electrons flow from − to +. The IB uses conventional current direction for all circuit analysis, Kirchhoff's laws, and magnetic force rules (Fleming's left hand). If you mix conventions, every force and field direction will be backwards.

EMF is not a force. It's the energy per unit charge supplied by the source (units: volts). The term "electromotive force" is a historical misnomer that persists because it's entrenched.

QA battery has EMF 9.0 V and internal resistance 0.5 Ω. It drives current 2.0 A through an external resistor. What's the terminal pd?▶
Answer

Terminal pd = ε − Ir = 9.0 − (2.0)(0.5) = 8.0 V. The "lost volts" (Ir = 1.0 V) are dissipated as heat inside the battery. This is why terminal pd is always less than EMF when current flows.

NUCLEAR Nuclear Physics — Objects & Confusions

Terminology

Nuclide: specific (Z, A). Isotope: same Z, different A. Isotone: same N, different Z. Students often say "isotope" when they mean "nuclide".

Energy Concepts

Binding energy: energy to disassemble nucleus. Mass defect: Δm = parts − whole. BE/A: stability measure. Peak ≈ Fe/Ni.

Decay Types

Alpha: ⁴He emitted (ΔZ = −2, ΔA = −4). β⁻: n → p + e⁻ + ν̄. β⁺: p → n + e⁺ + ν. Gamma: photon, no Z/A change.

Key Distinctions

Fission (heavy → light) vs fusion (light → heavy). Both release energy by moving products toward the BE/A peak. Radioactive decay is random and spontaneous.

"Binding energy per nucleon is high" means MORE stable, not less. Students frequently invert this. High BE/A = hard to pull apart = stable. Iron has the highest BE/A ≈ 8.8 MeV — it is the most stable nucleus. Uranium has lower BE/A ≈ 7.6 MeV — it is less stable and can fission.

Fission releases energy because the products are MORE tightly bound than the parent. Not less. The products sit higher on the BE/A curve. Energy release = increase in total binding energy. This confuses students because "breaking apart" sounds like it should cost energy — and it does for individual nucleons, but the fragments are already bound internally.

Q1After alpha decay, does the daughter nucleus have more or less binding energy per nucleon than the parent?▶
Answer

More. Alpha decay releases energy, which means the products (daughter + alpha) have greater total binding energy than the parent. Since both products sit closer to the peak of the BE/A curve, their BE/A is higher. This is why the decay is energetically favourable — the system moves to a more tightly bound (lower energy) configuration.

Q2What's the difference between a nuclide and an isotope?▶
Answer

A nuclide is a specific nuclear species defined by its proton number Z and neutron number N (equivalently, Z and A). Carbon-12 is a nuclide. Carbon-14 is a different nuclide. An isotope is a relative term: C-12 and C-14 are isotopes of each other (same Z, different N). Iron-56 is a nuclide but is not an isotope of carbon. "Isotope" always implies a comparison.

QED Quantum Electrodynamics — Objects & Confusions

From Waves to Particles

Photon: quantum of EM field. Energy = hf. Zero rest mass. Carries momentum p = h/λ. Photoelectric effect proves particle nature.

Atomic Models

Bohr atom: quantised orbits, En = −13.6/n² eV. Works for hydrogen. Fails for multi-electron atoms. Replaced by Schrödinger's wave mechanics.

Particles

Quarks: up, down, charm, strange, top, bottom. Leptons: electron, muon, tau + neutrinos. Not subject to strong force.

Force Carriers

Photon: EM. Gluon: strong. W±, Z⁰: weak. These replace the classical concept of "action at a distance".

Wave-particle duality is not "sometimes wave, sometimes particle". It means that quantum objects have properties of both simultaneously — the experiment you perform determines which aspect you observe. The electron doesn't "decide" to be a wave when it diffracts. It has a wavefunction at all times; the diffraction pattern is a consequence of that wavefunction interacting with a crystal lattice.

QWhy can't the photoelectric effect be explained by wave theory?▶
Answer

Wave theory predicts: (1) any frequency should eject electrons if intensity is high enough, (2) emission should be delayed while the surface accumulates energy, (3) KE of emitted electrons should depend on intensity. All three predictions are wrong. Observations show: (1) threshold frequency exists, (2) emission is instantaneous, (3) KE depends on frequency, not intensity. Only a particle model (photons with E = hf) explains all three.

STD MODEL The Standard Model — Objects & Confusions

Matter Hierarchy

Elementary: quarks, leptons (no substructure). Composite: hadrons (made of quarks). Baryons: 3 quarks (proton = uud, neutron = ddu). Mesons: quark + antiquark (pions).

Conservation Laws

Energy, momentum, charge, baryon number, lepton number — all conserved always. Strangeness: conserved in strong + EM, violated in weak. Isospin: strong force symmetry.

Symmetry Breaking

Higgs mechanism: particles acquire mass through interaction with Higgs field. Without it, W/Z bosons would be massless and weak force would be long-range.

Quantum Fields

Every particle = excitation of a field. Photons = excitations of EM field. Electrons = excitations of electron field. This unifies particles and forces into one framework.

Protons and neutrons are NOT elementary particles. They're composite (baryons made of quarks). Electrons ARE elementary (leptons). Students often treat all three as the same category.

"Strangeness is always conserved" — wrong. Strangeness is conserved in strong and EM interactions but can change by ±1 in weak interactions. This is how strange particles decay (slowly, via the weak force). If a decay violates strangeness, it must proceed via the weak interaction.

QA particle interaction conserves charge, baryon number, and lepton number but violates strangeness by 1. What force mediates it?▶
Answer

The weak nuclear force. Strong and EM interactions conserve strangeness. Only the weak interaction permits ΔS = ±1. This is testable: weak decays are characteristically slow (long lifetimes, ~10⁻¹⁰ s) compared to strong decays (~10⁻²³ s).

ASTRO Astrophysics & Cosmology — Objects & Confusions

Cosmic Structures

Nebulae → stars → planetary systems → globular clusters → galaxies → galaxy clusters. Gravity dominates at every scale.

Cosmology

Big Bang: universe began hot, dense, expanding. CMB: remnant radiation from ~380,000 years after. Dark matter: gravitational effects, doesn't emit light. Dark energy: accelerating expansion.

"The Big Bang was an explosion in space" — wrong. It was an expansion of space itself. There is no centre and no edge. Every point in the universe was the Big Bang. The CMB is not coming from a specific direction — it's everywhere, in every direction, because it permeates all of space.

Dark matter ≠ dark energy. Dark matter is gravitationally attractive (holds galaxies together). Dark energy is repulsive (drives accelerating expansion). They're completely different phenomena that happen to share the word "dark" (meaning: we don't know what they are).

QWhat evidence supports the existence of dark matter?▶
Answer

Galaxy rotation curves: stars at the edges of galaxies orbit faster than predicted by visible mass alone — implying a halo of unseen mass. Gravitational lensing: light from distant galaxies bends more than visible mass can account for. CMB anisotropy: the pattern of fluctuations in the CMB requires dark matter in cosmological models. Bullet Cluster: the separation of visible matter (gas, via X-ray) from the centre of mass (via lensing) during a galaxy cluster collision.

ATMOSPHERE Atmospheric Physics — Objects & Confusions

Energy Budget

Solar constant: ~1361 W/m² at Earth's orbit. Albedo: fraction reflected (ice ≈ 0.8, ocean ≈ 0.06). Emissivity: ratio of actual IR emission to blackbody at same T.

Greenhouse Effect

UV in → absorbed → re-emitted as IR → absorbed by CO₂, CH₄, H₂O → re-emitted in all directions (including back to surface). Net effect: surface warmer than it would be without an atmosphere.

Radiation

UV: high energy, from Sun, absorbed by ozone. IR: lower energy, emitted by Earth's surface. Greenhouse gases are transparent to UV but absorb IR — that asymmetry drives the effect.

Energy Balance

Equilibrium: power in (absorbed solar) = power out (emitted IR). If greenhouse gases increase → less IR escapes → surface T rises until a new equilibrium is reached.

"Greenhouse gases trap heat" is misleading. They don't "trap" anything — they absorb and re-emit IR radiation. The re-emission is isotropic (all directions), so roughly half goes back toward the surface. The atmosphere becomes a less efficient radiator to space while remaining a good absorber, raising the equilibrium temperature.

Molecular resonance: CO₂ absorbs IR because the photon frequency matches a vibrational mode of the molecule (bending mode at ~15 μm). Not all molecules do this — N₂ and O₂ are transparent to IR because they lack a permanent or transient dipole moment.

Q1Why is the greenhouse effect described as "high energy in, lower energy out"?▶
Answer

Incoming solar radiation peaks in the visible/UV (short wavelength, high energy per photon). The Earth's surface absorbs this, warms up, and re-emits as IR (long wavelength, lower energy per photon). By Wien's law, a cooler object emits at longer wavelengths. The total energy is conserved — but it arrives as a few high-energy photons and leaves as many lower-energy photons. This is connected to entropy: energy is "degraded" from concentrated (UV) to dispersed (IR).

Q2If albedo increases (e.g. more ice cover), what happens to Earth's temperature?▶
Answer

Higher albedo → more sunlight reflected → less energy absorbed → surface temperature decreases. This is a positive feedback loop for cooling: lower T → more ice → higher albedo → even lower T. (Conversely, warming melts ice → lower albedo → more absorption → more warming. This is the ice-albedo feedback, a key mechanism in climate science.)
