What this section is about

A gas is described by four state variables: pressure (P), volume (V), temperature (T), and amount (n). The ideal gas equation PV = nRT constrains these -- it tells you what combinations are physically possible in equilibrium. It's a relationship between states, not a law of motion.

This learner covers: the equation of state and how to use it, the ideal gas assumptions vs real gas behaviour, PV diagrams with isotherms, the three named constant-variable processes, work done by an expanding gas, and thermodynamic cycles. Every IB gas problem reduces to identifying what's held constant, what changes, and which form of PV = nRT applies.

The Equation of State -- Two Forms

PV = nRT   (moles form: n = number of moles, R = 8.31 J/(mol K))

PV = NkBT   (molecules form: N = number of molecules, kB = 1.38 x 10-23 J/K)

Connected by: N = nNA and kB = R/NA. Use whichever form matches your given data. T must always be in kelvin.

SL Equation of State & Using PV = nRT

The equation of state is a thermodynamic constraint -- it describes which combinations of P, V, T are possible in equilibrium. It is NOT a dynamical law (it doesn't tell you how a system changes over time, only where it can be).

Units matter: P in Pa (N/m2), V in m3, T in K, n in mol. If the question gives litres, convert (1 L = 10-3 m3). If it gives atm, convert (1 atm ≈ 105 Pa). If it gives Celsius, add 273.

Worked Example -- Finding moles in a cylinder

Given: V = 2.0 x 10-2 m3, P = 20 MPa = 20 x 106 Pa, T = 17C = 290 K.

n = PV/(RT) = (20 x 106)(2 x 10-2) / [(8.31)(290)] = 400 000 / 2410 = 166 mol N = nNA = 166 x 6.02 x 1023 = 1.0 x 1026 atoms
Worked Example -- Constant volume heating

Given: Gas at 6.0 atm, 30C, heated to 330C at constant volume.

T1 = 303 K, T2 = 603 K. V constant, n constant. P1V = nRT1 and P2V = nRT2 --> P2/P1 = T2/T1 P2 = 6.0 x 603/303 = 12 atm

The kelvin trap: If you use Celsius in PV = nRT, you get nonsense. At 0C you'd compute PV = 0, implying zero pressure or zero volume -- obviously wrong. The gas law requires absolute temperature (kelvin), where T = 0 means genuinely zero molecular motion. This is by far the most common error in IB gas law problems.

Q1A balloon contains 0.50 mol of gas at 100 kPa and 300 K. What is its volume?▶
Answer

V = nRT/P = 0.50 x 8.31 x 300 / 100 000 = 0.0125 m3 = 12.5 L.

Q2What is the difference between an equation of state and a dynamical law like F = ma?▶
Answer

An equation of state (PV = nRT) describes a relationship between state variables -- it constrains what equilibrium states are possible but says nothing about how or how fast the system changes. A dynamical law (F = ma) is a time-evolution rule -- given the current state, it predicts the future state. Newton's law tells you how the system moves; the ideal gas law tells you where it can sit still.

SL Ideal Gas vs Real Gas

Ideal Gas (model)

Molecules are identical perfect spheres.

Collisions are perfectly elastic (no KE lost).

No intermolecular forces -- PE is constant.

Molecule volume is negligible compared to container.

Cannot be liquefied (no attractions to form a liquid).

Real Gas

Molecules are not identical spheres (polyatomic, irregular shapes).

Collisions are not perfectly elastic.

Intermolecular forces exist (van der Waals, dipole, etc.).

Molecules have finite volume.

Can be liquefied by cooling/compression.

When does a real gas behave ideally? At low pressure, moderate temperature, and low density. Under these conditions, molecules are far apart (forces negligible, volumes negligible), and PV = nRT works well.

When does ideal fail? At high pressure / low volume (molecules crammed together, forces and volumes matter) or very low temperature (molecules move slowly, attractive forces can "catch" them, leading to condensation). Near the condensation point, real gas behaviour deviates strongly from PV = nRT.

When does modelling of "ideal" situations become "good enough" to count as knowledge? The ideal gas doesn't exist -- but PV = nRT predicts real gas behaviour to within a few percent under everyday conditions. Engineers designing scuba tanks rely on it. At what point does an approximation graduate from "useful fiction" to "knowledge"? Is all scientific knowledge approximate, and if so, what distinguishes a good approximation from a bad one?

Q1PV = nRT describes real gas behaviour only at: (A) low P, large V (B) high T only (C) large V, large P (D) all P and V. Which?▶
Answer

(A) Low pressures and large volumes. Under these conditions, molecules are far apart -- their finite size and intermolecular forces are negligible fractions of the container volume and the thermal energy respectively. At high pressure or small volumes, real gas deviations become significant.

Q2Why can ideal gases never be liquefied?▶
Answer

Liquefaction requires intermolecular attractive forces that can hold molecules together in a condensed state. Ideal gas molecules have no intermolecular forces by definition -- there's nothing to make them stick together. No matter how much you compress or cool an ideal gas, the molecules simply bounce off each other elastically. Real gases liquefy because their attractive forces (van der Waals, hydrogen bonds, etc.) become significant at low temperature/high pressure.

Q3The temperature of an ideal gas is reduced. Which is true? (A) Molecules collide with walls less frequently (B) Molecules collide with each other more frequently (C) Contact time between molecules and wall is reduced (D) Contact time between molecules is increased.▶
Answer

(A). Lower T means lower average KE, so molecules move slower and hit the walls less often -- reducing both collision frequency and pressure. (B) is wrong: slower molecules collide with each other less often. (C) and (D) are wrong: for perfectly elastic collisions (ideal gas), the contact time is effectively zero regardless of speed -- the collision is instantaneous.

SL PV Diagrams, Isotherms & State Change Graphs

A PV diagram plots pressure vs volume for a fixed quantity of gas. Each point on the diagram represents an equilibrium state. Lines on the diagram represent processes (transitions between states).

Isotherms are curves of constant temperature: PV = nRT = const, so P = const/V -- a hyperbola. Higher T isotherms sit further from the origin (higher P at same V, or higher V at same P).

P vs T graphs (constant V): P = (nR/V)T -- a straight line through the origin if plotted in kelvin. If plotted in Celsius, the line intercepts the horizontal axis at -273C (absolute zero).

V vs T graphs (constant P): V = (nR/P)T -- a straight line through the origin in kelvin. Intercepts at -273C in Celsius. The gradient is nR/P for one mole, or R/V for the P-T graph.

P vs T(Celsius) graph: the line does NOT pass through the origin. It intercepts the x-axis at -273C. Students often draw it through (0,0) because they forget the axis is Celsius, not kelvin. On a P vs T(K) graph, it DOES pass through the origin (P = 0 at T = 0 K).

Q1On a P-T graph (kelvin, absolute temperature) for 1 mole at volume V, what is the gradient?▶
Answer

P = (nR/V)T. Gradient = nR/V. For n = 1 mol: gradient = R/V. The intercept on the T axis is at T = 0 K (origin). The gradient is steeper for smaller volumes (gas is more compressed, so the same temperature rise produces a bigger pressure increase).

Q2On a PV diagram, identify: A to B (along a hyperbola), B to C (horizontal line), C to A (vertical line).▶
Answer

A to B (hyperbola): isothermal (constant T, PV = const). B to C (horizontal = constant P): isobaric. C to A (vertical = constant V): isovolumetric/isochoric.

SL The Three Named Gas Processes

Isobaric (P = const)

Pressure constant.

V ∝ T (Charles's law).

On PV diagram: horizontal line.

W = PΔV

Isovolumetric (V = const)

Volume constant.

P ∝ T (Gay-Lussac).

On PV diagram: vertical line.

W = 0 (no expansion)

Isothermal (T = const)

Temperature constant.

PV = const (Boyle's law).

On PV diagram: hyperbola.

P1V1 = P2V2

Each process holds one variable constant, and PV = nRT then constrains how the other two relate. You derive the gas law by starting with PV = nRT and cancelling the constant quantities.

Q1Show that V ∝ T for an isobaric process of an isolated ideal gas.▶
Answer

From PV = nRT: V = (nR/P)T. Isolated means n is constant. Isobaric means P is constant. R is always constant. So V = (nR/P)T = (CONST) x T. Therefore V ∝ T.

Q2A syringe of gas at 15 Pa, V = 10 cm3 is compressed isothermally to V = 5 cm3. What is the new pressure?▶
Answer

Isothermal: P1V1 = P2V2. P2 = P1V1/V2 = 15 x 10/5 = 30 Pa. Halving the volume doubles the pressure (at constant T).

SL Work Done by an Expanding Gas

When a gas expands against a constant pressure (isobaric), it does work on its surroundings:

W = PΔV

Derivation: force on piston = PA (pressure x area). Displacement of piston = x. So W = Fx = PAx = PΔV (since Ax = change in volume).

On a PV diagram, work = area under the curve. For an isobaric process, this is a rectangle. For other processes, you calculate or estimate the area.

  • ΔV > 0 (gas expands): W > 0 -- gas does work ON surroundings.
  • ΔV < 0 (gas compressed): W < 0 -- surroundings do work ON gas.
  • ΔV = 0 (isovolumetric): W = 0 -- no work done.

W = PΔV only works for constant-pressure processes. If P changes during the expansion (e.g. isothermal), you must find the area under the PV curve, which requires integration (or geometric estimation for IB). For a straight sloping line on a PV diagram, the area is a trapezium. For a curve, you approximate.

Q1A gas expands at constant pressure 8 Pa from V = 10 m3 to V = 25 m3. What work does it do?▶
Answer

W = PΔV = 8 x (25 - 10) = 8 x 15 = 120 J.

Q2A gas is compressed isovolumetrically (V constant) from 2 Pa to 8 Pa. How much work is done?▶
Answer

W = 0. No volume change means no displacement of the piston, so no work is done regardless of how the pressure changes. On a PV diagram, this is a vertical line -- zero width, zero area underneath.

SL Thermodynamic Cycles & the Two-Container Problem

A thermodynamic cycle is a sequence of processes that returns the gas to its original state. On a PV diagram, a cycle is a closed loop.

Net work done in a cycle = area enclosed by the loop. Clockwise loop = positive net work (engine -- gas does work on surroundings). Anticlockwise loop = negative net work (refrigerator -- surroundings do work on gas).

Worked Example -- Cycle A → B → C → A

States: A(P=2, V=10), B(P=8, V=10), C(P=8, V=25). Straight-line processes.

A→B: ΔV = 0 (vertical) → W = 0 B→C: P = 8, ΔV = 15 → W = 8 x 15 = +120 J C→A: P varies (sloping line). Area = rectangle + triangle = (2)(15) + ½(6)(15) = 30 + 45 = 75. But ΔV < 0, so W = -75 J Wcycle = 0 + 120 - 75 = +45 J (clockwise = engine)

Reversing the cycle: W = -45 J (anticlockwise = refrigerator).

The Two-Container Valve Problem:

Worked Example -- Valve opened between two containers

Given: Container 1: 6 dm3 at 12 atm. Container 2: 3 dm3 at 6 atm. Same temperature. Valve opened.

Key insight: total moles are conserved, and temperature doesn't change.

n1 = P1V1/(RT), n2 = P2V2/(RT) After mixing: ntotal = n1 + n2, Vtotal = 6 + 3 = 9 dm3 Pf x 9 = P1 x 6 + P2 x 3 = 12 x 6 + 6 x 3 = 72 + 18 = 90 Pf = 90/9 = 10 atm
Q1A cycle goes A → C → B → A (anticlockwise version of the worked example). What is the net work?▶
Answer

W = -45 J. Reversing the direction of the cycle reverses the sign of the net work. Anticlockwise = net work done ON the gas (like a refrigerator or heat pump). The magnitude of the enclosed area is the same; only the sign changes.

Q2Two identical containers, each 5 L, one at 3 atm and one at 7 atm, same temperature. Valve opened. What is the final pressure?▶
Answer

Pf x 10 = 3 x 5 + 7 x 5 = 15 + 35 = 50. Pf = 50/10 = 5.0 atm.

With equal volumes, the final pressure is simply the average: (3 + 7)/2 = 5 atm. This only works because the volumes are equal.

Q3Why is the area enclosed by a PV cycle equal to the net work done?▶
Answer

Work done in each process = area under that process's curve on the PV diagram. Going forward (expansion), the area is positive. Going back (compression), the area is negative. The net work is the forward area minus the backward area = the area between the two paths = the enclosed area of the loop. This is a purely geometric result that holds for any shape of cycle.
