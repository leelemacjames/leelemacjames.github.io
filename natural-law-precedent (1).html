Natural Law
as Precedent

Geometry, Regularity, and the Status of Physical Laws

Natural law may be less a statute book written once and for all than a body of precedent accumulated by the universe as it unfolds. We are accustomed to treating the laws of physics as timeless prescriptions—fixed, universal, and independent of context. Yet both the practice of science and the structure of modern physical theories suggest a different picture. This paper examines whether laws of nature are better understood not as immutable decrees but as durable, geometry-dependent regularities that stabilise provisionally and may evolve with the universe itself.

§ 1

The Question: What Is a Law of Nature?

The traditional view presents laws as unconditional facts about the world, being universal, exceptionless, and independent of history. They resemble statute law: laid down once, applying everywhere without remainder.

  • universal in scope
  • exceptionless in application
  • independent of historical context

But scientific practice tells a subtler story. Laws are inferred from patterns, extended beyond observation, and revised when regimes change. The working scientist does not receive laws; she constructs them.

Are laws discovered as fixed truths, or inferred as stabilised regularities? The distinction is not merely epistemological touching as it does on the ontology of science itself.

§ 2

The Analogy: Statute Law vs Case Law

Legal systems offer two structural models that map, with some precision, onto competing philosophies of natural law.

Statute Law
  • fixed rules
  • externally imposed
  • applies by decree
  • immune to revision by use
Case Law
  • precedent from decisions
  • built by accumulation
  • extended by analogy
  • overturned when strained

Scientific practice aligns with the second model. Regularities are identified, extended, tested, and sometimes overturned. The history of mechanics, thermodynamics, and quantum theory is precisely this: a case-law tradition in which each settled principle holds until a new regime exposes its limits.

Physical laws resemble precedents that hold until they no longer do. They are not arbitrary and neither are they guaranteed a priori.

§ 3

The Philosophical Pressure: Induction and Ontology

Two distinct tensions motivate this shift in perspective, one epistemological and one ontological.

(i) Induction

We move from observed regularities to universal laws in which this step is not deductive. It is provisional, revisable, and dependent on continued coherence. The problem of induction does not dissolve when the laws concern physics rather than swans.

(ii) Ontology

We often treat mathematical structures as real, necessary, and definitive. Yet this risks conflating descriptions with things described. The tendency to mistake successful formalisms for final ontology is a persistent temptation in theoretical physics.

Together, these pressures suggest that the apparent necessity of natural laws may be less a feature of the world than a feature of our best current descriptions of it.

§ 4

The Physical Pressure: Geometry and Equivalence

Modern physics reinforces this instability from within. A key observation, easily obscured by the standard presentation of fundamental theories, is that different geometrical frameworks can encode the same physical behaviour.

  • Newtonian vs relativistic descriptions at low velocity
  • metric vs gauge formulations of field theories
  • alternative geometric structures with equivalent predictions
  • dual descriptions across holographic correspondences

This is not merely a matter of notational convenience. If distinct geometric languages generate indistinguishable physical predictions, no single formalism has privileged ontological status. The laws, as typically stated, are laws of a description—and that description may itself be contingent.

What appears as a "law" may be a stable description within a given geometric regime that is durable, predictive, and well-confirmed, but not necessary beyond that regime.

§ 5

The Consequence: Laws as Stable Regimes

Assembling these pressures, a coherent picture emerges. Laws are inferred rather than given. They are expressed in chosen formalisms. They are valid within domains, and replaced or extended when those domains shift.

  • laws are inferred, not received
  • expressed in contingent geometric languages
  • valid within specifiable domains
  • replaced or refined when domains shift

Laws are best understood as stable patterns of regularity that persist across a range of conditions, but not necessarily beyond them. This is precisely how precedent works: robust, widely applicable, binding in practice, but not absolute in principle.

§ 6

The Proposal: Natural Law as Precedent

We arrive at a revised picture that is neither the eliminativist claim that laws do not exist, nor the Platonist claim that they exist necessarily. It is something more interesting: a historically conditioned, geometrically indexed lawfulness.

Mathematics provides
  • possible structures
  • the space of descriptions
  • consistent frameworks
Physics selects
  • those that stabilise
  • under actual conditions
  • across cosmic history

History determines which persist. Natural law becomes neither arbitrary nor eternal, but a constrained and historically conditioned body of precedent that the universe has, so far, chosen not to overturn.

Laws of nature are not timeless prescriptions but durable, geometry-dependent regularities that stabilise within particular regimes and may evolve with the universe itself.

Closing

The universe may not follow a script written in advance, but rather sustain a body of law shaped by what has worked, what has endured, and what continues to hold, at least for now.
