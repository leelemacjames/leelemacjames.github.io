Pascal's Triangle is Not a Noun

On the pedagogical inversion that hides the verb behind the diagram, and what Penrose's account of the equivalence class can teach us about how to teach.

Architect Carbon · Lee McCulloch-James

In the preface to The Road to Reality, Roger Penrose recounts a memory from a maths lesson he sat through as a child. He was about eleven. The teacher asked the class what a fraction such as three-eighths actually is. Pupils volunteered the usual answers about cutting up pies. The teacher rejected them as imprecise references to physical situations rather than answers to the mathematical question. Other suggestions came forward. Penrose recalls one of his classmates offering the following:

"Other suggestions came forward, such as ⅜ is 'something with a 3 at the top and an 8 at the bottom with a horizontal line in between' and I was distinctly surprised to find that the teacher seemed to be taking these suggestions seriously!"

Penrose, The Road to Reality (2004), Preface, p. xvii

What the teacher was reaching for, Penrose later realised, was the equivalence-class definition of a fraction. A fraction is not the symbol on the page. It is not a slice of pie. It is the infinite collection of all pairs of integers (a, b), (2a, 2b), (3a, 3b), and so on, with each pair related to every other by the cancellation rule. The fraction three-eighths is the equivalence class of pairs (3, 8), (6, 16), (9, 24), (12, 32), and so on without end.

This is a heavy piece of machinery to invoke for a number that an eight-year-old can use to share a chocolate bar. Penrose is aware of that and treats it with characteristic grace:

"It hardly conveys to us the intuitive notion of what ⅜ is, for example! No wonder my mother's friend was confused."

Penrose, The Road to Reality (2004), Preface, p. xviii

His mother's friend was a former ballet dancer who could not get the hang of cancelling fractions. Penrose suggests that her difficulty was not a failure of intellect, since she was an elegant and highly intelligent woman whose father was a Fellow of the Royal Society. Her difficulty was perhaps a kind of philosophical scruple. She could not see what a fraction was, and she was therefore not willing to manipulate it. The mathematicians around her had developed the social competence to push fractions around without worrying about what they were. She had not, and the gap was uncrossable.

Penrose draws the moral carefully. The way mathematicians handle a fraction is to define it by its behaviour under operations. We say that ⅜ added to itself eight times gives 3. We say that ⅙ added to ⅙ gives ⅓ via cancellation. We say that any pair (a, b) is to be treated as the same fraction as any pair (na, nb) for non-zero n. The rules of behaviour come first, and an object emerges that obeys them. The object is real enough that mathematicians treat it as Platonic, but its accessibility to us runs entirely through the rules.

The mathematician's competence lives in the verbs. The nouns are placeholders that the verbs animate.

This is a deep observation about how mathematics actually proceeds. It says that working mathematicians routinely manipulate objects whose ultimate nature they cannot articulate, and that this is fine. The proof that it is fine is that the manipulations are consistent, that they reach agreement, that they connect to the world. The mathematics works whether or not we have settled what the objects are.

The pedagogical inversion

And yet in school we do the inverse. We hand the pupil the diagram and ask them to recognise it. Pascal's triangle is presented as an object: a downward-pointing arrangement of numbers, each interior number the sum of the two above. The Catalan numbers are presented as a sequence: 1, 1, 2, 5, 14, 42, 132, 429, and on. The Fibonacci numbers as a list with a rule. The pupil is asked to remember these, to spot them in problems, to compute their next entries, to recognise their famous identities.

Pascal's triangle as it appears in textbooks. A diagram presented as a thing.

What is missing is the verb that produced the diagram. Pascal's triangle is what happens when a particular linear operator acts repeatedly on a particular starting vector. The operator is a lower bidiagonal matrix with ones on the main diagonal and ones on the subdiagonal. The starting vector is the column (1, 0, 0, 0, ...). Apply the operator once, you get (1, 1, 0, 0, ...). Apply it again, you get (1, 2, 1, 0, 0, ...). Apply it n times and you get the n-th row of Pascal's triangle.

The diagram is the orbit. The operator is the thing.

The diagram is the orbit. The operator is the thing.

Now this matters, because it tells us at once that Pascal's triangle is not alone in the universe. The Pascal operator is one point in a small structured family of bidiagonal operators. Change the entries of the operator slightly and you produce a different triangle. Take the entries to be (k+1) on the main diagonal and (n−k) on the subdiagonal, where n and k are the current row and column, and the operator now produces the Eulerian triangle, whose row sums are the factorials. Take the operator to be Pascal but post-compose it with a row-accumulator and seed the process with all ones, and you produce the Delannoy array, whose central diagonal is the famous sequence 1, 3, 13, 63, 321, 1683. Take the entries to be k and 1, and you produce the Stirling numbers of the second kind, whose row sums are the Bell numbers counting set partitions.

Four classical triangles. One template. The template is a lower bidiagonal operator with possibly position-dependent entries, optionally post-composed with a row accumulator, and seeded by an initial vector. Each of the four triangles is a single point in this space. The space contains other points, some of them giving named triangles, others giving sequences that no one has bothered to name.

A pupil who learns the four triangles as nouns has learnt four things to remember. A pupil who learns the operator and the parameters has learnt a structure that organises all four, and that contains room for more.

What the inversion costs and what it gives

To be honest about the cost here. we have that the verb is harder than the noun. To talk about a bidiagonal operator acting on a vector requires a pupil to have met matrices, vectors, and the idea that a matrix is an action on a vector rather than a passive arrangement of numbers. That is sixth-form work in most curricula, or first-year university for some. It is not Year 9. So the pedagogical inversion I am proposing is not a wholesale replacement for the way Pascal's triangle is introduced at thirteen. It is something to layer in once the pupil has met linear algebra, and the layering is what changes their relation to the diagram they already know.

What the inversion gives is twofold.

It gives them a structure where there was only a list. The four triangles in the table above stop being four separate facts and become four points in a parametrised family. The pupil who has internalised this can ask their own questions. What if I take main-diagonal entries equal to 2k+1? What if the subdiagonal entry is k? What if I compose the Pascal operator with itself? What happens to the row sums? They have moved from the receiving end of named objects to the producing end of a structured search.

It gives them a Penrosean orientation toward the subject. Penrose's point about the equivalence class was that an object is constituted, for mathematical purposes, by its behaviour under operations. The pupil who has seen Pascal's triangle arise as the orbit of an operator has met an instance of this orientation. They have met the idea that the diagram is downstream of the rule, that the rule is the explanation, that two rules with the same behaviour produce the same diagram regardless of how they are written. That orientation transfers. It is the same orientation that, much later, will let them understand why a tensor and its array of components are the same object viewed two ways, or why two presentations of a group can present the same group, or why the equivalence-class definition of a real number through Dedekind cuts produces an object that behaves like the real number it ought to be.

What to do with this

To aid the teaching of this I have built a small browser tool that makes the operator visible. The pupil chooses a preset from Pascal, Eulerian, Delannoy, or Stirling, and sees the operator displayed as a small numeric matrix. They slide the row index n and watch the entries of the operator change for Eulerian, or stay fixed for Pascal, or trigger the accumulator for Delannoy. They edit the diagonal and subdiagonal expressions and watch the triangle change in response. They see, in the same screen, the operator, its action, and the resulting array. The diagram and the rule are presented as two views of the same object.

The longer point, and the one I want to leave with, is that the way we present mathematics to children carries a tacit answer to Penrose's question about what mathematical objects are. The textbook that hands them Pascal's triangle as a finished diagram is telling them, without saying so, that mathematical objects are things you recognise. The textbook that hands them the operator and lets them watch the triangle emerge is telling them that mathematical objects are things you act on, or things that arise from acting. These two pedagogies produce two different orientations toward the subject, and the second is closer to what working mathematicians actually do.

Penrose's eleven-year-old self was surprised that the teacher took the "3 at the top and 8 at the bottom" answer seriously. The reason the teacher took it seriously was that the answer is not foolish. It is one description of a fraction, just as the equivalence-class definition is another description, and just as "the thing that when added to itself eight times gives 3" is another. None of these descriptions is the fraction. The fraction is what they all describe.

Pascal's triangle is what the bidiagonal operator describes. The triangle is not the noun. The operator is the verb. The pedagogy that names the verb names the thing.
