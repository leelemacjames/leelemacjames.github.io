IB Physics — Topic B

Heat Transfer, Radiation & Energy Balance

Three mechanisms move thermal energy — conduction, convection, and radiation — and only one works in a vacuum. That one lights the universe.

B2 B3–4 B5 B6 B7 B8–9

What this section is about

Energy moves from hot to cold — always. The question is how. Conduction passes energy through collisions between neighbouring particles. Convection moves energy by physically transporting the hot material. Radiation sends energy as electromagnetic waves and needs no medium at all.

This learner covers slides B2–B9: microscopic vs macroscopic, Q = mcΔT and Q = mL, thermal conductivity, convection and Newton's law of cooling, black-body radiation (Wien's law, Stefan-Boltzmann), emissivity, albedo, and the planetary energy balance model. These topics bridge thermal physics, atmospheric physics, and astrophysics — they appear across multiple IB papers.

The Equation Toolkit

Q = mcΔT — temperature change. Q = mL — phase change (no ΔT).

λmaxT = 2.9 × 10⁻³ m·K — Wien's law (peak wavelength).

Albedo α = reflected/incident. Equilibrium: absorbed = emitted.

SL B2 — Microscopic, Macroscopic & Internal Energy

Microscopic: individual particle behaviour — speeds, collisions, energies. Macroscopic: bulk system properties — temperature, pressure, volume. Thermodynamics connects both levels.

Internal energy U is the total of all kinetic energies (translational, rotational, vibrational) and potential energies (intermolecular forces) of the particles in a system. For an ideal gas: U = KE only (no intermolecular PE).

Thermal equilibrium: two objects in contact with no net heat flow — they're at the same temperature. This is the zeroth law: if A is in equilibrium with C, and B is in equilibrium with C, then A and B are in equilibrium with each other. This is what makes thermometers work.

Q1A metal spoon at 20°C is placed in hot soup at 80°C. Describe the energy transfer in microscopic terms.▶
Answer

Soup molecules (high average KE) collide with spoon atoms (lower average KE) at the contact surface. In each collision, fast soup molecules transfer KE to slower spoon atoms. This continues molecule-by-molecule along the spoon (conduction) until the average KE — and hence the temperature — is equal throughout. The process stops at equilibrium because there's no net KE transfer when average energies are equal.

SL B3–4 — Specific Heat Capacity & Latent Heat

Two equations for two different situations:

Temperature changing: Q = mcΔT
Phase changing: Q = mL

Specific heat capacity c [J/(kg·K)]: the energy needed to raise 1 kg by 1 K. Water has c = 4180 J/(kg·K) — unusually high, which is why oceans moderate climate and why water is a good coolant.

Specific latent heat L [J/kg]: the energy needed to change the phase of 1 kg at constant temperature.

QuantitySymbolWater ValueNote
Specific heat (liquid)c4180 J/(kg·K)Highest of common liquids
Latent heat of fusionLf3.34 × 10⁵ J/kgIce → water at 0°C
Latent heat of vaporisationLv2.26 × 10⁶ J/kgWater → steam at 100°C

Notice Lv is almost 7× larger than Lf — breaking free of a liquid into a gas requires far more energy than breaking free of a solid into a liquid, because you're completely separating molecules rather than just loosening them.

During a phase change, temperature does NOT change. All the energy goes into breaking/forming intermolecular bonds (changing PE), not increasing KE. The flat sections on a heating curve (temperature vs time graph) are where phase changes occur. Students often assume "adding heat always raises temperature" — it doesn't during melting or boiling.

Q = mcΔT and Q = mL never apply at the same time. When temperature is changing, use mcΔT. When phase is changing (at constant T), use mL. A full heating problem (ice to steam) requires applying both alternately.

Q1Calculate the total energy needed to convert 0.50 kg of ice at −10°C to steam at 100°C. (cice = 2100 J/(kg·K))▶
Answer

Five stages:

1. Heat ice: Q₁ = mcΔT = 0.50 × 2100 × 10 = 10 500 J

2. Melt ice: Q₂ = mLf = 0.50 × 3.34 × 10⁵ = 167 000 J

3. Heat water: Q₃ = mcΔT = 0.50 × 4180 × 100 = 209 000 J

4. Boil water: Q₄ = mLv = 0.50 × 2.26 × 10⁶ = 1 130 000 J

5. Total = 10.5 + 167 + 209 + 1130 = ~1517 kJ ≈ 1.52 MJ

Note: boiling accounts for ~74% of the total. Steam burns are so dangerous because condensing steam releases this enormous Lv directly onto skin.

Q2200 g of water at 80°C is mixed with 300 g of water at 20°C. What is the final temperature? (Assume no heat loss.)▶
Answer

Heat lost by hot = heat gained by cold: m₁c(T₁ − Tf) = m₂c(Tf − T₂). The c cancels.

0.200(80 − Tf) = 0.300(Tf − 20) → 16 − 0.2Tf = 0.3Tf − 6 → 22 = 0.5Tf

Tf = 44°C. Closer to the cold water's temperature because there's more of it.

SL B5 — Thermal Conductivity

Thermal conductivity k measures how well a material conducts heat. The rate of heat flow through a slab:

ΔQ/Δt = kA(ΔT/Δx)

Where A = cross-sectional area, ΔT = temperature difference across the slab, Δx = thickness. Units of k: W/(m·K).

Materialk (W/(m·K))Type
Silver429Excellent conductor
Copper401Excellent conductor
Aluminium237Good conductor
Glass~1Poor conductor
Wood~0.15Insulator
Air0.025Excellent insulator

Good thermal conductors have free electrons that carry energy rapidly through the material. This is why good electrical conductors are also good thermal conductors — both depend on mobile electrons.

Q1A glass window (k = 1.0, thickness 5 mm, area 2.0 m²) has 20°C inside and 0°C outside. What is the rate of heat loss?▶
Answer

ΔQ/Δt = kAΔT/Δx = 1.0 × 2.0 × 20 / 0.005 = 8000 W = 8 kW.

This is enormous — equivalent to running 8 kettles. Double glazing works by adding a layer of trapped air (k = 0.025), which is 40× more insulating than glass.

SL B6 — Convection & Newton's Law of Cooling

Convection transfers heat by bulk fluid movement. Warm fluid is less dense → rises. Cool fluid is denser → sinks. This creates convection currents.

Examples: sea breezes (land heats faster than sea → air rises over land → cooler sea air flows in), radiators (hot air rises from the panel, cool air descends and is heated in turn), cumulus clouds (warm moist air rises, cools adiabatically, water condenses).

Newton's law of cooling — the rate of temperature change is proportional to the temperature difference with the surroundings:

dθ/dt ∝ (θobject − θsurroundings)

This gives exponential cooling: a hot object cools rapidly at first (large ΔT), then more and more slowly as it approaches room temperature. The same equation describes radioactive decay — and for the same mathematical reason (rate proportional to what remains).

Q1Why does blowing on hot soup cool it faster?▶
Answer

Two mechanisms. First, blowing replaces the warm, humid air layer above the soup with cooler, drier air — increasing the temperature gradient and hence the rate of convective/conductive heat loss. Second, the moving air enhances evaporation from the soup surface. Evaporation removes latent heat (Lv) from the soup, which is a very efficient cooling process. The evaporative cooling is typically the dominant effect.

SL B7 — Radiation: Black Bodies, Wien & Stefan-Boltzmann

Thermal radiation is EM radiation emitted by all objects with T > 0 K. It needs no medium — it's the only heat transfer mechanism that works in a vacuum.

A black body is a perfect emitter and absorber at all wavelengths. Real objects approximate this to varying degrees (emissivity e).

Wien's displacement law — hotter objects peak at shorter wavelengths:

λmax T = 2.9 × 10⁻³ m·K

Stefan-Boltzmann law — total radiated power scales as T⁴:

P = σAT⁴    (σ = 5.67 × 10⁻⁸ W m⁻² K⁻⁴)

The T⁴ dependence is devastating: double the temperature → 16× the power output. This is why stars that are only slightly hotter are enormously more luminous.

Intensity at distance d (inverse-square law):

I = P/(4πd²) = L/(4πd²)

Wien's law gives the peak wavelength, not the only wavelength. A black body emits at all wavelengths — the curve is continuous. Wien tells you where the peak is. A hotter body still emits in the infrared; it just emits more at shorter wavelengths. Saying "the Sun emits visible light" is incomplete — it also emits UV, IR, radio, etc. The peak is in the visible.

T must be in kelvin for Stefan-Boltzmann. P = σAT⁴ with T in °C gives nonsense (and at 0°C it gives zero, which is wrong — objects at 0°C still radiate).

Q1The Sun's surface is 5800 K. What is λmax? What part of the spectrum is this?▶
Answer

λmax = 2.9 × 10⁻³ / 5800 = 500 nm — this is green-yellow visible light. The Sun's peak emission is almost exactly in the middle of the visible spectrum. Not a coincidence: human vision evolved to exploit the most abundant photons available.

Q2Star A has surface temperature 3000 K and radius 10R☉. Star B has 12 000 K and radius R☉. Which is more luminous?▶
Answer

L = σ(4πR²)T⁴ ∝ R²T⁴.

LA/LB = (10)² × (3000)⁴ / (1)² × (12000)⁴ = 100 × (3/12)⁴ = 100 × (1/4)⁴ = 100/256 = 0.39.

Star B is more luminous despite being 10× smaller — because T⁴ wins. A 4× hotter star radiates 256× more power per unit area. Star A is a cool red giant; Star B is a hot blue main-sequence star.

Q3Earth's surface (T ≈ 288 K) radiates. What is λmax for this emission?▶
Answer

λmax = 2.9 × 10⁻³ / 288 = 10.1 μm — mid-infrared. This is exactly the spectral range where CO₂ and H₂O absorb. The greenhouse effect works because Earth's emission peak falls within the absorption bands of greenhouse gases. If Earth were hotter, its emission would shift toward shorter wavelengths and partially escape through the atmospheric "window".

SL B8–9 — Emissivity, Albedo & Planetary Energy Balance

Emissivity e: how effectively an object radiates compared to a perfect black body. A black body has e = 1. For real objects: P = eσAT⁴.

SurfaceeNote
Black body (theoretical)1.00Perfect emitter
Ice0.97Near-perfect IR emitter
Glass0.95
Brick0.90
Polished metal~0.05Poor emitter, good reflector

Albedo α: fraction of incoming solar radiation reflected by a surface. High albedo = reflective (ice ≈ 0.8), low albedo = absorptive (ocean ≈ 0.06, dark soil ≈ 0.1).

α = reflected power / incident power

Solar constant: S ≈ 1360 W/m² — the intensity of solar radiation at Earth's distance (1 AU).

Planetary energy balance: at equilibrium, absorbed power = emitted power.

Absorbed: Pin = (1 − α) × S × πR²E

Emitted: Pout = eσ(4πR²E)T⁴surface

πR² is the cross-sectional area intercepting sunlight; 4πR² is the total surface area emitting radiation. Setting Pin = Pout and solving for T gives the equilibrium surface temperature. Without a greenhouse effect, this gives ~255 K (−18°C). The observed ~288 K (15°C) shows the atmosphere adds ~33 K of warming.

The 33 K "greenhouse warming" is a model result, not a direct measurement. It compares the observed temperature (288 K) with the temperature a bare rock at Earth's orbit would have (255 K). The model assumes uniform temperature, perfect sphericity, and a single albedo value — all simplifications. At what point does a simplified model stop being "approximately right" and become "misleadingly simple"? Climate scientists use GCMs (general circulation models) with millions of grid cells to address this. The IB asks you to use the simple model and know its limits.

Q1Earth's average albedo is 0.29. Calculate the equilibrium temperature without a greenhouse effect. (e = 1, S = 1360 W/m²)▶
Answer

(1 − α)SπR² = σ(4πR²)T⁴. Cancel πR²:

T⁴ = (1 − α)S / (4σ) = 0.71 × 1360 / (4 × 5.67 × 10⁻⁸) = 965.6 / 2.268 × 10⁻⁷ = 4.258 × 10⁹

T = (4.258 × 10⁹)¼ = 255 K = −18°C.

Earth's actual surface temperature is 288 K (15°C). The 33 K difference is the greenhouse warming.

Q2Why does the model use πR² for absorption but 4πR² for emission?▶
Answer

The Earth intercepts sunlight over its cross-sectional disc (area = πR²) — this is the shadow it casts. But it emits radiation from its entire surface (area = 4πR²) — all sides radiate, day and night, toward and away from the Sun. The factor of 4 between these is why the effective solar input per unit area is S/4 ≈ 340 W/m² (the incident 1360 W/m² spread over 4× the area).

Q3Mars has S ≈ 590 W/m² and albedo ~0.25. Estimate its equilibrium temperature.▶
Answer

T⁴ = (1 − 0.25) × 590 / (4 × 5.67 × 10⁻⁸) = 442.5 / 2.268 × 10⁻⁷ = 1.951 × 10⁹

T = (1.951 × 10⁹)¼ = 210 K = −63°C.

Mars's observed average surface temperature is ~210 K — very close to the bare-rock prediction because Mars has almost no greenhouse effect (thin CO₂ atmosphere, ~0.6% of Earth's pressure).

Summary: the three heat transfer mechanisms

Conduction

Particle-to-particle collisions. Needs contact. Best in metals (free electrons). Rate ∝ kAΔT/Δx.

Convection

Bulk fluid movement. Needs a fluid. Driven by density differences (hot rises). Newton's cooling law.

Radiation

EM waves. No medium needed. All objects emit. P = eσAT⁴. Dominates at astronomical scales.
