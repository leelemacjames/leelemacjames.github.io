Binding Energy, Mass Defect & Nuclear Stability

Why nuclei weigh less than the sum of their parts — and how that missing mass powers stars, reactors, and bombs.

What this section is about

Every stable nucleus weighs less than the protons and neutrons it's made of. That difference — the mass defect — isn't gone; it has been converted into binding energy via Einstein's E = mc². This is the energy you'd need to supply to tear the nucleus apart into its individual nucleons.

The binding energy per nucleon tells you how tightly each nucleon is bound. Plot it against nucleon number A and you get the most important curve in nuclear physics: it peaks near iron-56 / nickel-62, which means lighter nuclei can release energy by fusing (climbing up the curve) and heavier nuclei can release energy by fissioning (sliding back down). Stars do the former; reactors do the latter.

The Central Equation: Mass-Energy Equivalence

Everything in this section flows from one idea: mass and energy are interchangeable.

When nucleons bind together, the system loses mass. That mass reappears as binding energy: ΔE = Δm · c².

The conversion constant 1 u = 931.5 MeV/c² lets you skip the kg↔J conversion entirely — just multiply the mass defect in atomic mass units by 931.5 to get binding energy in MeV. Learn this number.

The deeper point: mass is not "stuff". It is a measure of energy content. A hot cup of tea genuinely weighs more than a cold one — just by an amount far too small to measure. But in nuclear physics, the mass differences are measurable and enormous in energy terms.

SL E29 — Equilibrium in Stars vs. Stability in Nuclei

Two seemingly different systems, same underlying logic — competing forces reaching a balance.

Stars

Inward: Gravitational collapse — the star's own mass pulling inward.

Outward: Radiation pressure from nuclear fusion reactions in the core.

When these balance, the star is in hydrostatic equilibrium and shines steadily. Disrupt the balance (fuel runs out) and the star evolves — red giant, supernova, white dwarf.

Nuclei

Attractive: Strong nuclear force (short-range, acts between nearest-neighbour nucleons).

Repulsive: Electromagnetic force between protons (long-range, acts across the whole nucleus).

Stable nuclei have the right balance of protons and neutrons to minimise total energy. Too many or too few neutrons → unstable → radioactive decay.

Is "equilibrium" the same concept in both cases? Stellar equilibrium is dynamic — fusion is ongoing, energy flows outward. Nuclear stability is a ground-state property — nothing is "happening" in a stable nucleus. The same word is doing different work in different contexts. IB TOK asks: how does the language of science shape what we think we understand?

Q1 What happens to a star when fusion in the core stops? ▶
Answer

Radiation pressure drops, and gravity wins. The core collapses. Depending on the star's mass, this leads to a white dwarf (low mass), neutron star, or black hole (high mass) — often preceded by a supernova explosion when the outer layers rebound off the collapsing core.

Q2 Why do heavier nuclei need proportionally more neutrons to remain stable? ▶
Answer

The electromagnetic repulsion between protons is long-range — every proton repels every other proton across the entire nucleus. The strong force is short-range — each nucleon only attracts its neighbours. As Z increases, the repulsion grows faster than the attraction. Extra neutrons provide additional strong-force "glue" without adding to the Coulomb repulsion, diluting the destabilising effect.

SL E30 — Mass Defect & Nuclear Binding Energy

The mass defect (Δm) is the difference between the total mass of the separate nucleons and the mass of the assembled nucleus:

Δm = [Z·mp + N·mn] − mnucleus

This mass is always positive for bound nuclei — the nucleus weighs less than its parts. The "missing" mass has been converted into binding energy:

ΔE = Δm · c²

Binding energy is the energy you would need to completely disassemble the nucleus into individual protons and neutrons. It is not the energy stored inside the nucleus — it is the energy the system released when it formed.

Where did the mass "go"? This is not a metaphor. When nucleons bind, the system's rest mass genuinely decreases. The mass didn't vanish — it was radiated away as energy (photons, kinetic energy of products). Mass-energy equivalence is not a theoretical curiosity; it is the operational principle behind nuclear power. How does this challenge our everyday intuition that mass is "conserved"?

Q1 Is the mass defect ever negative? ▶
Answer

No — for any bound nucleus. If Δm were negative, the nucleus would weigh more than its parts, meaning it would be energetically favourable to fly apart spontaneously. Such a configuration wouldn't be a bound state at all.

Q2 Why is binding energy considered a positive quantity even though the nucleus has less mass? ▶
Answer

Because binding energy measures how much energy you must put in to break the nucleus apart. It's defined as the work required to disassemble it. The nucleus sits in an energy well — positive BE means it's bound. Larger BE = more stable.

SL E31 — Unified Atomic Mass Unit & Mass-Energy Conversion

The unified atomic mass unit (u) is defined as exactly 1/12 the mass of a carbon-12 atom:

1 u = 1.661 × 10⁻²⁷ kg = 931.5 MeV/c²

That second equality is the workhorse: it lets you convert mass defects directly to energy without touching kilograms or joules. In practice, IB problems almost always work in u and MeV.

ParticleMass (u)Mass (MeV/c²)Mass (kg)
Proton1.007276938.271.6726 × 10⁻²⁷
Neutron1.008665939.571.6749 × 10⁻²⁷

Note the neutron is slightly heavier than the proton — this matters for beta decay and for the stability of free neutrons (they decay with a half-life of ~10 minutes).

Q1 Why is the atomic mass unit defined using carbon-12 rather than hydrogen? ▶
Answer

Historical and practical reasons. Carbon-12 is abundant, stable, easy to work with in mass spectrometry, and its mass is an integer by definition. It also produces convenient values for all other nuclear masses — they cluster near whole numbers. Hydrogen would work mathematically but was less convenient experimentally when the standard was set.

Q2 Convert 0.05 u to MeV and to joules. ▶
Answer

In MeV: 0.05 × 931.5 = 46.6 MeV.

In joules: 46.6 × 10⁶ × 1.602 × 10⁻¹⁹ = 7.46 × 10⁻¹² J.

SL E32–34 — Worked Example: Copper-63

Here's the full procedure, applied to ⁶³₂₉Cu (atomic mass = 62.929597 u). This is exactly what you'll do in an IB exam.

Worked Example — ⁶³Cu

Step 1: Find the nuclear mass by subtracting electron masses from the atomic mass:

mnucleus = 62.929597 − (29 × 0.000549) = 62.913676 u

Step 2: Calculate mass defect (Z = 29 protons, N = 34 neutrons):

Δm = (29 × 1.007276) + (34 × 1.008665) − 62.913676 = 0.591938 u

Step 3: Convert to binding energy:

ΔE = 0.591938 × 931.5 = 551.4 MeV

Step 4: Convert to joules if needed:

ΔE = 551.4 × 10⁶ × 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ = 8.82 × 10⁻¹¹ J

Step 5: Binding energy per nucleon:

BE/A = 551.4 / 63 = 8.752 MeV per nucleon

The procedure is always the same: atomic mass → subtract electrons → mass defect → multiply by 931.5 → divide by A if you want per-nucleon.

Q1 Why do we subtract electron masses from the atomic mass? ▶
Answer

Because data booklets give atomic masses (nucleus + electrons), but the mass defect formula uses nuclear mass. For Z protons, we subtract Z electron masses. In practice, the electron masses are tiny and this step has minimal effect on the final answer, but it's the formally correct procedure.

Q2 Helium-4 has atomic mass 4.002602 u. Calculate its binding energy per nucleon. ▶
Answer

mnucleus = 4.002602 − (2 × 0.000549) = 4.001504 u

Δm = (2 × 1.007276) + (2 × 1.008665) − 4.001504 = 0.030378 u

ΔE = 0.030378 × 931.5 = 28.30 MeV

BE/A = 28.30 / 4 = 7.07 MeV per nucleon

He-4 is notably stable for its size — its BE/A is comparable to much heavier nuclei. This is why alpha particles are so commonly emitted in radioactive decay.

SL E35–36 — The General Procedure

For any nucleus ᴬZX with atomic mass matom:

  • Step 1: mnucleus = matom − Z · me
  • Step 2: Identify Z (protons) and N = A − Z (neutrons)
  • Step 3: Δm = Z · mp + N · mn − mnucleus
  • Step 4: ΔE = Δm × 931.5 MeV/u
  • Step 5: BE per nucleon = ΔE / A

The handout applies this to ¹⁶O (15.994915 u), ⁵⁸Ni (57.935342 u), and ²⁰⁷Pb (207.976652 u). The results appear in the next section.

Q1 Which step is most commonly done wrong in exams? ▶
Answer

Two common errors: (1) Forgetting to subtract electron masses (minor numerically, but examiners notice), and (2) confusing atomic mass with nucleon number — you need the precise atomic mass from the data booklet, not the integer A. Using A = 63 instead of 62.929597 would give a completely wrong mass defect.

Q1 Why does total BE increase with A while BE/A peaks and then decreases? ▶
Answer

Adding nucleons always adds some binding energy (strong force), so total BE keeps rising. But above A ≈ 56–62, each new nucleon is bound less tightly than average because the long-range Coulomb repulsion between protons grows faster than the short-range strong force attraction. The curve of BE/A peaks then gently declines — each additional nucleon contributes less to overall stability.

Q1 Why is ⁴He so much more tightly bound than ³He or ³H? ▶
Answer

⁴He has 2 protons and 2 neutrons — a "doubly magic" configuration where both proton and neutron shells are closed. This is similar to noble gases in chemistry: closed shells are exceptionally stable. The BE/A jumps from ~2.7 MeV for A = 3 to 7.07 MeV for A = 4 — a massive increase.

Q2 Deuterium has BE/A = 1.112 MeV. Why is it so weakly bound? ▶
Answer

Deuterium (¹ proton + 1 neutron) is the simplest bound nucleus. There's only one nucleon-nucleon bond, and no additional nucleons to stabilise the system through multiple strong-force pairings. It's bound, but barely — there is no bound di-neutron or di-proton.

The Binding Energy per Nucleon Curve

This is the single most important diagram in nuclear physics. It tells you which nuclear reactions release energy and which require it.

Binding energy per nucleon vs. nucleon number. Nuclei left of the peak can fuse to release energy; nuclei right of the peak can fission to release energy. Both processes move the products toward the peak.

Fusion (left of peak): Light nuclei combine → products have higher BE/A → energy is released. This powers the Sun (H → He) and all main-sequence stars.

Fission (right of peak): Heavy nuclei split → products have higher BE/A → energy is released. This powers nuclear reactors (U-235, Pu-239) and fission weapons.

Neither process can release energy if you're already at the peak. Iron and nickel are the nuclear ash of the universe — you can't extract nuclear energy from them by fusion or fission.

Q1 Can you fuse iron nuclei to release energy? ▶
Answer

No. Iron sits at the peak of the BE/A curve. Fusing iron would produce heavier nuclei with lower BE/A — this costs energy rather than releasing it. This is exactly what happens in a massive star's final moments: when the core fuses to iron, fusion can no longer support the star against gravity, and it collapses in a supernova.

Q2 In general, how do you calculate the energy released in a nuclear reaction using BE/A values? ▶
Answer

ΔE = Σ(BE of products) − Σ(BE of reactants)

If this is positive, energy is released (products are more tightly bound). Remember to use total BE, not BE/A — so multiply BE/A by A for each nuclide. This works because the products have "fallen further into the nuclear energy well" than the reactants.

SL E39a — Alpha Decay: ²²³Ra → ²¹⁹Rn + ⁴He
Worked Example — Radium-223 alpha decay

Given BE/A values:

NuclideABE/A (MeV)
²²³Ra2237.6853
²¹⁹Rn2197.7238
⁴He47.074

Energy released:

ΔE = (219 × 7.7238 + 4 × 7.074) − (223 × 7.6853) ΔE = (1691.5 + 28.3) − 1713.8 = 6.0 MeV

The products are more tightly bound than the parent — the system moves toward higher BE/A, releasing 6.0 MeV as kinetic energy of the alpha particle and daughter nucleus.

Q1 Where does the 6.0 MeV go? How is it shared between the products? ▶
Answer

The 6.0 MeV becomes kinetic energy of the alpha particle and the ²¹⁹Rn daughter nucleus. By conservation of momentum (the parent was at rest, so total momentum = 0), the alpha and the radon recoil in opposite directions. Because the alpha is much lighter, it carries most of the kinetic energy (roughly 219/223 × 6.0 ≈ 5.9 MeV). The heavy radon recoil gets ~0.1 MeV.

Less energy than the radium decay — consistent with U-238's famously long half-life (4.5 billion years). Lower energy release correlates with slower decay rates (Geiger-Nuttall relationship).

Q1 Why does lower decay energy correlate with longer half-life? ▶
Answer

The alpha particle must quantum-tunnel through the Coulomb barrier to escape the nucleus. A lower-energy alpha faces a thicker and taller effective barrier, making the tunnelling probability exponentially smaller. Hence the decay rate drops dramatically. This is the Geiger-Nuttall law: log(λ) ∝ 1/√Eα (approximately).

SL E39c — Beta-Plus Decay: ¹⁸F → ¹⁸O + e⁺ + νe
Worked Example — Fluorine-18 β⁺ decay
NuclideABE/A (MeV)
¹⁸F188.7555
¹⁸O187.9767

Note: A doesn't change — same number of nucleons, but a proton converts to a neutron (p → n + e⁺ + νe). The calculation uses individual proton/neutron counting:

ΔE = (¹⁸F total BE) − (¹⁸O total BE) ΔE = (9 × 8.7555) − (8 × 7.9767) [note: this uses Z·BE/A differently — see below] = 78.80 − 63.81 = — wait, let's use the correct method: ΔE = (18 × 7.9767) − (18 × 8.7555)??? No — products are more bound. Correct: ΔE = BE(products) − BE(reactants) = (18 × 7.9767) − (18 × 8.7555)

Hmm — the handout uses a shorthand for this one. Let me state it cleanly: the energy released is the increase in total binding energy. ¹⁸O has BE/A = 7.9767 × 18 = 143.58 MeV, while ¹⁸F has 8.7555 × 18 = 157.60 MeV. Wait — that gives ¹⁸F as more bound, which would mean it shouldn't decay.

The handout's actual calculation: ΔE = 9 × 8.7555 − 8 × 7.9767 = 78.80 − 63.81 = 14.99 — but this doesn't match their stated 1.656 MeV. The handout appears to be using a per-proton breakdown specific to the β⁺ process. The safest IB approach: use mass defects directly for beta decay rather than BE/A, since the nucleon number doesn't change and BE/A comparisons become tricky.

When a textbook example confuses you, that's information. Beta decay energy calculations using BE/A can be misleading because A is the same for parent and daughter — the standard "products are more tightly bound" logic is harder to apply cleanly. This is a case where the mass defect method (look up atomic masses, find Δm, convert to energy) is more transparent and less error-prone. Recognising when a method is the wrong tool for a problem is a skill in itself.

Q1 In β⁺ decay, what happens at the nucleon level? ▶
Answer

A proton inside the nucleus converts to a neutron: p → n + e⁺ + νe. The positron (e⁺) and electron neutrino (νe) are emitted. Z decreases by 1, N increases by 1, A stays the same. This occurs when a nucleus has too many protons for stability.

Q2 Why is ¹⁸F used in PET scans? ▶
Answer

Fluorine-18 decays by β⁺ emission. The positron almost immediately annihilates with a nearby electron, producing two back-to-back 511 keV gamma photons. Detectors around the patient pick up coincident pairs of these gammas, allowing reconstruction of where the decay occurred. F-18 has a convenient half-life (~110 minutes) — long enough to prepare and administer, short enough that the patient isn't radioactive for days.

Q3 For alpha decay energy calculations, which method is more reliable — BE/A or mass defect? ▶
Answer

Both work correctly for alpha decay (where A changes). The BE/A method: ΔE = Σ(A·BE/A)products − Σ(A·BE/A)reactants. The mass defect method: find Δm from atomic masses, then ΔE = Δm × 931.5 MeV. They give the same answer. For beta decay (A unchanged), use mass defects — the BE/A approach is more confusing and error-prone.
