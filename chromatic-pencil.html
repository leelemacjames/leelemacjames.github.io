The Chromatic Pencil

Every rational slope through (−1, 0) strikes the unit circle at a rational point — and every rational point encodes a Pythagorean triple. Colour-code the triples by their homogeneous coordinates, and the projective circle becomes a chromatic map of number theory.

The Construction

Rational Points from a Pencil of Lines

Consider the pencil of lines through (−1, 0) on the unit circle x² + y² = 1. A line with rational slope m = p/q intersects the circle at a second point with coordinates:

(x, y) = ( 1 − m² 1 + m² ,  2m 1 + m² )
Rational parametrisation of the unit circle

Clearing denominators with m = p/q gives the Pythagorean triple [q²−p², 2pq, q²+p²]. These are homogeneous coordinates in ℙ² — and we assign each triple an RGB colour by mapping its three components to the red, green, and blue channels, normalised to the hypotenuse.

Three structures unified

The pencil (sheaf of lines through a point) is the projective structure. The Pythagorean triple is the number-theoretic content. The colour is the homogeneous coordinate made visible. Select any four triples to compute the cross-ratio of their slopes — the projective invariant of the pencil.

The Colour Key

The Chromatic Triangle of Homogeneous Coordinates

Before reading the interactive diagram, recall how homogeneous coordinates map to colour. Each Pythagorean triple [a, b, c] is a point in projective space ℙ², and we assign its three components to the RGB channels. The chromatic triangle shows the pure cases at the vertices and the mixtures between them:

In the interactive diagram below, each Pythagorean triple [a, b, c] maps its first leg to red, second leg to green, and hypotenuse to blue, normalised so the brightest component is at full intensity. Since c (the hypotenuse) is always the largest component, all triples carry a blue tint — but the relative strength of the red and green channels encodes the shape of the triangle.

Reading the palette

Red-dominant → first leg a is large relative to b (flat, wide triangles).
Green-dominant → second leg b is large relative to a (tall, narrow triangles).
Yellow-ish → a ≈ b (near-isosceles).
Blue-violet → one leg is very small (very thin triangles, nearly degenerate).

Reading the Palette

What the Colours Encode

Each Pythagorean triple [a, b, c] is mapped directly to RGB as (a, b, c), normalised so the largest component is at full brightness. This is the chromatic triangle construction applied to number theory: the triple is the homogeneous coordinate, and the colour is its projective ratio made visible.

Triples with a dominant first leg appear reddish. A dominant second leg gives green. The hypotenuse (always the largest) drives the blue channel. Near-isosceles triples (a ≈ b) blend yellow-green. Very thin triples (one tiny leg) tend toward blue-violet.

Crucially, the colour is projective: scaling the triple [a, b, c] → [ka, kb, kc] does not change the chromaticity. Primitive triples and their multiples share the same hue — the colour sees only the ratios, as homogeneous coordinates should.

The cross-ratio of slopes

When you select four points on the circle, you are selecting four lines of the pencil through (−1, 0). Their slopes m₁, m₂, m₃, m₄ define a cross-ratio ξ(m₁, m₂, m₃, m₄) which is invariant under any projective (Möbius) transformation of the circle. The cross-ratio of the slopes equals the cross-ratio of the four points viewed as elements of ℙ¹ — because the pencil is a ℙ¹ parametrised by slope.

Synthesis

Where Everything Meets

A pencil of rational lines through a point on the circle generates Pythagorean triples as homogeneous coordinates, colours them by their projective ratios, and measures their mutual arrangement by the cross-ratio — the invariant that survives when all metric structure is stripped away.
