01Time Dilation

Consider two observers: O' rides inside a moving vehicle, and O stands on the platform watching it pass. A light pulse bounces vertically inside the vehicle. For O', the light travels straight up and down over a distance \(2d\). For O, the vehicle has moved, so the light traces a longer diagonal path. Since both must measure \(c\) for the speed of light, the time O records must be longer than the time O' records.
The light-clock thought experiment: O measures a longer path than O'.
Time Dilation
\[\Delta t = \gamma\,\Delta t_p = \frac{\Delta t_p}{\sqrt{1 - \dfrac{v^2}{c^2}}}\]
Proper time \(\Delta t_p\) — the time interval measured by the observer who sees both events occur at the same point in space. This is always the shortest measured interval.
Lorentz factor — \(\gamma = \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{1 - v^2/c^2}}\). Since \(v < c\), the denominator is less than 1, so \(\gamma > 1\) always. At everyday speeds, \(\gamma \approx 1\).
Deriving the formula is pure Pythagoras. In O's frame the light travels the hypotenuse of a right triangle with vertical leg \(d\) and horizontal leg \(v\Delta t/2\). Setting the hypotenuse equal to \(c\Delta t/2\) and solving for \(\Delta t\) in terms of \(\Delta t_p = 2d/c\) gives the result above.
Q1 A muon is created in the upper atmosphere travelling at \(0.98c\) relative to Earth. In its own rest frame, its half-life is \(2.2\;\mu\text{s}\). What half-life does an Earth-based observer measure? ▼
The muon's rest frame measures the proper time. Calculate \(\gamma\) at \(v = 0.98c\) first.
\(\gamma = \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{1-0.98^2}} = \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{1-0.9604}} = \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{0.0396}} = \dfrac{1}{0.199} \approx 5.03\)

\(\Delta t = \gamma\,\Delta t_p = 5.03 \times 2.2 = 11.1\;\mu\text{s}\)

The Earth observer measures a half-life of approximately 11 μs, roughly five times longer. This is why muons created at altitude actually reach sea level in detectable numbers, despite their short proper lifetime.
Q2 Explain why the observer at rest with respect to a clock always measures the shortest time interval between two events occurring at that clock. ▼
Think about what "proper" means geometrically: both events happen at the same spatial location for this observer.
For the observer at rest with the clock, both events (e.g. emission and return of a light pulse) occur at the same point in space. The light path is purely vertical (shortest possible path). Any other observer, for whom the clock is moving, sees the light travel a diagonal path. Since light speed is invariant at \(c\), the longer path demands a longer time. The factor \(\gamma \geq 1\) quantifies exactly this increase.
Q3 A spacecraft clock ticks once every second in its own frame. It passes Earth at \(0.80c\). How many seconds elapse on Earth between successive ticks of the spacecraft clock? ▼
\(\Delta t_p = 1\;\text{s}\). Find \(\gamma\) at \(v=0.80c\): you should get \(\gamma = 5/3\).
\(\gamma = \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{1-0.64}} = \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{0.36}} = \dfrac{1}{0.6} = \dfrac{5}{3} \approx 1.667\)

\(\Delta t = \gamma \times 1\;\text{s} = 1.67\;\text{s}\)

Earth observers see each tick of the moving clock take 1.67 s. The moving clock runs slow.

02The Lorentz Factor γ

At everyday speeds, \(\gamma\) is indistinguishable from 1. At \(v = 0.1c\), \(\gamma \approx 1.005\). The effect only becomes pronounced above about \(0.5c\), where \(\gamma = 1.155\). Near the speed of light, \(\gamma\) diverges: at \(0.99c\), \(\gamma \approx 7.09\); at \(0.999c\), \(\gamma \approx 22.4\).
γ vs v/c. The curve is nearly flat until about 0.5c, then rises steeply.
Q4 Show that for \(v \ll c\), the Lorentz factor can be approximated as \(\gamma \approx 1 + \frac{v^2}{2c^2}\). Why does this matter physically? ▼
Use the binomial expansion \((1-x)^{-1/2} \approx 1 + \tfrac{1}{2}x\) for small \(x\).
Set \(x = v^2/c^2\). For \(v \ll c\), \(x \ll 1\):

\(\gamma = (1-x)^{-1/2} \approx 1 + \tfrac{1}{2}x = 1 + \dfrac{v^2}{2c^2}\)

This shows that relativistic corrections are second-order in \(v/c\). At \(v = 100\;\text{m/s}\) (fast car), \(v^2/2c^2 \approx 5.6 \times 10^{-15}\), utterly negligible. This is why Newtonian mechanics works perfectly at human scales. The approximation also appears when deriving relativistic kinetic energy: \((\gamma - 1)mc^2 \approx \tfrac{1}{2}mv^2\) recovers the Newtonian expression.
Q5 At what speed is \(\gamma = 2\)? Express your answer as a fraction of \(c\). ▼
Set \(\gamma = 2\) and solve for \(v/c\). You should end up with \(v^2/c^2 = 3/4\).
\(2 = \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{1 - v^2/c^2}}\)

\(\sqrt{1-v^2/c^2} = \tfrac{1}{2}\), so \(1-v^2/c^2 = \tfrac{1}{4}\), giving \(v^2/c^2 = \tfrac{3}{4}\).

\(v = \dfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2}\,c \approx 0.866\,c\)

At 86.6% of the speed of light, clocks run at half speed and lengths contract to half. A good benchmark to memorise.

03Experimental Evidence

Time dilation is not a mathematical curiosity. It has been measured directly in multiple ways. In 1972, Hafele and Keating flew caesium clocks around the world on commercial jets and compared them with ground-based clocks; the discrepancies matched the relativistic predictions (with a general-relativistic gravitational contribution too). More strikingly, muon experiments at CERN and in cosmic-ray physics provide a clean particle-physics test.
Muons produced in the upper atmosphere at around \(0.998c\) have a rest-frame half-life of \(2.2\;\mu\text{s}\). Without relativity, they would travel roughly \(660\;\text{m}\) before half decayed. Yet they are detected in large numbers at sea level, some 10 km below their point of creation. Time dilation (from the Earth's frame) or equivalently length contraction (from the muon's frame) explains this: both perspectives give the same observable result.
Q6 A muon travels at \(0.995c\). (a) Calculate \(\gamma\). (b) In the Earth frame, how far does the muon travel in one proper half-life? (c) From the muon's frame, explain how the muon reaches sea level using length contraction. ▼
For (a), compute \(1 - 0.995^2\) carefully. For (b), use Earth-frame dilated time. For (c), contract the atmospheric depth from the muon's perspective.
(a) \(\gamma = \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{1-0.990025}} = \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{0.009975}} = \dfrac{1}{0.0999} \approx 10.0\)

(b) Dilated half-life: \(\Delta t = 10.0 \times 2.2\;\mu\text{s} = 22\;\mu\text{s}\). Distance in Earth frame: \(d = v\Delta t = 0.995 \times 3 \times 10^8 \times 22 \times 10^{-6} \approx 6600\;\text{m}\).

(c) In the muon's rest frame, the atmosphere rushes past at \(0.995c\). The 10 km atmospheric depth is length-contracted to \(L = L_p/\gamma = 10{,}000/10 = 1{,}000\;\text{m}\). At \(0.995c\), the muon traverses this in about \(3.4\;\mu\text{s}\), well within its proper half-life. Both frames agree: the muon reaches the ground.

04The Twin Paradox

Speedo travels to Planet X (20 light-years away) at \(0.95c\), then returns immediately. Goslo stays on Earth. When Speedo returns, Goslo has aged 42 years but Speedo only 13. The "paradox" is: since motion is relative, why can't Speedo say that Goslo was the one moving?
The resolution is that the situation is not symmetric. Speedo must decelerate, turn around, and re-accelerate. He changes inertial frames. Goslo remains in a single inertial frame throughout. Special relativity's time-dilation formula applies in Goslo's frame, and the result is unambiguous: the twin who accelerates ages less.
Q7 Verify the twin paradox numbers: Speedo travels 20 ly each way at \(0.95c\). Show that Goslo ages 42 years and Speedo ages approximately 13 years. ▼
In Goslo's frame, the total distance is 40 ly at \(0.95c\). Calculate total time, then apply \(\gamma\) to find Speedo's proper time.
In Goslo's frame: total distance = \(2 \times 20 = 40\;\text{ly}\). Travel time = \(\dfrac{40}{0.95} = 42.1\;\text{years}\).

\(\gamma = \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{1-0.9025}} = \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{0.0975}} = \dfrac{1}{0.3122} = 3.203\)

Speedo's proper time: \(\Delta t_p = \dfrac{\Delta t}{\gamma} = \dfrac{42.1}{3.203} = 13.1\;\text{years}\).

So Goslo ages 42 years, Speedo ages ~13 years. The asymmetry is real: Speedo switched frames, Goslo did not.
Q8 Explain why the twin paradox is not actually a paradox. What breaks the symmetry between the two observers? ▼
Focus on the word "inertial". Which twin remains in a single inertial frame?
Special relativity's time-dilation formula applies between inertial reference frames (constant velocity, no acceleration). Goslo stays in a single inertial frame for the entire experiment. Speedo must accelerate to leave Earth, decelerate and reverse at Planet X, and decelerate again on return. He occupies at least three different inertial frames. Because Speedo is the one who changes frames, the symmetry is broken. There is a definite, measurable answer: the accelerating twin ages less. A full treatment using either acceleration calculations or the spacetime interval confirms this.

05Length Contraction

Length contraction is the spatial counterpart of time dilation. An object moving relative to an observer is measured to be shorter along the direction of motion than its rest length. The effect applies only along the direction of motion; perpendicular dimensions are unaffected.
Length Contraction
\[L = \frac{L_p}{\gamma} = L_p\sqrt{1 - \frac{v^2}{c^2}}\]
Proper length \(L_p\) — the length of an object measured in the rest frame of the object, i.e. by an observer for whom both endpoints remain fixed in space.
Note the structural difference between proper time and proper length. Proper time is measured by the observer who sees both events at the same place. Proper length is measured by the observer who sees both endpoints at rest. These are generally different observers.
An object at rest has proper length Lₚ. In a frame where it moves at v, its measured length is Lₚ/γ.
Q9 A spacecraft has a proper length of 120 m. It passes a space station at \(0.90c\). What length does the station crew measure for the spacecraft? ▼
The spacecraft is moving relative to the station, so the station measures the contracted length. Compute \(\gamma\) at \(0.90c\).
\(\gamma = \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{1-0.81}} = \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{0.19}} = \dfrac{1}{0.4359} = 2.294\)

\(L = \dfrac{L_p}{\gamma} = \dfrac{120}{2.294} = 52.3\;\text{m}\)

The station crew measures the spacecraft as only 52 m long, less than half its rest length.
Q10 A metre stick moves past you at \(0.60c\). (a) What length do you measure? (b) Does the metre stick appear thinner or shorter (or both)? ▼
Contraction only occurs along the direction of motion. Perpendicular dimensions are unchanged.
(a) \(\gamma = 1/\sqrt{1-0.36} = 1/\sqrt{0.64} = 1/0.8 = 1.25\). \(L = 1.00/1.25 = 0.80\;\text{m}\).

(b) Only shorter along the direction of motion. Its width and height are unchanged. Length contraction is a purely longitudinal effect.

06Spacetime Diagrams & Light Cones

A spacetime diagram plots \(ct\) on the vertical axis and position \(x\) on the horizontal. Using \(ct\) (rather than \(t\)) gives both axes the same dimension (length), and light travels at 45° lines. A trajectory through spacetime is a world-line. An object at rest traces a vertical line; an object moving at constant velocity traces a straight line with slope \(> 1\) (since \(v < c\)).
Rotating the 45° light lines about the \(ct\)-axis sweeps out a light cone. Events inside your future light cone are causally accessible. Events outside it cannot be reached or influenced, because reaching them would require faster-than-light travel.
Spacetime diagram for the twin paradox. Goslo's world-line is vertical; Speedo's bends at the turnaround.
Q11 On a spacetime diagram, why must all massive particles have world-lines with slopes steeper than 45° (measured from the \(x\)-axis)? ▼
A 45° line corresponds to \(v = c\). What does a shallower slope imply about the speed?
On a \(ct\) vs \(x\) diagram, slope = \(\Delta(ct)/\Delta x = c/v\). For light, \(v = c\), so slope = 1 (i.e. 45°). For \(v < c\), the slope is greater than 1 (steeper than 45°). A slope less than 1 would mean \(v > c\), which is forbidden for massive particles. Hence all massive-particle world-lines must remain inside the light cone, with slopes steeper than 45°.
Q12 Two events occur: event A at the origin, and event B at \(x = 5\;\text{ly}\), \(ct = 3\;\text{ly}\). Are these events causally connected? Could a signal from A reach B? ▼
Compute the spacetime interval \(s^2 = (c\Delta t)^2 - (\Delta x)^2\). The sign tells you: positive = timelike (causal), zero = lightlike, negative = spacelike (no causal connection).
\(s^2 = (c\Delta t)^2 - (\Delta x)^2 = 3^2 - 5^2 = 9 - 25 = -16\;\text{ly}^2\)

The interval is spacelike (\(s^2 < 0\)). Event B lies outside event A's light cone. No signal travelling at or below \(c\) can connect them. They are not causally connected. To reach B from A would require a speed of \(5/3 \approx 1.67\) times the speed of light.

07Proper Time vs Proper Length

A common IB exam trap: proper time and proper length are measured by different observers in the same scenario. The observer who measures proper time sees both events happen at the same place. The observer who measures proper length sees both endpoints of the object at rest. In a typical muon problem, the muon's rest frame gives the proper time (birth and decay happen at the same place for the muon) and the proper length of the atmosphere (the atmosphere is at rest relative to Earth, so Earth gives \(L_p\)).
Q13 A pion is created and decays 25 m from its creation point as measured in the lab. In the pion's rest frame, it lives for \(2.6 \times 10^{-8}\;\text{s}\). (a) Identify who measures the proper time and who measures the proper length. (b) Find the pion's speed. ▼
The pion's rest frame measures proper time (birth and decay at the same spatial point). The 25 m is the lab-frame distance. Use \(d = v \gamma \Delta t_p\) or work from the lab-frame time.
(a) The pion's rest frame measures proper time \(\Delta t_p = 2.6 \times 10^{-8}\;\text{s}\) (both events at the same place for the pion). The lab measures the proper length of the distance between creation and detection points (these endpoints are fixed in the lab).

(b) In the lab frame, the pion travels \(d = 25\;\text{m}\) in time \(\Delta t = \gamma \Delta t_p\). Also \(d = v\Delta t = v\gamma \Delta t_p\).

\(25 = v \cdot \dfrac{2.6 \times 10^{-8}}{\sqrt{1-v^2/c^2}}\)

Squaring: \(625(1-v^2/c^2) = v^2(2.6 \times 10^{-8})^2\)
\(625 = v^2\left(\dfrac{(2.6\times10^{-8})^2 \cdot c^2 + 625}{c^2}\right)\)

Solving numerically: \(v \approx 0.95c\).
