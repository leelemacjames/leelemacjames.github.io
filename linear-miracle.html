The Linear Miracleand where it breaks

A nonzero determinant guarantees injectivity for linear maps. For polynomial maps, that guarantee fails, and here is the exact place it does.

The miracle we take for granted

A map is injective, one-to-one, when no two inputs share an output. It can then be uniquely undone on its image. For a linear map from ℂn to itself, injectivity also guarantees surjectivity and hence a genuine inverse. Picture such a map acting on the unit cube. Its determinant is the oriented volume of the image. If that volume is nonzero, the cube is carried to a genuine solid and no direction is lost. If the determinant is zero, the cube is crushed into a lower-dimensional set: at least one direction collapses completely, and every point displaced along that direction acquires the same image. For a linear map, nonzero determinant forbids this collapse and guarantees global injectivity.

So for a linear map the determinant carries several jobs at once. It records oriented volume distortion and certifies invertibility: det ≠ 0 ⟺ injective ⟺ invertible, across the whole space at once. In the multiplication maps considered below, the determinant also agrees with the algebraic norm. That agreement is special to this setting; a determinant is not, in general, a matrix norm.

The quadratic rings make this concrete. Multiplication by a fixed element z = a + bθ is a linear map, and in the Gaussian integers ℤ[i] multiplication by z = 2 + i sends x + yi to (2 + i)(x + yi) = (2x − y) + (x + 2y)i. As a map of the plane that is

The determinant is 5, the norm of 2 + i, and because it is nonzero the map is injective: every Gaussian integer has at most one predecessor under multiplication by 2 + i. Injectivity fails only where the norm falls to zero, which in the elliptic rings happens at the origin alone, and in the hyperbolic and parabolic rings happens along the null directions. For a linear map the only way to lose injectivity is to lose det ≠ 0.

What a Jacobian is, and the conjecture

A polynomial map F is usually not linear, so it has no single matrix. Instead, at each point it has a best linear approximation, the map you would see if you looked at F under a microscope at that point. That approximation is recorded by the Jacobian matrix JF, whose entries are the first partial derivatives ∂Fi/∂xj, one for each way an output can respond to a nudge in an input. Its determinant measures the local volume distortion: the factor by which F stretches a tiny cell of volume sitting at that point.

A linear map is the special case where this approximation is not an approximation at all. If L(x) = Mx, then component i is the plain sum Li = Mi1x1 + Mi2x2 + …, so the derivative ∂Li/∂xj is simply the constant Mij. The Jacobian of a linear map is the matrix M itself, at every point, unchanging.

Take the multiplication-by-2+i map from a moment ago, written out as L(x, y) = (2x − y, x + 2y). Differentiating each output by each input gives ∂L₁/∂x = 2 and ∂L₁/∂y = −1 from the first component, and ∂L₂/∂x = 1 and ∂L₂/∂y = 2 from the second. Not one of these depends on where you are, so the Jacobian is the same matrix at every point of the plane:

The Jacobian recovers the original matrix exactly, with nothing left over. A linear map is its own derivative, and so the local volume factor det JL and the global injectivity certificate det M are the very same number, both equal to 5 here. There is no gap between the microscope and the whole space, because the microscope shows the whole map.

The Jacobian Conjecture, posed by Keller in 1939, asked whether that coincidence survives when F is genuinely curved. If det JF is a nonzero constant at every point, so that F never squashes volume anywhere, must F be globally injective and invertible by a polynomial? The intuition is the linear certificate carried across to curved maps: if every infinitesimal cell is scaled by the same nonzero factor with the same orientation, how could the map ever fold two points together? For eighty-seven years no one could produce a map that did.

The answer, announced over a weekend in July 2026, is that the coincidence does not survive. There is an explicit polynomial map of ℂ³ whose Jacobian determinant is the constant −2 at every single point, and which nonetheless sends three distinct points to one.

The map, with the shorthand u = 1 + xy:

F₁ = u³z + y²u(4 + 3xy)
F₂ = y + 3xu²z + 3xy²(4 + 3xy)
F₃ = 2x − 3x²y − x³z

Constant determinant, restless matrix

A constant determinant does not mean a constant matrix. In the quadratic rings the Jacobian of multiplication is M(a, b) itself, frozen, so its determinant being constant tells you nothing further and the local data settles everything. In this map the Jacobian is instead a matrix of degree-six polynomials that lurches from point to point, and only its determinant is pinned. Move the point and watch the nine entries change while the determinant refuses to.

The entries roam across the integers and beyond, yet the determinant sits at −2 through every choice. The Jacobian is built from first derivatives, and its determinant is a single number extracted from them. Whether that number stays constant is one question; whether the matrix itself stays constant is another. The variation of the Jacobian first appears in the second derivatives, and that variation can accumulate into global overlap even while the determinant remains fixed. For this map the second derivatives are large and busy although the determinant they feed into never budges. The determinant reads one infinitesimal cell at a time; it never compares how neighbouring cells turn relative to one another, still less how widely separated regions of the source meet in the target.

Certify the collision

Three genuinely different points share a single image. You do not need to trust a solver for this; substitution alone certifies it, since plugging numbers into a polynomial is arithmetic and nothing more. Pick a point and watch it arrive at the same target.

(0, 0, −1/4)
(1, −3/2, 13/2)
(−1, 3/2, 13/2)

Three inputs, one output, and the determinant constant and nonzero throughout the domain. The map is locally invertible at every point, yet globally several separated regions of the source overlap in the target. This resembles the many-sheeted behaviour of a covering map, although this polynomial map should not be identified with the exponential map or assumed to be a covering of the entire target. The surprise is that such global overlap is achieved by an explicit polynomial of degree seven with no local singularity anywhere.

A glimpse of the three-sheet structure

The collision is not merely an isolated numerical accident. The third coordinate factors as

F₃ = x(2 − 3xy − x²z).

On the target plane where F₃ = 0, a preimage must therefore lie either on the plane x = 0 or on the curved surface x²z = 2 − 3xy. The first branch supplies one possible sheet, while the second can supply two further solutions after the remaining coordinates are imposed. This does not by itself prove the full generic fibre count, but it reveals the algebraic mechanism by which several locally regular branches can reach the same target point.

What the determinant cannot see

The quadratic-ring picture and this one fit together cleanly. Multiplication in the rings charts the boundary of the linear world, where the determinant governs injectivity completely and the only failures live on the null cone, exactly where the norm falls to zero. This map charts the first stretch of country just past that boundary, and its message is that the polynomial world holds a second mechanism for losing injectivity, one with no linear shadow at all.

Every infinitesimal cell is transformed nonsingularly, with the same local complex-volume factor, yet globally distinct regions can still arrive at the same place. The Jacobian determinant continues to record local volume scaling faithfully. What fails is the additional global conclusion that the linear case had trained us to expect. For a linear map, the determinant also certifies injectivity because the derivative is the whole map. For a curved polynomial map, that extra job does not survive.

For a linear map, the derivative at one point is the entire map, so a nonzero determinant settles the local and global questions at once. For a polynomial map, the Jacobian determinant settles only the local question. It says that no infinitesimal neighbourhood is crushed; it does not prevent distant neighbourhoods from overlapping. The conjecture carried the exceptional global power of the linear determinant into a setting where only its local meaning remained.
