The Linear Miracleand where it breaks

A nonzero determinant guarantees injectivity for linear maps. For polynomial maps, that guarantee fails, and here is the exact place it does.

The miracle we take for granted

A map is injective, one-to-one, when no two inputs share an output, so the map can always be undone. For a linear map there is a single clean obstruction to this. Picture the map acting on the unit cube. Its determinant is the volume of the image. If that volume is nonzero the cube is carried to a genuine solid and no direction is lost, but if the determinant is zero the cube is crushed flat, some whole direction collapses to nothing, and every point along that direction piles onto the same image. A zero determinant is exactly a fold, and a nonzero one forbids it.

So for a linear map the determinant carries four jobs at once. It is the norm, it is the volume distortion, it is the orientation indicator, and bundled in with these it is a certificate of injectivity: det ≠ 0 ⟺ injective, across the whole space at once.

The quadratic rings make this concrete. Multiplication by a fixed element z = a + bθ is a linear map, and in the Gaussian integers ℤ[i] multiplication by z = 2 + i sends x + yi to (2 + i)(x + yi) = (2x − y) + (x + 2y)i. As a map of the plane that is

The determinant is 5, the norm of 2 + i, and because it is nonzero the map is injective: every Gaussian integer has at most one predecessor under multiplication by 2 + i. Injectivity fails only where the norm falls to zero, which in the elliptic rings happens at the origin alone, and in the hyperbolic and parabolic rings happens along the null directions. For a linear map the only way to lose injectivity is to lose det ≠ 0.

What a Jacobian is, and the conjecture

A polynomial map F is usually not linear, so it has no single matrix. Instead, at each point it has a best linear approximation, the map you would see if you looked at F under a microscope at that point. That approximation is recorded by the Jacobian matrix JF, whose entries are the first partial derivatives ∂Fi/∂xj, one for each way an output can respond to a nudge in an input. Its determinant measures the local volume distortion: the factor by which F stretches a tiny cell of volume sitting at that point.

A linear map is the special case where this approximation is not an approximation at all. If L(x) = Mx, then component i is the plain sum Li = Mi1x1 + Mi2x2 + …, so the derivative ∂Li/∂xj is simply the constant Mij. The Jacobian of a linear map is the matrix M itself, at every point, unchanging.

Take the multiplication-by-2+i map from a moment ago, written out as L(x, y) = (2x − y, x + 2y). Differentiating each output by each input gives ∂L₁/∂x = 2 and ∂L₁/∂y = −1 from the first component, and ∂L₂/∂x = 1 and ∂L₂/∂y = 2 from the second. Not one of these depends on where you are, so the Jacobian is the same matrix at every point of the plane:

= M(2, 1), everywhere

The Jacobian recovers the original matrix exactly, with nothing left over. A linear map is its own derivative, and so the local volume factor det JL and the global injectivity certificate det M are the very same number, both equal to 5 here. There is no gap between the microscope and the whole space, because the microscope shows the whole map.

The Jacobian Conjecture, posed by Keller in 1939, asked whether that coincidence survives when F is genuinely curved. If det JF is a nonzero constant at every point, so that F never squashes volume anywhere, must F be globally injective and invertible by a polynomial? The intuition is the linear certificate carried across to curved maps: if every infinitesimal cell is scaled by the same nonzero factor with the same orientation, how could the map ever fold two points together? For eighty-seven years no one could produce a map that did.

The answer, announced over a weekend in July 2026, is that the coincidence does not survive. There is an explicit polynomial map of ℂ³ whose Jacobian determinant is the constant −2 at every single point, and which nonetheless sends three distinct points to one.

The map, with the shorthand u = 1 + xy:

F₁ = u³z + y²u(4 + 3xy)
F₂ = y + 3xu²z + 3xy²(4 + 3xy)
F₃ = 2x − 3x²y − x³z

Constant determinant, restless matrix

A constant determinant does not mean a constant matrix. In the quadratic rings the Jacobian of multiplication is M(a, b) itself, frozen, so its determinant being constant tells you nothing further and the local data settles everything. In this map the Jacobian is instead a matrix of degree-six polynomials that lurches from point to point, and only its determinant is pinned. Move the point and watch the nine entries change while the determinant refuses to.

The entries roam across the integers and beyond, yet the determinant sits at −2 through every choice. The reason folding can hide here is that it lives one level up from the determinant. The Jacobian is built from first derivatives, and its determinant is a number read off from them. Whether that number stays constant is one question; whether the matrix itself stays constant is another, and the difference between them is second-order. How the Jacobian turns as you move from point to point is governed by the second derivatives of F, and for this map those second derivatives are large and busy even though the determinant they feed into never budges. All of the map's global mischief is stored in that turning. The determinant is blind to it because the determinant reads only one cell at a time and never compares one cell to the next.

Certify the fold

Three genuinely different points share a single image. You do not need to trust a solver for this; substitution alone certifies it, since plugging numbers into a polynomial is arithmetic and nothing more. Pick a point and watch it collapse onto the target.

(0, 0, −1/4)
(1, −3/2, 13/2)
(−1, 3/2, 13/2)

Three inputs, one output, and the determinant constant and nonzero the entire time. The map is a local diffeomorphism at every point, invertible in any small neighbourhood, and still it wraps the space over itself the way the exponential wraps the plane, except that here it is achieved by an honest polynomial of degree seven, which is the whole surprise.

What the determinant cannot see

The quadratic-ring picture and this one fit together cleanly. Multiplication in the rings charts the boundary of the linear world, where the determinant governs injectivity completely and the only failures live on the null cone, exactly where the norm falls to zero. This map charts the first stretch of country just past that boundary, and its message is that the polynomial world holds a second mechanism for losing injectivity, one with no linear shadow at all.

Volume and orientation are conserved in every infinitesimal cell, and the map folds anyway, because global folding is a covering phenomenon that pointwise volume data cannot detect. The determinant remained the norm, the volume distortion, and the orientation indicator, all three, faithfully, at every point. It simply was never the certificate of injectivity that the linear case had trained us to expect. That fourth job, the one the linear miracle bundled in for free, was the one that did not survive the passage to curved maps.

The linear determinant answers a global question with local data. The Jacobian determinant answers only the local question, and answers it perfectly. The eighty-seven-year gap was the width of everything the local answer leaves out.
