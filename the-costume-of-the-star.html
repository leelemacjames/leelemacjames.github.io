McMurmerings · on the geometry of measurement

The Costume of the Star

How the cross product and the area of a parallelogram turn out to be the same disguise, and what a lattice supplies once the inner product has been taken away.

Every student of physics meets the Hodge star long before anyone gives it a name, because the first time a teacher draws two arrows on a board and produces a third standing square to both of them, calling the result their cross product, the star has already done its work unannounced. The new arrow is offered as though it were the same species as the two that bred it, a thing with a length and a heading, and yet the smallest attention to its making shows that it cannot be, since its heading was fixed by a right-hand rule that presupposes a chosen orientation of space, and its length was set equal to the area of the parallelogram the original two arrows span, which is a measurement belonging to the plane they share rather than to any line crossing it. What the cross product returns is therefore an oriented patch of area dressed in the costume of a vector, and the tailor who cuts the costume so well that the seams disappear is not a distance function but an inner product.

The honest object underneath the costume is the thing geometers write as \(u \wedge v\), the exterior product, which records the plane spanned by the two vectors together with the magnitude and sense of the area they enclose, and which makes no claim whatever to point anywhere. In three dimensions the space of these area-elements happens to have the same dimension, three, as the space of vectors, and it is precisely this numerical coincidence that lets the inner product set up a correspondence between an area-element and an arrow transverse to it; the cross product lives only in three dimensions for the simple reason that this is the only place the bookkeeping balances. The quantities physicists have learned to call axial vectors, among them angular momentum and torque and the magnetic field, are area-elements of exactly this kind, and they betray their true nature under a mirror, where an honest vector reverses while these stubbornly refuse to, the discrepancy being the costume slipping for a moment to show what is underneath.

0 The word metric has been wearing two coats

Before the star is blamed or praised, the word metric has to be made to change clothes. In the metric-space sense a metric is only a two-point distance \(d(x,y)\), and it asks for no vector space, no bilinear form, no angles, and no Hodge star. A norm is already more structured, since it measures a single vector and induces the translation-invariant distance \(d(x,y)=\|x-y\|\). But the object that does the real tailoring in Euclidean geometry is neither of these; it is the inner product \(g(u,v)\), a bilinear pairing of two vectors.

The difference is one of arity. A norm is a one-vector gauge, homogeneous and convex, the function whose unit ball may be a circle, square, diamond, hexagon, or stranger convex body. An inner product is a two-vector tensor, linear in each slot. That bilinearity is what lowers an index, identifies \(V\) with \(V^\ast\), defines orthogonality, manufactures adjoints and projections, and supplies the canonical volume form that a Hodge star needs.

Structure lost at each step

inner product \(g(u,v)\) → norm \(\|v\|\) → distance \(d(x,y)\)

The first arrow forgets bilinearity but keeps lengths; the second forgets vector structure and keeps only two-point comparison.

The boundary is sharp. A norm comes from an inner product exactly when it satisfies the parallelogram law, \[ \|x+y\|^2+\|x-y\|^2=2\|x\|^2+2\|y\|^2. \] Geometrically, this says that the unit ball is an ellipse. Circles and ellipses belong to the inner-product world; squares, diamonds and hexagons belong to the broader Finsler or normed world, where length survives but canonical angle and canonical area do not.

I Three loans in one gesture

It is worth saying slowly what the inner product actually lent in that single fluent gesture, because the Euclidean case performs three distinct services at once and stitches them together so seamlessly that we stop believing there were ever three. The first service is orientation, the choice that distinguishes a right hand from a left and so fixes which of the two perpendicular directions the costume shall face, a service that costs only a sign and that an orientation alone can supply. The second is a scale, an answer to the question of how much area shall count as one unit, which amounts to selecting a single nonzero element of the one-dimensional space of top-degree forms, the very line in which every area-element of the plane already lives. The third is the genuinely inner-product one, a linear identification of the space of vectors with the space of linear functionals upon it, the operation a physicist performs without comment whenever an index is raised or lowered, and which in the plane takes the visible form of a quarter-turn rotation carrying each vector to the functional that reads its perpendicular component.

The Hodge star is the composite of these three, and the reason it feels like a single indivisible law rather than three independent loans is that an inner product is generous enough to advance all three together and to advance them canonically, since the rotations it admits act transitively on the frames it would call orthonormal and leave exactly one volume form fixed up to sign. When all three loans come from the same lender in one transaction, the borrower may be forgiven for taking them to be inseparable.

II One storey down

The clarifying move is to descend a single storey, from three dimensions to two, and to watch which of the three services survives the fall and what shape the survivors take. In the plane the space of area-elements is no longer three-dimensional but one-dimensional, so the star can no longer pair an area with a transverse arrow, there being in general no room for a transverse direction worth naming, and it instead pairs the area-element with a plain number, sending the oriented parallelogram to a scalar. The familiar quantity \(ad - bc\), the determinant of the array whose columns are the vectors \((a,c)\) and \((b,d)\), is exactly this scalar, which is to say that the schoolroom rule \(\;\text{area}=\det\;\) is the two-dimensional cross product carried out in full, the same costume fitted one storey down, with the inner product again standing in as tailor.

Seen in this light the determinant stops being an algebraic accident of arithmetic and becomes the planar instance of the star's disguising work, the place where an honest area-element is handed back to us wearing the modest costume of an ordinary number. The quarter-turn we met a moment ago survives the descent as the action of the star on one-forms, where it becomes the complex structure \(J\) of the plane, and the volume loan survives as the unit of area we have implicitly agreed to call one; the only loan that genuinely lapses is the axial one, the identification of an area with a transverse arrow, which had been an artefact of three dimensions all along.

III The honest form

Take the inner product away entirely and see what remains standing. The alternating form that assigns to an ordered pair of vectors the signed area of the parallelogram they span, the object written \(dx \wedge dy\) in coordinates, asks nothing of a metric for its definition, needing only the vector-space structure to be bilinear and alternating, and an orientation to be given its sign; it lives, complete and well defined, in the one-dimensional top exterior power before any norm, length, or angle has been introduced. This form is perfectly candid about what it is, an area-element and never a scalar, and the moment at which it seems to become a mere number is exactly the moment an inner product has been quietly invoked to read it through the star. Strip that inner product and the form neither vanishes nor grows vague; it simply ceases to carry a canonical numerical value, because the dictionary that would translate an area-element into a number has been withdrawn.

This is the whole of the matter put plainly, that area is underdetermined by the norm alone until something is chosen to play the part the inner product had been playing, while the alternating form, the determinant, and the oriented volume element remain available long before, and quite independently of, any such choice.

IV Who pays for area

If the inner product is no longer the lender, the question becomes who else can advance the volume loan, and there turn out to be several willing parties, each supplying a single nonzero element of that one-dimensional line of forms and so fixing what shall count as unit area. An inner product remains one such party, providing the volume element that coordinates would call \(\sqrt{\det g}\), and providing it canonically, which is the source of its old monopoly. A norm is a second party, though a more equivocal one, since the convex body it nominates as its unit ball can be assigned a volume by more than one reasonable convention, the two best known being Busemann's, which insists the unit ball receive the same measure it would carry in the Euclidean plane, and that of Holmes and Thompson, which reads the ball through its polar dual, the two conventions agreeing precisely when the ball is an ellipse and parting company over every polygon; a norm therefore offers area, yet only after a further decision has narrowed its offer to a single number.

Three independent gifts

norm → length without canonical area

inner product → length + angle + canonical area

lattice → canonical cell area without length

This is the triangle that dissolves the apparent puzzle: Pick's theorem lives on the lattice side, not on the Euclidean metric side.

The third party, and the one that matters most for the question we began with, is a lattice, which advances the volume loan with a directness the others cannot match, since a lattice singles out, without any ambiguity at all, the unique area-element that assigns its fundamental cell the value one. A lattice hands you a unit of area for nothing, requiring neither an inner product nor a Finsler convention, and this is the quiet fact on which the whole of Pick's theorem turns.

V The coefficient that was never metric

Pick's theorem counts the points of the integer lattice that a polygon encloses and carries on its boundary, and returns the combination \(I + \tfrac{1}{2}B - 1\), and the content of the theorem is that this combination is the polygon's measure in precisely the unit of area the lattice itself supplies, the coordinate of the polygon's area-element in the trivialization the lattice has freely given. Nothing in the statement or its proof calls for an inner product, an angle, or a length, because the theorem belongs to the geometry of an affine plane equipped with a lattice and nothing finer.

This is why the coefficient that surfaces when one moves from the Gaussian lattice to the Eisenstein lattice, the factor of \(\sqrt{3}/2\), is so often misattributed to the Euclidean metric when it owes the Euclidean inner product nothing. The Gaussian lattice spanned by \(1\) and \(i\) gives its fundamental cell the value one in coordinates, while the Eisenstein lattice spanned by \(1\) and \(\omega = e^{2\pi i/3}\) has a fundamental cell whose coordinate determinant is \(\sqrt{3}/2\), and this number is only the ratio between two elements of the same one-dimensional line, the lattice's own area-element and the coordinate area-element \(dx \wedge dy\), which is to say the determinant of the Eisenstein basis and a statement about how that lattice sits and is oriented in its affine coordinates. Were one to discard Euclidean length altogether and to introduce instead a hexagonal Finsler norm whose symmetry flatters the Eisenstein lattice's own, the determinant would stand exactly as it was, an unchanged algebraic object. The norm would alter lengths and geodesics, not the Pick count. Only if one chose a new Finsler area convention would the numerical reading of area move; the count, the orientation, the lattice cell, and the determinant would sit untouched while their interpretation as area was revised.

VI Euclid as a furnished room

The lower-to-higher hierarchy

projective structure → affine structure → orientation → lattice cell

→ connection or admissible directions → norm or Finsler length

→ inner product → Hodge star

Euclidean geometry is the bundled upper case, where duality, angle, length and volume arrive together. Pick's theorem sits lower, where affine area and lattice counting are already enough.

What this leaves us with is a rearrangement of the usual hierarchy, in which Euclidean geometry is best understood as a particularly well-furnished room several storeys up rather than as the floor on which everything else is built, the special case in which the norm happens to be quadratic, the identification of vectors with functionals happens to be linear, and the unit of area happens to be settled for free and without argument. The geometry in which Pick's theorem actually lives sits lower in the building, on the equiaffine floor where an alternating form and a lattice are the primary furniture and where area is exactly a count, and the choice of a norm, hexagonal or square or otherwise, is a decoration one may hang on the wall of that lower room rather than a part of its structure.

The Hodge star, read from this floor, is demoted from a law of nature to a convenience of one well-appointed room, a composite of separable choices that the inner product had bundled together so smoothly that we mistook the bundle for an atom. Orientation, volume, and duality can each be granted or withheld on its own, and once they are seen apart it becomes clear why a Finsler norm, lavish with lengths and stingy with everything else, can tell you exactly how far you have walked while not by itself telling you how much ground you have enclosed. A norm determines length but not canonical area; an inner product determines length, angle and canonical area; a lattice determines a unit of area without determining length.

There remains a further question, of why it should be the square and the hexagon in particular that arrive bearing the richest symmetries, and why these two lattices among all others should be the ones their arithmetic singles out, though that belongs to the deeper number theory beneath these two exceptional shapes, and it can keep for an essay of its own.
