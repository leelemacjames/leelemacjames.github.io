Assembling the Chandrasekhar Limit

A maximum mass for a white dwarf can be built from a handful of equations you already meet in the syllabus. Choose which ones to deploy. Some belong in the argument; others are decoys that look astronomical but lead nowhere.

What you are looking for

You need four kinds of physics: a source of electron momentum from confinement, the relativistic energy that takes over at high speed, the gravity that pulls the sphere inward, and the bookkeeping that links the star's mass to its electron number. Anything about temperature, thermal radiation, or photon scattering is a decoy, because the support that fails is cold and quantum-mechanical.

Why a limit needs relativity

If the electrons stayed slow, their degeneracy energy would behave as $E_{\text{deg}}\sim \hbar^{2}N^{5/3}/(m_e R^{2})$, which steepens faster than gravity's $-GM^{2}/R$ as the star shrinks, so the total energy has a true minimum and the star settles at a finite radius for any mass. No limit appears. The maximum emerges only when compression drives the electrons close to the speed of light, where $E\approx pc$ makes the degeneracy energy fall as $\hbar c\,N^{4/3}/R$, the same $1/R$ as gravity. Once the two share that scaling the radius cancels and mass alone decides the outcome.

The missing number

The scaling argument fixes the form exactly, $M_{\text{limit}}\propto(\hbar c/G)^{3/2}/(\mu_e m_{\mathrm H})^{2}$, yet it cannot pin the leading constant, because that depends on how density runs from centre to surface, which a single-radius model throws away. Keeping the order-unity factors of a uniform sphere already gives a rigorous upper bound, $M\lesssim 1.7\,M_\odot$; the full hydrostatic solution, the Lane–Emden equation of index three, sharpens it to $\omega_3^{0}\sqrt{3\pi}/2\approx 3.10$, and for a carbon–oxygen mix ($\mu_e=2$) that is the celebrated $1.44\,M_\odot$. The bare estimate with the constant set to one lands near $0.46\,M_\odot$, so the gap from $0.46$ to $1.44$ is precisely the coefficient.

Assembled · the Chandrasekhar mass

Both energies fall as $1/R$, so the radius cancels and only the sign of the bracket survives, $$E\sim\frac{1}{R}\Big[\,\hbar c\,N^{4/3}-G(\mu_e m_{\mathrm H})^{2}N^{2}\,\Big].$$ Setting the bracket to zero gives the largest stable electron count, and hence the mass:

$M_{\text{limit}}=\left(\dfrac{\hbar c}{G}\right)^{3/2}\dfrac{1}{(\mu_e m_{\mathrm H})^{2}}$

You built the whole structure from the syllabus. The one thing the argument cannot supply is the leading number; open Coefficient & 1.44 to see where the famous figure comes from.
