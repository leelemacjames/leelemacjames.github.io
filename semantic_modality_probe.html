Semantic Modality Probe · Cross-curricular Language

Words do work.
Which work, exactly?

Wittgenstein's question about language was simple and devastating: what does this word do here, in this sentence, in this practice? Not what does it name, but what work does placing it perform? For scientific and mathematical vocabulary, that question has unusually clean answers.

Words like field, entropy, and resonance carry structural commitments. To use them correctly in a model is to accept certain inferences and foreclose others. A student who writes "energy is conserved" and a student who understands that conservation follows from time-translation symmetry via Noether's theorem are separated by something that no amount of vocabulary fluency closes on its own. The probe here is designed to make that gap visible and measurable across different registers of use.

There is a second motivation. Effective writing — in science, in formal argument, in prompting AI systems — requires that words do their work without decoration. Poetry and allegory have their own rigour, but that rigour depends on controlled ambiguity, on resonance between possible readings. When precision is required, vagueness is not a stylistic choice but a failure of thought. Much contemporary scientific writing, and almost all padded institutional prose, mistakes the accumulation of correct-sounding terms for the articulation of a structure. This probe tests the opposite skill.

The core claim

A semantic operator is a word that preserves its structural invariant across four representational regimes: lyrical-in/lyrical-out, lyrical-in/logical-out, logical-in/lyrical-out, and logical-in/logical-out.

A small class of words survives all four without losing the invariant that makes them precise: field, wave, energy, symmetry, entropy, resonance, phase, curvature, gradient, information. What distinguishes them is historical depth — each was first a metaphor, became a model, cashed out as a formal object, and then returned to teaching as image again.

On padding

The failure mode in weak scientific writing is not wrong words but structureless ones: nouns stacked into noun phrases, passive constructions hiding agents, intensifiers standing where quantities belong. This probe is a diagnostic for the opposite — words whose usefulness scales directly with the precision of their deployment.
