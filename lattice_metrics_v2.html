Introduction

Distance on a lattice
four different answers

The integer lattice ℤ² is the grid of points with whole-number coordinates. It is familiar from graph paper. But distance on this grid is not a single thing — it depends on what you are allowed to do.

Are you a crow flying straight between points? A taxi constrained to city blocks? A chess king moving one step at a time? Or a surveyor counting how many irreducible lattice steps lie between two points? Each question gives a different metric, and each reveals a different geometric structure of the lattice.

This learner introduces the four natural distance measures on ℤ², from the most familiar to the most intrinsic to the lattice itself. The culmination is Pick's theorem — which turns out to be a statement about the lattice's own notion of boundary length.

The thread: Euclidean, Chebyshev, and Manhattan are all restrictions or distortions of the ambient geometry of ℝ². The lattice word length is different in kind — it lives entirely on ℤ², with no reference to ℝ². Pick's theorem is written in this fourth language.

What each metric privileges

Euclidean: straight-line distance in ℝ². The metric neighbourhood at radius r is a circle — all directions equally weighted. This is the metric of the ambient space; the lattice is just a discrete subset.

Chebyshev (L∞): max(|Δx|, |Δy|). The neighbourhood is a square aligned with the axes. Diagonal steps cost the same as axis steps — a king on a chessboard. This coarsens the Euclidean metric by collapsing all eight shortest directions to the same cost.

Manhattan (L¹): |Δx| + |Δy|. The neighbourhood is a diamond. Only axis steps are primitive — diagonal movement must be decomposed into two axis steps. This privileges the four axis directions over all others.

Lattice word length

Lattice word length
all primitive directions equally

The three classical metrics all measure distances by importing structure from ℝ² — lengths, axis directions, or the ambient plane. The lattice has its own intrinsic notion of distance, which needs no reference to ℝ² at all.

Primitive vectors

A vector (a,b) ∈ ℤ² is primitive if gcd(|a|,|b|) = 1 — it cannot be written as n·(p,q) for integer n > 1. Primitive vectors are the irreducible steps of the lattice: (1,0), (1,1), (2,1), (3,2), (5,3) and infinitely many others, one for every rational slope.

Every lattice vector decomposes uniquely: (a,b) = gcd(|a|,|b|) · (a/g, b/g) where (a/g, b/g) is primitive. The lattice word length assigns:

‖(a,b)‖_B = gcd(|a|, |b|)

So (6,4) has word length gcd(6,4) = 2. The vector (3,2) has word length 1 — it is itself primitive, one irreducible step regardless of its Euclidean length √13.

Why gcd? The lattice points on a segment

Before defining the word length formula, ask a concrete question: how many lattice points lie strictly inside the segment from (0,0) to (a,b)?

The points on the segment are parametrised as (ta, tb) for t ∈ [0,1]. Such a point is a lattice point exactly when both ta and tb are integers — which happens for t = k/gcd(a,b), k = 0, 1, …, gcd(a,b). So the segment contains gcd(a,b)+1 lattice points in total (including endpoints) and gcd(a,b)−1 strictly interior ones.

interior lattice points on [(0,0),(a,b)] = gcd(|a|,|b|) − 1

A vector is primitive when gcd(a,b) = 1 — meaning the segment has no interior lattice points. The lattice cannot subdivide it further. (3,4) is primitive: the segment from (0,0) to (3,4) passes through no other lattice point. (6,8) has gcd = 2: the midpoint (3,4) lies on the segment, splitting it into two primitive sub-steps.

The word length of (a,b) is therefore the number of primitive sub-steps the segment decomposes into: gcd(|a|,|b|). This is not an arbitrary formula — it counts how many irreducible pieces the lattice sees in the vector.

No preferred directions

Chebyshev privileges 8 directions. Manhattan privileges 4. The lattice word length privileges none — every primitive direction costs 1. There are infinitely many such directions, one for each rational slope. The generating set is the set of all primitive vectors:

𝒫 = {(a,b) ∈ ℤ² : gcd(|a|,|b|) = 1}

This is not a ball or neighbourhood in ℝ² — it is a countably infinite set of directions, densely distributed around the origin. Calling it a "unit ball" would import Euclidean language where it does not belong. It is better understood as the generating set of the lattice, or the set of atomic steps.

Chebyshev and Manhattan as coarsenings

Both Chebyshev and Manhattan are special cases of the word metric — they just use a finite generating set rather than all of 𝒫. Chebyshev uses the 8 shortest primitive vectors. Manhattan uses the 4 axis vectors. The lattice word length uses all of 𝒫 and is therefore finer than either: it distinguishes directions that Chebyshev collapses to the same cost.

(6,8) costs 2 because it decomposes into two (3,4) steps. The word length sees only the combinatorial structure of ℤ² as a group — how many irreducible generators are needed — not the geometry of ℝ².
Pick's theorem

Pick's theorem
written in word length

Pick's theorem counts boundary lattice points B. Each boundary edge from vertex to vertex contributes gcd(|Δx|,|Δy|) to B — exactly its lattice word length. So B is the total word length of the boundary cycle, measured in primitive generators.

B = total lattice word length of the polygon boundary

Pick's theorem then reads:

A = Δ · (I + B/2 − 1)

where Δ = det(M_basis) is the area of the fundamental domain. The determinant Δ converts word length into Euclidean area. Each primitive boundary step contributes Δ/2 to the area — this is the geometric content of the unimodular condition.

Why B/A fails as a shape measure

Scale any polygon by n: each boundary step (Δx, Δy) becomes (nΔx, nΔy) with word length gcd(n|Δx|, n|Δy|) = n·gcd(|Δx|,|Δy|). So B → nB, A → n²A, giving B/A → 0 for any shape. Word length and area scale differently under dilation.

B/A → 0 under dilation — carries no shape information

The scale-invariant ratio B²/A

Under dilation by n: B → nB and A → n²A, so B²/A → B²/A exactly. This is the natural scale-invariant measure of boundary efficiency in word-length units:

B²/A = B² / [Δ·(I + B/2 − 1)] — invariant under lattice dilation

For an n×n square: B = 4n, A = n², B²/A = 16 for all n. Scale invariance is exact.

B²/A versus P²/A

The classical isoperimetric ratio P²/A uses Euclidean perimeter P. Replacing P with B (word length) gives a related but distinct quantity. They agree when all boundary steps have the same Euclidean length — as for the six shortest Eisenstein directions, all of length 1. In general B ≤ P (word length undercounts Euclidean length for oblique steps like (1,1) which has word length 1 but Euclidean length √2), so B²/A ≤ P²/A. The classical bound P²/A ≥ 4π holds for Euclidean perimeters; it says nothing directly about B²/A.

Pick's theorem is written in word length. The boundary count B is not a Euclidean measure — it is the word length of the boundary in the lattice's own metric. The determinant Δ is the exchange rate between word length and Euclidean area. Together they give the formula A = Δ(I + B/2 − 1): a statement about ℤ² as a group, converted to Euclidean area by Δ.

The full picture

Euclidean, Chebyshev, and Manhattan all live in ℝ² — the lattice is just a discrete subset of the ambient plane, and the metrics are restrictions of ℝ² geometry. The word length lives entirely on ℤ², needing no ambient space. It is the metric the lattice generates from itself.

Pick's theorem uses word length because it is counting lattice structure, not Euclidean length. The determinant Δ is then the bridge: it converts the intrinsic word-length count into Euclidean area. Change the lattice (square to Eisenstein), change Δ, and the same word-length count maps to a different area. The basis is the exchange rate between the two worlds.

The hierarchy of metrics on ℤ²: word length (all primitives) → Chebyshev (8 shortest) → Manhattan (4 axis) → Euclidean (continuous). Each step coarsens or enriches the notion of distance. Pick's theorem lives at the word-length end. The classical isoperimetric inequality P²/A ≥ 4π lives at the Euclidean end. B²/A is the word-length version of the isoperimetric ratio — a genuinely lattice-theoretic quantity, not a discretisation of a Euclidean one.
