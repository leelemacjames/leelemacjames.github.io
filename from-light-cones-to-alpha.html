Conformal Electrodynamics

From Light Cones to α

We don't introduce ε₀ because the vacuum has a property — we introduce it because measurement forces us to choose a scale.

Overview

The Arc of This Argument

Geometry → Fields → Sources → Measurement → α → α(μ)
Erlangen: Projective ⊃ Conformal ⊃ Metric ⊃ Empirical

We begin with spacetime geometry, which fixes how light propagates. We then ask what happens when we try to measure the fields — and discover that measurement forces us to introduce constants that are not properties of the vacuum but artefacts of our choice of units. The fine-structure constant α is what remains when every such choice is stripped away. Quantum theory then reveals that α itself is not a fixed number but a function of the energy scale at which we probe — though it remains the sole carrier of physical content about interaction strength.

Read through Klein's Erlangen programme, this is a descent from Platonic structure to empirical engagement — from the geometry that needs no measuring scale to the physics that cannot exist without one.

Stage I

The Stage: Conformal Spacetime

Spacetime provides a conformal structure: the pattern of light cones at every event. This structure tells us which directions are null (lightlike), timelike, or spacelike — but it does not fix an absolute scale. It encodes how space and time relate, but not how big anything is.

Maxwell's equations in vacuum can be written in two complementary forms. In vector calculus — the language most physicists meet first:

no charges∇ · E = 0
no currents∇ × B − ∂E/∂t = 0
no monopoles∇ · B = 0
Faraday∇ × E + ∂B/∂t = 0
Vacuum Maxwell — no ε₀, no μ₀, no charge, no scale

In the language of differential forms, the same content compresses to two statements:

dF = 0    d★F = 0
Bianchi identity + vacuum field equation — pure geometry, no constants

Notice what is absent. These equations contain no ε₀, no μ₀, no charge, no coupling. They are purely geometric — they describe how a field propagates on the conformal structure, nothing more.

Source equations

When sources are present, the second equation becomes d★F = J (or equivalently ∇ · E = ρ/ε₀ and ∇ × B − ∂E/∂t = μ₀J). Now ε₀ and μ₀ appear — but only as the bridge between field quantities (E, B) and source quantities (ρ, J) in our chosen system of units.

Constitutive relations — the "state" of the vacuum

In a material medium, constitutive relations describe the response: D = εE and B = μH. They encode the material's state — its polarisability, magnetisability, density.

In vacuum, the constitutive relations reduce to D = ε₀E and B = μ₀H. But this is deceptive. The vacuum has no polarisable molecules, no internal structure to respond. These are not describing a "state" of the vacuum — they are defining a system of units. They tell us how we have chosen to split the electromagnetic field into "field" and "response" components, not what the vacuum is doing.

The vacuum does not have a state. It has a causal structure. ε₀ and μ₀ are how we project that structure onto our measurement conventions.

The speed-of-light identity

c = 1 / √(μ₀ε₀)

This relation is often presented as deriving the speed of light from vacuum properties. But read it the other way: ε₀ and μ₀ are defined so that Maxwell's equations reproduce a universe whose causal structure is set by c. The speed of light is the geometric fact; ε₀ and μ₀ are chosen to be consistent with it. Equivalently, we could define α via μ₀ and c just as naturally as via ε₀ — because α = e²μ₀c / (2h) — confirming that ε₀ and μ₀ carry the same information, packaged differently by unit convention.

Core claim

Vacuum Maxwell theory is a conformal, scale-free theory. Only null structure (light cones) matters. Rescaling spacetime by g → Ω²g leaves the equations invariant.

Conformal invariance of Maxwell's equations is special to four dimensions. The electromagnetic action ∫ F∧★F is conformally invariant only when the spacetime dimension equals four. In other dimensions, a scale creeps into the action and conformal symmetry breaks. This is not a coincidence — it is why electromagnetism has the mathematical structure it does.

Stage II

Masslessness, Scale, and Null Propagation

The electromagnetic field is massless. This is not an incidental property; it is the structural reason for everything in Stage I. A massless field has no rest frame, no intrinsic length, and no preferred energy scale. It therefore cannot distinguish large from small, and the theory must be scale-invariant.

The field equations force disturbances to propagate along null surfaces — the light cones themselves. Not timelike (massive propagation), not spacelike (superluminal). Only null.

Three views of one fact

Masslessness, conformal invariance, and null propagation are not three independent properties. They are the same structural statement seen from three angles:

massless → no intrinsic scale → conformal invariance → null propagation

Adding a mass term mAμAμ (the Proca theory) destroys all three simultaneously: it introduces a length scale ℏ/mc, breaks conformal invariance, and permits timelike propagation. Even a tiny mass collapses the entire conformal structure — there is no smooth limit.

Stage III

The Problem of Measurement

If the vacuum theory is scale-free, a fundamental question arises: how do we measure anything?

To measure a field, we must relate it to sources — charges and currents. The moment we write Gauss's law with a source term, we introduce ingredients that the conformal geometry did not provide.

∇ · E  =  ρ / ε₀
Gauss's law — the moment measurement enters

The pedagogical shift

ε₀ is not a property the vacuum has. It is a conversion factor that becomes necessary once we choose a system of units that separates electric and magnetic quantities. It is a normalisation bridge between fields and sources — not a material property, and not fixed by geometry.

Two different kinds of freedom

It is easy to conflate these, but they are structurally different:

Conformal symmetry says: only angles and causal structure matter. You can rescale the metric g → Ω²g and the physics is unchanged. This is a property of spacetime geometry.

Unit freedom says: you can choose how strong a unit of charge or field is. You can absorb ε₀ into the definition of e, or trade ε₀ for μ₀ via c = 1/√(μ₀ε₀). This is a property of human convention.

ε₀ and μ₀ are not gauge freedoms of spacetime. They are conversion factors between human-defined units.

Fields are primary, not sources

The traditional framing — "sources produce fields, and ε₀ controls how fields respond to sources" — inverts the modern understanding. In quantum field theory, fields are the fundamental objects. What we call "sources" (charges, currents) are localised excitations of other fields — the electron field, the quark fields. The coupling between the electromagnetic field and these matter fields is the interaction; ε₀ is how we choose to normalise the bookkeeping of that interaction in SI units.

Stage IV

Two Layers of Structure

It helps to see this as a layered construction. The field itself has intrinsic properties; coupling to matter adds something on top.

Layer 1 — The Field Itself

Maxwell field is massless. No intrinsic scale. Conformally invariant. Field normalisation is arbitrary. This is the field's own nature, not an accident of emptiness.

Layer 2 — Coupling to Matter

Introducing charge brings scale (mass, density, characteristic lengths). This forces a normalisation choice, introducing ε₀ and coupling strength e. Conformal symmetry is broken — not because the field changed, but because we now must compare fields to sources.

What survives both layers

The fine-structure constant α — the dimensionless measure of how strongly the electromagnetic field interacts with charge. It cannot be absorbed, rescaled, or conventionalised away.

Key distinction

It is not that vacuum removes scale — it reveals that the electromagnetic field never had one to begin with. Matter hides conformal symmetry by introducing scale; vacuum exposes the underlying scale-free nature of the massless field.

Stage V

The Fine-Structure Constant

In SI Units

α = e² / (4π ε₀ ℏc)

ε₀ is visible. Normalisation is explicit. The underlying structure is obscured by bookkeeping.

In Natural Units

α = e² / 4π

ε₀ disappears. The structure is clear: coupling over geometry. But the need for normalisation has not vanished — it is absorbed into the definition of e.

— The Invariant That Cannot Be Conventionalised Away

This sequence is not merely a repackaging of known facts. It is a claim about what is fundamental and what is conventional. The traditional presentation — in which ε₀ and μ₀ appear as "properties of the vacuum" — conflates normalisation artefacts with physical constants. The conformal viewpoint reveals that only α carries physical content that survives all choices of convention. The rest is how we choose to talk about the physics, not the physics itself.

But this is the classical picture. Quantum theory complicates it in a precise and important way — see Stage VII.

Stage VII

The Quantum Complication: Vacuum Polarisation and Running α

Everything in Stages I–VI is correct classically. But quantum field theory introduces a complication that is not a technicality — it is structurally important, and ignoring it would leave the story incomplete.

In quantum theory, the vacuum is not empty. Charged fields (electrons, muons, quarks) fluctuate even in the absence of real particles, and these fluctuations polarise the vacuum. A virtual electron–positron pair, briefly pulled apart by an electric field, screens the bare charge — exactly as a dielectric medium screens a charge embedded in it.

The key distinction

This is not a breakdown of classical electrodynamics. The photon remains massless. Electromagnetic waves still propagate on null cones. The conformal structure of the free field is untouched.

What changes is the interaction. The coupling α, which classically is a fixed number, now depends on the energy scale at which it is measured.

What survives

Kinematics: null propagation, light cones, conformal structure of the free photon field. The propagation law remains scale-free.

Photons still travel on null geodesics. The causal structure is unmodified.

What acquires scale

Dynamics of interaction: the coupling α(μ) runs with energy scale μ. Vacuum polarisation introduces a logarithmic scale dependence.

At low energies: α ≈ 1/137. At the Z-boson mass: α ≈ 1/128. The interaction gets stronger at shorter distances.

α(μ)  =  α(μ₀)  /  [ 1 − (α(μ₀) / 3π) ln(μ² / μ₀²) + ⋯ ]
One-loop running of α — vacuum polarisation introduces logarithmic scale dependence

This is the precise sense in which quantum theory complicates the classical picture. The conformal invariance of the free Maxwell field is preserved: photons are still massless, still null-propagating, still scale-free in isolation. But the moment the field interacts with charged matter — even the virtual matter of the quantum vacuum — scale enters the coupling.

Free photon propagation

Conformal. Scale-free. Null cones. Survives quantisation unchanged.

Classical interaction

Coupling to charge introduces ε₀ (normalisation) and e (coupling). But α is fixed: one number, no scale dependence. This is the picture of Stages I–VI.

Quantum interaction

Virtual charged pairs polarise the vacuum. The coupling α now runs: α = α(μ). Scale enters the dynamics — not the kinematics. The interaction is no longer conformally invariant, even though propagation still is.

What this means for the classical story

Stages I–VI are not wrong. They are the correct classical picture, and they identify the right conceptual architecture: normalisation (ε₀) is conventional, geometry (4π, c) is structural, and α is the surviving physical content. Quantum theory refines this by revealing that α is not a single number but a function of scale — yet it remains dimensionless, invariant under unit changes, and the only quantity that carries physical content about the strength of interaction.

The propagation law stays conformal; the interaction does not. Kinematics sees no scale; dynamics acquires one. α remains the irreducible measure of interaction — but quantum theory reveals it is a function, not a constant.

— The Refined Punchline

Classical Maxwell theory is conformally invariant and scale-free, but the quantum vacuum breaks this symmetry subtly: fluctuations introduce a reference scale, and the fine-structure constant becomes energy-dependent — not because the laws change, but because the vacuum itself acquires structure.

Conformal symmetry removes scale at the classical level, but quantum fluctuations reintroduce scale through the act of measurement itself.

— The Philosophical Compression

The running of α is sometimes cited as evidence that "constants aren't really constant." This is misleading. What runs is the effective coupling at a given energy scale — the result of integrating out quantum fluctuations above that scale. The bare coupling (defined at infinite energy, or equivalently at zero distance) is a fixed parameter of the theory. What quantum field theory teaches us is that the physically measurable interaction strength depends on the resolution at which we probe — and this is a feature of the theory, not a failure of the concept of a coupling constant.

The deeper point: even in the quantum theory, α(μ) at any given scale μ remains dimensionless, convention-independent, and the sole carrier of physical content about interaction strength. The classical story identified the right object; quantum theory reveals its richer structure.

Stage VIII

The Erlangen Programme: From Pure Structure to Empirical Engagement

There is a deeper way to see what has happened across Stages I–VII — one that reveals the entire arc as an epistemological descent, not just a physics construction. It comes from Klein's Erlanger Programm (1872), which classified geometries by their symmetry groups: the more symmetry you preserve, the less structure you have; the more you break, the more you can distinguish.

Map this hierarchy onto our story, and something striking emerges. Each stage of the explainer corresponds to a step down Klein's ladder — from maximal symmetry and minimal structure toward the point where we can finally make contact with experiment.

Physical analogue → Empiricism. Contact with experiment. The act of holding a measuring instrument to the world and reading off a number.

The nesting is the point. Each inner box lives inside the symmetry of the outer one. Each step down the hierarchy breaks a symmetry and adds distinguishable structure. And the entire arc of this explainer — from light cones to α — is this descent.

The epistemological reading

At the top of the hierarchy, geometry is Platonic: pure incidence, pure structure, no empirical content. Nothing can be measured because nothing has been distinguished.

Conformal structure adds causality — the pattern of what can influence what — but still no scale. This is the world of vacuum Maxwell: beautiful, self-contained, and empirically inaccessible without further structure.

Metricity adds rulers and clocks. General relativity lives here. But electromagnetism still floats freely — its normalisation is not yet fixed.

Only at the final step — when we introduce coupling to charge, fix a normalisation, and extract α — do we leave the Platonic world of pure geometry and enter the empirical world of measurement. ε₀ is the price of admission; α is what we find on the other side.

The Erlangen hierarchy is a map from structure to substance: from the Platonic realm of pure geometry, through causality and metricity, to the empirical world where we hold a measuring scale to nature and read off α.

Why projective geometry is the right starting point

Projective geometry is not merely "geometry without distances." It is the geometry of perspective — of how structure looks from different vantage points, before any standard of comparison has been introduced. Its fundamental invariant, the cross-ratio, measures the relative position of four collinear points without needing a ruler. This is why it sits at the top: it captures what can be said about incidence and connection before any measurement apparatus exists.

The descent through conformal, metric, and finally measurement geometry is then precisely the progressive introduction of standards of comparison. Conformal geometry introduces a standard for angles (null vs timelike vs spacelike). Metricity introduces a standard for distances. And the final step — the coupling α — introduces a standard for interaction strength: how strongly the electromagnetic field responds when we bring a charged test particle into its presence.

Each step is an act of restricting symmetry in order to gain empirical traction. The entire Erlangen hierarchy is, in this reading, a map of the cost of making physics measurable.

There is a sense in which the entire history of physics — from Euclid through Riemann and Klein to Einstein and Dirac — can be read as a negotiation between symmetry and measurability. Symmetry tells us what is structurally possible; measurement tells us what is actually the case. The Erlangen programme makes this negotiation explicit: each geometry is defined by what it refuses to distinguish, and physics progresses by choosing, at each level, which distinctions to impose.

The fine-structure constant sits at the bottom of this hierarchy — the final, irreducible distinction that no symmetry can absorb and no convention can remove. It is, in the deepest sense, the point where geometry ends and empirical physics begins.

Coda

A Curiosity: What Dimensions Make Visible

There is a small example, far from electrodynamics, that illuminates the same point from the other side.

From dimensional analysis alone — combining the Hubble parameter H, Newton's constant G, and the speed of light c — one can recover the mass density of the universe up to a geometric factor:

ρ  ≈  H² / G
and more precisely
ρc  =  3H² / (8πG)
The critical density of the universe — derivable from dimensions alone

This works because each constant contributes a distinct piece of dimensional scaffolding: H sets a time scale (the inverse age of the universe), G sets a gravitational coupling scale (the strength of the link between mass and geometry), and c links space and time. Together they force the correct dimensions for a density — mass per volume — with no further input.

Yet in natural units, where ħ = c = 1 and these distinctions collapse, this structure becomes far less visible. The dimensional scaffolding that made the derivation almost inevitable is absorbed into the formalism and hidden.

G in natural units

With ħ = c = 1, Newton's constant acquires dimensions of length² (equivalently 1/mass²). It becomes:

G = 1 / MPl²

The Planck mass is the statement that gravity has a dimensionful coupling. G is not a pure number — it carries a scale. This single fact is the root of the non-renormalisability problem.

H in natural units

The Hubble parameter has dimensions of mass (equivalently 1/length or 1/time — all identical when ħ = c = 1). It is an energy scale:

H ~ 10−33 eV

An absurdly small energy — the current expansion rate of the universe expressed as a mass. The vast hierarchy between H and MPl encodes the age and size of the cosmos.

What the dimensional scaffolding made visible

In SI units, ρ_c = 3H²/(8πG) is almost derivable by inspection: you need a density, you have a rate² and a coupling, the dimensions lock. In natural units, this becomes ρ_c = 3H²M_Pl²/(8π) — still correct, but the scaffolding that guided the dimensional argument is no longer doing the work. The structure is there, but you need to already know where to look.

Two different geometric origins

The 4π in the fine-structure constant α = e²/(4πε₀ħc) comes from the solid angle of a sphere: it is the geometry of how a point source distributes its field isotropically in three spatial dimensions. It is Coulomb's law, Gauss's law, the surface area of S².

The 3/(8π) in the Friedmann equation has a different ancestry. The 8π comes from Einstein's field equations, Gμν = 8πG Tμν, where the conventional normalisation of G is chosen so that the Newtonian limit reproduces Poisson's equation ∇²Φ = 4πGρ. The factor of two between 4π and 8π reflects the contribution of pressure as a source of gravity in general relativity — a relativistic effect absent in Newton's theory. The 3 comes from the spatial curvature term: three spatial dimensions contribute to the Friedmann equation through the trace of the spatial Ricci tensor.

Both factors are geometric. But they encode different geometries: 4π encodes spherical symmetry around a point source; 3/(8π) encodes homogeneous curvature of a 3-dimensional space with relativistic gravitational sources. The former is the geometry of radiation; the latter is the geometry of cosmology.

The contrast that matters

This is where the Coda connects back to the main argument — and reveals why electromagnetism and gravity are fundamentally different at the quantum level.

Electromagnetism: the coupling α ≈ 1/137 is dimensionless. It is a pure number. Scattering amplitudes at tree level do not grow with energy. Perturbation theory converges. The theory is renormalisable. This is a direct consequence of what Stages I–VI established: the electromagnetic coupling carries no intrinsic scale.

Gravity: the coupling G = 1/MPl² is dimensionful. In natural units it has dimensions of 1/mass². This means the effective gravitational coupling at energy E is not a fixed number but scales as:

Geff(E)  ~  E² / MPl²
Gravitational scattering grows with energy — the coupling is dimensionful

At low energies (E ≪ MPl), gravity is negligibly weak — which is why we experience it as the feeblest force. But as E → MPl, the effective coupling approaches unity, perturbation theory breaks down, and the theory becomes non-renormalisable: each order in the perturbative expansion introduces new divergences that cannot be absorbed by redefining a finite number of parameters.

The dimensionfulness of G is not a unit artefact — it is the reason quantum gravity is hard. Electromagnetism's coupling is a pure number and can be treated perturbatively at all energies (modulo the Landau pole at absurdly high scales). Gravity's coupling carries a mass scale, and this scale is where classical general relativity ceases to be a complete description.

Put differently: α tells us how strongly the electromagnetic field interacts, full stop. G tells us how strongly gravity interacts relative to the energy involved. One is a number; the other is a ratio that waits for a scale to be supplied. This is the deepest sense in which dimensions are not merely bookkeeping — they encode the structure of the quantum theory itself.

The parallel

This is precisely the same tension we have seen throughout this explainer. Just as ε₀ disappears into normalisation and only α survives, dimensional analysis in SI units makes certain structural relationships — like the fact that H and G together determine a density — obvious. Natural units reveal the invariant content but hide the scaffolding that guided us to it.

Neither representation is wrong. But they make different things visible. And knowing which structures your formalism foregrounds — and which it buries — is itself a form of physical understanding.

Dimensionful analysis builds structure; natural units reveal invariants — but hide the scaffolding.

α is a number; G is a number that waits for a scale. This is why electromagnetism is quantum-mechanically tame, and gravity is not.
