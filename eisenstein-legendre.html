Legendre's Theorem in Practice

Which Ellipses Have Rational Points?

Applying the classical criterion for ternary quadratic forms to the Eisenstein norm x² − xy + y² = d, and generating the triple families that result.

The Setup

The Eisenstein norm form x² − xy + y² defines a family of ellipses in the plane, one for each positive integer d. When d = 1, the curve passes through six lattice points — the units of the Eisenstein integers ℤ[ω] — and a line of rational slope through any one of them sweeps out every rational point on the curve. This is the starting point for Eisenstein-Pythagorean triples: integer solutions of X² + 3Y² = Z², generated by the same parametric mechanism that produces classical Pythagorean triples from the unit circle.

But what happens when we change the right-hand side? For which values of d does the ellipse x² − xy + y² = d contain any rational point at all? If it does, can we generate all rational points — and do the resulting integer triples form a structured family, analogous to Pythagorean triples?

The existence question has a clean answer, and it comes from a theorem that predates most of modern number theory.

Legendre's Criterion

Legendre's Theorem (1785) characterises when a homogeneous equation ax² + by² = cz² has nontrivial integer solutions. After reducing the coefficients to squarefree, pairwise coprime form, the conditions are:

Legendre's Theorem ax² + by² = cz² has a solution (x,y,z) ≠ (0,0,0) if and only if:
 (i)  ac is a square mod b
 (ii)  bc is a square mod a
 (iii) −ab is a square mod c
 (iv) a and b do not both have the opposite sign from c.

This is a local-to-global principle: three congruence conditions (one for each prime dividing abc) plus a sign condition (the "real place") together determine a global arithmetic fact. The idea matures into the Hasse–Minkowski theorem for quadratic forms, but Legendre's version is already strong enough for our purpose.

Applying It to Eisenstein Norms

To bring x² − xy + y² = d into Legendre's scope, we first complete the square: the left side becomes (x − y/2)² + 3y²/4. Substituting X = x − y/2, Y = y and clearing denominators converts the equation to the homogeneous form

X² + 3Y² = dZ²

with a = 1, b = 3, c = d (after absorbing any square factors of d into Z). Since a = 1, condition (ii) — "bc is a square mod a" — is trivially satisfied (everything is a square mod 1). Condition (i) — "d is a square mod 3" — requires d ≡ 0 or 1 mod 3. And the substantive condition (iii) asks: is −3 a quadratic residue modulo d?

By quadratic reciprocity and the Chinese Remainder Theorem, this breaks into a condition prime-by-prime. The result translates neatly into the language of Eisenstein arithmetic:

The classification
d is represented by x² − xy + y² if and only if every prime p ≡ 2 (mod 3) divides d to an even power.

Equivalently: every prime factor of d either splits (p ≡ 1 mod 3), ramifies (p = 3), or appears with even exponent (p ≡ 2 mod 3 — these are the inert primes in ℤ[ω]).

This is exactly the condition for d to be a norm in the Eisenstein integers: d = N(α) for some α = a + bω ∈ ℤ[ω]. Legendre's congruence conditions, translated through the specific form X² + 3Y² = dZ², recover the splitting behaviour of primes in ℤ[ω]. The abstract reciprocity law and the concrete factorisation theory say the same thing.

What the Triples Mean

The Pythagorean Analogy

For d = 1, the equation X² + 3Y² = Z² is the Eisenstein analogue of X² + Y² = Z² (Pythagorean triples). Classical Pythagorean triples are generated by multiplication in the Gaussian integers ℤ[i]; Eisenstein-Pythagorean triples are generated by multiplication in ℤ[ω]. The parametric formula — intersecting lines from (−1, 0) with the Eisenstein unit ellipse — is the geometric face of this algebraic engine.

For d ≠ 1, something analogous happens. The integer triples (X, Y, Z) satisfying X² + 3Y² = dZ² form what might be called level-d Eisenstein triples. They are generated by taking an Eisenstein integer α with N(α) = d — which exists precisely when Legendre's conditions pass — and multiplying it by arbitrary β ∈ ℤ[ω]. The product αβ has norm d · N(β), and when N(β) is a perfect square (i.e. β = nε for an integer n and a unit ε), the triple lands cleanly back at level d.

To get genuinely new primitive triples rather than rescaled copies, you multiply α by Eisenstein integers whose norms are perfect squares but who aren't real integers themselves — products of split primes, or more generally, elements whose argument in the complex plane is nontrivial. This is the multiplicative engine that produces infinite families at each level.

Existence vs. Generation

It's worth being precise about the division of labour. Legendre's Theorem answers the existence question: for a given d, is the projective conic X² + 3Y² = dZ² nonempty over ℚ? This is a yes/no gate — three modular conditions and a sign check. It does not produce solutions; it certifies their possibility.

Once existence is established, the parametric method takes over. Find a single rational point (x₀, y₀) on x² − xy + y² = d (equivalently, a base point on the homogeneous curve). Then every other rational point is the second intersection of the ellipse with a line of rational slope through (x₀, y₀). This generates all rational points, and hence all primitive triples, by sweeping through ℚ.

The two methods answer different questions at different scales: Legendre is a sieve that partitions the positive integers into "admissible d" and "obstructed d" before any search begins. The parametric method is a generator that exhausts the solution space for each admissible d. Between them, the problem is fully determined.

The Obstructed Cases

When Legendre says no, the obstruction is arithmetic and absolute. For d = 5, condition (iii) requires −3 to be a square mod 5; since −3 ≡ 2 mod 5 and the squares mod 5 are {0, 1, 4}, this fails. No rational point exists on x² − xy + y² = 5, and no amount of searching will find one. The prime 5, being inert in ℤ[ω] (since 5 ≡ 2 mod 3), cannot appear to an odd power in any Eisenstein norm. This is the same obstruction viewed from three different heights: a congruence failure, a quadratic residue non-existence, and a prime remaining inert in a ring extension.

For d = 2, the situation is subtler. Legendre's conditions for X² + 3Y² = 2Z² pass (the squarefree part is 2, and −3 ≡ 1 mod 2 is trivially a square). The homogeneous equation does have nontrivial solutions — for instance (1, 1, ≈ something)... actually, it doesn't: 1 + 3 = 4 ≠ 2·Z² for any integer Z. The point is that Legendre addresses the ternary form over ℤ, while representability of d by the binary form x² − xy + y² is a strictly stronger condition. The primes p ≡ 2 mod 3 are inert in ℤ[ω]: they don't split, so they aren't norms. Their squares are norms (since N(p) = p², with p viewed as an element of ℤ[ω]), and this is why d = 4 = 2² works while d = 2 does not.

Legendre vs. Representability
Legendre's Theorem certifies rational points on the projective conic — the homogeneous equation in three variables. Representability by the norm form is about integer points on the affine curve — two variables, specific right-hand side. For our form X² + 3Y² = dZ², the gap appears precisely at the inert primes: Legendre can pass while the binary form still fails, because the three-variable equation has more room to manoeuvre than the two-variable one.

Connection to the Eisenstein Ellipse

The interactive Eisenstein ellipse parametrises rational points on x² − xy + y² = 1 by sending lines of slope m through (−1, 0) and recording the second intersection. The present tool extends this in two directions: it uses Legendre's Theorem to determine, for any d, whether the corresponding ellipse x² − xy + y² = d has rational points at all; and when it does, it exhibits the primitive Eisenstein triples that encode those points.

Together, they present the complete picture: the d = 1 ellipse generates the Eisenstein-Pythagorean triples parametrically; the Legendre sieve identifies which other values of d admit their own triple families; and the arithmetic of ℤ[ω] — splitting, ramification, inertia — is the unified language that organises the whole structure.
