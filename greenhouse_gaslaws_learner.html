Greenhouse Effect, Gas Laws & the Kinetic Model

From why CO₂ warms the planet to why temperature is just molecules moving — the bridge between atmospheric physics and molecular theory.

What this section is about

These four slides connect two seemingly different parts of the IB syllabus: atmospheric physics (how greenhouse gases warm the Earth) and thermal physics (how gas molecules behave and what temperature actually means at the molecular level). The connection is real — greenhouse gas absorption is a molecular resonance phenomenon, and the kinetic model explains why temperature controls which molecules escape which atmospheres.

Slides B10–B11: the greenhouse mechanism, global warming causes and feedbacks. Slides B12–B13: gas laws, the ideal gas equation, and the kinetic model with its central result that temperature is proportional to average molecular KE.

Two Equations That Underpin Everything

The ideal gas law and the kinetic theory equation are the two pillars:

PV = nRT = NkBT

½mv² = (3/2)kBT

The first connects macroscopic quantities (P, V, T) to the amount of gas. The second connects temperature to the microscopic world — it says temperature is average kinetic energy per molecule. Together they explain gas behaviour from both ends: the container-scale and the molecule-scale.

SL B10 — Greenhouse Gases & the Greenhouse Effect

The atmosphere is 99.96% N₂ and O₂ — but these molecules are transparent to infrared. The remaining 0.04% — greenhouse gases — does almost all the thermal work.

GasFormulaKey absorption bandNote
Carbon dioxideCO₂~15 μm (bending)Main anthropogenic driver
Water vapourH₂O~6 μm, broadMost abundant GHG; natural feedback
MethaneCH₄~3.3 μm, ~7.7 μm~80× CO₂ warming per molecule (20-yr)
Nitrous oxideN₂O~4.5 μm, ~7.8 μmLong atmospheric lifetime

The mechanism: Solar radiation (mostly UV and visible) passes through the atmosphere and is absorbed by the Earth's surface. The warm surface re-emits this energy as infrared radiation (longer wavelength, lower energy per photon — Wien's law). Greenhouse gas molecules absorb this IR because it matches their vibrational resonance frequencies (bending and stretching modes). They then re-emit IR in all directions — including back toward the surface. This "recycling" of outgoing IR warms the surface above what it would be without an atmosphere.

Why don't N₂ and O₂ absorb IR? Because they are symmetric diatomic molecules — they have no permanent or transient electric dipole moment during vibration. IR absorption requires a changing dipole moment (the molecule must "look" like an oscillating charge to the electromagnetic wave). CO₂ is linear but its bending mode creates a transient dipole. H₂O is permanently polar. N₂ and O₂ are symmetric and don't interact with IR at all.

"Greenhouse gases trap heat" is misleading. They don't trap anything — they absorb and re-emit IR isotropically. Roughly half the re-emitted energy goes back toward the surface. The atmosphere becomes a less efficient radiator to space, raising the equilibrium temperature. It's an energy balance shift, not a "trap".

Q1Earth receives UV/visible in and emits IR out. Why the wavelength difference?▶
Answer

The Sun's surface is ~5800 K → peak emission at ~500 nm (visible), by Wien's law: λmax = 2.9 × 10⁻³ / T. The Earth's surface is ~288 K → peak emission at ~10 μm (mid-infrared). A cooler object emits at longer wavelengths. Total energy is conserved across the system, but each individual photon out is lower-energy than the photon in. Many more IR photons leave than visible photons arrive — this is connected to the second law and entropy.

Q2CO₂ is only 0.04% of the atmosphere. How can such a tiny fraction matter?▶
Answer

Because the absorption is wavelength-specific and very efficient. CO₂ absorbs almost 100% of outgoing IR in the 14–16 μm band — even at low concentrations. Adding more CO₂ broadens the absorption band and raises the effective altitude of emission to space, where it's colder and radiates less efficiently. It's not about the percentage — it's about the optical thickness at specific wavelengths. A thin coat of paint completely changes a surface's colour; a thin layer of CO₂ completely changes the atmosphere's IR transparency.

SL B11 — Global Warming & Climate Change

Global warming is the long-term increase in Earth's average surface temperature. Contributing factors span natural and anthropogenic:

  • Solar variability: ~0.1% fluctuation in solar output over 11-year cycles. Contributes to short-term variation but cannot explain the post-1950 warming trend.
  • Orbital (Milankovitch) cycles: changes in Earth's eccentricity, axial tilt, and precession over 10,000–100,000 year timescales. Drives ice ages. Far too slow to explain current rates.
  • Anthropogenic greenhouse gas increase: CO₂ from ~280 ppm (pre-industrial) to ~420 ppm (2024). CH₄ roughly tripled. N₂O up ~20%. This is the dominant driver of observed warming.

Positive feedback loops amplify the initial warming:

Ice-albedo feedback: warming → ice melts → darker ocean/land exposed → lower albedo → more solar absorption → more warming. This is why the Arctic warms faster than the global average.

Water vapour feedback: warming → more evaporation → more H₂O in atmosphere → stronger greenhouse effect → more warming. (H₂O is a feedback, not a forcing — humans don't directly control it.)

Ocean CO₂ solubility: warming → oceans dissolve less CO₂ → more CO₂ stays in atmosphere → more warming.

Each loop makes the original perturbation worse. Positive feedback doesn't mean "good" — it means self-reinforcing.

How do we know the current warming is anthropogenic? The IB expects you to distinguish evidence from argument. Evidence: the isotopic signature of atmospheric CO₂ matches fossil fuel combustion (depleted in ¹³C). The stratosphere is cooling while the surface warms — this fingerprint matches greenhouse forcing but not solar forcing. The rate of warming exceeds any natural cycle in the paleoclimate record. When is a scientific consensus sufficiently strong to compel policy action? Who decides?

Q1Why does melting Arctic ice increase warming, but melting icebergs don't raise sea level?▶
Answer

Two separate effects. Ice-albedo: melting any ice (sea ice or land ice) exposes darker surface → lower albedo → more absorption → more warming. Sea level: floating ice (icebergs, sea ice) already displaces its weight in water — melting it doesn't change sea level (Archimedes). Land ice (Greenland, Antarctica) is not floating — when it melts and flows into the ocean, it adds new water and raises sea level. Both types of melting affect albedo; only land ice melting affects sea level.

Q2Explain why water vapour is a feedback, not a forcing.▶
Answer

A forcing is an external perturbation to the energy balance (adding CO₂, changing solar output). A feedback is a response of the climate system to that perturbation. Humans don't directly add water vapour to the atmosphere in climatically significant amounts — it evaporates naturally. But when CO₂ warms the surface, more water evaporates, increasing the greenhouse effect further. H₂O amplifies the warming but doesn't initiate it. If CO₂ were removed, water vapour would condense out within weeks.

SL B12 — Gas Laws & the Ideal Gas Equation

Pressure = force per unit area: P = F/A [Pa = N/m²]. For a fluid column: P = ρgh.

Avogadro's number: NA = 6.022 × 10²³ molecules per mole. N = nNA.

The three empirical gas laws (each holding the other variables constant):

LawRelationHeld constantMeaning
Boyle'sPV = constT, nCompress → pressure rises
Charles'sV/T = constP, nHeat → gas expands
Gay-Lussac'sP/T = constV, nHeat at fixed volume → pressure rises

All three are special cases of the ideal gas equation:

PV = nRT    (moles form)
PV = NkBT    (molecules form)

Where R = 8.31 J/(mol·K) and kB = R/NA = 1.38 × 10⁻²³ J/K (Boltzmann constant). T must be in kelvin — always. The gas laws break down if you use °C.

Your handout labels Gay-Lussac's law as "Avogadro's Law". Strictly, Avogadro's law states V ∝ n (at constant T, P) — equal volumes of gas at the same T and P contain equal numbers of molecules. The P/T = const relation (at fixed V) is properly Gay-Lussac's law. The IB data booklet doesn't name the individual laws — it gives PV = nRT — so just use the combined equation and you can't go wrong.

T in kelvin, always. PV = nRT only works in kelvin. If T is in °C, the equation gives nonsense. Convert: T(K) = T(°C) + 273. This is the single most common error in gas law calculations.

Q1A gas at 300 K and 100 kPa occupies 2.0 L. It's heated to 600 K at constant pressure. What is the new volume?▶
Answer

Charles's law: V/T = const (at constant P). V₂ = V₁ × T₂/T₁ = 2.0 × 600/300 = 4.0 L. Doubling the kelvin temperature doubles the volume.

Q2How many molecules are in 1.0 L of gas at STP (0°C, 101.3 kPa)?▶
Answer

PV = NkBT → N = PV/(kBT) = (101 300 × 1.0 × 10⁻³) / (1.38 × 10⁻²³ × 273)

N = 101.3 / 3.767 × 10⁻²¹ = 2.69 × 10²² molecules.

This is the Loschmidt number per litre — a useful reference value.

Q3A fixed container of gas at 20°C has pressure 150 kPa. It's heated to 80°C. What is the new pressure?▶
Answer

Convert to kelvin: T₁ = 293 K, T₂ = 353 K. Fixed volume: P/T = const.

P₂ = P₁ × T₂/T₁ = 150 × 353/293 = 181 kPa.

Note: if you used °C you'd get 150 × 80/20 = 600 kPa — completely wrong. Kelvin is non-negotiable.

SL B13 — Kinetic Model of an Ideal Gas

The kinetic model connects the macroscopic behaviour of gases (pressure, temperature) to the microscopic behaviour of molecules. Assumptions of the ideal gas model:

  • Molecules are point masses — they have mass but negligible volume.
  • No intermolecular forces except during collisions.
  • Collisions are perfectly elastic (no kinetic energy lost).
  • The duration of collisions is negligible compared to time between collisions.
  • Molecules move randomly in all directions.

Pressure from molecular collisions:

P = ⅓ρv̄²    (ρ = density, v̄² = mean square speed)

The central result — temperature IS molecular KE:

½mv̄² = (3/2)kBT

This is the microscopic definition of temperature: it's proportional to the average translational kinetic energy per molecule. At T = 0 K, all molecular motion ceases (classically).

Total internal energy of an ideal monatomic gas: U = (3/2)NkBT = (3/2)nRT. Internal energy depends only on temperature — not on pressure or volume.

Root mean square speed:

vrms = √(3kBT/m) = √(3RT/M)

Where m = mass per molecule, M = molar mass. For dry air at 20°C (293 K), using M = 0.029 kg/mol:

vrms = √(3 × 8.31 × 293 / 0.029) ≈ 502 m/s

Air molecules at room temperature move at ~500 m/s — faster than sound (343 m/s). They don't travel far in a straight line because they collide with other molecules every ~70 nm (the mean free path).

vrms ≠ average speed. The root-mean-square speed is √(⟨v²⟩), which is slightly higher than the mean speed ⟨v⟩ for a Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution (by a factor of ~1.09). The IB uses vrms because it connects directly to kinetic energy (KE = ½mv²rms).

"Internal energy is only kinetic for an ideal gas" — true for monatomic ideal gases (3 translational DOF). For diatomic ideal gases, there are also rotational contributions: U = (5/2)nRT at moderate temperatures. The IB syllabus uses (3/2)nRT as the standard formula, which applies to monatomic gases (He, Ar, Ne).

The kinetic model is a deliberate idealisation. Real molecules have volume, real forces exist between them, and real collisions can be inelastic. The model works because these effects are small for dilute gases at moderate temperatures. But near condensation (high pressure, low temperature), the ideal model fails — molecules attract each other and occupy real volume. At what point does a "useful approximation" become a "wrong model"? Is the distinction meaningful?

Q1Calculate the average KE of a single nitrogen molecule at 300 K.▶
Answer

KE = (3/2)kBT = 1.5 × 1.38 × 10⁻²³ × 300 = 6.21 × 10⁻²¹ J.

This is the same for every gas molecule at 300 K, regardless of its mass. Temperature is average KE — a heavy molecule at 300 K moves slowly, a light molecule moves fast, but both have the same average KE.

Q2At what temperature would hydrogen molecules (m = 3.34 × 10⁻²⁷ kg) have vrms equal to Earth's escape velocity (11 200 m/s)?▶
Answer

½mv² = (3/2)kBT → T = mv²/(3kB) = 3.34 × 10⁻²⁷ × (11200)² / (3 × 1.38 × 10⁻²³)

T = 3.34 × 10⁻²⁷ × 1.254 × 10⁸ / 4.14 × 10⁻²³ = ~10 100 K.

Earth's exosphere reaches ~1000 K — not high enough for bulk escape, but the Maxwell-Boltzmann tail means a small fraction of H₂ molecules do exceed vesc at any temperature. This is why Earth has lost most of its primordial hydrogen over 4.5 billion years — it leaks away molecule by molecule.

Q3If you double the kelvin temperature of an ideal gas at constant volume, what happens to (a) pressure, (b) average KE, (c) vrms?▶
Answer

(a) P doubles (P ∝ T at constant V).

(b) Average KE doubles (KE = 3/2 kBT, directly proportional).

(c) vrms increases by √2 ≈ 1.41 (vrms ∝ √T, since KE = ½mv² ∝ T → v ∝ √T).

This catches students: doubling T does NOT double speed. It doubles KE, but KE ∝ v², so v only increases by √2.
