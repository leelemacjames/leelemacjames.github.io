Volume and Balance

The sphere, the triangle, and two ways to remove a degree of freedom from a piece of arithmetic.

L. McCulloch-James

§ 1

Two constraints that look unrelated

There are two perfectly ordinary constraints in elementary mathematics that look like they have nothing to do with each other. The first is the condition that a square matrix have determinant one, $\det A = 1$, which is the defining equation of the special linear group $\mathrm{SL}$. The second is the condition that a triple of complex numbers sum to zero, $x + y + z = 0$, which cuts out a hyperplane inside $\mathbb{C}^3$ and plays the role of the Cartan subalgebra of $\mathfrak{sl}(3,\mathbb{C})$ in the theory of Lie algebras. The first is a multiplicative condition on a matrix; the second is an additive condition on a triple. They appear in different chapters of different books and feel like they belong to different categories of mathematical object.

They are, however, the same kind of constraint. Both impose codimension-one restrictions on their respective ambient spaces. Both are trace-free, in a precise sense to be developed. Both have a visible geometric residue once one passes to the subspace they define: the unit sphere on the volumetric side, the equilateral triangle on the barycentric side. And both, viewed structurally, are responses to the same question: how does one normalize the data of a finite-dimensional object so that it stops carrying scale information and starts carrying intrinsic information?

The point of this essay is to draw out the duality between these two constraints and to identify the geometric pictures behind them, with some care about which pictures sit at which structural level. The sphere and the triangle are visible objects, but they are not the constraints themselves. The constraints are the codimension-one conditions $\det = 1$ and $x + y + z = 0$. The sphere and the triangle arise as the visible orbits inside those constrained spaces, once further symmetry is imposed. Keeping the constraint level and the orbit level distinct sharpens the duality considerably.

The question to keep in view: what does it mean, structurally, for a constraint to remove one degree of freedom from a piece of arithmetic? We will see that there are two natural ways, and that the difference between them is what distinguishes volumetric from barycentric geometry.

§ 2

The volumetric side

Start with the determinant. For a $2 \times 2$ complex matrix $A$, the quantity $\det A$ measures the oriented area of the parallelogram spanned by the column vectors of $A$, interpreted as an element of the top exterior power $\bigwedge^2 \mathbb{C}^2 \cong \mathbb{C}$. The condition $\det A = 1$ is a codimension-two constraint on the eight-dimensional real Lie group $\mathrm{GL}(2,\mathbb{C})$, cutting out the six-dimensional real Lie group $\mathrm{SL}(2,\mathbb{C})$. The codimension is two because $\det A \in \mathbb{C}^\times$ has two real degrees of freedom, magnitude and phase, both removed by the unit-determinant condition.

Passing to the Lie algebra changes the picture in an instructive way. The condition $\det A = 1$ at the group level corresponds, by the identity $\det(\exp X) = \exp(\operatorname{tr} X)$, to the condition $\operatorname{tr} X = 0$ at the Lie algebra level. The trace-free elements of $\mathfrak{gl}(2,\mathbb{C})$ form the subalgebra $\mathfrak{sl}(2,\mathbb{C})$, again of real dimension six inside real dimension eight. So the volumetric condition $\det = 1$ on the group is, infinitesimally, the additive trace-free condition $\operatorname{tr} = 0$ on the algebra. Multiplicative and additive become the same kind of thing once one passes to the infinitesimal level, and this is the first hint that constraints labelled differently in different settings can have the same underlying structural character.

Where the sphere appears

The sphere is not the constraint itself. The constraint is the codimension-one (or in the complex case codimension-two) condition $\det = 1$. The sphere arises later, as a visible orbit of the further symmetries that act on the constrained space. Specifically, restricting $\mathrm{SL}(2,\mathbb{C})$ to its compact real form $\mathrm{SU}(2) \subset \mathrm{SL}(2,\mathbb{C})$ gives a group whose orbit on the projective space $\mathbb{CP}^1$ is the Riemann sphere. The Bloch sphere of two-state quantum mechanics is exactly this orbit, and it is the visible geometric residue of the volumetric constraint once one has projectivised down to a single complex line.

The structural progression is therefore

$$ \mathbb{C}^2 \;\xrightarrow{\det \,=\, 1}\; \mathrm{SL}(2,\mathbb{C}) \;\rightsquigarrow\; \mathbb{CP}^1. $$

The first arrow is the constraint, going from the unconstrained two-dimensional spinor space to the special linear group acting on it. The second arrow is projectivisation, going from the group to the visible orbit space. The sphere is what one sees at the end. The volumetric constraint is what gets one there.

Why this distinction matters

Conflating the constraint with the orbit makes the duality on the Eisenstein side look more mysterious than it is. The triangle and the sphere are visible objects in the same way: each is the geometric residue of a codimension-one condition, and each becomes visible only once the constraint has been imposed and further symmetry restricted. If one tries to match the triangle directly against the sphere as defining structures, the analogy breaks. The triangle is not the analogue of the sphere as a constraint. The triangle is the analogue of the sphere as a visible orbit.

§ 3

The barycentric side

The analogous structure on the additive side begins with the hyperplane constraint

$$ x \,+\, y \,+\, z \;=\; 0 $$

inside $\mathbb{C}^3$. This is a single complex linear equation, codimension one in $\mathbb{C}^3$ in the same sense that $\det = 1$ is codimension one (or two over $\mathbb{R}$) in $\mathrm{GL}(2,\mathbb{C})$. The hyperplane it defines is the trace-free subspace of $\mathbb{C}^3$, equivalently the Cartan subalgebra of $\mathfrak{sl}(3,\mathbb{C})$, and the natural ambient space of the $A_2$ root system. Where $\det = 1$ sets the volume of a parallelogram to a fixed value, $x + y + z = 0$ sets the centroid of a triple of points to the origin. Where the first removes a multiplicative degree of freedom, the second removes an additive degree of freedom. Both are codimension-one trace-free conditions; they live on different ambient spaces and constrain different objects.

The triangle as visible orbit

Inside the hyperplane $x + y + z = 0$, the equilateral triangle is the visible geometric residue, analogous to the sphere on the volumetric side. The triangle is not itself the constraint. It is the configuration that becomes visible once the further symmetry $\{|x| = |y| = |z| = 1\}$ is imposed on top of the hyperplane. The unique solution, up to overall rotation, is the triple $(1, \omega, \omega^2)$ of cube roots of unity, satisfying the characteristic identity

$$ 1 + \omega + \omega^2 \;=\; 0. $$

This identity is sometimes presented as the defining property of the Eisenstein roots. The point of the structural reading developed here is that it is more accurately presented as the statement that the specific triple $(1, \omega, \omega^2) \in \mathbb{C}^3$ lies on the hyperplane. The hyperplane is the constraint. The triple is one distinguished point on it, picked out by the magnitude condition. The equilateral triangle is what one sees in the complex plane when the three coordinates are plotted separately.

Why this is trace-free

The hyperplane condition has the same trace-free reading that the determinant condition has at the Lie-algebra level. Consider the cyclic permutation matrix

$$ P \;=\; \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}, \qquad P^3 \;=\; I. $$

This matrix has characteristic polynomial $\lambda^3 - 1 = 0$ and therefore three eigenvalues, which are precisely $1$, $\omega$, and $\omega^2$. The trace of $P$ as a matrix is zero, since all its diagonal entries vanish, and the trace equals the sum of the eigenvalues, so $1 + \omega + \omega^2 = \operatorname{tr}(P) = 0$. The cube-root identity is therefore the eigenvalue-level expression of the same trace-free condition $\operatorname{tr} = 0$ that defines $\mathfrak{sl}(2,\mathbb{C})$ on the volumetric side, applied here to the operator generating $\mathbb{Z}_3$. The hyperplane $x + y + z = 0$ is the image of the trace-free condition at the matrix level, lifted to a constraint on triples; the cube-root identity is its eigenvalue shadow.

The progression

The structural progression therefore parallels the volumetric one:

$$ \mathbb{C}^3 \;\xrightarrow{x \,+\, y \,+\, z \,=\, 0}\; A_2 \;\rightsquigarrow\; \{1, \omega, \omega^2\}. $$

The first arrow is the constraint, cutting the hyperplane out of the ambient $\mathbb{C}^3$. The second arrow is the orbit-restriction, going from the hyperplane to the unique magnitude-one configuration on it. The triangle is what one sees at the end. The hyperplane is what gets one there.

§ 4

The two sides side by side

With the constraint level and the orbit level kept distinct, the duality between the volumetric and barycentric sides becomes a two-row affair. At the constraint level, $\det = 1$ is paired with $x + y + z = 0$. At the orbit level, the sphere is paired with the triangle. The same trace-free principle organises both, but on different ambient spaces and with different geometric residues.

Ambient space $\mathbb{C}^2$ (spinor space) $\mathbb{C}^3$ (regular rep of $\mathbb{Z}_3$)
Constraint $\det = 1$ (codimension-one) $x + y + z = 0$ (codimension-one)
What is preserved Volume of a parallelogram Centroid of a triple of points
Residual structure $\mathrm{SL}(2,\mathbb{C})$ $A_2$ hyperplane $\subset \mathbb{C}^3$
Visible orbit $\mathbb{CP}^1$, Riemann/Bloch sphere Equilateral triangle $\{1, \omega, \omega^2\}$
Lie-algebraic shadow $\operatorname{tr} X = 0$ on $\mathfrak{gl}(2,\mathbb{C})$ $\operatorname{tr} P = 0$ for the cyclic operator $P$

The two structural progressions, displayed side by side, give the cleanest summary:

$$ \begin{array}{l} \mathbb{C}^2 \;\xrightarrow{\;\det \,=\, 1\;}\; \mathrm{SL}(2,\mathbb{C}) \;\rightsquigarrow\; \mathbb{CP}^1 \quad\text{(sphere)} \\[8pt] \mathbb{C}^3 \;\xrightarrow{\;x \,+\, y \,+\, z \,=\, 0\;}\; A_2 \;\rightsquigarrow\; \{1, \omega, \omega^2\} \quad\text{(triangle)} \end{array} $$

On the volumetric side, the global object at the end is the projective line $\mathbb{CP}^1$, on which $\mathrm{SU}(2) \subset \mathrm{SL}(2,\mathbb{C})$ acts to produce the Bloch sphere. On the barycentric side, the global object at the end is the equilateral triangle as the unique unit-norm orbit in the hyperplane. The sphere is the projective residue of the volumetric constraint; the triangle is the unit-magnitude residue of the barycentric constraint. Neither is the constraint itself.

Two structural progressions. The sphere is the visible orbit on the volumetric side; the equilateral triangle is the visible orbit on the barycentric side. The constraints themselves are codimension-one conditions on their ambient spaces. Both residues become visible only after further symmetry, projectivisation on the left, magnitude normalisation on the right.

Where the Hurwitz arithmetic comes in

The duality is suggestive at the level of pure mathematics. Its physical content begins when one asks whether the same algebra can carry both kinds of constraint at once. The chiral $\mathrm{GL}(2,\mathbb{C})$ formalism for gravity, which has the volumetric constraint built in via the $\mathfrak{sl}(2,\mathbb{C})$ summand of its gauge algebra, already carries the geometry of volume. The question is whether it can also carry the geometry of balance.

The ordinary complex summand $\mathbb{C}$ in the decomposition $\mathfrak{gl}(2,\mathbb{C}) = \mathfrak{sl}(2,\mathbb{C}) \oplus \mathbb{C}$ cannot support a barycentric structure on its own, because $\mathbb{C}$ has no canonical cyclic-three configuration whose centroid is forced to the origin. The fundamental cube root $\omega$ does sit inside $\mathbb{C}$, but the embedding is arbitrary, and the cube-root identity does not interact with the $\mathrm{GL}$ structure in any forced way. The barycentric constraint exists, but it does not interlock with the volumetric one in a way that gives the gauge algebra access to it.

The picture changes when the trace summand is replaced by the Hurwitz quaternion order $\mathbb{H}_{\mathrm{H}}$. The Hurwitz arithmetic contains a copy of the Eisenstein integers $\mathbb{Z}[\omega]$ as a commutative subring, via the embedding $\omega \mapsto \rho^2$ with $\rho = \tfrac{1}{2}(1 + i + j + k)$, and the cube-root identity $1 + \rho^2 + \rho^4 = 0$ holds inside $\mathbb{H}_{\mathrm{H}}$ as the non-commutative analogue of $1 + \omega + \omega^2 = 0$. The quaternionic version of the cyclic permutation $i \to j \to k \to i$ acts trace-freely on $\operatorname{Im}(\mathbb{H})$, and the corresponding hyperplane condition lives inside the Hurwitz arithmetic as a forced feature, not an imposed one. The Hurwitz arithmetic is the first arithmetic extension of the trace sector capable of carrying both kinds of constraint simultaneously.

A corollary on the 24-cell

The full $24$-element unit configuration $2T \subset \mathbb{H}_{\mathrm{H}}^\times$ is itself barycentric. The eight Lipschitz units $\{\pm 1, \pm i, \pm j, \pm k\}$ pair into antipodes and sum to zero; the sixteen half-integer units pair similarly. So $\sum_{u \in 2T} u = 0$ in $\mathbb{H}$, and the centroid of the $24$-cell vertex configuration is the origin. The barycentric reading therefore extends from the embedded Eisenstein cube roots to the full Hurwitz unit group, with the $24$-cell as the four-dimensional analogue of the equilateral triangle, the visible orbit on a much larger barycentric hyperplane.

§ 5

Where this lives

The duality between the volumetric and barycentric constraints is, by itself, a structural observation about elementary mathematics. The determinant condition $\det = 1$ on $\mathrm{GL}$ and the hyperplane condition $x + y + z = 0$ on $\mathbb{C}^3$ are both codimension-one trace-free conditions, with the sphere and the triangle as their respective visible orbits. The infinitesimal-versus-additive correspondence between $\det$ and $\operatorname{tr}$ is the technical bridge between the multiplicative and additive descriptions; the sphere-and-triangle picture is the geometric residue.

Beyond the duality as such, the observation has a use. The deeper claim suggested by it can be stated as follows. The chiral first-order formalism for gravity, due to Plebański and developed in the Penrose-Rindler spinor tradition, already carries the geometry of volume through the determinant in its $\mathfrak{sl}(2,\mathbb{C})$ summand. The Hurwitz extension of the abelian trace summand of $\mathfrak{gl}(2,\mathbb{C})$ introduces the geometry of balance through the embedded Eisenstein $\mathbb{Z}_3$. The extended algebra $\mathfrak{sl}(2,\mathbb{C}) \oplus \mathbb{H}_{\mathrm{H}}$ therefore carries two independent conservation principles: preservation of volume on the spinor sector, preservation of centroid on the arithmetic sector. The unmodified $\mathfrak{sl}(2,\mathbb{C}) \oplus \mathbb{C}$ of the standard chiral formalism carries only the volumetric piece. The Hurwitz refinement is the first arithmetic extension of the trace sector capable of carrying both at once, and that coexistence is what makes the construction physically interesting.

The interesting consequence of carrying both is that the unit group of the Hurwitz order is the binary tetrahedral group $2T$, whose irreducible representations have dimensions $(1, 1, 1, 2, 2, 2, 3)$ saturating the constraint $\sum d_i^2 = 24$. These dimensions can be made to match the discrete skeleton of the Standard Model fermion sector, with three generations from the cyclic quotient $2T / Q_8 \cong \mathbb{Z}_3$ which is the barycentric piece, electroweak doublets from a secondary subgroup chain, and charge conjugation from the centre. The arithmetic that the volumetric-barycentric duality identifies turns out to encode a piece of physics whose discrete skeleton is otherwise mysterious.

The duality between the two trace-free constraints, with the sphere and the triangle as their visible orbits, is the piece that explains why the determinant condition and the hyperplane condition feel like they belong in the same family despite their superficial difference. The Hurwitz refinement is what allows both to live in the same algebra at the same time, and that coexistence is what makes the construction physically interesting.

§ 6

The same duality in quantum foundations

A recent construction in the foundations of quantum theory exhibits the volumetric-barycentric duality at a different structural level, and the connection is sharp enough to be worth recording. Hoffreumon and Woods have shown (Hoffreumon and Woods 2025) that quantum theory does not in fact require complex numbers, contrary to what was previously thought on the basis of a 2021 Nature article by Renou and collaborators. Their construction is built on the observation that the Kronecker product is only one possible matrix representation of the tensor product, not the only one, and that a suitably twisted alternative can be made to respect the algebra of complex numbers globally rather than merely locally. The technical content of the twist is a new tensor-product representation, $\otimes_r$, which they prove satisfies the postulates of quantum theory over the real numbers while preserving representation locality and reproducing the statistics of standard quantum theory exactly.

Brahmagupta-Fibonacci at the operator level

The scalar-level content of the Hoffreumon-Woods construction is the classical Brahmagupta-Fibonacci identity

$$ (a^2 + b^2)(c^2 + d^2) \;=\; (ac - bd)^2 + (ad + bc)^2, $$

which expresses the multiplicativity of the Gaussian norm $|a + bi|^2 = a^2 + b^2$ on $\mathbb{Z}[i]$. The Hoffreumon-Woods $\otimes_r$ is exactly this identity lifted from scalars to operators acting on the bipartite matrix space. At the level of $2 \times 2$ matrices, complex multiplication is represented by the pair $\{I, J\}$ with $J^2 = -I$, where $J = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & -1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$ stands in for the imaginary unit. The twisted tensor product respects this relation globally, in the sense that $J \otimes_r J = -I \otimes_r I$ at the operator level, mirroring $i \cdot i = -1$ at the scalar level. The discrete symmetry that their construction respects is the dihedral group $D_8$ of order eight, which is the symmetry group of the square, and which contains the cyclic group $\mathbb{Z}_4 = \langle J \rangle$ as its maximal cyclic subgroup. The fourth roots of unity are the privileged discrete subgroup of $\mathbb{C}$ that the construction takes as fundamental.

The Eisenstein companion

The Eisenstein analogue is available at the scalar level immediately. The cube root of unity $\omega$ has a $2 \times 2$ integer matrix representation

$$ W \;=\; \begin{pmatrix} 0 & -1 \\ 1 & -1 \end{pmatrix}, \qquad W^2 + W + I \;=\; 0, \qquad W^3 \;=\; I, $$

the companion matrix of $x^2 + x + 1$. The pair $\{I, W\}$ spans a real algebra isomorphic to $\mathbb{C}$ as a field but presented through the cubic balance relation rather than through the quadratic relation. The Eisenstein norm $N(a + b\omega) = a^2 - ab + b^2$ has its own multiplicativity identity, the explicit Eisenstein analogue of Brahmagupta-Fibonacci:

$$ (a^2 - ab + b^2)(c^2 - cd + d^2) \;=\; (ac - bd)^2 - (ac - bd)(ad + bc - bd) + (ad + bc - bd)^2, $$

which corresponds to the product $(a + b\omega)(c + d\omega) = (ac - bd) + (ad + bc - bd)\omega$ worked out using $\omega^2 = -1 - \omega$. So the algebra works out cleanly. A hypothetical Eisenstein quantum mechanics would lift this identity to operators in the same way Hoffreumon and Woods lift Brahmagupta-Fibonacci, twisting the tensor product so that it respects $W^2 + W + I = 0$ globally on the bipartite matrix space.

Square versus triangle, again

The discrete symmetry group that the Eisenstein construction would privilege is the dihedral group of order six, $D_6 \cong S_3$, the symmetry group of the equilateral triangle. The cyclic subgroup $\langle W \rangle \cong \mathbb{Z}_3$ corresponds to the cube roots of unity, and the Galois conjugation $\sigma: \omega \mapsto \omega^2$ acts as the reflection generator, giving the standard semidirect-product presentation $S_3 = \mathbb{Z}_3 \rtimes \mathbb{Z}_2$. So the volumetric-barycentric duality of the earlier sections reappears at exactly the structural level the construction predicts. Hoffreumon and Woods build a real quantum theory whose privileged discrete symmetry is the square $D_8$, on the volumetric, $\mathbb{Z}_4$-cyclic side. The Eisenstein analogue would build a real quantum theory whose privileged discrete symmetry is the triangle $D_6$, on the barycentric, $\mathbb{Z}_3$-cyclic side. The geometric residues match the two visible orbits identified in §4 of this essay precisely.

An obstruction worth flagging

The construction faces a non-trivial obstruction at the level of self-adjointness. The Hoffreumon-Woods notion of a "special symmetric matrix" is the image of a complex Hermitian matrix under the splitting $H = H^{\mathrm{Sy}} + i H^{\mathrm{An}}$ into real symmetric and real antisymmetric parts, and this splitting is the mechanism by which Hermiticity is encoded in the real picture. The Eisenstein analogue has no immediately obvious counterpart, because the natural Galois involution on $\mathbb{Z}[\omega]$ sends $\omega$ to $\omega^2$ rather than to $-\omega$, and self-adjointness under this involution collapses to ordinary real-ness rather than producing a rich subspace. A plausible reformulation replaces Hermitian self-adjointness with cyclic invariance, taking a matrix to be "Eisenstein self-adjoint" if it commutes appropriately with the $\mathbb{Z}_3$ generator $W \otimes_K \mathbb{1}_n$ in the way that a Hoffreumon-Woods special symmetric matrix commutes with $J \otimes_K \mathbb{1}_n$. The resulting subspace would have the structure of a free $\mathbb{Z}[\omega]$-module, parallel to the $\mathbb{Z}[i]$-module structure Hoffreumon and Woods exploit. Whether the positivity-preservation and probability-reproduction theorems then go through for this subspace is a genuine open question, requiring the kind of careful analysis that occupies most of their paper.

Hurwitz as the natural unification

The reason the question matters beyond the immediate technical interest is the connection back to the Hurwitz programme of §§4–5. The binary tetrahedral group $2T \cong Q_8 \rtimes \mathbb{Z}_3$, which is the unit group of the Hurwitz quaternion order, contains both kinds of structure at once. The quaternion subgroup $Q_8$ of order eight carries the Gaussian fourth-root structure that the Hoffreumon-Woods construction builds its real quantum theory on, and the cyclic quotient $2T / Q_8 \cong \mathbb{Z}_3$ carries the Eisenstein cube-root structure that an Eisenstein-style real quantum theory would build on. The Hoffreumon-Woods construction therefore corresponds, in this reading, to the $Q_8$ half of the Hurwitz unit group, and a hypothetical Eisenstein construction would correspond to the $\mathbb{Z}_3$ half. A theory built on $2T$ itself, rather than on either of its halves separately, would naturally support both.

The asymmetric Hurwitz extension is on this reading the natural arithmetic substrate for a real-number quantum theory carrying both the Gaussian and the Eisenstein discrete symmetries at once, with both the square and the triangle present as visible orbits and both the volumetric and barycentric constraints acting on orthogonal sectors of the algebra. The two conservation principles identified in §5 are therefore not just a feature of the gauge-algebra picture for chiral gravity, on this reading. They are present at the foundations of quantum theory itself, with the asymmetric Hurwitz extension as the place where they coexist. Whether this can be made into a working construction at the level of detail that Hoffreumon and Woods achieve for the Gaussian case is a genuinely open question, and one I would not be surprised to see settled, in one direction or the other, in the next few years.
