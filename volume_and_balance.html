Volume and Balance

The sphere, the triangle, and two ways to remove a degree of freedom from a piece of arithmetic.

L. McCulloch-James

§ 1

Two constraints that look unrelated

There are two perfectly ordinary constraints in elementary mathematics that look like they have nothing to do with each other. The first is the condition that a square matrix have determinant one, $\det A = 1$, which is the defining equation of the special linear group $\mathrm{SL}$. The second is the condition that the three cube roots of unity sum to zero, $1 + \omega + \omega^2 = 0$, where $\omega = e^{2\pi i / 3}$. The first is a multiplicative condition on a matrix; the second is an additive condition on a finite list of complex numbers. They appear in different chapters of different books. They feel like they belong to different categories of mathematical object.

They are, however, the same kind of constraint. Both impose trace-freeness, in a precise sense to be developed. Both remove one complex degree of freedom from an ambient space, leaving behind a fixed configuration whose internal symmetry is the residue. Both have a geometric interpretation as the locus of a single condition imposed on many vectors at once. And both, viewed structurally, are responses to the same question: how does one normalize the data of a finite-dimensional object so that it stops carrying scale information and starts carrying intrinsic information?

The point of this essay is to draw out the duality between these two constraints and to identify the geometric pictures behind them. The determinant condition has the unit sphere as its fixed locus. The cube-root condition has the triangle. These two pictures, the sphere and the triangle, are doing the same structural work in different ambient spaces. Once one notices the duality, several familiar mathematical structures, including some that are studied in different parts of the literature, start to look like instances of a single underlying pattern.

The question to keep in view: what does it mean, structurally, for a constraint to remove one degree of freedom from a piece of arithmetic? We will see that there are two natural ways, and that the difference between them is what distinguishes volumetric from barycentric geometry.

§ 2

The sphere

Start with the determinant. For a $2 \times 2$ complex matrix $A$, the quantity $\det A$ measures the oriented area of the parallelogram spanned by the column vectors of $A$, interpreted as an element of the top exterior power $\bigwedge^2 \mathbb{C}^2 \cong \mathbb{C}$. The condition $\det A = 1$ fixes this area and orientation, and the locus of matrices satisfying it is the special linear group $\mathrm{SL}(2,\mathbb{C})$. This is a six-dimensional real Lie group sitting inside the eight-dimensional real Lie group $\mathrm{GL}(2,\mathbb{C})$ as a codimension-two subgroup, the codimension coming from the fact that $\det A \in \mathbb{C}^\times$ is a complex-valued condition with two real degrees of freedom (magnitude and phase).

Passing to the Lie algebra changes the picture in an instructive way. The condition $\det A = 1$ at the group level corresponds, by the identity $\det(\exp X) = \exp(\operatorname{tr} X)$, to the condition $\operatorname{tr} X = 0$ at the Lie algebra level. The trace-free elements of $\mathfrak{gl}(2,\mathbb{C})$ form the subalgebra $\mathfrak{sl}(2,\mathbb{C})$, again of real dimension six inside real dimension eight. So the volumetric condition $\det = 1$ on $\mathrm{GL}$ is, infinitesimally, the additive trace-free condition $\operatorname{tr} = 0$ on $\mathfrak{gl}$. Multiplicative and additive become the same thing once one passes to the infinitesimal level. This is a structural feature of how Lie groups and their algebras relate, and it is the first hint that volumetric constraints have an additive shadow that one might be tempted to call something else entirely.

Where the sphere comes in

Restricting to real matrices for simplicity, the constraint $\det A = 1$ on $A \in \mathrm{GL}(n,\mathbb{R})$ cuts out a smooth manifold inside $\mathrm{GL}(n,\mathbb{R})$ that is topologically homotopy-equivalent to a sphere of dimension $n^2 - 2$. So the unit sphere is doing real geometric work, not just providing an image. The volumetric constraint literally lands the configuration on a sphere, with the trace-free condition the tangent-space description of the same locus.

What the determinant captures, in physical terms, is the scale or density freedom of the matrix as a linear transformation. A matrix with $\det A = 5$ takes the unit parallelogram and stretches it into one of area five. Asking that this stretching factor be removed entirely is the move from $\mathrm{GL}$ to $\mathrm{SL}$. In the spinor formalism of relativity, the same move is the descent from the eight-dimensional $\mathrm{GL}(2,\mathbb{C})$ of arbitrary chiral transformations to the six-dimensional $\mathrm{SL}(2,\mathbb{C})$ of Lorentz transformations, and the surviving spinors transform without any complex density weight. The sphere encodes the scale information; passing to the sphere removes it; the result is the pure projective-spinorial geometry of relativity.

§ 3

The triangle

Now consider the cube roots of unity. The three solutions of $z^3 = 1$ in the complex plane are $1$, $\omega = e^{2\pi i / 3}$, and $\omega^2 = e^{4\pi i / 3}$. They sit at the vertices of an equilateral triangle inscribed in the unit circle, and they satisfy the identity

$$ 1 + \omega + \omega^2 \;=\; 0. $$

This is so basic an identity that one rarely stops to ask what it is. It is a constraint, removing one complex degree of freedom from the six-dimensional real space of triples of complex numbers. The condition $z_1 + z_2 + z_3 = 0$ on a triple $(z_1, z_2, z_3)$ is a single complex linear equation, cutting out a four-dimensional real subspace. Adding the further constraints $|z_1| = |z_2| = |z_3| = 1$ pins down the configuration to a one-parameter family, and modding out by the overall rotation gives a unique triple up to permutation: the three cube roots of unity.

What kind of constraint is $1 + \omega + \omega^2 = 0$? The first thing to notice is that it is additive, not multiplicative. It is a sum, not a product. The second thing to notice, and this is the structural observation that drives the rest of this essay, is that it is a trace-free condition, in the same sense that $\operatorname{tr} X = 0$ is a trace-free condition on the matrix $X \in \mathfrak{gl}(2,\mathbb{C})$.

To see this, consider the cyclic permutation matrix

$$ P \;=\; \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}, \qquad P^3 \;=\; I. $$

This matrix has characteristic polynomial $\lambda^3 - 1 = 0$ and therefore three eigenvalues, which are precisely $1$, $\omega$, and $\omega^2$. The trace of $P$ as a matrix is zero (it has only off-diagonal entries), and the trace equals the sum of the eigenvalues, so

$$ 1 + \omega + \omega^2 \;=\; \operatorname{tr}(P) \;=\; 0. $$

The Eisenstein identity is therefore the statement that the cyclic permutation of three things has vanishing trace as an operator. It is the trace-free condition $\operatorname{tr} = 0$ applied not to a generic matrix in $\mathfrak{gl}(2,\mathbb{C})$ but to the specific representation of $\mathbb{Z}_3$ as a permutation of three elements. The same kind of condition that defined the sphere now defines the triangle.

What the triangle encodes

Geometrically, the condition $1 + \omega + \omega^2 = 0$ says that the three roots are the unique three equal-magnitude unit vectors in $\mathbb{C}$ whose centroid is at the origin. Their balance point is the origin. If one were to physically place equal masses at the three vertices of the triangle, the centre of mass would coincide with the geometric centre. This is barycentric geometry, not volumetric. The quantity being fixed is not an area but a centroid; the configuration that achieves the fixing is not a sphere but a regular polygon.

The structural duality has come into view. The determinant fixes a volume form, the parallelogram of fixed area, the unit sphere. The Eisenstein identity fixes a centroid, the triangle of equal vertices, the cyclic configuration. Both are trace-free conditions, in the infinitesimal sense for the determinant and in the eigenvalue-sum sense for the cube roots. Both remove one complex degree of freedom from their respective ambient spaces. Both leave behind a fixed configuration whose internal symmetry is the residue of what was constrained.

§ 4

The bridge between them

The duality is suggestive, but the question naturally arises whether the sphere and the triangle ever live in the same algebra at the same time. They obviously do not in $\mathrm{GL}(2,\mathbb{C})$ alone, where the determinant gives the sphere but no canonical cube-root configuration appears. The ordinary complex plane $\mathbb{C}$ is too small to carry both kinds of constraint simultaneously, because $\mathbb{C}$ has no preferred cyclic-three subgroup of its multiplicative group with the right normalization properties. The fundamental cube root $\omega$ does sit inside $\mathbb{C}$, of course, but the embedding is somewhat arbitrary, and the cyclic structure does not interact with the $\mathrm{GL}$ structure in any forced way.

The picture changes when one moves to the quaternions. Hamilton's quaternions $\mathbb{H} = \mathbb{R}\langle 1, i, j, k\rangle$ admit a canonical cyclic permutation of the three imaginary directions $i \to j \to k \to i$, which is the analogue at the spatial level of the Eisenstein cyclic structure at the planar level. This permutation has trace zero on the three-dimensional space of imaginary quaternions, just as the matrix $P$ of the previous section had trace zero on $\mathbb{C}^3$. So the barycentric condition has a natural quaternionic analogue, and it lives precisely where the chiral $\mathrm{GL}(2,\mathbb{C})$ spinor formalism would like to put it: on the abelian summand of $\mathfrak{gl}(2,\mathbb{C}) = \mathfrak{sl}(2,\mathbb{C}) \oplus \mathbb{C}$, once that summand has been enlarged from $\mathbb{C}$ to a quaternionic arithmetic capable of supporting cyclic-three structure.

The arithmetic that does this most naturally is the Hurwitz quaternion order $\mathbb{H}_{\mathrm{H}}$, the maximal $\mathbb{Z}$-order in the rational quaternion algebra, whose unit group is the binary tetrahedral group $2T$ of order twenty-four. The Hurwitz order contains a copy of the Eisenstein integers $\mathbb{Z}[\omega]$ as a commutative subring, via the embedding $\omega \mapsto \rho^2$ where $\rho = \tfrac{1}{2}(1 + i + j + k)$ is the canonical half-integer generator. The cube-root structure of $\mathbb{Z}[\omega]$ is therefore preserved in the lift, and the trace-free condition $1 + \rho^2 + \rho^4 = 0$ inside $\mathbb{H}_{\mathrm{H}}$ is the non-commutative analogue of the Eisenstein identity.

Two fixed loci of trace-free constraints. The unit sphere fixes a volume; the triangle fixes a centroid. The first removes a multiplicative degree of freedom from a matrix; the second removes an additive degree of freedom from a triple of unit vectors.

What emerges is a structural hierarchy worth recording. The chiral spinor formalism for gravity carries the volumetric constraint via the $\mathfrak{sl}(2,\mathbb{C})$ summand of its gauge algebra, where the trace-free condition defines the Lorentz sector. The Hurwitz arithmetic, brought in on the abelian summand of the same gauge algebra, carries the barycentric constraint via the embedded cube-root structure of $\rho^2 \in \mathbb{H}_{\mathrm{H}}$. The two trace-free conditions live on orthogonal pieces of the extended algebra. They do not interfere. Together they make the trace-free structure of the $\mathfrak{sl}(2,\mathbb{C}) \oplus \mathbb{H}_{\mathrm{H}}$ algebra genuinely two-fold: volumetric on the spinor sector, barycentric on the arithmetic sector. The original $\mathfrak{sl}(2,\mathbb{C}) \oplus \mathbb{C}$ of the unmodified chiral formalism carries only the volumetric piece, because $\mathbb{C}$ has no canonical cube-root structure to support the barycentric piece. The Hurwitz refinement is the place where both constraints coexist.

A corollary on the 24-cell

The full $24$-element unit configuration $2T \subset \mathbb{H}_{\mathrm{H}}^\times$ is itself barycentric. The eight Lipschitz units $\{\pm 1, \pm i, \pm j, \pm k\}$ pair into antipodes and sum to zero; the sixteen half-integer units pair similarly. So $\sum_{u \in 2T} u = 0$ in $\mathbb{H}$, and the centroid of the $24$-cell vertex configuration is the origin. The barycentric reading therefore extends from the embedded Eisenstein cube roots to the full Hurwitz unit group. The $24$-cell is to four-dimensional quaternionic arithmetic what the regular triangle is to two-dimensional complex arithmetic.

§ 5

Where this lives

The duality between the volumetric and barycentric constraints is, by itself, a structural observation about elementary mathematics. The determinant condition $\det = 1$ on $\mathrm{GL}$ and the Eisenstein condition $1 + \omega + \omega^2 = 0$ on the cube roots of unity are both trace-free conditions, in different ambient spaces, with the sphere and the triangle as their respective fixed loci. The infinitesimal-versus-additive duality between $\det$ and $\operatorname{tr}$ is the technical bridge between the multiplicative and additive descriptions; the unit-sphere-versus-triangle duality is the geometric shadow of the same fact.

Beyond the duality as such, the observation has a use. The chiral first-order formalism for gravity, due to Plebański and developed in the Penrose-Rindler spinor tradition, takes its gauge group to be $\mathrm{GL}(2,\mathbb{C})$ and decomposes the gauge algebra as $\mathfrak{sl}(2,\mathbb{C}) \oplus \mathbb{C}$. The first summand is the volumetric / spinor sector; the second summand carries the abelian density-weight data, and in the unmodified formalism is just a featureless copy of the complex numbers. Replacing the abelian summand by the Hurwitz quaternion order, while leaving the gravitational $\mathfrak{sl}(2,\mathbb{C})$ summand intact, equips the trace sector with the barycentric structure that ordinary $\mathbb{C}$ could not support. The two trace-free constraints then coexist on the same algebra, and the discrete arithmetic of the Hurwitz unit group $2T$ becomes available as internal structure of the resulting theory.

The interesting consequence of this move is that $2T$ has irreducible representations of dimensions $(1, 1, 1, 2, 2, 2, 3)$, saturating the constraint $\sum d_i^2 = 24$ that the group order imposes, and that these dimensions can be made to match the discrete skeleton of the Standard Model fermion sector, with three generations from the cyclic quotient $2T / Q_8 \cong \mathbb{Z}_3$, electroweak doublets from a secondary subgroup chain, and charge conjugation from the centre. The arithmetic structure that the volumetric-barycentric duality identifies turns out to encode a piece of physics whose discrete skeleton is otherwise mysterious.
