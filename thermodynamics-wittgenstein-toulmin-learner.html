Before you begin

Two kinds of failure — and how to avoid both

In thermodynamics, students often fail in one of two ways. The first is using a term loosely — saying "boiling" when they mean "evaporation," or treating temperature and heat as the same quantity. The second is stating a correct conclusion without showing why it follows — a claim without a warrant.

This learner targets both. At each stage you will be asked a question, given time to think, offered a conceptual preamble to sharpen your reasoning, and then shown the answer. The question always comes first. The preamble helps you understand what kind of thinking is required. The answer is not the destination — the reasoning structure is.

Why isn't it enough to know what a thermodynamics term means — why does the MYP care about how you use it?
Before you see the answer — consider this

Think about the difference between knowing the definition of "temperature" and being able to use it correctly in a situation you have not seen before. A definition tells you what a word means in isolation. A language-game tells you what moves the word licenses — what follows from it, what contradicts it, when it applies and when it does not.

The philosopher Ludwig Wittgenstein argued that meaning is not a mental image associated with a word. It is the pattern of use within a practice. Scientific terms have precise conditions of correct use, and those conditions are exactly what examinations probe.

The answer

The MYP Approaches and Learning criteria are not asking whether you have memorised correct definitions. They are asking whether you can operate within the practice of science: whether you can frame a question worth asking, track how your understanding changed as evidence accumulated, construct an argument that survives challenge, and engage with a perspective different from your own.

A student who can write "temperature is the average kinetic energy of particles" but then says "the bath contains more temperature than the cup" has the definition but not the use. That gap is what this learner addresses.

The two tools, simply stated

Wittgenstein asks: is the term being used correctly — does it fit the conditions of the relevant language-game? Toulmin asks: is the claim being justified — is there a warrant linking evidence to conclusion? A fluent claim without a warrant is not scientific argument. A correct term use without justification is not scientific reasoning.

Thermal equilibrium

The zeroth law — temperature is not heat

The zeroth law is often treated as a dry definition. It is worth reading it as the rule that makes thermometry possible at all — and as the source of the most persistent confusion in school thermodynamics.

Zeroth Law If A is in thermal equilibrium with B, and B with C, then A is in thermal equilibrium with C.
A metal spoon and a wooden spoon are left in the same room overnight. You touch them both. The metal spoon feels colder. Are they at different temperatures — and what does your answer imply about what "temperature" means?
How to think about this

The word "temperature" is in tension here with the word "feels." Feeling is not measuring. The sensation of cold from the metal spoon is not a report of the spoon's temperature — it is a report of the rate at which your hand is losing heat to the spoon.

Before looking at the answer: distinguish between the property a thermometer would measure, and the sensation produced by contact. These are governed by different physics. Temperature is an intensive property — it describes the state of an object regardless of what it is touching. The sensation of cold or warm is about heat flow rate — which depends on the materials in contact and their thermal conductivities.

This is the Wittgenstein move: "feels colder" and "is at lower temperature" are not the same language-game. Confusing them is a use error, not a knowledge error.

The answer

They are at the same temperature. Both spoons have been in the same room overnight, which means they have reached thermal equilibrium with the room air — and with each other. A thermometer placed on each would give identical readings.

The metal spoon feels colder because metal conducts heat rapidly away from your hand. Your hand loses energy faster to the metal than to the wood. The sensation is a measure of conduction rate — not temperature. This distinction is critical: temperature determines the direction of heat flow; thermal conductivity determines the rate.

A student who answers "the metal is colder" is using "feels colder" and "is at lower temperature" as synonyms. That is a language-game error. The zeroth law is precisely what forbids it: both spoons, in equilibrium with the same room, are in equilibrium with each other.

Macroscopic description

Heat flows from a hotter body to a cooler body until thermal equilibrium is reached. Temperature predicts which direction heat flows.

Microscopic mechanism

Particles in the hotter body have greater average kinetic energy. Collisions at the boundary transfer energy from more-energetic to less-energetic particles until averages equalise.

Examiner error — language-game failure

"The bath contains more temperature than the cup." Temperature is an intensive property — it does not scale with volume. The intended quantity is thermal energy (extensive). Using "temperature" where "thermal energy" or "internal energy" is meant is a use error at the level of category.

Heat transfer

Conduction and convection — same destination, different game

Both conduction and convection transfer thermal energy. Using them as interchangeable synonyms is one of the most common language-game errors in thermodynamics. They describe different mechanisms, with different operative conditions — and the distinction matters when choosing between them for an explanation.

A student writes: "Warm air conducts upward from the radiator, heating the room." Identify the error — and explain why it matters.
How to think about this

Ask what each word requires. Conduction needs: two bodies in contact, or energy passing through a material, with no bulk movement of that material. The operative condition is a temperature gradient through a stationary medium.

Now ask: when warm air rises above a radiator, is the air stationary? No — it moves. Is energy being passed through air while the air stays still? No — the air itself is moving, carrying energy with it. That is what bulk motion means.

The student has correctly identified that heat is transferred upward. The error is in the mechanism word. The Wittgenstein question is: does "conducts" correctly describe a process where the medium itself moves?

The answer

The error is "conducts." Conduction is energy transfer through a stationary medium via particle collisions — no bulk motion of the conducting material. Above a radiator, the air is heated, expands, becomes less dense, and rises as a moving parcel — this is convection, not conduction.

Why does it matter? Because the two mechanisms have completely different dependencies. Conduction depends on thermal conductivity of the material and the temperature gradient. Convection depends on density differences, gravity, and fluid properties. If you confuse them, you cannot correctly predict how a change in conditions would affect heat transfer — whether suppressing the convection current (as in microgravity) or changing the conductive material would make a difference.

The corrected sentence: "Warm air rises by convection above the radiator, transferring thermal energy upward through the room by bulk fluid movement."

Conduction — conditions of correct use

  • Bodies in contact, or energy through a material
  • Medium remains stationary
  • Driven by temperature gradient
  • Applies in solids; also in fluids at rest
  • Error: using it when the medium moves

Convection — conditions of correct use

  • Fluid medium in bulk motion
  • Driven by density differences (often thermal)
  • Requires a fluid: liquid or gas
  • Natural convection requires gravity or equivalent
  • Error: calling it "air conduction"
Construct a Toulmin-structured argument for the claim: "Natural convection is suppressed in microgravity." What is the warrant — the logical bridge?
How to think about this

A Toulmin argument needs: Claim → Data → Warrant → Backing → Qualifier/Rebuttal. The claim is given. Your job is to supply the warrant — the mechanism that connects the data (observations about microgravity) to the claim (convection suppressed).

Start from the mechanism of natural convection: a fluid parcel is heated, expands, becomes less dense than its surroundings, and rises because the buoyant force exceeds its weight. Now ask: which part of this chain requires gravity? The buoyant force is the weight of displaced fluid minus the weight of the parcel. If g → 0, what happens to that force?

The warrant should make explicit what link in the causal chain is broken in microgravity.

The Toulmin structure
Claim
Natural convection is suppressed in microgravity.
Data
Experiments aboard the International Space Station show that candle flames burn as symmetric spheres rather than rising plumes; smoke does not rise; heated fluid does not circulate.
Warrant
Natural convection requires a buoyant force to drive the density-stratified fluid upward. Buoyant force = (ρ_fluid − ρ_parcel) × g × V. When g ≈ 0, this force vanishes — a less-dense warm parcel has no net upward force and remains in place.
Backing
Archimedes' principle for fluids; equation of state linking temperature to density; Newton's second law governing fluid parcel acceleration.
Rebuttal
Forced convection (fans, pumps) can still operate in microgravity because it does not depend on gravity-driven buoyancy. Heat transfer is not zero — conduction and radiation continue. Only natural buoyancy-driven convection is suppressed.
Buoyancy learner link

The rebuttal above is where thermodynamics and buoyancy meet. A heated fluid parcel that rises is the buoyancy learner's ship-in-water, now cast as a thermal mechanism. The language of floating becomes the language of heat transport — same physics, different domain, same operative structure.

Phase change

Evaporation, boiling, melting — one word changes the rules

All three involve phase transitions. All three absorb energy. But they describe different processes with different conditions of correct use — and confusing them leads to incorrect predictions.

TermCorrect use conditionTypical error
EvaporationSurface escape of high-energy molecules; possible at any temperature below boiling point."Liquid turns to gas only at boiling point."
BoilingBulk vapour bubble formation when vapour pressure equals external pressure."Boiling is just fast evaporation."
MeltingSolid → liquid transition; temperature may remain constant while energy is added."Adding heat always raises temperature."
A puddle of water disappears on a cool day (12°C). Your kettle boils at 100°C. A student says: "Both are the same process — liquid turning into gas." What is wrong, and what would you say to them?
How to think about this

The student is right about the outcome: in both cases, liquid water becomes water vapour. But "process" means more than outcome. Ask: where does the transition happen? In a puddle disappearing, only molecules at the surface escape — and only those with enough kinetic energy to overcome intermolecular attraction. This is a surface phenomenon.

In boiling, vapour bubbles form throughout the bulk of the liquid — interior molecules form gas pockets that push outward. That requires vapour pressure (the pressure the liquid's own vapour exerts) to be high enough to push back against the external pressure bearing down on the liquid surface.

The two processes have different operative conditions. Evaporation can happen at any temperature; boiling requires a specific pressure-temperature relationship. This is why the boiling point of water changes at altitude.

The answer

The student has used "same process" to mean "same end state." But in science, process includes mechanism and conditions — not just outcome. The language-game error is treating evaporation and boiling as one concept when they have different operative conditions.

Evaporation is a surface process: individual molecules at the liquid surface that happen to have kinetic energy exceeding the intermolecular binding energy escape into the gas phase. It can occur at any temperature because the Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution always has a high-energy tail — some molecules always have enough energy. No external pressure threshold is required.

Boiling is a bulk process: vapour pressure within the liquid builds until it equals external atmospheric pressure, allowing vapour bubbles to form and expand throughout the liquid body. This requires a specific temperature at a given pressure — the boiling point. Lower the pressure (higher altitude), and the threshold is reached at a lower temperature.

The corrected response to the student: "The outcome is the same, but the mechanisms differ. The puddle evaporates — a surface process that works at 12°C. The kettle boils — a bulk process requiring vapour pressure to match atmospheric pressure at 100°C. They are related but not identical language-games."

Why does evaporation cool the liquid left behind? Construct the warrant — the logical bridge between the microscopic mechanism and the macroscopic cooling effect.
How to think about this

Many students say "evaporation cools because the liquid loses water" or "because water is associated with coolness." These are not warrants — they are restatements of the observation with added vagueness. A warrant must name the mechanism that makes the conclusion follow.

Think about which molecules leave. Evaporation is not a random process — it is selective. The molecules that escape are those with enough kinetic energy to overcome intermolecular attraction. What does that tell you about the kinetic energy distribution of the molecules that remain?

Temperature is related to the average kinetic energy of the particles. If the molecules with the highest kinetic energy preferentially leave, what happens to the average?

The Toulmin structure — full warrant
Claim
Evaporation cools the liquid left behind.
Data
A wet surface registers lower temperature than a dry one in the same environment. Evaporation is faster in dry, moving air — and cooling is also faster.
Warrant
Molecules escape the surface only if their kinetic energy exceeds the intermolecular binding energy. These are disproportionately the high-energy tail of the distribution. Their removal lowers the average kinetic energy of those remaining — which is a fall in temperature.
Backing
Maxwell-Boltzmann kinetic energy distribution; temperature as average kinetic energy per particle.
Qualifier
The cooling effect requires actual evaporation. In saturated air (high humidity), evaporation rate approaches zero, and no cooling occurs even if the surface is wet.
Rebuttal
The skin feels cool when wet even without full evaporation — but this is conduction from a wet surface, not the evaporative mechanism. The two should not be conflated.
A heating curve for ice shows a flat plateau at 0°C while heat is being supplied. A student says: "This must be wrong — adding heat always raises the temperature." Diagnose the error and give the correct explanation.
How to think about this

The student's rule "adding heat always raises temperature" is a plausible generalisation from everyday experience — and it works in many situations. The question is: what does the rule miss?

Heat, at the particle level, can do two things: increase the kinetic energy of particles (which raises temperature), or overcome intermolecular/interionic bonds (which changes phase without raising temperature). During a phase transition, energy is going into the second task, not the first.

Ask: why doesn't the temperature rise during melting? What is the energy actually being used for? And what term describes the energy required for a phase transition at constant temperature?

The answer

The student's rule is a generalisation that breaks down at a phase transition. The error is applying a true-but-scoped rule outside its scope: "adding heat raises temperature" is true only when no phase change is occurring.

During melting, the supplied energy is used to break the regular lattice structure of the solid — disrupting the intermolecular bonds that hold ice in its crystalline arrangement. This energy increases the potential energy of the system, not the kinetic energy. Since temperature measures average kinetic energy, it does not rise while bonds are being broken.

The energy required to melt a substance at constant temperature is called the specific latent heat of fusion. For water it is approximately 334 kJ/kg. Only after all the solid has melted does additional heat raise the temperature of the resulting liquid.

The plateau on the heating curve is not an error — it is direct evidence of latent heat, and one of the strongest macroscopic signatures of the particle model of matter.

Kinetic theory made visible

Brownian motion — the microscopic world leaks through

Brownian motion is the strongest bridge concept in this learner. It is directly observable but only explicable by appeal to invisible molecular collisions. Getting the explanation right requires using "random," "collision," and "uneven force" correctly — and then justifying the claim with a warrant that connects what you can see to what you cannot.

You observe pollen grains in still water under a microscope. They jiggle irregularly, with no apparent pattern. A student says: "The water must have tiny currents causing this." What is wrong — and what would the correct explanation require?
How to think about this

The student's explanation is not irrational — it proposes an invisible cause for a visible effect, which is scientifically legitimate. The question is whether that cause is the right one, and whether it can be distinguished from the actual mechanism.

Consider: if tiny currents were the cause, what would you expect? A consistent drift direction (even if slow), or a pattern that reflects temperature gradients in the water. What do you actually observe? Completely random, zig-zag motion — equally likely in all directions, with no preferred drift.

Now ask: what could produce force on a visible particle that is random in direction, fluctuating in magnitude, and equally likely in all directions? What kind of agent could do that — and why would it produce zig-zag motion?

The warrant must bridge the invisible agent (molecules) to the visible observation (zig-zag motion) by explaining the force mechanism.

The answer

The student's "tiny currents" hypothesis predicts a drift — a slowly varying but directional motion. Brownian motion shows no drift: the zig-zag is genuinely random, reversing direction constantly with no statistical preference. This rules out systematic currents.

The correct explanation is molecular collision. Water molecules — far too small to see under a light microscope — are in constant, rapid, random motion. At any instant, more molecules happen to collide with one face of the pollen grain than another, producing a net force in some direction. A moment later, the imbalance is in a different direction. The visible particle is buffeted by these random fluctuating forces.

The key warrant: the pollen grain is small enough that statistical fluctuations in the number of collisions from each side are significant relative to its mass. A large object (a marble in water) is also being hit by molecules, but the statistical fluctuations average out over the enormously larger surface — the net force is effectively zero at human scales.

Claim
Brownian motion is caused by random molecular collisions, not currents.
Data
Pollen grains show zig-zag motion with no preferred direction in still, temperature-uniform water.
Warrant
Water molecules strike the pollen grain from all sides, but randomly unequal numbers collide from each direction at any moment, producing a fluctuating net force with no preferred direction.
Backing
Kinetic theory: matter consists of moving particles. Statistical mechanics: fluctuations matter when the particle is small relative to molecular scales.
Rebuttal
A steady drift in one direction indicates a current or temperature gradient — not pure Brownian motion. The genuine Brownian signal is directionless and increases in amplitude as particle size decreases or temperature rises.
Macroscopic description

Small particles show irregular, unpredictable zig-zag motion when suspended in a fluid, even when the fluid appears perfectly still.

Microscopic mechanism

Invisible molecules collide with the particle unevenly from different sides at each instant, producing a fluctuating net force. The motion is the direct signature of molecular motion.

Why Brownian motion matters for this learner

Brownian motion does exactly what a warrant does: it connects something visible to something invisible. The observation (zig-zag motion) is the data. The molecular collision mechanism is the warrant. Kinetic theory is the backing. Without the warrant, the observation is merely curious. With it, it becomes evidence for the particle model of matter — one of the most important theoretical commitments in physics.

MYP A&L · Assessment

What a high-band response actually looks like

Read this student response: "At first I thought evaporation and boiling were the same because both make liquid turn to gas. Then I learned they are different. Now I know evaporation happens below 100°C." Which band is this — and what is needed to reach 7–8?
How to think about this

Read the response against criterion B (Development of Thinking). It acknowledges that thinking changed — that is positive. It identifies a specific revision: "they are different." But ask: does it explain the mechanism of the revision? Does it evaluate the prior model — identify what was wrong about it and why? Does it show how evidence or reasoning drove the change?

Also read it against criterion C (Reasoning and Justification). Is there a claim? Is there evidence? Crucially: is there a warrant — a logical bridge between evidence and claim? And does the response anticipate or address counterarguments?

A 3–4 band response acknowledges change with limited explanation. A 7–8 band response evaluates the prior model, gives the mechanism of revision, and includes warrant and qualifier.

Band diagnosis and model response

The student response is 3–4 band for both criteria B and C. It correctly notes a change in thinking and identifies a factual distinction, but it gives no mechanism, no warrant, and no evaluation of the prior model's failure.

Here is what a 7–8 band response looks like:

Model 7–8 paragraph

"I initially treated evaporation and boiling as the same process because both involve liquid becoming gas. That classification was too coarse — it identified the outcome correctly but missed the operative distinction in mechanism and conditions. Evaporation is a surface process: individual molecules with kinetic energy above the intermolecular binding threshold escape from the liquid surface; this can occur at any temperature because the Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution always has a high-energy tail. Boiling is a bulk process: vapour bubbles form throughout the liquid only when vapour pressure equals external pressure. This pressure-dependence is why water boils at a lower temperature at altitude. The warrant for evaporative cooling — which I initially could not explain — is that escaping molecules are preferentially high-energy, so their departure lowers the average kinetic energy, and therefore the temperature, of the remaining liquid. This claim is qualified by humidity: near-saturated air suppresses evaporation rate and therefore suppresses cooling."

This paragraph: uses terms with correct conditions of use; explains how thinking changed and what was wrong with the prior model; supplies a warrant; gives backing (Maxwell-Boltzmann); and includes a qualifier. That is the 7–8 signature across criteria B and C simultaneously.

Criterion3–4 band signal7–8 band signal
A · Questioning Testable question; variables vague. "Does temperature affect evaporation?" Conceptually grounded; conditions, level, and contrast explicit. "To what extent does the Maxwell-Boltzmann high-energy tail determine evaporation rate at fixed external pressure, and how does this change when humidity suppresses the rate?"
B · Development Acknowledges thinking changed. "I learned they are different." Evaluates prior model; explains what was wrong and why; traces the revision to a specific constraint or evidence.
C · Reasoning Claim present; evidence cited; warrant absent. "Evaporation cools because water is lost." Full Toulmin structure: claim, data, warrant (mechanism), backing (theory), qualifier, rebuttal.
D · Collaboration Responds to an alternative view without integrating it. "That is an interesting point." Uses the challenge to refine the claim. "You are right that the effect depends on humidity — so the cooling claim needs to be qualified: it applies only when evaporation is not suppressed."
Final synthesis

Wittgenstein asks whether the concept is being used correctly — whether the term fits the conditions of the language-game. Toulmin asks whether the claim is justified — whether a warrant links evidence to conclusion. Thermodynamics needs both: precise language for macroscopic patterns, and kinetic theory to warrant the mechanisms beneath them. The MYP criteria operationalise exactly this demand, with observable behaviours at each band.
