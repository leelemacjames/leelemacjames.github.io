When the urn has neighbours

A single Pólya urn reinforces its own past: draw a colour, add a ball of that colour, and the early draws bias everything that follows. The limiting fraction is random, fixed once and never argued with again.

This lattice gives every site its own urn, then couples each urn to the four around it. Two pressures now act at once. The Pólya term is a site's memory of its own path through time. The Ising term is the pull of locality, a logistic response to whatever the neighbours currently show. The coupling slider sets the blend, and the regime in between is where the hybrid lives: domains that lock in early and then refuse to flip.

The model, briefly

Every site i stores two integers: b_i black balls drawn so far, and n_i total draws. Its Pólya fraction is the obvious thing, b_i / n_i, and it carries the entire path history in a single number.

At each step the site computes a draw probability that interpolates between memory and locality:

p_i = (1 - w) · (b_i / n_i) + w · σ(J · h_i)

Here h_i is the local field, the sum over the four neighbours of their centred fractions (2·frac − 1), so a neighbourhood that leans black pushes h_i positive. The function σ is the logistic squashing that turns that field into a probability, and this is exactly the Ising coupling: nearest-neighbour, alignment-favouring, temperature-controlled. The coupling J plays the role of inverse temperature.

The site then draws a colour with probability p_i and reinforces. Rather than always adding one ball, it adds r worth of a ball, so r tunes how sharply the past grips the future. Small r keeps sites pliable; large r means the first few draws are nearly destiny.

What the three regimes look like

Slide J to zero and the Ising term vanishes. Each site becomes a textbook Pólya urn, its fraction converging to its own Beta-distributed limit, and the lattice settles into salt-and-pepper noise with no spatial structure at all.

Push J high and the Ising term dominates. Neighbours align fast, domains sweep across the lattice, and you are watching something close to Glauber dynamics on the Ising model with the urn memory just along for the ride.

The middle is the point of the construction. There, a domain that forms early gets written into the path histories of all its sites, and that written-in memory resists the neighbour pressure that would otherwise erode the boundary. Domains coarsen, then freeze before they finish. The lattice keeps a record of its own youth.

Reading it as a Python loop

If you want to rebuild this in NumPy, the core update is one vectorised step: compute the centred fraction array, convolve with a four-neighbour kernel to get h, form p = (1-w)*frac + w*sigmoid(J*h), draw rand < p for the whole grid at once, then do b += r*draw and n += r. The convolution is the only line doing the Ising work, and everything else is the urn.
