§ 1 · The Language of Physics

Power Law Relations

Physics classifies natural laws by their power. A power law has the form:

y = k · xn

where k is a constant and n is the exponent (usually rational). The magic trick: plot log(y) vs log(x) and a power law becomes a straight line with slope n.

log(y) = log(k) + n·log(x)  ↔  Y = c + n·X

Good. Taking logs linearises the power law: log(kx³) = log(k) + 3·log(x). The slope on a log–log plot is the exponent. This is how physicists identify power laws from data — they plot log vs log and measure the gradient.

Why should we expect a slope of exactly 3 for tightly-packed spherical objects?
Correct. Vsphere = (4/3)πr³ ∝ D³. Since the peas are densely and uniformly packed (uniform spacing between peas, like atoms in a solid), the bulk density ρ = M/V is approximately constant. So M = ρV ∝ D³. This is the pea law — and the same logic applies to rocks, planets, and neutron stars.
Think about the volume formula: V = (4/3)πr³. Volume scales as the cube of linear size. If density is constant (same packing everywhere), then M ∝ V ∝ D³. The slope 3 comes from 3D geometry.

📌 The Big Picture: Atoms themselves pack like peas. That's why planets, asteroids, molecules, and humans all lie along a slope-3 line on a mass–size diagram of the universe. They are all made of closely-packed atoms with roughly atomic density (~10³ kg/m³). The slope 3 is the fingerprint of solid, dense matter.

The slope is ~2.38 in the fit (≈ 5/2 = 2.5 theoretically). Why is the slope less than 3?
Precisely. As the ball gets bigger, a larger fraction of its volume is air (trapped in the hierarchical folds). The effective bulk density is not constant — it falls as D grows. That's the fractal signature: ρ_eff ∝ D−1/2, so M = ρ·D³ ∝ D2.5. The exponent encodes the fractal dimension.
Think about what changes as you add more paper sheets to the ball. The outer layers trap more air. The density is not uniform — it gets effectively lower as the ball grows. That's why mass scales as D2.5 rather than D³.

What is a fractal dimension?

For a solid 3D sphere: mass ∝ D³ → dimension = 3
For a flat 2D sheet: mass ∝ D² → dimension = 2
For crumpled paper: mass ∝ D2.5 → dimension = 2.5

The crumpled ball lives between 2D and 3D. It's more than a sheet but less than a solid. This is a fractal dimension — and it arises naturally when you have structure at every scale.

§ 4 · The Signature of Life

Is Life More Like Paper Than Peas?

On the cosmic mass–size diagram, dead inert things (planets, asteroids, stars) follow slope 3 — like bagged peas. But what about living things?

💀 Dead–Inert (slope ≈ 3)

Clusters of galaxies, stars, planets, moons, asteroids, comets — all close-packed matter, all same atomic density.

M ∝ D³

🌿 Living–Purposeful (slope > 3?)

Trees, humans, insects, bacteria — life builds hierarchical internal structure: cells → tissues → organs → organisms.

M ∝ D?

Life organises itself into purposeful organs with structure at every scale — like crumpled paper. Lungs branch at 23 levels. Neural dendrites ramify fractally. Vasculature supplies every cell via hierarchical tubes.

Kleiber's Law: metabolic rate scales as M3/4

Max Kleiber found in 1932 that the basal metabolic rate of mammals follows:

BMR = k · M3/4

This holds across 8 orders of magnitude — from a mouse to a blue whale. The slope on a log–log plot is 3/4, not 1 (as you'd expect for simple scaling). Why?

Kleiber's Law: log(BMR) vs log(Mass). Slope = 3/4 across all mammals.
If metabolic rate scales as M3/4 and lifespan ∝ M/BMR, what does lifespan scale as?
Correct: tlife = M / BMR = M / (kM3/4) = M1/4. An elephant lives longer than a mouse — roughly in proportion to M1/4. But here's the remarkable twist: if you count total heartbeats over a lifetime, it's approximately the same for all mammals (~1.5 billion). Life burns at a fractal rate, not a linear one.
Lifespan ∝ M / BMR. If BMR ∝ M3/4, then lifespan ∝ M / M3/4 = M1/4. Bigger animals live longer — but only as M1/4, a weak dependence. The fractal geometry of the vascular system (a network with hierarchical branching) gives rise to this 3/4 law.

🫁 Why 3/4? The West–Brown–Enquist model (1997) shows that a space-filling fractal network supplying energy to all cells in a body produces BMR ∝ M3/4 naturally. The fractional exponent arises from the geometry of hierarchical branching — the same geometry that gives crumpled paper its 5/2 exponent. Life is paper, not peas.

Stars vs Animals: a striking comparison

⭐ Stars: L ∝ M7/2

Luminosity grows steeply with mass. A star 10× heavier burns 103.5 ≈ 3000× brighter. Massive stars exhaust their fuel rapidly.

Lifespan ∝ M/L ∝ M−5/2 — bigger stars die much faster.

🦣 Animals: BMR ∝ M3/4

Metabolic rate grows gently. A 10× heavier animal burns only 100.75 ≈ 5.6× more energy.

Lifespan ∝ M1/4 — bigger animals live longer.

Stars and animals are both governed by power laws — but with opposite signs for lifespan scaling. The difference is in the geometry: stars are fusion reactors, animals are fractal supply networks.

§ 5 · Reflection

Putting It Together

Answer these consolidation questions in your notes or out loud.

1. In your own words: why does a log–log plot turn a power law into a straight line?
Apply log to both sides of y = kxn. You get log y = log k + n log x. This is Y = c + nX — a linear equation in the variables (log x, log y). The gradient equals n, and the y-intercept is log k.
2. A planet has radius R. How does its mass scale with R, assuming uniform atomic density?
V ∝ R³, and ρ ≈ constant (atomic density). So M = ρV ∝ R³. On a log–log plot of mass vs radius for solar system planets, you expect slope ≈ 3.
3. A crumpled paper ball has fractal dimension ~2.5. If you double the diameter, by what factor does mass increase?
M ∝ D2.5. If D → 2D, then M → (2D)2.5 = 22.5 · D2.5 = 5.66 M. Compare with solid sphere (slope 3): doubling D multiplies mass by 2³ = 8. The paper ball gains less mass per unit diameter because more of the volume is air.
4. The "peas or paper" question is ultimately about the geometry of matter. Identify one living structure in your body that is clearly fractal (paper-like) rather than solid (pea-like).
• Lungs — 23 levels of branching from trachea to alveoli, increasing surface area enormously
• Blood vessels — capillary networks ramify to reach every cell
• Neurons — dendritic arbors branch fractally to maximise connectivity
• Intestinal villi — increase absorptive surface area through hierarchical folds
All of these are strategies for packing surface into volume — the essential trick of fractal geometry applied to life.
5. (Challenge) A mouse (mass ~20 g) has a BMR of ~0.14 W. Predict the BMR of an elephant (mass ~4000 kg) using Kleiber's law.

The central claim

The slope of the mass–size relation encodes the geometry of organisation. Dead matter packs like peas → slope 3 (integer, Euclidean). Living matter organises hierarchically like crumpled paper → fractional slope between 2 and 3. The fractal exponent is not a curiosity — it is the geometric signature of purposeful, multi-scale organisation. Life is paper, not peas.
