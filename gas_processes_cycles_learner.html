Gas Processes, PV Cycles & Molecular Kinetic Energy

How gases do work, why PV diagrams encode everything, and what temperature really means at the molecular level.

What this section is about

Gas processes are what happens when you heat, compress, or expand a gas under controlled conditions. Each process holds one variable constant and lets the other two change according to PV = nRT. This section covers the three named processes in detail (with derivations and worked examples), PV diagrams (the visual language of thermodynamics), work done by expanding gases, thermodynamic cycles, and finally the kinetic energy equation that connects temperature to molecular motion.

This is the companion to the previous learner (slides 25-36). Together they cover the full IB 3.2 specification.

The Strategy for Every Gas Process Problem

Step 1: Identify what's constant (P, V, or T). This tells you the process type.

Step 2: Start with PV = nRT and cancel the constants. What remains is the gas law for that process.

Step 3: Convert T to kelvin. Always.

Step 4: Solve. For work questions, use W = PΔV (isobaric) or area under the PV curve.

SL Isobaric Process (Constant Pressure)

In an isobaric process, pressure doesn't change. Physically: a gas in a cylinder with a weighted, frictionless piston. The weight provides constant force, so P = F/A stays fixed regardless of volume changes.

Derivation: from PV = nRT with P, n, R all constant:

V = (nR/P)T = (CONST) × T   →   V ∝ T   (Charles's Law)

Double the kelvin temperature, double the volume. On a V-T graph: straight line through the origin (kelvin) or through -273°C (Celsius).

Work done by the gas:

W = PΔV

Derivation: the gas pushes the piston with force F = PA through displacement x. Work = Fx = PAx = PΔV (since Ax = ΔV). If the gas expands (ΔV > 0), W > 0 -- the gas does work on the piston. If compressed (ΔV < 0), W < 0 -- work is done on the gas.

Q1A gas at constant pressure 200 kPa expands from 0.010 m³ to 0.035 m³. How much work does it do?▶
Answer

W = PΔV = 200 000 × (0.035 - 0.010) = 200 000 × 0.025 = 5000 J = 5.0 kJ.

Q2An ideal gas at 400 K and 2.0 L is heated isobarically until its volume is 6.0 L. What is the final temperature?▶
Answer

V ∝ T at constant P: V₁/T₁ = V₂/T₂ → T₂ = T₁ × V₂/V₁ = 400 × 6.0/2.0 = 1200 K.

Tripling the volume triples the kelvin temperature.

SL Isovolumetric Process (Constant Volume)

In an isovolumetric (or isochoric) process, volume doesn't change. Physically: a gas in a rigid, sealed container. Heat it and the pressure rises; cool it and the pressure falls.

Derivation: from PV = nRT with V, n, R constant:

P = (nR/V)T = (CONST) × T   →   P ∝ T   (Gay-Lussac's Law)

Work done: W = PΔV = P × 0 = 0. No volume change means no piston displacement, so no work. All heat added goes directly into internal energy.

This is the process used in the classic "absolute zero" experiment: measure P vs T in a sealed container, extrapolate the linear graph to P = 0, and read off T = -273°C = 0 K.

Q1A sealed container of gas at 300 K has pressure 150 kPa. It's heated to 450 K. What is the new pressure?▶
Answer

P ∝ T: P₂ = P₁ × T₂/T₁ = 150 × 450/300 = 225 kPa.

A 50% increase in kelvin temperature gives a 50% increase in pressure.

Q2During an isovolumetric process, 500 J of heat is added to a gas. How much work does the gas do?▶
Answer

W = 0. No volume change, no work. All 500 J goes into increasing the internal energy (and hence temperature) of the gas. From the first law: Q = ΔU + W → 500 = ΔU + 0 → ΔU = 500 J.

SL Isothermal Process (Constant Temperature)

In an isothermal process, temperature doesn't change. The gas must be in thermal contact with a heat reservoir that absorbs or supplies heat to maintain T.

Derivation: from PV = nRT with T, n, R constant:

PV = nRT = CONST   →   P₁V₁ = P₂V₂   (Boyle's Law)

On a PV diagram, isotherms are hyperbolas (P = const/V). Higher temperature isotherms sit further from the origin.

Why do we wait? In the syringe experiment, you compress the gas slowly and wait before recording values. This ensures the gas reaches thermal equilibrium with the surroundings -- otherwise the compression heats the gas (adiabatic effect) and the reading reflects a higher T, not Boyle's law.

Isothermal ≠ adiabatic. Isothermal: T stays constant because heat flows in/out to compensate. Adiabatic: no heat flows at all, so T changes. A slow compression in contact with the environment is roughly isothermal. A fast compression (like a diesel engine stroke) is roughly adiabatic.

Q1A gas at 4.0 atm occupies 3.0 L. It expands isothermally to 12.0 L. What is the final pressure?▶
Answer

P₁V₁ = P₂V₂ → P₂ = 4.0 × 3.0/12.0 = 1.0 atm.

Quadrupling the volume cuts pressure to a quarter.

Q2For an ideal gas undergoing isothermal expansion, does the internal energy change?▶
Answer

No. Internal energy of an ideal gas depends only on temperature (U = 3/2 nRT). If T is constant, U is constant, so ΔU = 0. From the first law: Q = ΔU + W → Q = 0 + W → Q = W. Every joule of work the gas does comes from heat flowing in from the reservoir. The gas acts as a conduit, converting heat to work with no change in its own state.

SL PV Diagrams: Reading the Map of Thermodynamics

A PV diagram is the complete description of a gas process. Every point is an equilibrium state (P, V, T). Lines connecting points are processes. The process type is determined by the line shape:

Vertical line

V = const

Isovolumetric

W = 0

Horizontal line

P = const

Isobaric

W = PΔV

Hyperbola

PV = const

Isothermal

P₁V₁ = P₂V₂

A thermodynamic cycle on a PV diagram. A→B: isochoric (vertical). B→C: isobaric (horizontal). C→A: isothermal (hyperbola). The enclosed area = net work done per cycle.
Q1On a PV diagram, how can you tell which isotherm has the higher temperature?▶
Answer

The isotherm further from the origin has the higher temperature. At any given volume, a higher-T gas has higher pressure (P = nRT/V, so P ∝ T at fixed V). At any given pressure, a higher-T gas has larger volume. So the upper-right isotherm is hotter.

SL Work Done by a Gas: Area Under the PV Curve

For any process, the work done by the gas equals the area under the process curve on a PV diagram.

  • Isobaric: area = rectangle = PΔV.
  • Isovolumetric: area = 0 (vertical line, no width).
  • Sloping line: area = trapezium or triangle + rectangle.
  • Expansion (right): W > 0. Compression (left): W < 0.
Worked Example -- Full Cycle A→B→C→A

States: A(P=2, V=10), B(P=8, V=10), C(P=8, V=25). All lines straight.

A→B (vertical, V const): W = 0 B→C (horizontal, P=8): W = PΔV = 8 × (25-10) = +120 J C→A (sloping, ΔV = 10-25 = -15, P varies from 8→2): Area under line = rectangle(2×15) + triangle(½×6×15) = 30+45 = 75 But going LEFT (compression) → W = -75 J Wcycle = 0 + 120 + (-75) = +45 J

Net work = area enclosed by the cycle = 45 J. Clockwise = positive (engine).

W = PΔV only works when P is constant (isobaric). For a sloping or curved process, P is NOT constant, so you must find the area geometrically. The most common IB mistake is applying W = PΔV to a non-isobaric process using the wrong pressure.

Compression has negative work. When ΔV < 0, the area is below the curve but counted as negative because the gas is being pushed inward. Students often forget the sign and get positive work for compression.

Q1The same cycle is run in reverse (A→C→B→A, anticlockwise). What is the net work?▶
Answer

W = -45 J. Reversing the cycle reverses every process's sign. Anticlockwise = net work done ON the gas (refrigerator/heat pump). The magnitude of the enclosed area is the same; only the sign changes.

Q2Why does the net work of a cycle equal the enclosed area on the PV diagram?▶
Answer

The forward path (expansion) has a certain area under it (+ve work). The return path (compression) has a different area under it (-ve work). The net work = (area under expansion path) - (area under compression path) = the area between the two paths = the enclosed area. This holds for any cycle shape -- rectangular, triangular, or curved.

SL Thermodynamic Cycles & Heat Engines

A thermodynamic cycle returns a gas to its original state after a sequence of processes. Because it returns to the start, ΔU = 0 for the complete cycle (internal energy is a state function).

From the first law over a cycle: Qnet = ΔU + Wnet = 0 + Wnet. So Qnet = Wnet: the net heat absorbed equals the net work done.

Heat engine: clockwise cycle. Gas absorbs Qh from hot source, rejects Qc to cold sink, does net work W = Qh - Qc. The internal combustion engine (Otto cycle) is an example.

The internal combustion engine is thermodynamics made mechanical. Four strokes: intake (isobaric expansion at atmospheric P), compression (roughly adiabatic), power (combustion + expansion), exhaust (roughly isobaric). Each stroke corresponds to a segment on a PV diagram. The area inside the loop is the useful work per cycle. Is understanding the PV diagram "the same" as understanding the engine? Or does the diagram abstract away something essential about the physical machinery?

Q1A cycle absorbs 800 J from the hot reservoir and rejects 550 J to the cold reservoir. What is the net work and the efficiency?▶
Answer

W = Qh - Qc = 800 - 550 = 250 J.

η = W/Qh = 250/800 = 31.3%.

Q2After a complete cycle, the gas returns to its original state. What has changed?▶
Answer

The gas itself hasn't changed -- same P, V, T, U. What has changed is the surroundings: heat has been transferred from the hot reservoir to the cold reservoir, and net work has been done on the environment. The gas is just the working fluid -- the intermediary that converts heat flow into mechanical work. This is the core insight of a heat engine.

SL Average Kinetic Energy & Internal Energy of an Ideal Gas

The connection between temperature and molecular motion:

E̅K = (3/2) kBT = 3RT / (2NA)

Where kB = 1.38 × 10-23 J/K (Boltzmann constant). This gives the average translational kinetic energy per molecule. It depends only on temperature, not on the type of gas, not on pressure, not on volume.

Total internal energy of N molecules of an ideal monatomic gas:

U = N × E̅K = (3/2)NkBT = (3/2)nRT

Since ideal gas molecules have no intermolecular PE, internal energy IS kinetic energy. U is proportional to kelvin temperature and amount of gas.

Worked Example -- Hydrogen gas at 175°C

Given: 2.50 mol H gas, V = 1.25 m³, T = 175°C = 448 K.

(a) Average KE per atom:

E̅K = (3/2)kBT = 1.5 × 1.38 × 10-23 × 448 = 9.27 × 10-21 J

(b) Total internal energy:

N = nNA = 2.50 × 6.02 × 1023 = 1.51 × 1024 U = N × E̅K = 1.51 × 1024 × 9.27 × 10-21 = 14 000 J ≈ 14 kJ

(c) Pressure:

P = nRT/V = 2.50 × 8.31 × 448 / 1.25 = 7450 Pa

Average KE per molecule is the same for all ideal gases at the same temperature. A heavy argon atom and a light hydrogen molecule at 300 K have the same average KE (= 3/2 kBT). The argon moves slower (vrms = √(3kBT/m) -- smaller for larger m), but its greater mass compensates exactly. Temperature is average KE, not average speed.

U = (3/2)nRT is for monatomic ideal gases only. Diatomic gases (N₂, O₂, H₂) have additional rotational degrees of freedom, giving U = (5/2)nRT at moderate temperatures. The IB syllabus uses (3/2)nRT as the standard formula.

Q1Calculate the average KE of a gas molecule at 20°C.▶
Answer

T = 20 + 273 = 293 K.

E̅K = (3/2)kBT = 1.5 × 1.38 × 10-23 × 293 = 6.07 × 10-21 J.

This is the same for any ideal gas molecule at this temperature -- helium, neon, nitrogen, whatever.

Q2If you double the kelvin temperature, what happens to: (a) average KE, (b) internal energy, (c) vrms?▶
Answer

(a) Doubles. E̅K = (3/2)kBT is directly proportional to T.

(b) Doubles. U = (3/2)nRT, also directly proportional to T.

(c) Increases by √2 ≈ 1.41. Since E̅K = ½mv² ∝ T, and v² ∝ T, we get v ∝ √T. Doubling T gives v → √2 × v. Students often say "speed doubles" -- this is wrong. KE doubles, but KE depends on v², so v only increases by √2.

Q33.0 mol of an ideal monatomic gas is at 500 K. What is its total internal energy?▶
Answer

U = (3/2)nRT = 1.5 × 3.0 × 8.31 × 500 = 18 700 J ≈ 18.7 kJ.
