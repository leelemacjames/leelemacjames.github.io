From conic sections to projectile motion

Start with the maths of conics, then move into the physics story: why school projectile motion is drawn as a parabola, why that works locally, and why the fuller inverse-square picture gives an arc of an ellipse instead.

Conic sections in a maths-class style

A conic is the curve made by slicing a cone. In school maths, they usually appear first through their standard Cartesian equations.

Circle

x² + y² = r²

All points a fixed distance from the centre.

Ellipse

x²/a² + y²/b² = 1

A stretched circle. Planetary and projectile orbits live here in the bound case.

Parabola

y = ax² + bx + c

The familiar school projectile shape in a uniform gravitational field.

Hyperbola

x²/a² − y²/b² = 1

An open curve. In orbital physics it appears for more-than-escape energy.

Hint at parametric form

Circle: x = r cos t, y = r sin t

Ellipse: x = a cos t, y = b sin t

Parabola: x = t, y = at² + bt + c

These forms are useful later because physics often tracks motion using a parameter such as time rather than solving first for y in terms of x.

Bridge to physics
In maths, the equations describe shapes. In physics, the same shapes appear as trajectories. The key question becomes: which assumptions make a moving object trace a parabola, and which make it trace an ellipse instead?

Why school projectile motion gives a parabola

In school physics, we model gravity as a uniform field: straight downward, same strength everywhere nearby. That means gravity affects the vertical direction only.

So the motion splits neatly into two independent parts:

Horizontal

x = vₓ t

No horizontal acceleration, so the speed stays constant.

Vertical

y = vᵧ t − ½gt²

Constant downward acceleration from gravity.

Exercise: eliminate t

Use the two equations above to write y directly in terms of x. Try it yourself before revealing the steps.

Method

  1. Start with x = vₓ t.
  2. Rearrange to make t the subject: t = x / vₓ.
  3. Substitute that expression for t into y = vᵧ t − ½gt².
  4. Simplify carefully.

Worked solution

x = vₓ t gives t = x / vₓ.

Now substitute into the vertical equation:

y = vᵧ(x / vₓ) − ½g(x / vₓ)²

So

y = (vᵧ/vₓ)x − g x²/(2vₓ²)

This has the form y = ax² + bx + c, so the path is a parabola.

Important idea
The reason x and y can be treated independently is that the field is being approximated as locally flat and non-converging. The force is taken to be the same at every nearby point and always vertical.

Why the approximation works near Earth

Real gravity points toward the centre of the Earth, so the field lines converge. That means the true path is part of a conic, usually an ellipse for ordinary launch speeds.

But over a short distance compared with the Earth's radius, the field barely changes. So we replace the curved, converging field by a flat local patch.

This is why a small arc of an ellipse can look almost exactly like a parabola.

Preamble: what a Taylor series does

A Taylor series replaces a complicated function by a polynomial that matches it closely near one chosen point.

A simple example is

Extension: exact conics and the escape case

In the exact inverse-square theory, conics are not approximations. They are the true orbit shapes.

Ellipse

Bound motion. Ordinary projectiles are tiny pieces of this, interrupted by the ground.

Parabola

Exactly the escape-energy case. This is a true conic in the exact theory, not the classroom approximation.

Hyperbola

More than escape energy. The object flies by and never returns.

Show the Taylor-series note

A local expansion replaces the exact field by a simpler one. First term: constant gravity, giving a parabola. Next term: a slight correction. That is why the classroom path and the true path can agree closely at first and then slowly separate.

g(R+y) = g(R) [1 − 2y/R + higher terms]
