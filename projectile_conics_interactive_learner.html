Bridge to physics
In maths, these equations describe shapes. In physics, the same shapes can describe trajectories. The key question is: which assumptions make a moving object trace a parabola, and which lead instead to an ellipse or some other conic?

Circle

A circle is the set of points a fixed distance from the centre.

Why school projectile motion gives a parabola

In school physics, gravity is modelled as a uniform field: straight downward and the same strength everywhere nearby.

This lets us split the motion into two independent parts.

Horizontal

x = vₓ t

No horizontal acceleration, so the speed stays constant.

Vertical

y = vᵧ t − ½gt²

Constant downward acceleration from gravity.

Exercise: eliminate t

Use the two equations above to write y directly in terms of x.

Method

  1. Start with x = vₓ t.
  2. Rearrange it to make t the subject: t = x / vₓ.
  3. Substitute that expression into y = vᵧ t − ½gt².
  4. Simplify carefully.

Worked solution

x = vₓ t gives t = x / vₓ.

Substitute into the vertical equation:

y = vᵧ(x / vₓ) − ½g(x / vₓ)²

Then simplify:

y = (vᵧ/vₓ)x − g x²/(2vₓ²)

This has the form y = ax² + bx + c, so the path is a parabola.

Important idea
The reason x and y can be treated independently is that the field is being approximated as locally flat and non-converging. The force is taken to be vertical and nearly the same at every nearby point.

What to notice

  • Changing vₓ stretches the path horizontally.
  • Changing vᵧ changes the maximum height.
  • For large radius R, the three curves become harder to tell apart.
  • The extra curves show how the simple classroom parabola sits inside a fuller near-Earth picture.

Why the approximation works near Earth

Real gravity points toward the centre of the Earth, so the field lines converge. That means the true path is part of a conic, usually an ellipse for ordinary launch speeds.

But over a short distance compared with the Earth’s radius, the field barely changes. So we replace the curved, converging field by a flat local patch.

This is why a small arc of an ellipse can look almost exactly like a parabola.

Preamble: what a Taylor series does

A Taylor series replaces a complicated function by a polynomial that matches it closely near one chosen point.

1/(1-u) ≈ 1 + u + u² + u³ + ...

for small u.

Another standard example is the sine function for small angles:

sin θ ≈ θ − θ³/3! + θ⁵/5! − ...

Near θ = 0, the first approximation is just sin θ ≈ θ. Adding more terms gives a better fit.

Applying the same idea to gravity

Write the height above the surface as y. Then the gravitational strength at radius R + y can be expanded near the surface as:

g(R + y) ≈ g(R)(1 − 2y/R + ...)

Keeping only the first term gives constant gravity. That is the school model. Keeping the next term gives a small correction to the parabola.

Global and local gravity picture

Globally, gravity arrows converge toward the centre of the Earth. Locally, over a small patch, those arrows are nearly parallel, which is why the school model can separate horizontal and vertical motion.

Extension: exact conics in orbital motion

In the exact inverse-square theory, conics are not approximations. They are the true orbit shapes.

Ellipse

Bound motion. Ordinary projectiles are tiny pieces of this, interrupted by the ground.

Parabola

The exact escape-energy case: right on the border between bound and unbound motion.

Hyperbola

More than escape energy. The object flies by and never returns.

Energy classification

negative energy → ellipse zero energy → parabola positive energy → hyperbola

This is a different use of the word parabola from the school projectile approximation. There, the parabola comes from assuming constant vertical acceleration in a local flat field.

Big idea
The school parabola is a local approximation. The exact orbital parabola is a true conic that appears at exactly escape energy.
