§ 1

What is a Factor?

⟡ Think First

List all the factors of 12. Then do the same for 18. Before scrolling — what do you expect them to share?

Factors of 12: 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 12

Factors of 18: 1, 2, 3, 6, 9, 18

Common factors: 1, 2, 3, 6

The highest of those common factors is 6 — that's the HCF.

A factor of n is any integer that divides n exactly — zero remainder. In Python: n % f == 0. The % operator is the modulo — it returns the remainder after division.

⚠ Common Error

Students often list multiples when asked for factors. Factors are smaller than or equal to the number; multiples grow beyond it. "6 is a factor of 12" ≠ "12 is a factor of 6."

§ 2

Highest Common Factor (HCF)

The HCF (also called GCD — Greatest Common Divisor) is the largest number that divides into both integers exactly.

Definition HCF(a, b) = largest d such that d | a and d | b
⟡ Think First

What is HCF(36, 42)? Try to reason from prime factors before checking.

36 = 2² × 3²  ·  42 = 2 × 3 × 7

Common prime factors: 2¹ × 3¹ = 6

HCF(36, 42) = 6

⚠ Common Error — Staircase question

When Candice (2-step), Gerald (3-step) and Johnny (4-step) start together, the next shared step is LCM(2,3,4) = 12, not HCF. HCF answers "what divides evenly in?" — LCM answers "when do cycles realign?"

§ 3

Lowest Common Multiple (LCM)

The LCM is the smallest positive integer that is a multiple of both numbers — the first point where both "timetables" coincide.

Key identity — connects HCF and LCM LCM(a, b) = (a × b) / HCF(a, b)
⟡ Think First

Without computing, predict: will LCM(13, 17) be large or small relative to 13 × 17 = 221? Why might that matter for a cicada?

HCF(13, 17) = 1 (both are prime, so they share no factors). Therefore:

LCM(13, 17) = (13 × 17) / 1 = 221

The LCM equals the product — the cycles won't realign for 221 years. If a predator comes every 2 or every 6 years, it hits a cicada with a prime cycle far, far less often than a cicada with a composite cycle.

§ 4

Euclid's Algorithm

The flowchart you saw asks: what is the integer part of a ÷ b? Use it to strip away the multiple of b, leaving a remainder. Repeat until the remainder is zero. The last non-zero divisor is the HCF.

⟡ Think First

Walk through HCF(133, 56) by hand. Write out each mod step. Only then check below.

133 mod 56=21
56 mod 21=14
21 mod 14=7
14 mod 7=0 ✓
HCF(133, 56) = 7

§ 5

Prime Factorisation Method

The Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic states every integer greater than 1 has a unique prime factorisation. Once you have the prime factors of each number, reading off HCF and LCM is mechanical.

HCF — take minimum powers of shared primes HCF = ∏ p^min(α,β)
LCM — take maximum powers of all primes LCM = ∏ p^max(α,β)
⟡ Think First

1092 = 2² × 3 × 7 × 13. What do you expect HCF(1092, 84) to be? Factorise 84 first.

84 = 2² × 3 × 7

1092 = 2² × 3 × 7 × 13

HCF = 2² × 3 × 7 = 84 (84 divides 1092 exactly — 1092 / 84 = 13)

LCM = 2² × 3 × 7 × 13 = 1092

§ 6

The Venn Diagram Method

Place prime factors in a Venn diagram: shared factors go in the intersection, unique factors in the outer regions.

  • HCF = product of factors in the intersection only
  • LCM = product of all factors across the whole diagram
⟡ Think First

For 30, 42 and 63 — before computing, which prime(s) do you expect to appear in all three?

30 = 2 × 3 × 5  ·  42 = 2 × 3 × 7  ·  63 = 3² × 7

The only prime in all three is 3 → HCF = 3

LCM = 2 × 3² × 5 × 7 = 630

The Survivorship Bias Twist

The slide you saw identifies a three-layer story:

Fast Thinking: Primes maximise LCM, so 13- and 17-year cycles avoid predators. Clean, satisfying, done.

Survivorship Bias: We only observe the lineages that survived. The 12-year and 15-year cicadas were eaten into extinction. Our dataset is a graveyard with the corpses removed.

The Heuristic Trap: The LCM argument is completely true — and stops short of the full truth. Like 2/7 as a decimal approximation: correct as far as it goes, but truncated.

§ 8 — Python

The Algorithm in Python

Python's math module gives you gcd directly. From Python 3.9, math.lcm is also built in. But you should be able to write both from scratch:

# Euclid's algorithm — recursive
def hcf(a, b):
    if b == 0:
        return a
    return hcf(b, a % b)

# LCM via the identity LCM = (a*b) / HCF
def lcm(a, b):
    return (a * b) // hcf(a, b)

# Cicada beetle — which colony survives longest?
colonies = {'A': 13, 'B': 15, 'C': 17, 'D': 18}
predator = 6

for name, cycle in colonies.items():
    encounter = lcm(cycle, predator)
    print(f"Colony {name}: next encounter at year {encounter}")

# Output:
# Colony A: next encounter at year 78
# Colony B: next encounter at year 30
# Colony C: next encounter at year 102
# Colony D: next encounter at year 18
⚠ IB CS note

The recursive form of Euclid's algorithm is a clean example of a recursive algorithm with a base case — a key IB CS HL concept. The call stack depth is O(log min(a,b)), which is very efficient.
