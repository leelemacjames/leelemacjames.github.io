Two Lengths for a King

A king measures distance by the Chebyshev rule, the same displacement counted as a single cheapest chain of moves. The board he crosses is a graph, and its resistance distance, a Mahalanobis-shaped quadratic form fed the Laplacian pseudo-inverse, measures the whole remaining lattice instead. Both numbers are measured between the two fixed corners, so stepping the king is only narrative; carving squares is what thins the lattice and drives the two lengths apart.

Since the start: the geodesic has moved +0 while resistance has moved +0.0000. When you carve squares off the cheapest chain, the geodesic holds while resistance climbs, and that gap is the shrinking of possibilities a single-path length cannot see.

Why not the raw adjacency matrix A? Because A has a zero diagonal and eigenvalues of both signs, so it is not positive-definite and A⁻¹ would hand back negative squared lengths. The positive-semidefinite stand-in is the Laplacian L = D − A, whose pseudo-inverse L⁺ occupies exactly the slot Σ⁻¹ holds in the Mahalanobis distance. Everything here is computed live in the browser: the geodesic by breadth-first search, the resistance by a Moore–Penrose pseudo-inverse of the current Laplacian.
