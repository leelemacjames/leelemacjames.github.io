Electromagnetic Induction

From moving charges to moving fields — and back again
IB Physics HL — Topic D.3 & D.4: Motion in EM Fields & Induction

The conceptual chain

Three discoveries, made in three decades, underpin the entire subject:

1820 — Oersted: A current-carrying wire deflects a compass needle. Moving charges create magnetic fields.

1821 — Faraday/Ampère: A current-carrying wire in a magnetic field experiences a force. Magnetic fields exert forces on moving charges. This gives us the motor.

1831 — Faraday: A changing magnetic flux through a loop induces an emf. Changing fields create currents. This gives us the generator.

These three ideas — and the minus sign (Lenz's law, which is just conservation of energy) — are the entirety of electromagnetic induction at IB level. Every equation below is a specific case of one of these three facts.

§1 What Is a Magnetic Field?
How is magnetic field strength \(B\) defined?▾

A magnetic field is a region of space where a moving charge experiences a force. The field strength (flux density) \(B\) is defined by the force it exerts:

\(B = \dfrac{F}{qv\sin\theta}\)

For a charge moving perpendicular to the field (\(\theta = 90°\)):

\(B = \dfrac{F}{qv}\)

Units: tesla (T) = kg s⁻² A⁻¹ = Wb m⁻².

Worked example: An electron (\(q = 1.6 \times 10^{-19}\) C) travels at \(2.0 \times 10^6\) m s⁻¹ perpendicular to a field and experiences a force of \(6.4 \times 10^{-14}\) N. Find \(B\).

\(B = \dfrac{6.4 \times 10^{-14}}{1.6 \times 10^{-19} \times 2.0 \times 10^6} = \dfrac{6.4 \times 10^{-14}}{3.2 \times 10^{-13}} = 0.20 \text{ T}\)
If a charge is stationary (\(v=0\)) or moving parallel to \(\vec{B}\) (\(\theta=0\)), the magnetic force is zero. Electric fields push all charges; magnetic fields only push moving charges, and only the perpendicular component of their motion.
What is the key difference between \(B\), \(\Phi\), and flux linkage \(N\Phi\)?▾
QuantitySymbolWhat it measuresUnit
Magnetic flux density\(B\)Field strength at a point (flux per unit area)T = Wb m⁻²
Magnetic flux\(\Phi = BA\cos\theta\)Total field threading one loopWb (weber)
Flux linkage\(N\Phi\)Total field threading all \(N\) turns of a coilWb (or Wb-turns)
Think of \(B\) as rain intensity (mm per hour per m²), \(\Phi\) as total rain through one bucket, and \(N\Phi\) as total rain through a stack of \(N\) buckets. Faraday's law cares about the rate of change of the total — the flux linkage.
§2 Direction Rules for Magnetic Fields
How do you find the direction of \(\vec{B}\) around a straight wire, a coil, and a solenoid?▾

All three use the same underlying rule — the right-hand grip:

1. Straight wire: Point your right thumb along the current. Your curled fingers give the direction \(\vec{B}\) loops around the wire (concentric circles).

2. Circular coil: Curl right-hand fingers in the direction of current. Thumb points along \(\vec{B}\) through the centre.

3. Solenoid: Same as coil — or use the corkscrew: turn a right-handed screw in the current direction; it advances along \(\vec{B}\).

All three rules are the same physics. The field always curls around the current in a right-handed sense. Don't memorise three separate rules — understand one rule applied to three geometries.
A long straight wire carries 5.0 A. What is the field strength 2.0 cm from the wire?▾

The field around a long straight wire (from Ampère's law, given in the data booklet for HL):

\(B = \dfrac{\mu_0 I}{2\pi r} = \dfrac{4\pi \times 10^{-7} \times 5.0}{2\pi \times 0.020} = \dfrac{2.0 \times 10^{-6}}{0.020} = 5.0 \times 10^{-5} \text{ T}\)

That's about the same strength as Earth's magnetic field — which is why a nearby current can deflect a compass.

Notice the \(1/r\) dependence. Double the distance → halve the field. This is weaker than the \(1/r^2\) fall-off of an electric field from a point charge, because the wire is a line source (field spreads over a cylinder) not a point source (sphere).
§3 The Motor Effect — Forces on Currents & Charges
What is the force on a current-carrying conductor in a magnetic field?▾
\(F = BIL\sin\theta\)

where \(\theta\) is the angle between the conductor and the field lines. Maximum at 90°; zero at 0°.

Worked example: A wire of length 0.30 m carries 4.0 A perpendicular to a 0.50 T field. Find the force.

\(F = BIL = 0.50 \times 4.0 \times 0.30 = 0.60 \text{ N}\)
This is the operating principle of every electric motor: a current-carrying coil in a magnetic field experiences a torque because the forces on opposite sides act in opposite directions. The commutator reverses the current every half-turn to keep the torque going the same way.
How does \(F = BIL\) become \(F = qvB\) for a single charge?▾

Current is moving charge: \(I = q/t\). A charge moving at speed \(v\) covers length \(L = vt\) in time \(t\). Substituting:

\(F = B \cdot \frac{q}{t} \cdot vt = qvB\)

With angle dependence: \(F = qvB\sin\theta\). This is the magnetic part of the Lorentz force.

Crucially, \(\vec{F}\) is always perpendicular to \(\vec{v}\). This means the magnetic force does no work — it changes direction, never speed. Kinetic energy is conserved.

Worked example: A proton (\(m = 1.67 \times 10^{-27}\) kg, \(q = 1.6 \times 10^{-19}\) C) moves at \(3.0 \times 10^5\) m s⁻¹ perpendicular to a 0.40 T field. Find (a) the force, (b) the acceleration.

(a) \(F = qvB = 1.6 \times 10^{-19} \times 3.0 \times 10^5 \times 0.40 = 1.92 \times 10^{-14}\) N

(b) \(a = F/m = \dfrac{1.92 \times 10^{-14}}{1.67 \times 10^{-27}} = 1.15 \times 10^{13}\) m s⁻²
This enormous acceleration doesn't increase the proton's speed — it only curves the path. The acceleration is centripetal, not tangential.
What if the charge enters at an angle to the field — not 90°?▾

Decompose the velocity into components parallel and perpendicular to \(\vec{B}\):

\(v_\parallel = v\cos\theta\) — parallel to \(\vec{B}\), feels no force, continues at constant speed.

\(v_\perp = v\sin\theta\) — perpendicular to \(\vec{B}\), experiences circular motion with radius \(r = mv_\perp/(qB)\).

The combined motion is a helix: the particle spirals along the field lines. The pitch of the helix depends on \(v_\parallel\).

This is how charged particles from the solar wind get trapped in Earth's magnetic field — they spiral along field lines toward the poles, producing the aurora. The Van Allen radiation belts are helix-shaped prisons.
§4 Force Between Parallel Current-Carrying Wires
Derive the force per unit length between two parallel wires carrying currents \(I_1\) and \(I_2\).▾

Step 1: Wire 1 creates a field at the location of wire 2 (distance \(r\) away):

\(B_1 = \dfrac{\mu_0 I_1}{2\pi r}\)

Step 2: Wire 2 (length \(L\), carrying \(I_2\)) sits in this field and feels a force:

\(F = B_1 I_2 L = \dfrac{\mu_0 I_1 I_2 L}{2\pi r}\)

Force per unit length:

\(\dfrac{F}{L} = \dfrac{\mu_0 I_1 I_2}{2\pi r}\)

Direction: Currents in the same direction → attract. Opposite directions → repel.

Worked example: Two wires 5.0 cm apart each carry 10 A in the same direction. Force per metre:

\(\dfrac{F}{L} = \dfrac{4\pi \times 10^{-7} \times 10 \times 10}{2\pi \times 0.050} = \dfrac{4 \times 10^{-5}}{0.10} = 4.0 \times 10^{-4}\text{ N m}^{-1}\)
This formula historically defined the ampere: two parallel wires 1 m apart carrying 1 A each exert \(2 \times 10^{-7}\) N per metre on each other. The 2019 SI redefinition now fixes the elementary charge instead, but the physics is unchanged.
§5 How Fields Fall Off with Distance
Why do different fields have different power laws — \(1/r\), \(1/r^2\), \(1/r^3\)?▾

The power law tells you the geometry of the source:

SourceFieldLawWhy
Point charge / point mass\(E\) or \(g\)\(\propto 1/r^2\)Field lines spread over a sphere: area \(= 4\pi r^2\)
Long straight wire\(B\)\(\propto 1/r\)Field lines spread over a cylinder: area \(= 2\pi r L\)
Magnetic dipole (bar magnet)\(B\)\(\propto 1/r^3\)Two poles partially cancel — extra factor of \(1/r\)
You can read the source's dimensionality from the exponent: 3D source (point) → \(1/r^2\), 2D source (line) → \(1/r\). The dipole's \(1/r^3\) is a point source with cancellation — deeper than geometry alone.
§6 Charged Particles in \(\vec{B}\) Fields: Orbits & the Mass Spectrometer
Why does a charged particle move in a circle, and what determines the radius?▾

The magnetic force is always perpendicular to \(\vec{v}\) → constant magnitude, perpendicular direction → circular motion. Equate magnetic force to centripetal force:

\(qvB = \dfrac{mv^2}{r} \implies r = \dfrac{mv}{qB}\)

Alternative forms using momentum \(p = mv\) or kinetic energy \(E_k = p^2/(2m)\):

\(r = \dfrac{p}{qB} = \dfrac{\sqrt{2mE_k}}{qB}\)

Practical consequences:

• Faster particles → larger radius (curve less tightly).

• Heavier particles → larger radius (more momentum at same speed).

• Stronger field or larger charge → smaller radius.

• KE is constant — magnetic force does no work (\(\vec{F} \perp \vec{v}\)).

What is the period of one orbit? Does it depend on speed?▾

Circumference \(= 2\pi r\). Speed \(= v\). Period:

\(T = \dfrac{2\pi r}{v} = \dfrac{2\pi m}{qB}\)

The \(v\) cancels — the period is independent of speed! Faster particles orbit at larger radii, but they cover the extra distance at exactly the right rate. This is called the cyclotron condition.

This is why a cyclotron works: the oscillating electric field between the dees can be tuned to a single frequency \(f = qB/(2\pi m)\), and it stays in sync with the spiralling particle regardless of how fast the particle gets. (This breaks down at relativistic speeds, where \(m\) increases — hence the synchrotron.)
How does a mass spectrometer derive the charge-to-mass ratio \(q/m\)?▾

Stage 1 — Acceleration: Ions start from rest and are accelerated through a potential difference \(V\):

\(qV = \tfrac{1}{2}mv^2 \implies v = \sqrt{\dfrac{2qV}{m}}\)

Stage 2 — Deflection: Ions enter a uniform \(B\) field and follow a semicircle of radius \(r = mv/(qB)\).

Substitute \(v\) from stage 1:

\(r = \dfrac{m}{qB}\sqrt{\dfrac{2qV}{m}} = \dfrac{1}{B}\sqrt{\dfrac{2mV}{q}}\)

Solve for \(q/m\):

\(\dfrac{q}{m} = \dfrac{2V}{B^2 r^2}\)

Worked example: Singly-charged ions (\(q = 1.6 \times 10^{-19}\) C) are accelerated through \(V = 500\) V into a field \(B = 0.20\) T. They hit the detector at radius \(r = 0.12\) m. Find the ion mass.

\(m = \dfrac{qB^2r^2}{2V} = \dfrac{1.6 \times 10^{-19} \times 0.04 \times 0.0144}{1000} = \dfrac{9.22 \times 10^{-23}}{1000} = 9.2 \times 10^{-26}\text{ kg}\)

In atomic mass units: \(9.2 \times 10^{-26} / 1.66 \times 10^{-27} \approx 55\) u — this is iron-56.

Different isotopes of the same element have the same \(q\) but different \(m\), so they land at different radii on the detector. A mass spectrometer literally draws the isotopic composition as a series of spots — each spot a different mass.
Thomson measured \(e/m_e\) for the electron. How did he interpret the result?▾

Thomson (1897) found \(e/m_e \approx 1.76 \times 10^{11}\) C kg⁻¹. The known value for hydrogen ions was \(e/m_H \approx 9.58 \times 10^7\) C kg⁻¹ — about 1,800 times smaller.

Since \(e\) was expected to be the same for both (one elementary charge), this meant:

\(m_e \approx \dfrac{m_H}{1800} \approx 9.1 \times 10^{-31}\text{ kg}\)

The cathode ray "particle" was far lighter than any atom — it was a subatomic particle. Thomson had discovered the electron.

The mass spectrometer is not just a chemistry tool — it was the instrument that cracked open the atom. Thomson's measurement is one of the founding experiments of modern physics.
§7 Magnetic Flux & Faraday's Law
What is magnetic flux \(\Phi\), and why does the angle matter?▾

Magnetic flux measures the total magnetic field threading through a surface:

\(\Phi = BA\cos\theta\)

Here \(\theta\) is the angle between \(\vec{B}\) and the normal to the surface (not between \(\vec{B}\) and the plane). Field perpendicular to the plane → \(\theta = 0\) → max flux. Field parallel to the plane → \(\theta = 90°\) → zero flux.

Units: weber (Wb) = T·m² = V·s.

The angle convention catches many students. When the field goes straight through the face of the loop, \(\theta = 0\), not 90°. Think of \(\theta\) as "how far from head-on the field is hitting the loop."
State Faraday's law and derive the motional emf \(\varepsilon = BLv\).▾

Faraday's law: The induced emf in a circuit equals the negative rate of change of magnetic flux linkage:

\(\varepsilon = -N\dfrac{d\Phi}{dt}\)

For a single loop (\(N = 1\)): \(\varepsilon = -d\Phi/dt\).

Motional emf derivation: A straight conductor of length \(L\) moves at velocity \(v\) perpendicular to a uniform field \(B\).

In time \(dt\), the wire sweeps out area \(dA = L \cdot v\,dt\). The change in flux:

\(d\Phi = B \cdot dA = BLv\,dt\)

So \(d\Phi/dt = BLv\), and the magnitude of the induced emf is:

\(|\varepsilon| = BLv\)

Worked example: An aircraft with wingspan 40 m flies at 250 m s⁻¹ through Earth's field (\(B_\text{vert} \approx 5.0 \times 10^{-5}\) T). The emf across the wings:

\(\varepsilon = BLv = 5.0 \times 10^{-5} \times 40 \times 250 = 0.50\text{ V}\)
Half a volt across a 747's wings — small, but real and measurable. This is the same physics as a generator: the wings are a conductor moving through a field.
What are the three ways to change the flux through a loop — and what does each give you?▾

Since \(\Phi = BA\cos\theta\), flux changes if you change any of the three factors:

ChangeHowResult
Change \(B\)Move a magnet toward/away from a coil\(\varepsilon = -NA\cos\theta \cdot dB/dt\)
Change \(A\)Move a wire on rails through a field\(\varepsilon = BLv\) (motional emf)
Change \(\theta\)Rotate a coil in a uniform field\(\varepsilon = NBA\omega\sin(\omega t)\) — the AC generator

The AC generator in detail: If \(\theta = \omega t\), then \(\Phi = BA\cos(\omega t)\), and:

\(\varepsilon = -N\dfrac{d\Phi}{dt} = NBA\omega\sin(\omega t)\)

Peak emf: \(\varepsilon_0 = NBA\omega\). The output is sinusoidal — this is the origin of AC.

Worked example: A 500-turn coil of area \(0.02\) m² rotates at 50 Hz in a 0.30 T field. Find the peak emf.

\(\varepsilon_0 = NBA\omega = 500 \times 0.30 \times 0.02 \times 2\pi(50) = 500 \times 0.30 \times 0.02 \times 314 = 942\text{ V}\)
§8 Lenz's Law, Flux Linkage & Applications
What is Lenz's law, and why is it just conservation of energy in disguise?▾

Lenz's law: The induced current flows in a direction that opposes the change in flux that caused it. This is the physical meaning of the minus sign in \(\varepsilon = -d\Phi/dt\).

Thought experiment: Push a north pole toward a coil. The flux through the coil increases. The induced current must create a field that opposes this increase — so it creates its own north pole facing the incoming magnet, repelling it.

Why this must be true: If the induced current aided the change, the flux would increase further → more current → more flux → perpetual motion. Conservation of energy forbids this. You must do work to push the magnet in — that work is the source of the electrical energy.

Lenz's law is not a separate postulate. It is conservation of energy applied to induction. The minus sign in Faraday's law does the same job as the minus sign in \(F = -kx\) for a spring — it ensures a restoring tendency.
How do you use Lenz's law to find the direction of the induced current in practice?▾

Step 1: Determine whether the flux through the loop is increasing or decreasing.

Step 2: The induced current must create a field that opposes that change:

• Flux increasing → induced \(\vec{B}\) opposes the external field (points opposite).

• Flux decreasing → induced \(\vec{B}\) supports the external field (points same direction).

Step 3: Use the right-hand rule to find which direction of current produces the required induced field.

Example: A bar magnet (N pole leading) is pulled away from a coil. The flux through the coil is decreasing. The induced current must oppose the decrease — it must try to maintain the flux, so it creates a field in the same direction as the departing magnet's field. By the right-hand rule, the current flows so as to make the coil face nearest the magnet into a south pole (attracting the departing north pole).

The phrase is "opposes the change" — not "opposes the field". If the field is there but constant, there is no change and no induced emf. If the field is increasing, the induced current opposes the increase (not the field itself).
What is flux linkage, and what units does it have?▾

For a coil of \(N\) turns, each turn threads flux \(\Phi\). The total flux linkage is:

\(\text{Flux linkage} = N\Phi = NBA\cos\theta\)

Units: Wb (weber) — or sometimes written "Wb-turns" for clarity, though dimensionally it's the same. Note that 1 Wb = 1 V·s.

Faraday's law in terms of flux linkage:

\(\varepsilon = -\dfrac{d(N\Phi)}{dt}\)

This is the most general form. It covers all cases — changing \(B\), changing \(A\), changing \(\theta\), or changing \(N\) (though that's rare).

A coil of 200 turns and area 0.05 m² sits perpendicular to a field that drops uniformly from 0.80 T to 0.20 T in 0.10 s. Find the induced emf and the direction of the induced current.▾

\(\theta = 0°\), so \(\cos\theta = 1\).

Change in flux per turn:

\(\Delta\Phi = \Delta B \cdot A = (0.80 - 0.20) \times 0.05 = 0.030 \text{ Wb}\)

Induced emf:

\(|\varepsilon| = N\dfrac{|\Delta\Phi|}{\Delta t} = 200 \times \dfrac{0.030}{0.10} = 60 \text{ V}\)

Direction: The flux is decreasing. By Lenz's law, the induced current opposes the decrease — it flows to maintain the field. Using the right-hand rule: the current flows in the same sense that would produce a field in the same direction as the original (supporting it).

A square loop (side 0.10 m, resistance 2.0 Ω) is pulled at 5.0 m s⁻¹ out of a uniform 0.40 T field. Find the induced emf, current, and force required to maintain constant speed.▾

Only one side of the square is cutting field lines (the trailing edge still inside the field). This side has length \(L = 0.10\) m.

Emf:

\(\varepsilon = BLv = 0.40 \times 0.10 \times 5.0 = 0.20 \text{ V}\)

Current:

\(I = \dfrac{\varepsilon}{R} = \dfrac{0.20}{2.0} = 0.10 \text{ A}\)

Force to maintain constant speed: The trailing edge carries current \(I\) in field \(B\), so it feels a retarding force (Lenz's law — the force opposes the motion):

\(F = BIL = 0.40 \times 0.10 \times 0.10 = 4.0 \times 10^{-3} \text{ N}\)

Energy check: Power input \(= Fv = 0.004 \times 5.0 = 0.020\) W. Power dissipated \(= I^2R = 0.01 \times 2.0 = 0.020\) W. ✓

The power you put in mechanically (pulling the loop) equals the electrical power dissipated as heat. This is Lenz's law as an energy accounting statement — you can't get the electrical energy for free; you must do mechanical work.
How does a simple AC generator work, and what does its output look like?▾

A rectangular coil of \(N\) turns, area \(A\), rotates at angular frequency \(\omega\) in a uniform field \(B\). The angle between the field and the coil normal is \(\theta = \omega t\).

Flux linkage: \(N\Phi = NBA\cos(\omega t)\)

Induced emf:

\(\varepsilon = -\dfrac{d(N\Phi)}{dt} = NBA\omega\sin(\omega t) = \varepsilon_0\sin(\omega t)\)

where \(\varepsilon_0 = NBA\omega\) is the peak emf.

The output is a sine wave. The emf is maximum when the coil plane is parallel to \(\vec{B}\) (flux is changing fastest — coil is sweeping through field lines edge-on). The emf is zero when the coil plane is perpendicular to \(\vec{B}\) (flux is at a maximum or minimum — momentarily not changing).

Common confusion: Max emf ≠ max flux. The emf is the rate of change of flux, which is greatest when the flux is crossing zero (sin is max when cos crosses zero). Think of it like a pendulum: max speed at the bottom (equilibrium), max displacement at the top (momentarily at rest).

To increase the peak emf, increase any of: \(N\) (more turns), \(B\) (stronger magnets), \(A\) (larger coil), \(\omega\) (spin faster). Increasing \(\omega\) also increases the frequency of the AC output.

But every motor is a generator waiting to spin, and every generator is a motor waiting to be plugged in. The physics is one thing, seen from two directions.
