Electromagnetic Induction

From moving charges to moving fields — and back again
IB Physics HL — Topic D.3: Motion in Electromagnetic Fields & D.4: Induction

The conceptual chain

The story of electromagnetism in IB Physics runs in a single logical thread:

Moving charges create magnetic fields (Oersted, 1820). A current-carrying wire produces a \(\vec{B}\) field that curls around it. Direction: right-hand rule.

Magnetic fields exert forces on moving charges (the motor effect). A charge \(q\) moving at velocity \(v\) through a field \(B\) feels a force \(F = qvB\sin\theta\), always perpendicular to both \(\vec{v}\) and \(\vec{B}\). This gives us electric motors, mass spectrometers, and circular orbits.

Changing magnetic flux induces an emf (Faraday, 1831). If the magnetic flux \(\Phi\) through a circuit changes — by moving a wire, changing the field, or rotating a coil — an electromotive force appears: \(\varepsilon = -d\Phi/dt\). This gives us generators, transformers, and the entire electrical grid.

These three ideas — and the minus sign in Faraday's law (Lenz's law) — are the whole of electromagnetic induction at IB level. Everything below is detail.

§1 Magnetic Fields & Direction Rules
What is a magnetic field, and how is field strength \(B\) defined? ▾

A magnetic field is a region of space where a moving charge experiences a force. The magnetic field strength (also called magnetic flux density) \(B\) is defined operationally:

\(B = \frac{F}{qv\sin\theta}\)

where \(F\) is the force on a charge \(q\) moving at speed \(v\) at angle \(\theta\) to the field. Units: tesla (T) = kg s⁻² A⁻¹.

For a charge moving perpendicular to the field (\(\theta = 90°\), \(\sin\theta = 1\)):

\(B = \frac{F}{qv}\)
Notice: if a charge is stationary (\(v = 0\)) or moving parallel to \(\vec{B}\) (\(\theta = 0\)), the magnetic force is zero. Magnetic fields only act on moving charges, and only on the component of motion perpendicular to the field. This is fundamentally different from electric fields, which act on all charges regardless of motion.
How do you find the direction of the magnetic field produced by a current? ▾

Three configurations, one underlying rule:

1. Straight wire — Right-hand grip rule: Point your right thumb in the direction of conventional current. Your curled fingers show the direction the \(\vec{B}\) field loops around the wire (concentric circles).

2. Circular coil — Same rule applied: Curl your right-hand fingers in the direction of current flow around the loop. Your thumb points in the direction of \(\vec{B}\) through the centre. Viewed from above: clockwise current → field points away from you; anticlockwise → field points toward you.

3. Solenoid — Right-hand corkscrew rule: Imagine turning a right-handed corkscrew in the direction of current flow through the coil. The direction the corkscrew advances is the direction of \(\vec{B}\) inside the solenoid.

All three are the same rule seen from different angles. If you can remember the straight-wire version, the others follow. The field always curls around the current in a right-handed sense.
§2 The Motor Effect
What is the force on a current-carrying conductor in a magnetic field? ▾

A wire carrying current \(I\) over a length \(L\) in a magnetic field \(B\) experiences a force:

\(F = BIL\sin\theta\)

where \(\theta\) is the angle between the wire and the field lines. Maximum force when perpendicular (\(\theta = 90°\)); zero force when parallel (\(\theta = 0°\)).

Direction: Fleming's left-hand rule (the motor rule). Point your left-hand index finger along \(\vec{B}\) (field), your middle finger along \(I\) (current), and your thumb gives the direction of \(\vec{F}\) (force/motion).

This is the operating principle of every electric motor: a current-carrying coil in a magnetic field experiences a torque because the forces on opposite sides of the coil act in opposite directions.
How does the force on a wire become the force on a single moving charge? ▾

Current is moving charge: \(I = q/t\). The length \(L\) a charge travels in time \(t\) is \(L = vt\). Substituting into \(F = BIL\):

\(F = B \cdot \frac{q}{t} \cdot vt = qvB\)

With angle dependence:

\(F = qvB\sin\theta\)

This is the magnetic part of the Lorentz force. The full Lorentz force including the electric field is \(\vec{F} = q(\vec{E} + \vec{v} \times \vec{B})\).

Key property: the magnetic force is always perpendicular to the velocity. It therefore does no work — it changes the direction of motion but never the speed.

This is why a magnetic field alone cannot accelerate a particle (increase its KE). You need an electric field for that. In a cyclotron, the magnetic field bends the path; the electric field between the dees provides the energy.
§3 Force Between Parallel Wires
Two long parallel wires carry currents \(I_1\) and \(I_2\), separated by distance \(r\). What is the force per unit length between them? ▾

Wire 1 creates a magnetic field at the location of wire 2. Wire 2 sits in that field and feels a force. The result:

\(\frac{F}{L} = \frac{\mu_0 I_1 I_2}{2\pi r}\)

where \(\mu_0 = 4\pi \times 10^{-7}\) T m A⁻¹ is the permeability of free space.

Direction:

• Currents in the same direction → wires attract.

• Currents in opposite directions → wires repel.

This formula was historically used to define the ampere: one ampere is the current that, flowing in two infinite parallel wires one metre apart, produces a force of exactly \(2 \times 10^{-7}\) N per metre of length. (The SI was redefined in 2019 to fix the elementary charge instead, but the physics is unchanged.)
§4 How Fields Fall Off with Distance
How do the different field strengths depend on distance — and why? ▾
SourceFieldLawWhy

Long straight wireMagnetic field \(B\)\(\propto 1/r\)Field lines spread over a cylinder (\(2\pi r L\))
Magnetic dipoleMagnetic field \(B\)\(\propto 1/r^3\)Dipole field — cancellation at distance

The pattern: the power of \(r\) in the denominator reflects the geometry of the source. A point source radiates in 3D → \(1/r^2\). A line source radiates in 2D (cylindrically) → \(1/r\). A dipole's two poles partially cancel → \(1/r^3\).

You can read the geometry of the source from the power law. If an exam asks "how does the force between two parallel wires vary with separation?", the answer is \(1/r\) — because each wire is a line source, not a point source.
§5 Charged Particles in Magnetic Fields
Why does a charged particle move in a circle in a uniform magnetic field? ▾

The magnetic force \(F = qvB\) is always perpendicular to \(\vec{v}\). A constant-magnitude force that is always perpendicular to the velocity is exactly the definition of centripetal force. So:

\(qvB = \frac{mv^2}{r}\)

Solving for the radius of the circular orbit:

\(r = \frac{mv}{qB}\)

This can also be written in terms of momentum \(p = mv\) or kinetic energy \(E_k = p^2/(2m)\):

\(r = \frac{p}{qB} = \frac{\sqrt{2mE_k}}{qB}\)

Key observations:

• Larger momentum → larger radius. Fast particles curve less.

• Larger charge or stronger field → smaller radius. Tighter curve.

• KE is constant — the magnetic force does no work (force ⊥ displacement).

If the particle also has a velocity component parallel to \(\vec{B}\), that component is unaffected (no force along \(\vec{B}\)). The result is a helical path — circular motion in the perpendicular plane combined with uniform motion along the field.
How does a mass spectrometer use this to measure \(e/m\)? ▾

A mass spectrometer has two stages:

Stage 1 — Acceleration: Ions are accelerated through a potential difference \(V\). The KE gained is:

\(qV = \tfrac{1}{2}mv^2 \implies v = \sqrt{\frac{2qV}{m}}\)

Stage 2 — Deflection: The ions enter a uniform magnetic field \(B\) and follow a semicircular path of radius \(r\):

\(r = \frac{mv}{qB}\)

Substituting the expression for \(v\):

\(r = \frac{m}{qB}\sqrt{\frac{2qV}{m}} = \frac{1}{B}\sqrt{\frac{2mV}{q}}\)

Rearranging for the charge-to-mass ratio:

\(\frac{q}{m} = \frac{2V}{B^2 r^2}\)

You measure \(r\) (where the ions land on the detector), you know \(V\) and \(B\), and you get \(q/m\). Different isotopes have different masses → different radii → separated spots on the detector.

Thomson used exactly this principle in 1897 to measure \(e/m\) for the electron, proving that cathode rays were particles with a specific charge-to-mass ratio — about 1800 times larger than for hydrogen ions, meaning the electron was far lighter than any atom.
§6 Magnetic Flux & Faraday's Law
What is magnetic flux, and how does it differ from magnetic field strength? ▾

Magnetic flux density \(B\) is the field strength at a point — "how many field lines per unit area pass through here." Units: T (tesla) = Wb m⁻².

Magnetic flux \(\Phi\) is the total field threading through a given area — "how many field lines pass through this loop." It depends on \(B\), the area \(A\), and the angle \(\theta\) between the field and the area's normal vector:

\(\Phi = BA\cos\theta\)

Units: weber (Wb) = T·m² = V·s.

When \(\theta = 0°\) (field perpendicular to the loop's plane, i.e. parallel to the normal): \(\Phi = BA\) — maximum flux. When \(\theta = 90°\) (field parallel to the loop's plane): \(\Phi = 0\) — no flux.

Angle convention: \(\theta\) is the angle between \(\vec{B}\) and the normal to the surface, not between \(\vec{B}\) and the plane itself. If the field is "face-on" to the loop, \(\theta = 0\) and \(\cos\theta = 1\). This catches many students.
What is Faraday's law, and where does the motional emf \(\varepsilon = BvL\) come from? ▾

Faraday's law of electromagnetic induction states:

\(\varepsilon = -\frac{d\Phi}{dt}\)

An emf is induced in a circuit whenever the magnetic flux through it changes. The magnitude of the emf equals the rate of change of flux. The minus sign is Lenz's law (see below).

For a coil of \(N\) turns, the flux linkage is \(N\Phi\), so:

\(\varepsilon = -N\frac{d\Phi}{dt}\)

Deriving motional emf: A straight conductor of length \(L\) moves at velocity \(v\) perpendicular to a uniform field \(B\). In time \(dt\), it sweeps out an area \(dA = Lv\,dt\). The change in flux is \(d\Phi = B\,dA = BLv\,dt\). Therefore:

\(\varepsilon = \frac{d\Phi}{dt} = BLv\)

(We drop the minus sign when calculating magnitude.)

Motional emf is not a separate law — it is Faraday's law applied to the special case where the flux changes because the circuit area changes (a wire moves). Every generator works on this principle: rotating a coil changes \(\theta\), which changes \(\cos\theta\), which changes \(\Phi\), which induces \(\varepsilon\).
What are the three ways to change the flux through a loop? ▾

Since \(\Phi = BA\cos\theta\), flux changes if you change any of the three factors:

ChangeExampleHow it appears
Change \(B\)Push a magnet toward a coil\(d\Phi/dt = A\cos\theta \cdot dB/dt\)
Change \(A\)Stretch or move a wire through a field\(d\Phi/dt = B\cos\theta \cdot dA/dt\) → gives \(\varepsilon = BLv\)
Change \(\theta\)Rotate a coil in a uniform field\(d\Phi/dt = BA \cdot d(\cos\theta)/dt\) → gives AC generator

In an AC generator, \(\theta = \omega t\), so \(\Phi = BA\cos(\omega t)\) and:

\(\varepsilon = NBA\omega\sin(\omega t)\)

Peak emf \(\varepsilon_0 = NBA\omega\). The output is sinusoidal — this is where AC comes from.

What is Lenz's law, and why is it just conservation of energy? ▾

Lenz's law is the minus sign in Faraday's law. It states: the induced current flows in a direction that opposes the change in flux that caused it.

If you push a north pole toward a coil, the induced current creates its own north pole facing the incoming magnet — opposing the approach. If you pull the magnet away, the coil becomes a south pole facing the retreating magnet — opposing the departure.

Why this must be true: If the induced current aided the change instead of opposing it, the flux would increase further, inducing more current, increasing flux more — an ever-growing current from nothing. This violates conservation of energy. The opposition guarantees that you must do work to change the flux, and that work is where the electrical energy comes from.

Lenz's law is not an independent postulate — it is conservation of energy applied to electromagnetic induction. The minus sign in \(\varepsilon = -d\Phi/dt\) is doing the same job as the minus sign in \(F = -kx\) for a spring: it ensures a restoring tendency.
A coil of 200 turns and area 0.05 m² is in a field that drops uniformly from 0.8 T to 0.2 T in 0.1 s. Find the induced emf. ▾

The field is perpendicular to the coil (\(\theta = 0\), \(\cos\theta = 1\)).

\(\Delta\Phi = \Delta B \cdot A = (0.8 - 0.2) \times 0.05 = 0.03 \;\text{Wb}\)
\(|\varepsilon| = N\frac{|\Delta\Phi|}{\Delta t} = 200 \times \frac{0.03}{0.1} = 60 \;\text{V}\)

By Lenz's law, the induced current opposes the decrease in flux, so it flows in a direction that tries to maintain the original field — i.e., in the same sense as the original field would require (use the right-hand rule to find the current direction).

The entire subject rests on two reciprocal facts: currents make fields, and changing fields make currents. Maxwell unified them into one theory; the IB gives you the two halves separately. But every motor is a generator waiting to spin, and every generator is a motor waiting to be plugged in. The physics is one thing, seen from two directions.
