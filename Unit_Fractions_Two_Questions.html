Two Questions About Primes in Unit Fractions

Egyptian Fractions · The Splitting Identity · An Investigation

What is a unit fraction?

A unit fraction is any fraction with 1 in the numerator: 12, 13, 15, 17 … They are the atoms from which ancient Egyptian mathematicians built every other fraction.

Unlike general fractions, unit fractions have a beautifully transparent ordering: a larger denominator always means a smaller fraction. You can read the size directly from the bottom number — no calculation required.

The unit fraction rule: for unit fractions, bigger denominator = smaller value. So 13 > 14 > 15 > … without doing any arithmetic at all.

Using unit fractions to compare general fractions

This ordering power extends to comparing any two fractions. The classic technique: express both as sums of unit fractions, then read off which is larger from the first place where they differ — just like comparing decimal expansions digit by digit.

Here is a concrete example. Which is larger, 34 or 45?

34
= 0.75
45
= 0.80

The unit-fraction way, without converting to decimals:

34 = 12 + 14
45 = 12 + 14 + 120
Both start with 12 + 14. The second has an extra 120 on top — so 45 > 34. ✓ No common denominator needed.

Classic unit fraction identities

Several unit fraction identities appear again and again in Egyptian papyri and in modern number theory. Each one expresses a relationship that is invisible when you write the fractions as decimals.

1 = 12 + 13 + 16 (the smallest Egyptian fraction for 1 with 3 distinct terms)
12 = 13 + 16
23 = 12 + 16
1 = 12 + 14 + 18 + 116 + ⋯ (geometric series — denominators all powers of 2)
Unit fraction decompositions expose arithmetic structure that decimal representations hide entirely.

Why this matters: the denominators in these sums are not random. They are controlled by multiplicative properties of the integers — in particular, by which numbers are prime and how primes relate to their neighbours. The next two questions make this precise.

Setup — The Splitting Identity

Every unit fraction can be split into exactly two smaller unit fractions. The identity is:

1n = 1n+1 + 1n(n+1)
Verification:  nn(n+1) + 1n(n+1) = n+1n(n+1) = 1n   ✓

We start from the geometric series — an infinite sum of unit fractions whose denominators are all powers of 2:

1 = 12 + 14 + 18 + 116 + 132 + ⋯
Every denominator is a power of 2. The only prime present is 2.

Now apply the splitting identity to each term. Watch what happens to the primes:

14 = 15 + 120 prime 5 appears ✓
16 = 17 + 142 prime 7 appears ✓ (1/6 itself came from splitting 1/2 = 1/3 + 1/6)
18 = 19 + 172 9 = 3² — no new prime, just 3 recycled
The left child 1/(n+1) sometimes introduces a new prime, sometimes a composite. The right child 1/n(n+1) is always composite.

Keep splitting. A tree of denominators grows. Some primes appear early as denominators. Some appear only as factors of composite denominators. Some seem absent entirely. The two questions below investigate exactly this structure.

The missing primes

Question 1

The first question
Look at the primes that are missing at depth 3. Now increase to depth 4, then 5. Are any primes permanently excluded from the splitting tree, or is every prime merely delayed?
If some primes are permanently excluded, what property distinguishes them? If all primes eventually appear, what controls how long they take?
Before revealing the answer, form a hypothesis. Look at the missing primes and their predecessors (p−1). What do you notice about the factorisations of p−1 for the late-arriving primes vs the early ones?
Reveal ▾

No prime is permanently excluded

Every prime p eventually appears as a denominator. The mechanism: to introduce p, we need 1p−1 in the tree, since splitting it produces 1p + 1(p−1)p. Since p is prime and p > 2, p is odd, so p−1 is even. We need p−1 to appear as a denominator.

The question reduces to: can the splitting tree reach every even number as a denominator? It can, because the successor chain from any denominator n walks through n+1, n+2, … and the product chain generates n(n+1), which grows without bound. Between these two mechanisms, every positive integer is eventually reachable.

What controls the delay?

The speed at which prime p arrives is controlled by how easily p−1 is reached from the starting denominators. This depends on the prime factorisation of p−1.

SpeedPattern in p−1ExamplesWhy
Fast p−1 is a power of 2 3 (p−1=2), 5 (p−1=4), 17 (p−1=16), 257 (p−1=256) Powers of 2 are the starting denominators
Medium p−1 is a product of small primes already in the tree 7 (6=2·3), 13 (12=2²·3), 31 (30=2·3·5), 43 (42=2·3·7) All factors of p−1 arrived earlier, so p−1 is reachable
Slow p−1 has a large prime factor that is itself slow 47 (46=2·23), 59 (58=2·29) Must wait for the large factor to arrive first

The single-sentence version: a prime arrives early if its predecessor is smooth (built from small primes), and late if its predecessor is rough (contains large prime factors that must themselves be bootstrapped first).

This creates a dependency chain. 47 needs 23, which needs 11, which needs 5. The chain 5 → 11 → 23 → 47 shows how "roughness propagates": each link waits for the previous one. The primes are not randomly scattered through the tree; they arrive in an order dictated by the multiplicative structure of the integers immediately below them.

Question 2

The second question
The splitting identity applied to every term of the geometric series produces two children per node. But the left child 1n+1 of one branch can equal an existing denominator from another branch. For instance, splitting 12 gives 13, and the successor chain from 13 reaches 14, which is already a base term. Does this matter?
Egyptian fractions require distinct denominators. If the splitting tree can produce collisions, is the tree actually a valid decomposition of 1? And if not, what does that do to the prime analysis?
Think about it concretely. Write out the first two levels of the tree. Can you find a denominator that appears twice? If so, what goes wrong if you try to use both copies in a sum?
Reveal ▾

Yes, collisions happen. No, the tree is not automatically a valid Egyptian fraction.

The geometric series starts: 12 + 14 + 18 + 116 + ⋯

Split 12: gives 13 + 16.

Split 13: gives 14 + 112. Now 14 appears both as a base term and as a child — collision.

Two distinct consequences

Consequence 1: The full tree overcounts. If we naively sum every leaf of every branch, we get more than 1. The tree is a mechanism for generating denominators, not a valid representation of 1 as a sum of distinct unit fractions. To get a valid Egyptian fraction, you must prune: choose which branches to split and which to leave as leaves, ensuring no denominator is repeated.

Consequence 2: Pruning constrains prime coverage. If a collision forces you to avoid a particular splitting path, the primes that path would have introduced may be delayed further. However, the splitting identity is not the only decomposition. The generalised identity 1n = 1n+k + kn(n+k) (for any k where the right term simplifies to a unit fraction) provides alternative routes. The Fibonacci–Sylvester greedy algorithm avoids collisions entirely by construction, tracing a collision-free path through the primes — but along a different route, with a different prime signature.

So what is the tree, exactly?

The splitting tree is not a decomposition of 1. It is a reachability map: it shows which denominators (and hence which primes) are accessible from the geometric series via the splitting identity. The primes it reaches are the primes that could appear in some valid Egyptian fraction representation of 1 built from these splits. Whether they do appear in a specific collision-free representation depends on the pruning strategy chosen.

The tree answers "which primes are structurally reachable?" Pruning answers "which primes actually participate in a given valid decomposition?"
