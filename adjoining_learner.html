Adjoining ρ

What it means to add a half-integer point to a lattice, why $\rho = \tfrac{1}{2}(1+i+j+k)$ in particular is forced, and how the act of adjoining it grows the Lipschitz order's $8$ unit-norm points to the Hurwitz order's $24$.

L. McCulloch-James · Working Note · v1

§ 1

What this learner does

Part I established that the Lipschitz order $L = \mathbb{Z}\langle 1, i, j, k\rangle$ fails to be maximal in $\mathbb{Q} \otimes \mathbb{H}$ because the element $\rho = \tfrac{1}{2}(1 + i + j + k)$ sits outside $L$ but is an algebraic integer of the algebra. This learner takes that observation and does the construction: we adjoin $\rho$ to $L$, verify that the result is closed under multiplication, count its unit-norm elements, and end with the $24$-element unit group $\mathbb{H}_{\mathrm{H}}^\times = 2T$ that the main learner takes as its starting point.

The word "adjoining" gets used loosely. We will be careful: adjoining in ring theory means closing the original ring under multiplication and addition with a new element, not just adding the new element as an afterthought. So the result of adjoining $\rho$ to $L$ is the smallest ring containing both, which in general may be much bigger than just $L \cup \{\rho\}$. We will see whether the Hurwitz case is well-behaved.

The question to keep in view: when we adjoin a single new element to a lattice, what new elements get pulled in along with it by closure under multiplication? And does the result still satisfy the order conditions of Part I §4?

§ 2

What "adjoining" means

The basic operation of adjoining $\alpha$ to a ring $R$ produces a new ring denoted $R[\alpha]$, consisting of all polynomial expressions in $\alpha$ with coefficients from $R$:

$$ R[\alpha] \;=\; \{r_0 + r_1 \alpha + r_2 \alpha^2 + \cdots : \text{finitely many } r_i \in R\}. $$

In general this might be infinite-dimensional. But if $\alpha$ satisfies a polynomial relation $\alpha^n + c_{n-1}\alpha^{n-1} + \cdots + c_0 = 0$ with coefficients $c_i \in R$, then all powers $\alpha^n, \alpha^{n+1}, \ldots$ can be rewritten using lower powers, and $R[\alpha]$ collapses to $\{r_0 + r_1\alpha + \cdots + r_{n-1}\alpha^{n-1}\}$ — a free $R$-module of rank $n$.

A few examples to make the operation tangible:

AdjoiningToResultRelation
$i$$\mathbb{Z}$ $\mathbb{Z}[i]$, the Gaussian integers $i^2 = -1$
$\omega$$\mathbb{Z}$ $\mathbb{Z}[\omega]$, the Eisenstein integers $\omega^2 + \omega + 1 = 0$
$\sqrt{2}$$\mathbb{Z}$ $\mathbb{Z}[\sqrt{2}] = \{a + b\sqrt{2}\}$ $(\sqrt{2})^2 = 2$
$\tfrac{1}{2}$$\mathbb{Z}$ $\mathbb{Z}[\tfrac{1}{2}]$, dyadic rationals none — not an algebraic integer

The last example is the cautionary one: $\mathbb{Z}[\tfrac{1}{2}]$ contains $\tfrac{1}{4}, \tfrac{1}{8}, \tfrac{1}{16}, \ldots$ ad infinitum, because $\tfrac{1}{2}$ satisfies no monic polynomial over $\mathbb{Z}$. It is a ring, but not a lattice (not finitely generated as an additive group), so not an order in the sense of Part I §4.

The condition that makes adjoining well-behaved is precisely integrality: $\alpha$ must satisfy a monic polynomial with $\mathbb{Z}$-coefficients (or more generally with $R$-coefficients). When this holds, $R[\alpha]$ stays a finite-rank module over $R$, and the order conditions of Part I are preserved.

§ 3

Why $\rho$, specifically

There are many quaternions with half-integer coordinates. Why is $\rho = \tfrac{1}{2}(1+i+j+k)$ the right one to adjoin? Three reasons, of increasing depth.

Reason 1: $\rho$ is on the unit sphere

The norm of $\rho$ is

$$ N(\rho) \;=\; \rho\bar{\rho} \;=\; \tfrac{1}{4}(1^2 + 1^2 + 1^2 + 1^2) \;=\; \tfrac{4}{4} \;=\; 1. $$

So $\rho$ is a unit (norm-$1$ element) of $\mathbb{Q}\otimes\mathbb{H}$. If we are going to enlarge the Lipschitz unit group $Q_8$, $\rho$ is the sort of element we would want to add — something that already lives on the unit sphere.

Arithmetic miracle

The fact that $\tfrac{1}{4}(1+1+1+1) = 1$ depends crucially on being in four dimensions. In two dimensions, the analogous half-integer $\tfrac{1}{2}(1 + i) \in \mathbb{C}$ has norm $\tfrac{1}{4}(1+1) = \tfrac{1}{2}$, which is not $1$. In four dimensions there are four half-integer terms each contributing $\tfrac{1}{4}$ to the norm, summing to exactly $1$. This arithmetic feature is the reason the Hurwitz refinement exists in $\mathbb{H}$ but not in $\mathbb{C}$. Part III makes this comparison central.

Reason 2: $\rho$ is an algebraic integer

The trace and norm of $\rho$ are integers:

$$ \rho + \bar{\rho} \;=\; \tfrac{1}{2}(1+i+j+k) + \tfrac{1}{2}(1-i-j-k) \;=\; 1, \qquad N(\rho) = 1. $$

The minimal polynomial of any quaternion $q$ is $x^2 - (\text{tr}\, q)\,x + N(q) = 0$. For $\rho$ this is

$$ x^2 - x + 1 \;=\; 0, $$

which is monic with integer coefficients. So $\rho$ is integral over $\mathbb{Z}$, the condition flagged at the end of §2 as the well-behavedness criterion for adjoining.

Compare with attempting to adjoin $\sigma = \tfrac{1}{2}(1 + i)$ (a half-integer point with only two non-zero half-coordinates): $\sigma$ has norm $\tfrac{1}{4}(1+1) = \tfrac{1}{2}$, which is not an integer. Its minimal polynomial is $x^2 - x + \tfrac{1}{2} = 0$, which has the half-integer coefficient $\tfrac{1}{2}$. So $\sigma$ is not an algebraic integer, and adjoining it produces a ring containing $\tfrac{1}{2}$ — failing the integrality condition for orders.

Reason 3: half-integers in $\mathbb{H}$ are constrained

What other half-integer quaternions satisfy both conditions (unit norm and integral trace)? Direct enumeration of $\tfrac{1}{2}(\epsilon_0 + \epsilon_1 i + \epsilon_2 j + \epsilon_3 k)$ with $\epsilon_i = \pm 1$: every such element has norm $1$ and trace $\tfrac{1}{2}\epsilon_0 + \tfrac{1}{2}(\text{conjugate terms}) = \tfrac{1}{2}\epsilon_0 + \tfrac{1}{2}\epsilon_0 = \epsilon_0$, an integer. So all $16$ such sign patterns are algebraic integers of $\mathbb{Q}\otimes\mathbb{H}$, and $\rho$ is just one of them.

What is special about $\rho = \tfrac{1}{2}(1+i+j+k)$ is that it generates all $16$ via the action of $Q_8$ on it. Adjoining any one such half-integer to $L$ ends up generating the same Hurwitz order, because all $16$ are in the same $Q_8$-orbit. This is why we pick $\rho$ canonically: it has the all-plus sign pattern, and the Hurwitz order is the unique extension that contains it.

§ 4

Verifying $\rho^2$ and $\rho^3$

Two algebraic facts are essential to everything that follows. Both can be checked by direct calculation.

Fact A: $\rho^2 = \tfrac{1}{2}(-1 + i + j + k)$

Compute $\rho^2 = \tfrac{1}{4}(1+i+j+k)^2$. Expand:

$$ (1+i+j+k)^2 \;=\; \sum_{a,b \in \{1,i,j,k\}} ab. $$

The diagonal terms $1, i^2, j^2, k^2$ give $1, -1, -1, -1$, summing to $-2$. The off-diagonal terms come in pairs $(ab, ba)$; using $ij = k$, $ji = -k$, $jk = i$, $kj = -i$, $ki = j$, $ik = -j$, each pair cancels except for the contributions from $1 \cdot i, i \cdot 1$ (and the analogous terms with $j$ and $k$), which give $2i, 2j, 2k$. So the off-diagonal sum is $2(i+j+k)$. Total:

$$ (1+i+j+k)^2 \;=\; -2 + 2(i+j+k) \;=\; 2(-1+i+j+k), $$

and therefore

$$ \rho^2 \;=\; \tfrac{1}{4} \cdot 2(-1+i+j+k) \;=\; \tfrac{1}{2}(-1+i+j+k). $$

Fact B: $\rho^3 = -1$

This follows from Fact A and the minimal polynomial. From $\rho^2 - \rho + 1 = 0$ (equivalently $\rho^2 = \rho - 1$) we have $\rho^3 = \rho \cdot \rho^2 = \rho(\rho - 1) = \rho^2 - \rho = (\rho - 1) - \rho = -1$. Direct calculation gives the same: multiplying out $\tfrac{1}{2}(1+i+j+k) \cdot \tfrac{1}{2}(-1+i+j+k)$ gives $-1$ after collecting terms.

The element $\rho$ has multiplicative order $6$ in $(\mathbb{Q}\otimes\mathbb{H})^\times$. Equivalently $\langle \rho \rangle \cong \mathbb{Z}_6 = \mathbb{Z}_2 \times \mathbb{Z}_3$, with the $\mathbb{Z}_2$ being charge conjugation ($\rho^3 = -1$) and the $\mathbb{Z}_3$ being the Eisenstein cyclic structure ($\rho^2$ generates a 3-cycle: $\rho^2, \rho^4, \rho^6 = 1$).

The conclusion: $\rho$ is a sixth root of unity living inside $\mathbb{Q}\otimes\mathbb{H}$, with $\rho^3 = -1$ and $\rho$ generating a cyclic subgroup of order $6$. The cyclic group it generates is isomorphic to $\mathbb{Z}_6 = \mathbb{Z}_2 \times \mathbb{Z}_3$, just like the unit group of the Eisenstein integers $\mathbb{Z}[\omega]$. The Eisenstein subring $\mathbb{Z}[\omega] \hookrightarrow \mathbb{Q}\otimes\mathbb{H}$ exists, via the embedding $\omega \mapsto \rho^2$ (or equivalently via $\omega \mapsto -\bar{\rho} = \tfrac{1}{2}(-1 + i + j + k)$, depending on conventions). So the Hurwitz order contains the Eisenstein integers as a commutative subring.

§ 5

What gets pulled in by closure

We now do the adjoining proper. The Hurwitz order is the smallest subring of $\mathbb{Q}\otimes\mathbb{H}$ containing both $L$ and $\rho$. By the definition in §2, this is

$$ \mathbb{H}_{\mathrm{H}} \;=\; L[\rho] \;=\; \{r_0 + r_1\rho : r_0, r_1 \in L\} \big/ \text{relations}. $$

Because $\rho^2 = \rho - 1$, all powers $\rho^n$ for $n \geq 2$ collapse to linear combinations of $1$ and $\rho$ with $\mathbb{Z}$-coefficients. So $\mathbb{H}_{\mathrm{H}}$ is a free $L$-module of rank $2$: every element is of the form $r_0 + r_1\rho$ with $r_0, r_1 \in L$.

But this is not yet the right way to see the lattice. As a $\mathbb{Z}$-module, $\mathbb{H}_{\mathrm{H}}$ is generated by

$$ \{1, i, j, k, \rho\} $$

because $L = \mathbb{Z}\langle 1,i,j,k\rangle$ contributes those four generators and we add $\rho$. Naively this would give a rank-5 lattice in 4-dimensional $\mathbb{H}$, but $\rho$ is linearly dependent on $1, i, j, k$ over $\mathbb{Q}$ (it is $\tfrac{1}{2}(1+i+j+k)$), so the generating set is over-complete. Picking a minimal $\mathbb{Z}$-basis requires a bit more care: it turns out that

$$ \mathbb{H}_{\mathrm{H}} \;=\; \{a + bi + cj + d\rho : a, b, c, d \in \mathbb{Z}\} \;=\; L + \mathbb{Z}\rho $$

is the right description, with basis $\{1, i, j, \rho\}$ rather than the over-complete $\{1, i, j, k, \rho\}$. The element $k$ is recovered from $\rho$ via $k = 2\rho - 1 - i - j$.

A concrete description of all elements: $\mathbb{H}_{\mathrm{H}}$ consists of all $q = a + bi + cj + dk$ with

$$ \text{either all coordinates }(a,b,c,d) \in \mathbb{Z}, \quad\text{or all coordinates} \in \mathbb{Z} + \tfrac{1}{2}. $$

That is: either all four coordinates are integers (the Lipschitz part), or all four are half-integers ($\rho$ and its $Q_8$-translates). Mixed cases (some integer, some half-integer coordinates) are not in $\mathbb{H}_{\mathrm{H}}$. This is the precise constraint adjoining $\rho$ imposes.

Why mixed cases are excluded

Suppose $q = \tfrac{1}{2} + 1 \cdot i + 0 \cdot j + 0 \cdot k = \tfrac{1}{2} + i$ had been in $\mathbb{H}_{\mathrm{H}}$. Then so would $q - i = \tfrac{1}{2}$, by closure under subtraction. But $\tfrac{1}{2}$ has norm $\tfrac{1}{4}$, not an integer — failing integrality. So $\tfrac{1}{2}$ is not in any order containing $L$, and mixed half-integer/integer quaternions cannot be elements of $\mathbb{H}_{\mathrm{H}}$. The "all integer or all half-integer" constraint follows from this.

§ 6

How the lattice grows

Adjoining $\rho$ to $L$ doubles the lattice: every Lipschitz point $(a,b,c,d)$ with $a,b,c,d \in \mathbb{Z}$ gives rise to a Hurwitz-proper point $(a+\tfrac{1}{2}, b+\tfrac{1}{2}, c+\tfrac{1}{2}, d+\tfrac{1}{2})$ shifted by $\rho$. The result is the body-centred cubic lattice in 4 dimensions, also known as the $D_4$ root lattice (more precisely: the $F_4$ root lattice, which is the union $D_4 \cup (D_4 + \rho)$).

§ 7

Counting unit-norm points

The unit-norm elements of $\mathbb{H}_{\mathrm{H}}$ are the quaternions $q$ with $N(q) = 1$ and either all-integer or all-half-integer coordinates.

Integer-coordinate units

With $a, b, c, d \in \mathbb{Z}$ and $a^2 + b^2 + c^2 + d^2 = 1$, exactly one coordinate is $\pm 1$ and the rest are zero. This gives $\{\pm 1, \pm i, \pm j, \pm k\}$: the Lipschitz unit group $Q_8$ of order $8$.

Half-integer-coordinate units

With $a, b, c, d \in \mathbb{Z} + \tfrac{1}{2}$ and $a^2 + b^2 + c^2 + d^2 = 1$, each coordinate squared is at least $\tfrac{1}{4}$ (the closest a half-integer can get to zero). Four half-integers squared summing to $1$: each must equal exactly $\tfrac{1}{4}$, i.e. each coordinate is exactly $\pm\tfrac{1}{2}$. Number of such tuples: $2^4 = 16$.

So the total unit count is:

$$ |\mathbb{H}_{\mathrm{H}}^\times| \;=\; |Q_8| + 16 \;=\; 8 + 16 \;=\; 24. $$

These $24$ unit-norm elements form the binary tetrahedral group $2T$, the unit group of the Hurwitz order. The full structural analysis of $2T$ — conjugacy classes, character table, subgroup lattice — is in the main learner. The arithmetic structure $2T \cong Q_8 \rtimes \mathbb{Z}_3$ falls out of what we have already done: the $Q_8$ comes from the Lipschitz core, the $\mathbb{Z}_3$ from $\langle \rho^2 \rangle$ acting on $Q_8$ by cyclic rotation $i \to j \to k \to i$.

A lattice-theoretic perspective

The $24$ unit-norm points of $\mathbb{H}_{\mathrm{H}}$ are the vertices of the regular $24$-cell, the unique self-dual regular polytope in $4$ dimensions. As a root system, they are the short roots of $F_4$. So the Hurwitz refinement is geometrically the statement: the densest finite configuration of unit-norm lattice points in $\mathbb{Q}\otimes\mathbb{H}$ is the $24$-cell, achieved exactly by adjoining $\rho$ to $L$.

§ 8

What this learner has done

We started from the observation that the Lipschitz order $L = \mathbb{Z}\langle 1,i,j,k\rangle$ is not maximal because the algebraic integer $\rho = \tfrac{1}{2}(1+i+j+k)$ sits outside it. We defined the operation of adjoining $\rho$ to $L$ as the formation of the smallest subring containing both, which by integrality is a finite-rank lattice. We verified the algebraic relations $\rho^2 = \tfrac{1}{2}(-1+i+j+k)$, $\rho^3 = -1$, and $\rho^2 - \rho + 1 = 0$, identifying $\rho$ as a sixth root of unity generating $\mathbb{Z}_6$. We described the resulting Hurwitz order $\mathbb{H}_{\mathrm{H}}$ as the set of quaternions with either all-integer or all-half-integer coordinates, and counted its $24$ unit-norm elements: the $8$ Lipschitz units plus $16$ half-integer-coordinate units.

What we have not done: shown that $\mathbb{H}_{\mathrm{H}}$ is the unique maximal order in $\mathbb{Q}\otimes\mathbb{H}$ up to conjugacy (this is Voight's theorem; we have taken it on trust); analysed the multiplicative structure of the $24$ units beyond noting $Q_8 \rtimes \mathbb{Z}_3$; addressed the question of why the Hurwitz construction works in $\mathbb{H}$ but not in $\mathbb{C}$ (this is Part III); or related the $24$ units to the irreducible representations of $2T$ (this is in the main learner).

Part III takes up the comparison question directly. Why does adjoining $\rho$ work in 4 dimensions when no analogous refinement is possible for the Gaussian integers in 2 dimensions? The answer is purely arithmetic: in $4$D, $4 \times (\tfrac{1}{2})^2 = 1$ exactly. In $2$D, $2 \times (\tfrac{1}{2})^2 = \tfrac{1}{2} \neq 1$, so the corresponding half-integer construction yields a non-unit and the analogous refinement fails. This is the structural reason the Hurwitz refinement has no Gaussian analogue, and why $24$ units rather than some smaller count.
