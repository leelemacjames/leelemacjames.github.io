Why Water Is Linear
and Air Is Exponential

The same equation — pressure from the weight of fluid above — gives two radically different shapes. The difference? One assumption about density.

P = ρgh gives a straight line for water.
P = P₀e−Mgh/RT gives an exponential for air.
These aren't different physics. They're the same physics with a different assumption about ρ.

SECTION 01What You Already Know

From the algebra learner, you know how to identify three shapes:

Linear
y = mx + c

Straight line. Constant gradient. When you see this in physics, the quantity on the y-axis grows at a constant rate per unit of x.

Exponential
y = Aekx

Curve that accelerates (or decays). The quantity on the y-axis grows in proportion to its own current value. The rate of change depends on the current amount.

You also know the linearisation trick: if you have an exponential y = Aekx, plot ln y against x to get a straight line with gradient k. This is how you distinguish a power law from an exponential experimentally.

Now we meet both shapes in the same physical setting: pressure as a function of height. The question is why one fluid gives the first shape and another gives the second.

Quick check: what does P = ρgh assume about ρ?
Think about what each symbol is. Is ρ a constant or a variable?

P = ρgh treats ρ as a constant. It's pulled out of the equation as a fixed number — 1000 kg/m³ for water, say. The equation then says: pressure increases linearly with depth h, at a constant rate ρg.

But is density always constant? For water, approximately yes. For air? No — and that changes everything.

SECTION 02The Question That Splits the Story

Both water and air are fluids. Both have weight. Both create pressure on things below them. The basic argument is identical in both cases:

Pressure = weight of fluid above per unit area
This is the one physical idea. Everything else follows from how density behaves.

So here is the question that divides the physics into two branches:

When you go deeper (or higher), does the density of the fluid change?
Think about what happens to water at 100m depth vs air at 100m altitude...

Water: No, not significantly. Water at 100m depth has essentially the same density as water at the surface. Squeeze it and it barely compresses. This is what "incompressible" means: ρ doesn't depend on P.

Air: Yes, dramatically. Air at sea level is much denser than air at 5000m. Why? Because the air below is being compressed by the weight of all the air above it. More pressure → molecules squeezed closer → higher density. This is what "compressible" means: ρ depends on P.

This single difference — whether ρ is constant or pressure-dependent — is what makes one linear and the other exponential.

The Fork

If ρ is constant → P = ρgh → linear.
If ρ depends on P → the equation folds back on itself → exponential.
Same physics, different assumption, different shape. Cartwright would call P = ρgh a model that "lies beautifully" — it works for water, breaks for air.

SECTION 03Water: The Incompressible Case

Water is nearly incompressible. At the bottom of the Mariana Trench (11 km, ~1100 atm), water density increases by only about 5%. For any depth you'll encounter in a school lab or exam, ρwater ≈ 1000 kg/m³ is essentially exact.

The Argument

Consider a column of water with cross-sectional area A, from the surface down to depth h.

1
Volume of column = A × h
A cylinder of water sitting above the point where we want to know the pressure.
2
Mass = ρ × V = ρ × A × h
Because ρ is constant, we can just multiply. This is the crucial step.
3
Weight = mg = ρAhg
The gravitational force from this column of water.
4
Pressure = Weight / Area = ρAhg / A = ρgh
The A cancels — pressure doesn't depend on the size of the column, only its height.
P = ρgh
Linear in h. Structure: y = mx, with m = ρg ≈ 9800 Pa/m
Every extra metre of depth adds the same ~9800 Pa.
At 10m: ~98,000 Pa ≈ 1 atm (atmospheric pressure).
This is why divers feel 1 extra atmosphere per 10m.

This is the equation from the Language of Floating explorer. It's linear because the thing that multiplies h — the density ρ — is a constant. The gradient ρg never changes. Every layer of water contributes the same amount of pressure, regardless of what's above it.

SECTION 04Air: The Compressible Case — Where It Gets Interesting

Now try the same argument with air. You want to know: how does atmospheric pressure decrease as you go up?

Can we use P = ρgh for the atmosphere?
What would go wrong if we tried?

If we try P = ρgh with ρ = 1.225 kg/m³ (sea-level air density), we'd predict the atmosphere runs out at about P/(ρg) = 101325/(1.225 × 9.81) ≈ 8400m. Above that, no pressure at all — a hard ceiling.

But that's wrong. Pressure at 8400m is about 35 kPa, not zero. Aircraft fly at 12 km. Weather balloons reach 35 km. The atmosphere doesn't just stop — it fades out gradually.

The linear model fails because air density at 8400m is not 1.225 kg/m³. It's much less, because there's less air above compressing it. Density varies with height — so we can't pull ρ out as a constant.

The Key Difference: ρ Depends on P

For an ideal gas at constant temperature:

PV = nRT → ρ = PM / RT
Density is proportional to pressure. More pressure → denser gas.
M = molar mass of air ≈ 0.029 kg/mol
R = 8.314 J/(mol·K)
T = temperature (assumed constant here)

This is the crux. For water, ρ is independent of P. For air, ρ is proportional to P. When you try to compute "the weight of the column above," the density of every layer depends on the pressure at that layer — which depends on the weight of everything above that — which depends on the density up there — and so on. The equation refers to itself.

Self-Reference → Exponential

Whenever a quantity's rate of change is proportional to its own current value, you get an exponential. This is the defining property: dP/dh ∝ P. It's the same mathematics as radioactive decay (dN/dt ∝ N), population growth, and capacitor discharge. The shape is the same because the structure is the same.

SECTION 05The Derivation — One Step at a Time

We're going to derive the barometric formula. Don't panic — it's the same logic as the water case, but we can't shortcut past the varying density. We take a thin slice and ask what happens across it.

Thin Slice Argument

Consider a thin horizontal layer of air at height h, with thickness dh (an infinitesimally small slice).

1
dP = −ρg · dh
The pressure drop across a thin slice equals the weight of that slice per unit area. The minus sign: going up (increasing h) means less pressure. This is exactly the water argument, but only for one thin layer where ρ is approximately constant.
2
ρ = PM / RT
Substitute the ideal gas law. Now ρ is expressed in terms of P — the quantity we're trying to find.
3
dP = −(PM/RT) · g · dh
Replace ρ with PM/RT. Now we have P on both sides. Self-reference.
4
dP/P = −(Mg/RT) · dh
Divide both sides by P. The left side is the fractional change in pressure. The right side is a constant times dh. This is a separable differential equation.
5
∫dP/P = −(Mg/RT) ∫dh
Integrate both sides. The left integral is a natural log.
6
ln P = −(Mg/RT) · h + constant
At h = 0 (sea level), P = P₀. So the constant = ln P₀.
7
ln(P/P₀) = −Mgh / RT
Combine the log terms. This is the semi-log linearisation you met in the algebra learner.
8
P = P₀ · e−Mgh/RT
Exponentiate both sides. The barometric formula. Pressure decays exponentially with altitude.
P = P₀ e−Mgh/RT
The barometric formula — exponential decay of pressure with altitude
P₀ = 101,325 Pa (sea-level pressure)
M = 0.029 kg/mol, g = 9.81 m/s², R = 8.314 J/(mol·K)
At T = 288 K: scale height H = RT/Mg ≈ 8,400 m
Every 8.4 km, pressure falls by a factor of e ≈ 2.718
Why didn't this happen for water?
Go back to step 2. What's different?

At step 2, for water, ρ is constant. So the equation dP = −ρg · dh is just dP = −(constant) · dh. Integrate: P = constant × h + P₀. Straight line.

For air, ρ = PM/RT, so step 3 puts P into the right-hand side. The equation becomes dP/dh = −(Mg/RT) · P — the rate of change of P is proportional to P itself. This self-referential structure is what produces the exponential.

Linear: rate of change is constant. Exponential: rate of change is proportional to current value.

Cartwright's Warning

The barometric formula assumes constant temperature (isothermal atmosphere). Real temperature drops roughly 6.5°C per km in the troposphere, which changes the exponent and makes the real pressure profile neither purely linear nor purely exponential. Both P = ρgh and P = P₀e−Mgh/RT are models that "lie beautifully" — each works within its domain and fails outside it.

SECTION 07The Log Connection

From the algebra learner, you know that exponentials linearise on semi-log plots. Watch how this applies here:

Water (already linear)
P = ρgh

Plot P vs h → straight line. Gradient = ρg ≈ 9800 Pa/m.

No log transformation needed — it's already y = mx.

Air (exponential → linearise)
P = P₀e−Mgh/RT

Plot P vs h → exponential decay curve.

Plot ln P vs h → straight line!
Gradient = −Mg/RT.
Intercept = ln P₀.

If you measured pressure at different altitudes and plotted ln P vs h, what could you extract?
Think about what the gradient gives you in terms of physical constants...

Gradient = −Mg/RT. You know M (molar mass of air) and R (gas constant). So:

g = −gradient × RT / M

You've measured gravitational acceleration from atmospheric pressure data. Or, if you know g, you can find the atmospheric temperature T. A single straight-line gradient gives you a fundamental constant.

This is the power of linearisation: turning a curve into a line lets you extract hidden constants from data.

The Scale Height

The exponent −Mgh/RT has a natural grouping: let H = RT/Mg. Then:

P = P₀ e−h/H
H ≈ 8400m — the "scale height" of Earth's atmosphere
H is the altitude gain that reduces pressure by a factor of e.
At h = H: P = P₀/e ≈ 37% of sea-level pressure.
At h = 2H: P = P₀/e² ≈ 14%.
At h = 5H ≈ 42 km: P ≈ 0.7% — effectively space.

Compare this with the water case: water pressure increases by 1 atm per 10m, with no "scale depth." The linear model never fades — it keeps climbing at the same rate forever (until you run out of water). The exponential model fades asymptotically — pressure never quite reaches zero but gets arbitrarily close.

Structure Recap

Constant density (incompressible) → rate of change of P is constant → integrate to get a line.
Density ∝ P (compressible) → rate of change of P is proportional to P → integrate to get an exponential.
The algebra is the same as the algebra learner's two shapes. The physics just tells you which shape applies and why.

SECTION 08Check Your Understanding

1. Why is P = ρgh linear in h?
The linearity comes from ρ being independent of depth. The gradient ρg is constant, so P increases at the same rate per metre. If ρ varied with depth, we'd get a curve.
2. What makes atmospheric pressure exponential rather than linear?
While gravity does weaken and temperature does change, the dominant effect is that air is compressible: ρ ∝ P. This makes dP/dh ∝ P — the signature of exponential behaviour. (The isothermal model assumes constant T and g for simplicity.)
3. You have atmospheric pressure data at various altitudes. What do you plot to get a straight line?
Exponential relationships linearise on semi-log plots: ln y vs x. Here, ln P = −(Mg/RT)h + ln P₀. Straight line, gradient = −Mg/RT. A log-log plot would be wrong — that's for power laws, not exponentials.
4. The "scale height" H = RT/Mg ≈ 8400m. This means:
The scale height is the e-folding distance: every H metres, pressure drops by a factor of e. The atmosphere never truly "ends" — it fades exponentially. This is fundamentally different from the linear water model, where there's a definite surface.
5. If the linear model P = ρgh were applied to the atmosphere using sea-level density, it predicts the atmosphere ends at about 8400m. What's wrong with this?
The linear model overpredicts high-altitude mass by assuming all air is as dense as sea-level air. In reality, upper layers are much thinner, contribute less weight, and pressure tails off exponentially rather than hitting zero. This is a perfect example of Cartwright's point: the model works locally but fails when extended beyond its domain.
6. Which differential equation structure produces an exponential?
dy/dx = constant → y = mx + c (linear). dy/dx = ky → y = Aekx (exponential). dy/dx = kx → y = ½kx² (quadratic). The atmospheric case gives dP/dh = −(Mg/RT)·P, which is dy/dx = ky with k negative — exponential decay.
