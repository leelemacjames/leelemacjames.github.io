The Second Verb

In which the famous spines of the famous triangles turn out to have their own operators, and the choice of how to embed a recurrence in a matrix carries more information than the recurrence alone.

Architect Carbon · Lee McCulloch-James  ·  companion to Pascal's Triangle is Not a Noun

In the previous piece I argued that the diagram on the page, the triangle, is the orbit of a verb, the operator that produces it row by row. Pascal's triangle is what happens when a particular bidiagonal matrix acts on a column vector again and again. Eulerian, Delannoy, and Stirling fall out of small variations on the same template. The pupil who learns the operator inherits a structure that organises the named triangles into a single family.

There is more to say though, for inside each triangle live what can call spines: sequences read along chosen lines through the array. The row sums of Pascal are the powers of two. The shallow diagonals of Pascal are the Fibonacci numbers. The central diagonal of Delannoy is the famous 1, 3, 13, 63, 321, 1683, with its own folklore. The row sums of the Eulerian triangle are the factorials. The row sums of Stirling are the Bell numbers. Each of these is a named sequence in its own right, and each of them, like the triangle that produced it, is usually presented as a noun. A list. A thing.

But each spine also has its own verb. And the verb is not the same verb as the one that produced the triangle. It is a second verb, sometimes simpler than the first, occasionally more subtle, and it carries information that the first does not.

The continuant

Take Fibonacci. The sequence 1, 1, 2, 3, 5, 8, 13, 21 satisfies Fn = Fn−1 + Fn−2. The recurrence has depth two. The classical way to compute Fn is to start at the bottom and step up. The unclassical way, due in essence to Euler and recurring throughout combinatorics under the name continuant, is the following. Consider the n by n tridiagonal matrix with ones on the main diagonal and ones on the off-diagonals, with one of the off-diagonals carrying a minus sign:

The Fibonacci continuant at n = 5. Its determinant is F6 = 8.

Expand the determinant by cofactors along the last row. The two non-zero entries are the bottom-right 1 and the entry immediately to its left, the −1. Cofactor expansion gives det(Mn) = 1 · det(Mn−1) − (−1) · 1 · det(Mn−2) = det(Mn−1) + det(Mn−2), which is the Fibonacci recurrence exactly. With det(M1) = 1 and det(M2) = 2, the determinant of Mn is Fn+1.

This is the second verb. The Fibonacci sequence is more than the orbit of an iterative process being also the determinant of a tridiagonal matrix. The matrix grows by one row and one column as n increases, and its determinant tracks the sequence.

The recurrence and the determinant are the same calculation, organised two ways.

What makes this more than a curiosity is that the two organisations of the same calculation expose different parts of the structure. The iterative form makes the unfolding visible while the determinantal form makes the matrix visible, and a matrix has more than just recurrence. A matrix has shape, has eigenvalues as well as a transpose, an adjoint, and a notion of symmetry. None of these are properties of the recurrence in itself being rather properties of how the recurrence has been embedded into a two-dimensional object.

The choice of embedding

The Fibonacci continuant above is not unique. Any tridiagonal matrix whose diagonal entries are 1 and whose off-diagonal product equals −1 has the same determinant. We could put +1 and −1 in either order. We could put i in both off-diagonal positions, because i · i = −1. We cannot though put i above and −i below since i · (−i) = +1, and the recurrence collapses.

Let us though take that last possibility seriously, because it teaches a little lesson cleanly. Put i above the main diagonal and −i below so that the matrix is now Hermitian: it equals its own conjugate transpose. The recurrence for the determinant is no longer Fibonacci, instead it is Dn = Dn−1 − Dn−2, whose characteristic polynomial λ² − λ + 1 has roots at the primitive sixth roots of unity. The sequence of determinants is 1, 1, 0, −1, −1, 0, 1, 1, 0, ..., a period-six wave.

So this Hermitian matrix does not produce Fibonacci, but instead a different sequence of the same length of recurrence but with a different sign on the q term. The shape of the matrix carries information that the surface description of "Fibonacci-like recurrence" does not. Hermitian symmetry forces a specific sign convention that pushes the sequence from exponential growth into periodic oscillation in which the eigenvalues of the Hermitian version sit on the unit circle. The eigenvalues of the complex-symmetric version, with i on both off-diagonals, are the golden ratio and its conjugate, off the unit circle, producing exponential growth.

Two matrices, both built from the same recipe, the same diagonal of ones and the same off-diagonal magnitude of one, differ only in which we put above and below. The shape of symmetry is doing real work.

The shape of the matrix carries information that the recurrence alone does not.

A small catalogue of second verbs

The famous spines of the famous triangles fall into a hierarchy according to what kind of matrix determinant produces them.

Pascal central col 1, 2, 6, 20, 70, 252, … sn = (2(2n−1)/n) sn−1 Diagonal, rational entries
Pascal shallow diag 1, 1, 2, 3, 5, 8, 13, … sn = sn−1 + sn−2 Tridiagonal, integer entries
Eulerian row sums 1, 1, 2, 6, 24, 120, … sn = n sn−1 Diagonal, entries 1, 2, 3, …, n
Delannoy main diag 1, 3, 13, 63, 321, 1683, … n sn = 3(2n−1) sn−1 − (n−1) sn−2 Tridiagonal, rational and irrational entries
Stirling row sums 1, 1, 2, 5, 15, 52, 203, … Bn+1 = Σ C(n,k) Bk Upper Hessenberg, binomial entries

Three layers of matrix complexity are visible here. Depth-one recurrences (where each term depends only on the immediately previous one) collapse the matrix to a diagonal. Depth-two recurrences (where each term depends on the two preceding terms) require the matrix to be tridiagonal. Recurrences with full history (where each term depends on all preceding terms, as for the Bell numbers) require the matrix to be upper Hessenberg, an almost-triangular shape that allows non-zero entries arbitrarily far above the diagonal but only one step below it.

Inside each layer the ground ring stratifies further. Pascal row sums live over the integers, with constant matrix entries. The Eulerian row sums live over the polynomial ring in n, with entries 1, 2, 3, n. The central binomial coefficients live over the rational functions in n. The Delannoy diagonal requires irrational entries (specifically square roots of rationals in n) to make the matrix symmetric, although asymmetric realisations over the rationals also exist. The Bell numbers require binomial coefficients as matrix entries, which is to say they require the whole structure of Pascal's triangle itself encoded into the matrix.

This last point is the most interesting. The Bell numbers are the row sums of the Stirling triangle, which is itself a near-relation of Pascal in the bidiagonal classification. And the determinantal matrix that produces the Bell numbers has Pascal's binomial coefficients in its entries. The same arithmetic structure appears twice: once as the operator that produces the original Stirling triangle, and once as the entries of the matrix whose determinant produces Stirling's row sums. The second verb is built out of the first verb's neighbours.

What the duality says

There is a fact behind all of this that is worth stating cleanly. A linear recurrence of depth two is equivalent to a 2 by 2 companion matrix acting on a state vector. The state evolves by repeated application of that matrix, one step per step. The determinantal continuant is the same calculation, but instead of iterating a small matrix many times, you take the determinant of a large matrix once. The large matrix is the iteration unrolled. Its determinant is the result of running the recurrence.

Why bother with the larger view? Because the larger matrix is a different kind of object than the smaller. The 2 by 2 companion matrix encodes the recurrence rule. The n by n continuant encodes the entire history of the computation in one static object whose value is the answer. Symmetry properties of the n by n object, such as Hermitian or symmetric or skew, correspond to deeper constraints on how the recurrence can be realised. The Hermitian Fibonacci-shaped recurrence is forced to oscillate. The asymmetric Fibonacci-shaped recurrence is free to grow.

This is the second verb. It says that the recurrence is one face of the sequence, and the matrix whose determinant gives the sequence is another face, and the choice of how to embed the recurrence in a matrix carries information.

The shape is what the recurrence does not say out loud.

For pupils this is, I think, worth seeing once. It is not a curriculum topic and need not be a lesson, an aside, just the kind of thing that goes in a quietly-marked enrichment box. The point is to teach the orientation: that named sequences are not isolated facts to be remembered but products of operators, and that the operator can be presented two ways, and that the two ways carry different information.

Penrose's pupil, the one who asked what a fraction is, was right to ask. The answer is that a fraction is what behaves like a fraction. The same is true of the Fibonacci sequence. It is what satisfies the Fibonacci recurrence. This piece has presented two verbs that satisfy it: iteration of the 2 by 2 companion matrix, and the determinant of the n by n continuant. There are others in that Fibonacci numbers are also the convergents of the continued fraction [1; 1, 1, 1, …] for the golden ratio as well as being the coefficients in the power-series expansion of x / (1 − x − x²). Each of these is a further verb, and each deserves its own treatment, which I will defer to the next in this series. The point is the family. The sequence sits at the centre, and the verbs gather around it.

The noun-pedagogy hands the pupil one finished object, the triangle as a diagram to be recognised. The verb-pedagogy hands them the operator that produces the diagram and lets them see the orbit unfold. The second-verb-pedagogy hands them a second operator, of a different kind, whose determinant produces the orbit's interesting projections directly, by a different route. Three pedagogies in increasing depth, none of them sufficient on its own, all of them pointing at the same underlying mathematics from different angles.

That, I think, is what Penrose was reaching for when he took his eleven-year-old classmate's answer seriously. There is no single right description. There are descriptions, all of them partial, all of them illuminating, and the mathematics is the family of descriptions taken together.

Notes

  1. The continuant determinant theorem: a tridiagonal n by n matrix with diagonal entries a1, …, an and off-diagonal pairs (bk, ck) for k from 1 to n−1 has determinant satisfying Dn = an Dn−1 − bn−1cn−1 Dn−2. This follows directly from cofactor expansion along the last row. The proof is short and is one of the most elegant things in elementary linear algebra.
  2. The Hermitian Fibonacci-shaped matrix (i above, −i below the diagonal) gives the period-six sequence 1, 1, 0, −1, −1, 0, 1, 1, 0, … because i · (−i) = +1 reverses the sign in the cofactor recurrence. The complex-symmetric version (i above and i below) gives Fibonacci because i · i = −1 preserves the negative sign in the recurrence. The two matrices have the same magnitudes of entries but different spectral properties, and the Hermitian one has eigenvalues on the unit circle by general principles, forcing oscillation rather than growth.
  3. The central Delannoy numbers satisfy the recurrence n Dn = 3(2n−1) Dn−1 − (n−1) Dn−2. The tridiagonal continuant that produces them has main-diagonal entry 3(2k−1)/k at position k and off-diagonal product (k−1)/k. Choosing symmetric off-diagonals requires entries √((k−1)/k), which are irrational, although asymmetric realisations with rational entries also exist.
  4. The Bell numbers as determinant of a Hessenberg matrix: Aitken's formula expresses Bn as the determinant of an n by n upper Hessenberg matrix with binomial-coefficient entries. The exact formulation has a specific sign convention on the subdiagonal that I have not fully written out here; the canonical reference is Aitken's Determinants and Matrices, and a clean modern statement appears in standard combinatorics texts. The fact that matters here is the qualitative one: depth-of-recurrence corresponds to bandwidth of matrix.
  5. The companion-matrix-to-continuant correspondence makes precise the equivalence between iterative and determinantal computation. For depth-two recurrences this is essentially the Cayley-Hamilton theorem in disguise, applied to the 2 by 2 companion matrix; the continuant determinant is the resolvent unrolled into matrix form.
