The Algebra You Already Know

How to recognise the structure in physics equations — and why linearisation is the most powerful trick in experimental science.

y = mx + c
You've drawn this line a hundred times. Every physics equation you'll meet this year is either this — or can be turned into this. That's the whole trick.

SECTION 01Identify the Structure

Every equation is a relationship between quantities. Before you do anything with it, ask: what is the shape of this relationship? There are only a handful of shapes you need to recognise.

The Three Shapes

y = mx + c
Linear — straight line, constant rate of change
m → gradient (how steep)
c → y-intercept (where it crosses the axis)
Shape: straight line
y = kx²
Quadratic — parabola, rate of change itself changes
k → how tight or wide the curve
c = 0 here (passes through origin)
Shape: curve that steepens
y = Aekx
Exponential — growth or decay, rate proportional to current value
A → starting value (when x = 0)
k → growth rate (k > 0 grows, k < 0 decays)
Shape: curve that accelerates or flattens

Here's the claim: if you can tell which shape an equation has, you already understand its algebra. The letters change. The structure doesn't. Tap each card below to identify it.

SECTION 02The Letters Are Just Labels

A common stumbling block: the equation E = ½mv² looks harder than y = 3x². But algebraically they're identical. The difficulty is psychological — you recognise x and y as "maths" but E, m, v feel like "physics."

If I asked you to rearrange y = 3x² to find x, could you do it?
Try it in your head before revealing...

x² = y/3, so x = √(y/3). Done.

Now rearrange E = ½mv² to find v:

v² = 2E/m, so v = √(2E/m). Identical operation.

The algebra didn't change. Only the label on each box changed. This is what Wittgenstein meant by "meaning lives in use" — the symbol v is doing exactly the same mathematical work as x. The physics meaning (velocity) is a separate layer on top.

The Trick

When you see a physics equation: first, ignore what the letters mean. Identify the structure (linear? quadratic? exponential?). Do the algebra. Then reattach the physical meaning.

Common Error

Students plot s against t for s = ½at² and then try to draw a "line of best fit" through a curve. The examiners' mark scheme says: plot s against t² to produce a straight line whose gradient gives ½a. This is linearisation — and it's the subject of the next section.

SECTION 03Linearisation — Straightening Curves

Why do we want straight lines? Because a straight line is the only graph where you can reliably extract a gradient. With a curve, every point has a different gradient — you'd need calculus. With a straight line, you draw it, pick two points, and divide. That's why experimentalists linearise.

The Quadratic Case

Start with y = kx². If you plot y against x, you get a parabola. Useless for gradient extraction. But watch what happens if you define a new variable:

Let X = x² → then y = kX
Now it's y = mx — a straight line through the origin!

This is the entire trick. You haven't changed the physics. You haven't approximated anything. You've just relabelled the horizontal axis. Plot y against x² instead of y against x, and the curve becomes a line.

Walk through it: how do you linearise E = ½mv²?
What plays the role of y? What plays the role of x²?

Step 1: Identify the structure. E = ½mv² has the form y = kx².

Step 2: Assign roles. y → E, x → v, k → ½m.

Step 3: Define new variable. Let V = v². Then E = ½m · V.

Step 4: Plot E (y-axis) against v² (x-axis). Straight line through origin. Gradient = ½m.

Step 5: Read off gradient, double it → mass.

The Pendulum Example

The period of a simple pendulum is T = 2π√(l/g). Square both sides:

T² = (4π²/g) · l
This is y = mx — with y = T², x = l, m = 4π²/g

Plot T² against l. Straight line. Gradient = 4π²/g. Rearrange for g. This is how you measure gravitational acceleration with a piece of string and a stopwatch.

The Pattern

If you see y = kxn for any power n, plot y against xn and you get a straight line. Quadratics are the most common case (n = 2), but the method is universal. What about when the power itself is unknown? That's where logs come in.

SECTION 04Logarithms — The Exponent Extractor

A logarithm answers one question: what power do I raise the base to, in order to get this number?

if bn = x, then logb(x) = n
The log extracts the exponent. That's all it does.

Why does physics care? Because many physical relationships are power laws or exponentials — and logs turn both into straight lines.

The Three Log Rules You Need

RuleWhat It SaysWhy It Matters
log(AB) log A + log B Products become sums — pulls apart multiplied quantities
log(A/B) log A − log B Ratios become differences
log(An) n · log A Powers become multipliers — this is the linearisation rule

That third rule is the key. It takes an exponent (hidden up in the superscript, inaccessible) and brings it down as a multiplier. Once it's a multiplier, it's a gradient.

Linearising a Power Law

Suppose you have data that follows y = axn but you don't know n. Take logs of both sides:

log y = n · log x + log a
Compare with Y = mX + c
Y → log y
X → log x
m → n (the unknown power!)
c → log a (the log of the coefficient)

Plot log y against log x. Straight line. Gradient = the power n. Intercept = log a, so a = 10intercept.

Work through it: you suspect T = kln for a pendulum. How do you find n from data?
Take log of both sides, then identify gradient and intercept...

log T = n · log l + log k

Plot log T (y-axis) against log l (x-axis). Straight line.

Gradient = n. You should get n ≈ 0.5 (because T ∝ √l, i.e. T ∝ l0.5).

Intercept = log k = log(2π/√g). From this you can extract g.

You've determined the power law from data, without knowing it in advance.

Linearising an Exponential

Exponentials are different. The unknown is in the exponent itself, not the power of x. For y = Aekx, take the natural log (ln):

ln y = kx + ln A
Compare with Y = mX + c
Y → ln y
X → x (not log x — just x!)
m → k (the rate constant)
c → ln A

Plot ln y against x. Straight line. Gradient = rate constant k. This is how you find half-lives, decay constants, and time constants from experimental data.

Critical Distinction

Power law y = axn → plot log y vs log x (both axes logged).
Exponential y = Aekx → plot ln y vs x (only y-axis logged).
Getting these mixed up is one of the most common A-level errors. The test: is the variable in the base (power law) or in the exponent (exponential)?

The gradient of a log-log plot gives you the exponent directly. That's the whole point.

SECTION 05Log-Log and Semi-Log — Telling Them Apart

You have experimental data. It curves. Is it a power law or an exponential? Here's the diagnostic:

RelationshipPlot That Gives a Straight LineGradient Gives YouIntercept Gives You
y = axn log y vs log x n (the power) log a
y = Aekx ln y vs x k (the rate) ln A
y = Ae−x/τ ln y vs x −1/τ (neg. reciprocal of time constant) ln A
Radioactive decay: N = N₀e−λt. How do you find the decay constant λ from a graph?
Which type of plot? What's on each axis?

Take ln of both sides: ln N = −λt + ln N₀

Plot ln N (y-axis) against t (x-axis). Straight line.

Gradient = −λ. Intercept = ln N₀.

Half-life: t½ = ln 2 / λ ≈ 0.693 / λ.

This is a semi-log plot (only the y-axis is logged). If you tried log-log (logging both axes), you would not get a straight line — because the relationship is exponential, not a power law.

Kepler's Third Law: T² ∝ r³. How do you verify this from planetary data?
This is a power law: T = krn. What do you plot?

T² ∝ r³ means T ∝ r3/2, so n = 3/2.

Take logs: log T = (3/2) · log r + log k

Plot log T against log r. Straight line, gradient = 1.5.

Or equivalently: plot T² against r³. Straight line through origin. Both work — the log-log method is more powerful because it finds the exponent rather than assuming it.

SECTION 07Check Your Understanding

1. You have data following y = 5x³. What do you plot to get a straight line?
y = 5x³ has the form y = kxn. Plot y against x³ → straight line through origin, gradient = 5. (Alternatively, log y vs log x gives gradient 3.)
2. You plot log y against log x and get a straight line with gradient 0.5. The relationship is:
On a log-log plot, the gradient equals the power. Gradient = 0.5 means y ∝ x0.5 = √x. This is a power law, not exponential.
3. For radioactive decay N = N₀e−λt, which plot gives a straight line?
Exponential relationships linearise on a semi-log plot: ln y vs x. Here, ln N = −λt + ln N₀. Gradient = −λ. A log-log plot would NOT give a straight line — that's for power laws.
4. The gradient of a graph of E against v² (for E = ½mv²) gives you:
E = ½m · v². Plotting E vs v² gives y = (½m)·X. The gradient is ½m, not m. You need to double the gradient to get the mass. This is a classic mark-scheme trap.
5. What is the difference between plotting log y vs log x and ln y vs x?
This is the single most important distinction in experimental data analysis. Power laws put the variable in the base (xn) — use log-log. Exponentials put the variable in the exponent (ekx) — use semi-log. Getting them confused is like using a screwdriver on a nail.
6. You plot ln y against x and get a straight line with gradient −0.05 and y-intercept 4.6. What is the equation?
Semi-log plot → exponential. ln y = −0.05x + 4.6 means y = e4.6 · e−0.05x. Since e4.6 ≈ 100, we get y ≈ 100e−0.05x. This is exponential decay with rate 0.05 and initial value 100.
