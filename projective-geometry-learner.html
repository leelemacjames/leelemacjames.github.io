Projective Geometry: What Survives Projection

Projective geometry asks the most austere question in mathematics: what is preserved when everything but incidence is allowed to change?

I

The Geometry Without Distance

Euclidean geometry preserves lengths and angles. Affine geometry preserves parallelism but not lengths. Projective geometry strips away even parallelism — it preserves only incidence: which points lie on which lines, and which lines pass through which points.

This makes it the most symmetric geometry in Klein's hierarchy. It sits at the top of the Erlangen programme precisely because it refuses to distinguish the most. No distances, no angles, no notion of "parallel." Only the bare combinatorial structure of points, lines, and their intersections.

And yet, projective geometry is not structureless. It possesses a single numerical invariant — the cross-ratio — which survives every projective transformation. This invariant is the reason projective geometry has content, not merely emptiness.

The Erlangen position

In the Erlangen programme, a geometry is defined by its group of allowed transformations and the invariants those transformations preserve. Projective geometry is the geometry of the projective linear group PGL(n+1), and its fundamental invariant is the cross-ratio of four collinear points.

II

The Cross-Ratio

Given four collinear points A, B, C, D with coordinates a, b, c, d, the cross-ratio is defined as:

ξ(A, B, C, D) = (a − b)(c − d) (b − c)(d − a)
The cross-ratio — the only projective invariant of four collinear points

The defining property: no matter how these points are projected onto another line — stretched, compressed, perspectively transformed — the cross-ratio is preserved. Distances change. Ratios of distances change. But this particular combination of ratios does not.

Worked example

For points A = 2, B = 3, C = 5, D = 8:

ξ(2, 3, 5, 8) = (2 − 3)(5 − 8) / [(3 − 5)(8 − 2)] = (−1)(−3) / [(−2)(6)] = 3 / −12 = −1/4

IV

The Six Faces of the Cross-Ratio

Four points can be arranged in 24 permutations, but these yield only six distinct cross-ratio values. The six are related by two generating transformations: ξ → 1 − ξ and ξ → 1/ξ. Together these generate the symmetric group S₃ acting on the cross-ratio — the anharmonic group.

These six values form a group under composition of the maps ξ → 1−ξ and ξ → 1/ξ, isomorphic to S₃. This is not a coincidence: the permutation group on four objects modulo the Klein four-group (which fixes the cross-ratio) is exactly S₃. The algebraic structure of the cross-ratio is the combinatorics of four-point permutations.

Take ξ = −1/4 and verify that the two generators ξ → 1/ξ and ξ → 1−ξ produce all six values:

1 − 1/ξ = 1 − (−4) = 5 ✓
ξ/(ξ−1) = (−1/4)/(−5/4) = 1/5 ✓

All six values are reached by composing the two generators. The sixth transformation, ξ → ξ/(ξ−1), is the composition (1/ξ) then (1−·) then (1/·) — or equivalently ξ → 1 − 1/(1−ξ). V

The Fibonacci Cross-Ratio

Something remarkable happens when the four points are consecutive Fibonacci numbers Fn−3, Fn−2, Fn−1, Fn. The cross-ratio converges to an irrational limit involving the golden ratio.

Each rational cross-ratio of a Fibonacci quadruplet approaches a limiting value as n → ∞. The derivation uses the asymptotic form Fn ≈ φⁿ/√5:

Asymptotic substitution

Substituting Fk ≈ φk/√5 into the cross-ratio formula, the √5 factors cancel:

ξ = (φn−3 − φn−2)(φn−1 − φn) / [(φn−2 − φn−1)(φn − φn−3)]

Using the Fibonacci recurrence φn = φn−1 + φn−2:

The numerator becomes (φn−3 − φn−2)(−φn−2).
The denominator becomes (−φn−3)(2φn−2).

Simplifying: ξ = (φn−3 − φn−2) / (2φn−3) = 1/2 − φ/2

Since φ = (1 + √5)/2:

ξ∞ = 1 − √5 4  =  φ* 2  ≈ −0.30902
The limiting Fibonacci cross-ratio — half the conjugate golden ratio
Why φ* appears

The conjugate golden ratio φ* = (1 − √5)/2 is the second root of x² = x + 1. It appears here because the cross-ratio, as a ratio of differences, eliminates the dominant φⁿ growth and leaves behind the algebraic structure of the recurrence. The cross-ratio of Fibonacci quadruplets converges because the golden ratio governs the asymptotic spacing — and the cross-ratio extracts exactly the algebraic content of that spacing.

VI

Homogeneous Coordinates

Projective geometry's natural language is homogeneous coordinates: a point in projective n-space is represented not by n numbers but by n+1 numbers, defined only up to a common scalar multiple. The point [x, y, z] and [2x, 2y, 2z] are the same projective point.

This redundancy is not a defect — it is what makes projective geometry work. It allows "points at infinity" (where parallel lines meet) to be treated as ordinary points, and it makes projective transformations linear.

Euclidean point

A point in the plane: (x, y). Two coordinates, one point. No redundancy.

Parallel lines never meet.

Projective point

A point in ℙ²: [x, y, z] ∼ [λx, λy, λz]. Three coordinates, one equivalence class.

Parallel lines meet at [1, m, 0] — a point "at infinity" in the direction of slope m.

VII

The Chromatic Triangle

Homogeneous coordinates find a vivid application in colour theory. Assign the three RGB primaries to the vertices of a triangle with homogeneous coordinates [1,0,0], [0,1,0], [0,0,1]. The secondary colours then arise as midpoints under modular addition — and the centroid [1,1,1] represents white, the convergence of all primaries.

The projective reading

In homogeneous coordinates, [1,1,0] and [2,2,0] are the same projective point — both represent yellow. The colour triangle is a genuine projective space: colours are defined by ratios of components, not absolute values. This is why chromaticity (hue and saturation) is projective, while luminance (brightness) is the scaling degree of freedom that homogeneous coordinates quotient out.

Chromatic triples as a mapping

The RGB assignment defines a mapping fC : ℤ₊⁴ → ℝ³, where quadruples of positive integers (e.g. Fibonacci quadruplets) are mapped to RGB colour values via ratios. This mapping is neither injective (different quadruplets can produce the same colour) nor surjective (not all colours are reachable). The non-injectivity mirrors the non-injectivity of the perimeter-to-area ratio P/A for Pythagorean triples: different geometric objects can share the same projective invariant, because projective geometry deliberately discards the information that would distinguish them.

VIII

Why This Matters for Physics

Projective geometry sits at the top of Klein's Erlangen hierarchy because it has the most symmetry and the least structure. It cannot measure distances, angles, or even parallel directions. It can only say which points are collinear and compute their cross-ratio.

This is precisely its role in the conformal electrodynamics story. The descent from projective through conformal and metric geometry to empirical measurement is a progressive breaking of symmetry — each step adds structure (angles, then distances, then coupling strengths) at the cost of invariance. The cross-ratio, as the sole projective invariant, represents the most austere form of physical content: what can be said about four events without any notion of distance, duration, or field strength.

Projective geometry is the mathematics of what survives when everything but incidence is stripped away. The cross-ratio is its only invariant — the last thing standing before measurement begins.
