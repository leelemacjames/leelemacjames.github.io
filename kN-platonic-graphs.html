§1

Euler's Polyhedron Formula

Every convex polyhedron satisfies Euler's relation between vertices V, edges E, and faces F:

V − E + F = 2

The five Platonic solids are the only convex polyhedra whose faces are all congruent regular polygons meeting the same way at every vertex. Each is determined by a pair of integers: p, the number of edges per face, and q, the number of faces meeting at each vertex. The Schläfli symbol {p, q} encodes this — {3, 3} for the tetrahedron, {4, 3} for the cube, {3, 4} for the octahedron, {5, 3} for the dodecahedron, {3, 5} for the icosahedron.

From Euler's formula and the double-counting identity 2E = pF = qV, the entire geometry is fixed once you choose {p, q}.

The function k(N) returns the smallest integer greater than 1 that does not divide N. For every Platonic solid, k(F) equals the number of edges per face — the Schläfli parameter p. This is not a deep theorem but it is a pleasing coincidence rooted in how divisible the face-counts are: the dense divisor sets of 4, 6, 8, 12, and 20 each have their first gap landing exactly at the polygon order.

§3

Graph Duality & Adjacency Spectra

Each Platonic solid has a natural graph: vertices become nodes, geometric edges become graph edges. The adjacency matrix A encodes connectivity — entry Aij = 1 when vertices i and j share an edge, 0 otherwise. Since no vertex connects to itself, the trace is always zero, and so the eigenvalues sum to zero.

Duality swaps faces and vertices while preserving edges: the cube (8 vertices, 6 faces) is dual to the octahedron (6 vertices, 8 faces); the dodecahedron (20 vertices, 12 faces) is dual to the icosahedron (12 vertices, 20 faces). The tetrahedron is self-dual. This duality transposes the adjacency structure — rows in one correspond to faces in the other.

Chromatic Structure

The chromatic number χ is the minimum number of colours needed to colour vertices so that no two adjacent vertices share a colour. The graph below renders each solid's vertex graph with a valid colouring. Platonic symmetry constrains χ tightly: the tetrahedron is K4 so χ = 4, while the cube and dodecahedron (bipartite-like structures) need fewer.

By Vizing's theorem, the edge chromatic number (chromatic index) is either Δ or Δ+1, where Δ is the maximum vertex degree. For all five Platonic graphs, the edge chromatic number equals the vertex degree — they are all Class 1.

The Golden Ratio in the Spectrum

The eigenvalues of the dodecahedron and icosahedron are not arbitrary irrationals. They are built from exactly the same algebraic numbers that govern the Fibonacci sequence: the golden ratio φ = (1+√5)/2, its conjugate ψ = (1−√5)/2 = −1/φ, and their common radical √5.

Compare these with the three solids whose geometry involves no pentagons: the tetrahedron (eigenvalues 3, −1), the cube (3, 1, −1, −3), and the octahedron (4, 0, −2). All integers. No √5, no φ. The golden ratio enters the adjacency spectrum precisely when the solid has pentagonal symmetry — pentagonal faces for the dodecahedron, pentagonal vertex figures for the icosahedron.

The tridiagonal connection

In the Fibonacci essay "Why i Makes Fibonacci Inevitable", we saw that the tridiagonal continuant matrix with off-diagonal entries x has determinant satisfying the recurrence Kn = Kn−1 − x²Kn−2. Setting x = i flips the minus to a plus, giving Fibonacci. The characteristic polynomial of the 2×2 case is λ² − λ − 1 = 0, whose roots are exactly φ and ψ.

The adjacency spectra of both the dodecahedron and icosahedron contain ±√5 as eigenvalues. Since √5 = φ − ψ = 2φ − 1, these live in the same quadratic extension ℚ(√5) as the golden ratio. The characteristic polynomial of each must have λ² − 5 as a factor — the minimal polynomial of √5 over ℚ — and this is the same radical that appears in the quadratic formula for the Fibonacci companion matrix.

Pentagonal symmetry ↔ golden algebraic extension

The tridiagonal band structure that produces Fibonacci and the adjacency structure that produces the Platonic spectra are different matrices, but they share the same algebraic substrate. The eigenvalue φ is not merely a numerical curiosity appearing in unrelated places — it is the fundamental unit of the quadratic field ℚ(√5), and any matrix whose structure encodes pentagonal or golden-ratio geometry will have its spectrum fall in that field.

The cube and octahedron, built from squares and triangles, have integer eigenvalues — they live over ℚ. The dodecahedron and icosahedron, built from pentagons (or pentagonal vertex arrangements), require the richer number field. The tetrahedron, being self-dual and triangular, also stays over ℚ. The algebraic complexity of the spectrum tracks the geometric complexity of the underlying polygon.

Spectral summary

Triangles and squares → ℤ eigenvalues.   Pentagons → ℚ(√5) eigenvalues.   The smallest non-divisor k(N) detects the polygon order from the face count. The eigenvalue field detects it from the adjacency structure. Two different routes to the same geometric fact.
