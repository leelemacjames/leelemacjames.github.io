Ignorance in Portfolios

How network topology shapes belief convergence and the drift from equal-weight

I. The Markowitz Starting Point

Modern portfolio theory begins with a deceptively simple claim: investors are rational agents who balance expected return against risk. Given a universe of assets, each with expected return \(E_i\) and a covariance structure \(\Sigma\), the investor seeks to maximise a utility function:

\( U = \mathbf{w}^T \mathbf{E} \;-\; \lambda \, \mathbf{w}^T \Sigma \, \mathbf{w} \)

Here \(\mathbf{w}\) is the vector of portfolio weights (how much of your capital sits in each asset), and \(\lambda\) is the investor's risk aversion — a scalar that governs how aggressively they tilt away from the safe, diversified baseline toward assets they believe offer higher returns. A high \(\lambda\) means the investor demands a large expected return premium before concentrating their portfolio; a low \(\lambda\) means they chase even modest edges.

The critical insight is what happens at maximum ignorance. When an investor has no information to distinguish one asset from another — no edge, no signal, no view — the optimal portfolio is equal-weighted: \(w_i = 1/N\) for all \(N\) assets. This is the portfolio of total epistemic neutrality. Every departure from equal-weight is a knowledge claim — an assertion that some assets deserve more of your capital than others, backed by beliefs about return differences.

In the simple two-asset case (asset A versus asset B, where B has a true excess return \(\varepsilon > 0\) over A), the optimal weight in B reduces to:

\( w_B^* = \frac{1}{2} + \frac{p_i \cdot \varepsilon}{2\lambda\sigma^2} \)

where \(p_i\) is the investor's credence that B really is better — their subjective probability that \(\varepsilon > 0\). When \(p_i = 0.5\) (complete uncertainty), the tilt vanishes and we recover equal-weighting. When \(p_i \to 1\) (full conviction), the weight shifts maximally toward B, constrained only by risk aversion \(\lambda\).

The key question

How does \(p_i\) — the investor's credence — actually evolve? In textbook Markowitz, it's taken as given: the investor "knows" the expected returns. But in reality, beliefs about returns are socially constructed. Analysts talk to each other. Consensus estimates propagate through networks. Research gets shared, discounted, amplified, or ignored depending on who trusts whom. This is exactly the problem studied in the paper Ignorance in Social Networks — and the mapping between the two frameworks turns out to be structurally exact.

II. The Mapping: Credence Updates as Return Estimation

In the epistemic network model, each agent holds a credence \(p_i \in [0,1]\) that action B is superior to action A. Agents run experiments (binomial trials), share results with their network neighbours, and update their beliefs. Under Bayes' rule (full trust), agents treat all received evidence at face value. Under Jeffrey's rule with a homophily assumption, agents discount evidence from neighbours whose beliefs differ from their own — the greater the disagreement, the larger the discount.

The portfolio mapping works as follows. Each agent's credence \(p_i\) becomes their conviction about asset B's excess return. The portfolio weight \(w_B\) is then derived from that credence via the Markowitz optimality condition above. Three consequences follow immediately:

Mistrust acts as emergent risk aversion. When agents discount their neighbours' evidence (Jeffrey updating), their credences converge more slowly toward the truth. In portfolio terms, this is indistinguishable from having a higher \(\lambda\): the portfolio stays closer to equal-weight for longer than it should, given that the evidence is factive (true). Social distrust inflates effective risk aversion.

Network topology shapes the trajectory of portfolio weights. The paper's central finding is that networks with the same overall connectivity but different shapes produce significantly different convergence times. In portfolio terms: it's not just how many analysts inform your views, but how they're connected to each other, that determines how quickly the market portfolio departs from equal-weight toward the optimal allocation.

Polarisation is a barbell that never rebalances. When mistrust is strong enough (\(m > 1\)), the network can reach stable polarisation: one subgroup converges to near-certainty that B is better, while the rest refuse to update. The "market portfolio" — the mean across all agents — sits at an intermediate weight that nobody actually holds. This mirrors a market split between momentum believers and sceptics, where the consensus price is an artefact of averaging irreconcilable positions.

III. Network Topologies: A Primer

The simulation below lets you run the credence-update model on four network types. Each topology has a real-world archetype in financial information flow:

Complete Network
Archetype: Bloomberg terminal firehose
Every agent is connected to every other. Information floods in from all sides simultaneously. Convergence is fast, but the group is vulnerable to early misleading evidence cascading everywhere at once — producing false consensus. Think of a trading floor where every desk hears every rumour instantly.
Cycle Network
Archetype: Whisper chain / regional analyst circuit
Each agent talks only to their two immediate neighbours; information must propagate around the ring. Slow but reliable — misleading evidence gets diluted over many hops before reaching the whole group. Like a chain of regional analysts, each informing the next, with no shortcut to broadcast.
Star Network
Archetype: Dominant voice — Buffett, Dalio, central bank
One central hub connects to everyone; peripheral agents connect only to the hub. Convergence is the fastest of all — the hub anchors expectations. But if the hub is misinformed or captured (a compromised central analyst, a disinformant occupying the key node), the entire network is corrupted.
Barabási-Albert Network
Archetype: Twitter/X financial discourse, "rich get richer"
Built by preferential attachment — popular nodes attract more connections, creating a few highly-connected hubs and many peripheral agents. This mimics social media dynamics where a few influential voices dominate. Unequal neighbourhood sizes create heterogeneous information flow, and the paper finds this variance itself delays convergence.

Simulation Parameters
Parameter Guide

ε (epsilon) is the true excess return of B over A. At 0.001, the signal is nearly invisible in noise — agents need many trials to detect it. At 0.05, it's obvious quickly. Smaller ε magnifies all topology and trust effects.

Trials per step controls how many experiments each agent runs before sharing. More trials = clearer evidence per round, faster convergence.

Update rule is the crucial switch. Under Bayes, agents fully trust their neighbours' reports. Under Jeffrey (m = 1), agents discount evidence in proportion to credence distance — they trust those who agree with them more. At m = 2, discount is aggressive enough to produce stable polarisation: some agents permanently refuse to update.

λ (risk aversion) controls how much credence must accumulate before the portfolio weight shifts visibly from 0.5. Higher λ = more inertia at equal-weight, amplifying the delay caused by mistrust.

V. What This Means for Portfolio Theory

The standard Markowitz framework takes expected returns as inputs — exogenous numbers fed into the optimiser. But in practice, those numbers are the output of a social process: analysts estimating, sharing, debating, and discounting each other's research. This simulation shows that the structure of that social process — who talks to whom, and how much they trust each other — materially affects how quickly the portfolio converges from equal-weight ignorance to informed allocation.

The implication for technical analysis is also visible here. Technical analysis bypasses fundamental evidence entirely, updating beliefs based solely on other agents' revealed positions (price action). In this framework, that's equivalent to agents who never run their own experiments — they only observe neighbours' credences. The simulation predicts this is maximally susceptible to herding and false consensus, because there's no independent evidence generation to anchor beliefs to ground truth. The \(\varepsilon\) never gets discovered; the network just amplifies its own noise.

More broadly, these results suggest that the geometry of information flow deserves a place alongside covariance matrices and return estimates in any serious account of portfolio dynamics. The invisible hand moves at the speed of trust — and trust, it turns out, has a topology.
