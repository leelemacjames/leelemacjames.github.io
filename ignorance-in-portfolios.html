Ignorance in Portfolios

How network topology shapes belief convergence and the drift from equal-weight

I. The Markowitz Objective

The Markowitz utility function is

\( U = \mathbf{w}^T \mathbf{E} \;-\; \lambda \, \mathbf{w}^T \Sigma \, \mathbf{w} \)

where \(\mathbf{w}\) is the portfolio weight vector, \(\mathbf{E}\) the expected returns, \(\Sigma\) the covariance matrix, and \(\lambda\) a scalar encoding risk aversion. High \(\lambda\): the investor demands a large return premium before concentrating the portfolio. Low \(\lambda\): they chase modest edges.

At maximum ignorance — no information distinguishing one asset from another — the optimal portfolio is equal-weighted: \(w_i = 1/N\). Every departure from equal-weight is a knowledge claim.

In the two-asset case (A with return 0.5, B with true return \(0.5 + \varepsilon\)):

\( w_B^* = \frac{1}{2} + \frac{p_i \cdot \varepsilon}{2\lambda\sigma^2} \)

where \(p_i\) is the agent's credence that \(\varepsilon > 0\). At \(p_i = 0.5\): equal-weight. At \(p_i \to 1\): maximal tilt toward B. At \(p_i < 0.5\): the agent backs the wrong asset. In textbook Markowitz, \(p_i\) is taken as given. In reality, it is socially constructed — and that construction is the subject of this simulation. See the accompanying paper at leelemacjames.github.io.

II. The Mapping

Each agent holds credence \(p_i \in [0,1]\) that B is superior. Agents run binomial trials, share results with neighbours, and update. Under Bayes' rule, evidence is taken at face value. Under Jeffrey's rule, agents discount evidence from neighbours whose credences differ — the larger the disagreement, the larger the discount.

Each agent's credence \(p_i\) maps to conviction about B's excess return. The portfolio weight \(w_B\) follows from the Markowitz condition above. Three consequences:

Mistrust is emergent risk aversion. Jeffrey discounting slows credence convergence. In portfolio terms: the weights stay near equal-weight longer than warranted. Social distrust inflates effective \(\lambda\).

Topology shapes weight trajectories. Networks with identical connectivity but different shapes produce different convergence times. It is not how many analysts inform your views, but how they are connected to each other.

Polarisation is a barbell that never rebalances. At \(m > 1\), the network fractures: one subgroup converges to near-certainty, the rest refuse to update. The mean weight is held by nobody — a phantom portfolio.

III. Network Topologies

Four network types, each with a financial archetype:

Complete Network
Archetype: Bloomberg terminal firehose
Every agent is connected to every other. Information floods in from all sides simultaneously. Convergence is fast, but the group is vulnerable to early misleading evidence cascading everywhere at once — producing false consensus. Think of a trading floor where every desk hears every rumour instantly.
Cycle Network
Archetype: Whisper chain / regional analyst circuit
Each agent talks only to their two immediate neighbours; information must propagate around the ring. Slow but reliable — misleading evidence gets diluted over many hops before reaching the whole group. Like a chain of regional analysts, each informing the next, with no shortcut to broadcast.
Star Network
Archetype: Dominant voice — Buffett, Dalio, central bank
One central hub connects to everyone; peripheral agents connect only to the hub. Convergence is the fastest of all — the hub anchors expectations. But if the hub is misinformed or captured (a compromised central analyst, a disinformant occupying the key node), the entire network is corrupted.
Barabási-Albert Network
Archetype: Twitter/X financial discourse, "rich get richer"
Built by preferential attachment — popular nodes attract more connections, creating a few highly-connected hubs and many peripheral agents. This mimics social media dynamics where a few influential voices dominate. Unequal neighbourhood sizes create heterogeneous information flow, and the paper finds this variance itself delays convergence.

Simulation Parameters
Parameter Guide

ε (epsilon) is the true excess return of B over A. At 0.001, the signal is nearly invisible in noise — agents need many trials to detect it. At 0.05, it's obvious quickly. Smaller ε magnifies all topology and trust effects.

Trials per step controls how many experiments each agent runs before sharing. More trials = clearer evidence per round, faster convergence.

Update rule is the crucial switch. Under Bayes, agents fully trust their neighbours' reports. Under Jeffrey (m = 1), agents discount evidence in proportion to credence distance — they trust those who agree with them more. At m = 2, discount is aggressive enough to produce stable polarisation: some agents permanently refuse to update.

λ (risk aversion) controls how much credence must accumulate before the portfolio weight shifts visibly from 0.5. Higher λ = more inertia at equal-weight, amplifying the delay caused by mistrust.

V. Implications

The Markowitz framework takes expected returns as exogenous inputs. In practice, those numbers are outputs of a social process whose structure — who talks to whom, and how much they trust each other — materially affects convergence from ignorance to informed allocation.

Technical analysis bypasses fundamental evidence, updating beliefs from other agents' revealed positions. In this framework: agents who never run experiments, observing only neighbours' credences. No independent evidence generation, no mechanism for truth discovery. The \(\varepsilon\) is never found. The network amplifies its own noise. Fibonacci retracements and moving average crossovers are coordination artefacts: they work when enough agents act on them, not because they reveal anything about the return structure.

The formal treatment of these results, including the \(\lambda_{\text{eff}} = \lambda/\alpha\) isomorphism and the endogenous risk aversion specification, is in the accompanying paper: Networked Belief Formation and Portfolio Allocation.
