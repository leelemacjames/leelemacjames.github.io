How Tall
Is That?

On measuring heights and distances you cannot reach — using nothing but shadows, your thumb, a clinometer, and the geometry of similar triangles.

§ 01

The problem of the unreachable

How did the ancient Egyptians know how tall the pyramids were? They could not climb to the top with a measuring tape. Eratosthenes measured the circumference of the Earth in 240 BC — without ever leaving Egypt. Hipparchus estimated the distance to the Moon. None of them had telescopes, satellites, or electronic rangefinders.

Their tool was similarity — the idea that two shapes can have exactly the same proportions even though one is much larger than the other. If you understand this idea, you can measure almost anything.

The Core Idea

When two triangles have the same angles, their corresponding sides are always in the same ratio. It doesn't matter how big one is compared to the other. Shrink one, enlarge the other — the ratios stay fixed. This is what makes measurement possible at a distance.

Two similar triangles: same angles, sides in proportion. The ratio 1/3 appears everywhere.

Look at the two triangles above. The big one is three times the size of the small one — but the ratio of base to height is identical: $\frac{4}{3} = \frac{12}{9}$. This is not a coincidence. It is a theorem. And it lets you calculate an unknown height from three known measurements.

small height / small base = big height / big base
§ 02

Thales and the shadow method

Around 600 BC, the Greek mathematician Thales of Miletus reputedly measured the height of the Great Pyramid by its shadow. His method was elegantly simple: he waited until his own shadow was exactly as long as he was tall. At that moment, every shadow on Earth was equal in length to the object casting it. So the pyramid's shadow revealed its height directly.

But you don't need to wait for that special moment. On any sunny day, the Sun is at a fixed angle in the sky, so all vertical objects cast shadows in the same ratio:

The Sun's parallel rays create similar triangles. Person and tree cast shadows in the same ratio as their heights.

Shadow Method Formula

$$\frac{\text{your height}\ (h)}{\text{your shadow}\ (s)} = \frac{\text{tree height}\ (H)}{\text{tree shadow}\ (S)} \qquad \Rightarrow \qquad H = h \times \frac{S}{s}$$

Stand upright in sunlight. Measure your height $h$ and the length of your shadow $s$.

Walk to the tree. Measure the length of the tree's shadow $S$ from base to tip.

Calculate: $H = h \times \dfrac{S}{s}$. Done — no climbing, no guessing.

§ 03

The thumb method — a ruler at arm's length

You don't always have a sunny day, and some objects don't cast convenient shadows. Here is a method that works any time: hold your thumb at arm's length and use it as a ruler — but a ruler for measuring angles, not lengths directly.

The trick relies on a simple fact: at arm's length (roughly 60 cm), your thumb is about 2 cm wide. Any object that appears to be the same width as your thumb at arm's length is approximately 30 times its actual width away from you. Why? Because the ratio $\frac{2}{60} = \frac{1}{30}$ — the same similar-triangle ratio applies.

Your thumb at arm's length creates a tiny triangle. The building creates a similar larger one. If the building appears thumb-width, it is 30 thumb-widths away — and 30 thumb-widths tall.

Thumb Method — General Form

If an object appears to be $n$ thumb-widths tall at arm's length, and you know the distance $D$ to the object:

$$H = D \times \frac{\text{apparent thumb-widths} \times 2\,\text{cm}}{60\,\text{cm}} = D \times \frac{n}{30}$$ Equally: if the object is exactly one thumb-width and you know its real height $H$:

$$D = 30 \times H$$

§ 04

The angle of elevation and the clinometer

Both methods so far required you to measure lengths. But there is a third approach — use the angle at which you look up at the top of an object. This angle is called the angle of elevation. If you know this angle and your horizontal distance from the object, you can find its height — again using similar triangles.

The instrument for measuring angles of elevation is called a clinometer (or inclinometer). You can make one from a protractor, a straw, and a plumb-bob (a weight on a string). When you look through the straw at the top of a building, the string hangs straight down, and the angle it makes with the protractor gives the elevation angle.

The angle of elevation θ and the horizontal distance d define a right-angled triangle. The height x above eye level can be found from the ratio x/d.

Now here is where Pythagoras has already given you a head start. You know about right-angled triangles. In the diagram above, the horizontal distance $d$, the height above eye level $x$, and the line of sight form a right-angled triangle. The ratio $x/d$ depends only on the angle $\theta$. Later, you will learn that this ratio has a name — the tangent — but for now, you can read it from a table or calculate it using the similar-triangle principle.

Angle of Elevation Method

If you know the angle of elevation $\theta$ and the horizontal distance $d$:

The similar triangle gives: opposite / adjacent is a fixed ratio for each angle.

For $\theta = 45°$: the triangle is isosceles, so $x = d$ (the height equals the distance).
For $\theta = 30°$: $x \approx 0.577 \times d$ (roughly half the distance).
For $\theta = 60°$: $x \approx 1.732 \times d$ (taller than it is far).

Add your eye height $e$: Total height $H = x + e$

Making a clinometer

Take a protractor (half-circle, 0–180°). Tape a drinking straw along the straight edge.

Tie a small weight (a key, an eraser) to a piece of thread. Attach the thread to the centre point of the straight edge so it hangs freely as a plumb-bob.

Look through the straw at the top of the object. Hold still. Your partner reads the angle where the hanging thread crosses the protractor scale. The angle of elevation is 90° minus that reading.

Measure your horizontal distance to the base of the object. Use the calculator above to find the height.

§ 05

Choosing your method

You now have three different approaches. Which is best depends on what you can measure:

Method What you measure Best when Limitation
Shadow Your height, your shadow, object's shadow Sunny day, shadow accessible Needs sunshine; shadow may be inaccessible
Thumb Distance to object, apparent angular size Known distance or known height Rough estimate only (arm length varies)
Clinometer Horizontal distance, angle of elevation Any weather, most precise Need to measure horizontal distance

All three methods use the same underlying geometry: similar triangles. You are always constructing a small, measurable triangle that is similar to a large, unmeasurable one. The ratio between the two is the key.

Extension — for the curious

The Cosmic Distance Ladder

Everything we have done so far works for objects within a few kilometres. But astronomers use the exact same logic — the logic of similar triangles and known ratios — to measure distances to stars, galaxies, and the edges of the observable universe. The distances are so vast that each method only reaches so far. So astronomers build a ladder: each rung uses the previous one as its foundation.

1
Direct measurement — radar ranging

Bounce radio waves off nearby planets and time the echo. Speed of light × time / 2 = distance. Gives us the exact size of the Solar System. This is our anchor.

up to
~5 AU
2
Parallax — the geometry of two viewpoints

As Earth orbits the Sun, nearby stars appear to shift against the distant background — exactly like your thumb shifting when you close one eye, then the other. The angle of shift (parallax angle) and the known Earth-Sun distance form a similar triangle. This works out to about 100 light-years.

up to
~100 ly
3
Standard candles — Cepheid variables

Certain stars (Cepheids) pulsate with a period that reveals their true brightness. Compare true brightness to apparent brightness and the inverse-square law gives distance — the same ratio logic, now in light intensity. Hubble used this to prove the Milky Way was not the whole universe.

up to
~100 Mly
4
Type Ia supernovae — standard explosions

A certain type of stellar explosion always reaches the same peak brightness. Like a very reliable candle, comparing its apparent brightness to its known true brightness gives distance across billions of light-years. This is how we discovered the universe's expansion is accelerating.

up to
~10 Gly
5
Redshift and Hubble's Law

The most distant objects recede so fast that their light is stretched (redshifted). The amount of redshift, calibrated against the lower rungs of the ladder, gives distance to the edge of the observable universe — 46 billion light-years.

up to
46 Gly
The unbroken thread

From your thumb at arm's length to the edge of the observable universe — every distance measurement is built on the same principle: a known ratio, a measurable length, and the logic of similar triangles. The instruments change; the geometry does not.

Parallax and your two eyes: Close your left eye and point at something across the room. Now switch — open the left, close the right. Your finger appears to jump. The angle it jumps through, combined with the known distance between your eyes, forms a similar triangle that your brain uses unconsciously to perceive depth. You have been doing parallax geometry since you were a baby.
