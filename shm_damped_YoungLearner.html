Before we start — from triangles to waves

You already know sine from right-angled triangles: sin(θ) is the ratio of the opposite side to the hypotenuse. But that definition freezes sine at a single angle. There is a second way to think about it — one that makes sine into something that moves.

Imagine a point moving at constant speed around a circle. Watch only its height — how far up or down it sits. As the point goes around, the height rises, peaks, falls, passes through zero, goes negative, reaches a minimum, and returns. Over and over, identically. That smooth, endlessly repeating up-and-down trace is a sine wave.

Preamble — Sine from a rotating point

The gold point rotates at constant speed. Its vertical height — projected rightward — traces the sine wave. The connection to the triangle: at any moment, the height is the opposite side of a right triangle drawn from the centre.

Two numbers describe any sine wave completely. The amplitude is the maximum height — how far from the centre it reaches. The period is how long one full rotation takes. Change the period and the wave stretches or compresses horizontally. Change the amplitude and it grows or shrinks vertically.

This is the shape that oscillating systems trace out naturally. Springs, pendulums, sound, the voltage in your phone's circuit: all of them produce motion with this same smooth, repeating form.

§1 — Simple Harmonic Motion

Attach a mass to a spring. Pull it to one side and release it. The spring pulls the mass back toward the middle — but the mass overshoots. Now the spring pushes from the other side. The mass overshoots again. This back-and-forth continues, and the motion it traces is exactly that sine wave we just described.

We call this Simple Harmonic Motion (SHM). The key feature is that the spring always pulls or pushes proportionally to how far the mass has moved. Twice as far from the centre means twice the force pulling it back. This proportionality is what produces the smooth sinusoidal shape.

The central mechanism
Far from centre → strong restoring force → fast acceleration back. Near the centre → weak restoring force → gentle push. At the centre → no spring force at all → but the mass is now moving at full speed, so it overshoots. This cycle sustains itself indefinitely.

Amplitude, period, frequency

The amplitude is how far the mass travels from the centre to its extreme position. The period is the time for one complete back-and-forth. The frequency is how many complete cycles happen per second — measured in Hertz (Hz). One Hz means one full cycle per second.

Here is the most surprising fact about SHM: the period does not depend on amplitude. Pull the mass twice as far and it oscillates at exactly the same rate. A pendulum clock keeps time for this very reason — swing it harder and it doesn't speed up. The period is set entirely by the stiffness of the spring and the mass attached to it.

Where is the mass moving fastest?

At the extreme positions — the tips of the swing — the mass momentarily stops before reversing. All its energy is stored in the compressed or stretched spring, none in motion. At the centre, the spring is at its natural length — no stored energy — so all the energy has become speed. The mass moves fastest at the centre, slowest at the extremes. This constant trade-off between stored energy and kinetic energy is what keeps the oscillation going.

§2 — What Happens When Energy Leaks Out?

In reality, oscillations die away. A plucked guitar string goes quiet. A pendulum slows. The spring-mass system on your desk wouldn't bounce forever — air resistance, friction, vibrations escaping into the table: all of these drain energy from the system. This energy loss is called damping.

The visible effect is clear: the amplitude — the size of the swings — shrinks with each cycle. The mass doesn't suddenly stop; the peaks get progressively lower, the motion fading toward stillness. The shape of that fading follows an exponential decay — halving at regular intervals, getting smaller and smaller but in principle never quite reaching zero.

Three qualitatively different behaviours

Underdamped — the system oscillates, but each swing is smaller than the last. This is the familiar case: a swing, a tuning fork in air, a bouncy car suspension on a bumpy road.

Critically damped — the system returns to the centre as fast as physically possible, without overshooting even once. Engineers designing door dampers and car shock absorbers often target this: the door closes firmly, the car settles immediately, no wobble.

Overdamped — damping is so strong it overwhelms the oscillation entirely. The system creeps slowly back toward centre with no bouncing — but more slowly than critical damping would achieve. Think of lowering a heavy object into thick oil.

§3 — Submerging the Spring in a Fluid

Now make the damping physical. Lower the spring-mass system into a container of fluid. The fluid resists the motion of the mass: the faster it moves, the harder the fluid pushes back. This resistance is what drains energy from the oscillation — and different fluids resist motion very differently.

Water — ordinary drag

Water is an ordinary (Newtonian) fluid: its resistance to motion scales simply with speed. Move twice as fast, get twice the drag. This gives clean, well-behaved damping — the oscillation decays with the familiar shrinking-envelope shape from §2. The envelope is smooth and the rate of decay is constant throughout.

Cornstarch and water — something strange

Mix cornstarch and water in roughly a 2:1 ratio by mass and the result is deeply peculiar. It flows freely when handled gently. But impact it quickly — punch it, run on it, drop a ball onto it — and it momentarily solidifies. This is called shear thickening: the resistance to motion increases disproportionately with speed. This mixture is called oobleck, after Dr. Seuss.

The consequence for our oscillating mass is not obvious at first glance. Slow oscillations — gentle, small swings — barely feel the difference between oobleck and water. Fast oscillations — large swings, high velocity — are crushed far more aggressively than water would manage. So the decay is non-uniform: the first few large swings are squashed quickly, but the final small wobbles linger much longer than you might expect. The decay is fast at first, then slows.

Why does oobleck solidify under impact?
At rest, the cornstarch particles are loosely packed with water between them — the water lubricates and allows flow. A sudden force tries to compress the particles together faster than the water can escape. The particles jam against each other, creating a temporary rigid structure. Release the force, the water flows back in, and it liquefies again. You are making and breaking a microscopic traffic jam thousands of times per second.

§4 — A Different Way to See the Motion

So far we have plotted position against time — the familiar wave. There is a more revealing picture: plot position against velocity at every moment simultaneously. This is called a phase portrait.

In undamped SHM the phase portrait is a closed ellipse. This makes sense: the system returns to exactly the same state — same position, same velocity — every cycle, and the path loops back on itself perfectly. The ellipse is fat when there is a lot of velocity for a given position (fast oscillation) and tall when position is large relative to velocity.

For a damped oscillator, the path spirals inward. Each cycle the system arrives back with a little less amplitude and a little less velocity than before — the ellipse shrinks. The speed of the inward spiral reflects the strength of the damping. Set damping to zero and watch the closed ellipse re-appear.

§5 — What Have We Built?

We started with the sine function you know from triangles, recast it as the trace of a rotating point, and used it to describe the natural motion of a spring-mass system. Then we introduced damping — energy leaving the system — and discovered three qualitatively different regimes depending on how strong that damping is. Finally we submerged the system in increasingly strange fluids, arriving at oobleck, whose resistance to motion grows disproportionately with speed.

Damping is not a complication added to a perfect theory. It is the physics of the real world: every real oscillator loses energy, and engineers exploit this deliberately. Car suspensions aim for near-critical damping so the car settles without bouncing. Earthquake-resistant buildings use fluid dampers in their cores. Your eardrum's motion is coupled to fluid-filled chambers in the cochlea that damp different frequencies with different efficiencies — this is partly how you distinguish pitch.

Oobleck sits at an unusual boundary — a material that is neither solid nor liquid depending on how you treat it. Research into shear-thickening fluids is ongoing: flexible body armour, protective packaging for electronics, and even experimental road surfaces have drawn on this same physics.

Questions to carry with you
1. Why does the period of a spring-mass system not depend on amplitude? What in the mechanism explains this?
2. A heavier mass on the same spring — faster or slower, and why?
3. Why does critical damping return to equilibrium faster than overdamping, even though overdamping is stronger?
4. What would the phase portrait look like for the oobleck case compared to water?
5. Can you think of a situation in everyday life where you would actually want overdamping rather than critical damping?

Appendix — The Mathematics

The physics above can be fully experienced without these. They are here for those who want to know what is behind the simulations.

Hooke's Law

Restoring force
F = −kx
k is spring stiffness (N/m), x is displacement. The minus sign means the force always opposes displacement.

SHM — position as a sinusoidal function of time

Position in SHM
x(t) = A cos(ωt + φ)
A = amplitude, ω = angular frequency = √(k/m) in rad/s, φ = phase offset. Period T = 2π/ω, Frequency f = 1/T.

Adding a damping force

Damped equation of motion
mẍ + bẋ + kx = 0
b = damping coefficient (kg/s). Three forces always in balance: inertia (mẍ), drag (bẋ), restoring force (kx).
Damping ratio — determines regime
ζ = b / (2√mk)
ζ < 1: underdamped. ζ = 1: critically damped. ζ > 1: overdamped.
Underdamped solution
x(t) = A · e^(−γt) · cos(ω_d t)
γ = b/2m (decay rate of the exponential envelope). ω_d = √(ω₀² − γ²), the damped oscillation frequency — always slightly less than ω₀.

Non-Newtonian drag — oobleck

Power-law drag (Ostwald–de Waele)
F_drag = −K |v|^(n−1) · v
n = 1: water (linear). n > 1: shear-thickening (oobleck uses n ≈ 2–3). Simulator 3 uses Runge-Kutta numerical integration of this force.
